# Shelter Building and Electrical Basics

## Emergency Shelter Construction

### Principles
1. **Protection from elements**: Wind, rain, cold, heat, insects.
2. **Insulation**: Dead air space is the best insulator.
3. **Location**: High ground (avoid flooding), wind-sheltered, near water source, away from dead trees.
4. **Size**: Small shelters retain heat better. Just enough room to lie down and sit up.

### Debris Hut
Best for: Cold weather, solo shelter, no tools needed.
1. Find or create a ridgepole 9-12 feet long, propped at one end 3 feet high.
2. Lean branches along both sides at 45-degree angles.
3. Layer with smaller sticks to create a lattice.
4. Pile leaves, grass, or pine needles 2-3 feet thick on the lattice.
5. Add a final layer of branches to hold insulation in place.
6. Fill the interior with dry leaves for ground insulation (minimum 6 inches).
7. Build a small door plug of leaves.

### Lean-To
Best for: Warm weather, group shelter, fire reflection.
1. Lash a horizontal ridgepole between two trees, 4-5 feet high.
2. Lean poles from ground to ridgepole at 45 degrees.
3. Layer with branches, leaves, or tarp.
4. Build a reflector fire 4-6 feet in front — heat reflects off the lean-to back.
5. Can be upgraded with side walls.

### Tarp Shelters
With a 10x10 tarp and paracord:
- **A-frame**: Ridge line between trees, drape tarp over, stake edges. Best all-around.
- **Lean-to**: One edge high on a line, opposite edge staked to ground. Good for fire reflection.
- **Diamond fly**: Corner of tarp to tree as peak, opposite corner staked down, sides staked out. Quick setup.

## Basic Construction Techniques

### Lashing (Rope Joints)
- **Square lashing**: Joining two poles at right angles. Wrap alternating over/under in a square pattern. Minimum 3 wraps each direction, then frap (tighten between poles).
- **Diagonal lashing**: Joining two poles at angles. Start with a clove hitch, wrap diagonally, frap.
- **Shear lashing**: Joining two poles side by side (tripod). Wrap around both, frap between.

### Knots
- **Bowline**: Creates a fixed loop that won't slip. "Rabbit comes out of the hole, around the tree, back in the hole."
- **Clove hitch**: Quick attachment to a pole. Two loops, second over first.
- **Trucker's hitch**: Mechanical advantage for tightening lines. Creates a 3:1 pull.
- **Taut-line hitch**: Adjustable loop for guy lines.

## Electrical Systems Basics

### Understanding Electricity
- **Voltage (V)**: Pressure that pushes electrons. Think of it as water pressure.
- **Current (A)**: Flow rate of electrons. Think of it as water flow.
- **Resistance (Ohm)**: Opposition to flow. Like pipe diameter.
- **Power (W)**: Watts = Volts × Amps. How much work is being done.
- **Ohm's Law**: V = I × R (Voltage = Current × Resistance).

### Wire Sizing (AWG - American Wire Gauge)
Lower number = thicker wire = more current capacity.
- **14 AWG**: 15 amps (household lighting circuits)
- **12 AWG**: 20 amps (household outlets)
- **10 AWG**: 30 amps (dryers, large appliances)
- **8 AWG**: 40 amps (ranges, large equipment)
- **6 AWG**: 55 amps (heavy equipment, sub-panels)

### Basic Wiring

**Wire Color Codes (US Standard)**:
- Black: Hot (live)
- White: Neutral
- Green or bare copper: Ground
- Red: Second hot (240V circuits or switch legs)

**Connecting wires**:
1. Strip 3/4 inch of insulation from wire ends.
2. Twist wires together clockwise (lineman's splice).
3. Secure with a wire nut (twist clockwise until tight).
4. Wrap with electrical tape for additional security.
5. Never connect copper and aluminum wires directly — use approved connectors.

### Solar Power Basics

**Components of an off-grid solar system**:
1. **Solar panels**: Convert sunlight to DC electricity. Rated in watts.
2. **Charge controller**: Regulates voltage from panels to battery. MPPT type is more efficient.
3. **Battery bank**: Stores energy. Lead-acid (cheaper) or lithium (lighter, longer life).
4. **Inverter**: Converts DC (battery) to AC (household power). Pure sine wave preferred.

**Sizing a basic system** (example: 500W daily use):
- Daily energy: 500W × 8 hours = 4000 Wh = 4 kWh
- Solar panels: 4000 Wh ÷ 5 sun-hours ÷ 0.8 efficiency = 1000W of panels
- Battery: 4000 Wh ÷ 12V ÷ 0.5 depth of discharge = 667 Ah battery bank
- Inverter: 1.5× maximum simultaneous load

### Generator Maintenance
- **Oil**: Check before every use. Change every 50-100 hours.
- **Air filter**: Clean or replace every 50 hours.
- **Fuel**: Use fresh fuel. Stabilizer for storage >30 days.
- **Load**: Never run at >80% capacity continuously.
- **Grounding**: Always ground portable generators. Never use indoors (carbon monoxide).

## Tool Care

### Sharpening Edged Tools
- **Files**: Push strokes only (not back and forth). 20-25 degree angle for axes, 15-20 for knives.
- **Whetstones**: Coarse (400 grit) for reshaping, medium (1000) for sharpening, fine (3000+) for honing.
- **Technique**: Maintain consistent angle. 10-15 strokes per side, alternating. Test with paper or arm hair.

### Improvised Tools
- **Hammer**: Dense hardwood branch with a heavy stone lashed to the end.
- **Saw**: Twisted wire (Gigli saw) between two handles. Or a flexible steel strip with teeth filed in.
- **Drill**: Hardwood spindle in a bow drill configuration. Works for fire-starting and soft-material drilling.
