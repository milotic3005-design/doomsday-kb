## 1. MEDICATION STABILITY & SHELF LIFE EXTENSION

### FDA/DoD Shelf Life Extension Program (SLEP)

**Source:** FDA/DoD SLEP joint program; Stability Profiles of Drug Products Extended beyond Labeled Expiration Dates (Simons et al., *Journal of Pharmaceutical Sciences*, 2006); PMC7040264.

The U.S. Department of Defense and the Food and Drug Administration jointly operate the **Shelf Life Extension Program (SLEP)**, established in 1986, to evaluate long-term effectiveness of medications in government stockpiles.

#### Key Findings
- SLEP tested **122 different drug products across 3,005 lots**
- **88% of lots** were extended at least 1 year beyond original expiration
- **Average extension: 66 months** beyond labeled expiration date
- **Maximum observed extension: 278 months** (~23 years) for optimally stored products
- Some medications (naloxone, halothane, fentanyl) demonstrated 100% lot stability for at least 4–5 years post-expiration
- **Cost savings: $13–$94 per $1 spent** on testing
- During the 2009 H1N1 pandemic, the FDA authorized use of expired Tamiflu based on SLEP data

#### What SLEP Demonstrates
SLEP data supports the assertion that many drug products, if properly stored, can remain effective well past their expiration date. However, stability is highly lot-specific and can only be verified by laboratory testing.

> **SAFETY WARNING:** SLEP is a federal program for government stockpiles only. It does not authorize civilian use of expired medications. Expiration dates exist to guarantee potency. For medications requiring precise dosing (cardiac drugs, antiepileptics, insulin), expiration dates must be strictly respected due to risk of underdosage or toxicity. Cyclines (tetracyclines including doxycycline) can become toxic after expiration — never use expired tetracyclines.

#### Practical Field Principle (Based on Published Literature)
A peer-reviewed editorial (PMC10834166) notes: "If a product has been stored in adequate conditions (protected from humidity and light, packaging intact, at a median temperature) and if no modification of aspects or solubility has been detected, it is often preferable to use an expired drug than to leave a gravely ill patient without treatment." This applies only to a true wilderness emergency when no alternatives exist.

#### Signs a Medication May Be Compromised
- Color change from original
- Unusual or absent odor
- Tablets stuck together or crumbled
- Liquids turned cloudy, separated, or precipitated
- Gels melted or resolidified in abnormal form
- Packaging integrity lost

## 2. OTC MEDICATIONS FOR WILDERNESS EMERGENCIES

**Source:** WMS Clinical Practice Guidelines for Treatment of Acute Pain in Austere Environments (2024 Update, Fink et al.); NOLS Wilderness Medicine; Center for Wilderness Safety (CWS) OTC Pain Medication curriculum; OHSU Wilderness Medical Kit Reference (Hickey, 2020)

> **WARNING:** Dosing below reflects standard adult doses from published references. Always verify patient allergies, renal/hepatic function, and pregnancy status before administration. Pediatric dosing requires weight-based calculation — consult current references.

---

### 2a. Ibuprofen (e.g., Advil, Motrin)

**Classification:** Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drug (NSAID); analgesic, antipyretic, anti-inflammatory; OTC

**Standard Adult Dose:**
- **400–800 mg every 4–8 hours orally**
- Maximum: 3,200 mg/day (prescription range); 1,200 mg/day (OTC labeling)
- Take with food to reduce GI irritation

**Indications (Field):** Musculoskeletal pain, headache, fever, dental pain, dysmenorrhea, frostbite pain, inflammatory injuries

**WMS Combination Protocol (per WMS 2024 Pain Guidelines):**
> "Ibuprofen 400 mg oral with acetaminophen 1,000 mg oral can produce the analgesic effect of commonly used oral opioids/acetaminophen combinations. In the absence of contraindications, this should be the starting point in the treatment of almost every painful condition, with re-dosing every 6 hours as needed."

**Contraindications:**
- Known allergy or hypersensitivity to NSAIDs or aspirin (including aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease)
- Active GI bleeding or peptic ulcer disease
- Renal impairment (avoid or reduce dose)
- Significant hepatic impairment
- Age under 12 years (OTC use; pediatric prescription formulations exist)
- Last trimester of pregnancy
- Concomitant anticoagulant therapy (use with caution)
- Dehydration (increases risk of renal injury)

**Field Caution:** In dehydrated patients (common in wilderness emergencies), NSAIDs carry increased risk of acute kidney injury. Ensure adequate hydration before and during use.

---

### 2b. Acetaminophen / Paracetamol (e.g., Tylenol)

**Classification:** Non-narcotic analgesic, antipyretic; OTC

**Standard Adult Dose:**
- **500–1,000 mg every 4–6 hours orally**
- **Maximum dose: 4,000 mg (4 grams) per 24 hours**
- NOLS recommended field schedule: 1,000 mg every 8 hours (easy to remember; prevents exceeding maximum)

**Indications (Field):** Pain, fever, situations where NSAIDs are contraindicated (GI concerns, renal concerns)

**Critical Safety Note — Combination Products:** Acetaminophen is present in many combination OTC and prescription products (NyQuil, Percocet, Vicodin, DayQuil, etc.). Double-counting of acetaminophen across multiple products is a leading cause of overdose. Track total daily intake across ALL products used.

**Contraindications:**
- Hepatic impairment or active liver disease
- Chronic heavy alcohol use (lowers toxic threshold; maximum 2g/day in this population)
- Known allergy

**Toxicity:** Hepatotoxicity develops at 7.5–10 g/day in normal adults; 150 mg/kg in children. Early toxicity may be asymptomatic — delayed presentation is common. Requires hospital treatment (N-acetylcysteine protocol).

---

### 2c. Diphenhydramine (e.g., Benadryl)

**Classification:** First-generation antihistamine (H1-blocker); OTC

**Standard Adult Dose:**
- **25–50 mg every 4–6 hours orally**
- For anaphylaxis adjunct: 50 mg (see Section 6)
- Pediatric: 1 mg/kg per dose (maximum 50 mg)

**Indications (Field):**
- Allergic reactions (mild to moderate)
- Anaphylaxis adjunct (secondary to epinephrine — never a substitute)
- Motion sickness / seasickness
- Sleep aid (mild sedation)
- Dystonic reactions (medication side effects)
- Insect bite/sting itching

**Contraindications:**
- Acute asthma attack
- Narrow-angle glaucoma
- Prostatic hypertrophy (urinary retention risk)
- COPD (respiratory depression risk)
- Concomitant alcohol or sedative use
- Activities requiring alertness (sedation is significant)
- Hypertension (use with caution)

**Field Caution:** Sedation is a major side effect — may impair ability to navigate, climb, or manage emergencies. Not appropriate for lead climbers or anyone who must remain alert.

---

### 2d. Loperamide (e.g., Imodium)

**Classification:** Antimotility agent (peripheral opioid receptor agonist); OTC

**Standard Adult Dose:**
- **2 mg (1 capsule) after each loose stool**
- Maximum: **16 mg per day** (OTC label: 8 mg/day unless directed by physician)
- Not for use beyond 2 days without medical guidance

**Indications (Field):** Acute non-bloody, non-febrile traveler's diarrhea; symptomatic relief to allow movement/evacuation

**Contraindications:**
- Bloody diarrhea (may indicate invasive bacterial infection — dysentery)
- High fever accompanying diarrhea (suggests invasive pathogen)
- Suspected *Clostridioides difficile* (C. diff) infection
- Children under 2 years (use only under physician guidance in children)
- Abdominal distension

**Field Warning:** Loperamide suppresses diarrhea but does NOT treat underlying infection. It is appropriate for traveler's diarrhea when bloody stools and high fever are absent. Use with aggressive oral rehydration. If symptoms worsen, include blood, or fever exceeds 38.5°C (101.3°F), stop loperamide and consider antibiotics and evacuation.

---

### 2e. Antacids (Calcium Carbonate, Bismuth Subsalicylate)

**Calcium Carbonate (e.g., Tums, Rolaids):**
- **Dose:** 500–1,000 mg as needed (chewable); maximum 7,500 mg/day (OTC)
- **Use:** Heartburn, acid indigestion, GI upset, mild hypocalcemia supplement
- **Caution:** Avoid in renal failure; milk-alkali syndrome risk with high doses

**Bismuth Subsalicylate (e.g., Pepto-Bismol):**
- **Dose:** 525 mg (2 tablets or 30 mL) every 30–60 minutes as needed; max 8 doses/day
- **Use:** Diarrhea, nausea, heartburn, traveler's diarrhea prophylaxis
- **Caution:** Contains salicylate — avoid in aspirin allergy, children/teenagers with viral illness (Reye's syndrome risk), anticoagulant users, gout patients, renal impairment
- Turns stool/tongue black (normal, benign side effect)
- **Prophylactic use:** 525 mg 4 times daily can reduce traveler's diarrhea incidence

## 3. PRESCRIPTION MEDICATIONS IN WILDERNESS KITS

**Source:** WMS Clinical Practice Guidelines; OHSU Wilderness Medicine (Hickey, 2020); Wilderness Medical Systems Prescription Kit Reference; Expedition Drug Kit literature (ItaliaOutdoors/World Extreme Medicine)

> **CRITICAL WARNING:** All medications in this section require a valid prescription from a licensed physician. They should only be carried and administered by individuals with appropriate training (WFR, WEMT, or licensed medical professionals) under standing medical orders from a physician. Field use is appropriate only in genuine emergencies when evacuation is not immediately possible. Always consult a physician or travel medicine specialist before assembling an expedition drug kit.

---

### 3a. Epinephrine Auto-Injector (EpiPen / Auvi-Q)

**See Section 6 (Anaphylaxis Treatment Protocol) for full details.**

**Classification:** Catecholamine; sympathomimetic; Rx (though many states permit dispensing for anaphylaxis)

**Standard Doses (auto-injector):**
- Adult/child >55 lbs (>25 kg): **EpiPen 0.3 mg** (1:1,000 concentration, 0.3 mL) IM
- Child <55 lbs (<25 kg): **EpiPen Jr. 0.15 mg** IM
- Inject into anterolateral mid-thigh; may administer through clothing
- May repeat in 5–15 minutes if no improvement

---

### 3b. Azithromycin (Z-Pak)

**Classification:** Macrolide antibiotic; Rx

**Indications (Field):**
- Respiratory tract infections (bronchitis, community-acquired pneumonia)
- Traveler's diarrhea (preferred over fluoroquinolones due to resistance concerns)
- Skin and soft tissue infections
- Chlamydia (single 1g dose)

**Standard Dosing (per published expedition drug kit references):**
- **Respiratory/GI infections:** 500 mg on Day 1, then 250 mg daily on Days 2–5 (Z-Pak regimen)
- **Traveler's diarrhea:** 500 mg daily for 3 days, or 1,000 mg single dose (for non-dysenteric)
- **Single-dose regimen:** 1,000 mg (chlamydia, some GI pathogens)

**Contraindications:**
- Known macrolide allergy
- History of QT prolongation or concurrent QT-prolonging drugs
- Hepatic impairment (use with caution)
- Myasthenia gravis (may worsen)

**Field Note:** Azithromycin is preferred over ciprofloxacin for GI illness in many settings due to increasing fluoroquinolone resistance in *Campylobacter* and *Salmonella* species.

---

### 3c. Ciprofloxacin (Cipro)

**Classification:** Fluoroquinolone antibiotic; Rx

**Indications (Field):**
- Urinary tract infections (UTI)
- Infectious diarrhea / bacterial gastroenteritis
- Bone and joint infections
- Inhalational anthrax post-exposure prophylaxis (per public health guidance)

**Standard Dosing (per wilderness medicine references):**
- **UTI:** 250 mg every 12 hours orally for 3–7 days
- **Kidney infection (pyelonephritis):** 500 mg every 12 hours for 7–14 days
- **Infectious diarrhea:** 500 mg every 12 hours for 3–5 days
- **Bone/joint infection:** 500 mg every 12 hours for 4–6 weeks (with medical follow-up)

**Contraindications:**
- Known fluoroquinolone allergy
- Tendinopathy or tendon rupture history (black box warning: tendon rupture risk, especially Achilles)
- Age under 18 (generally avoided; cartilage concerns)
- Pregnancy and breastfeeding
- QT prolongation / concurrent QT-prolonging drugs
- Concurrent antacids (reduce absorption — separate by 2 hours)

**Field Cautions:**
- Fluoroquinolones carry FDA Black Box Warning for tendinitis and tendon rupture, peripheral neuropathy, and CNS effects
- Avoid in active outdoor expeditions if possible (tendon rupture risk with exertion)
- Photosensitivity — use sun protection

---

### 3d. Metronidazole (Flagyl)

**Classification:** Nitroimidazole antiprotozoal and antibacterial; Rx

**Indications (Field):**
- Giardia lamblia (giardiasis)
- Entamoeba histolytica (amoebiasis)
- Anaerobic bacterial infections (dental abscess, pelvic infections, aspiration pneumonia)
- Bacterial vaginosis
- *Clostridioides difficile* (C. diff) — secondary option

**Standard Dosing:**
- **Giardiasis:** 250 mg three times daily for 5–7 days
- **Amoebiasis (intestinal):** 500–750 mg three times daily for 5–10 days
- **Anaerobic infections:** 500 mg every 8 hours for 7–10 days
- **Dental abscess (field):** 400–500 mg every 8 hours for 5–7 days

**Contraindications:**
- Known metronidazole/nitroimidazole allergy
- First trimester of pregnancy (teratogenic concerns)
- **Absolute: No alcohol** during treatment and for 48 hours after (disulfiram-like reaction: severe nausea, vomiting, flushing, tachycardia)
- Hepatic impairment (reduce dose)
- Seizure disorder (use with caution; CNS side effects)

**Alternative:** Tinidazole 2 g as single dose for giardiasis — fewer side effects, higher cure rate, shorter course (preferred when available)

## 4. ORAL REHYDRATION SOLUTION (ORS) — WHO STANDARD

**Source:** WHO/UNICEF Oral Rehydration Salts publication (WHO-FCH-CAH-06.1); WHO Essential Medicines List; *Understanding the use of oral rehydration therapy* (PMC9464461)

### WHO Reduced-Osmolarity ORS Formulation (Current — Since 2003)

Dissolve the following in **1 liter (1,000 mL) of clean water:**

| Ingredient | Amount |
|---|---|
| Sodium chloride (table salt) | 2.6 g |
| Glucose, anhydrous (sugar) | 13.5 g |
| Potassium chloride | 1.5 g |
| Trisodium citrate, dihydrate | 2.9 g |

**Total osmolarity: 245 mOsm/L** (reduced from older 311 mOsm/L formulation)

#### Resulting Electrolyte Concentrations
- Sodium: 75 mEq/L
- Chloride: 65 mEq/L
- Glucose: 75 mmol/L
- Potassium: 20 mEq/L
- Citrate: 10 mEq/L

#### Field-Improvised ORS (When Commercial Packets Unavailable)
Per WHO and field medicine references, an acceptable improvised ORS can be made from:
- **1 liter clean water**
- **6 level teaspoons of sugar** (~24 g; substitutes for glucose)
- **1/2 level teaspoon of salt** (~2.5 g; substitutes for sodium chloride)

> **Note:** This improvised version lacks potassium and citrate. It is a temporary measure only. Commercial ORS packets are strongly preferred. The improvised recipe is adequate for mild dehydration when no alternative exists.

#### Usage Guidelines
- **Mild-to-moderate dehydration:** Administer 50–100 mL/kg over 2–4 hours; additional ORS for ongoing losses (approximately 10 mL/kg per loose stool in children)
- **Adults:** Drink small, frequent sips; approximately 200–400 mL after each loose stool
- Any prepared ORS solution not consumed within **24 hours should be discarded**
- ORS does **not** require refrigeration and has a shelf life of 2–3 years in sealed packaging

#### Clinical Indications
- Appropriate for: mild-to-moderate dehydration, diarrheal illness without shock
- **NOT appropriate for:** severe dehydration with shock, altered mental status, inability to drink, ileus — these require IV rehydration

**Evidence:** The reduced-osmolarity WHO ORS formula reduces the need for IV fluids by approximately 33% compared to the older formulation. Since WHO adopted ORS in 1978, annual childhood diarrhea mortality has fallen from approximately 5 million to 1.3 million deaths.

## 5. WOUND CARE MEDICATIONS

**Source:** WMS Practice Guidelines for Basic Wound Management in the Austere Environment (Quinn et al., 2014); WMS Wound Care Practice Guidelines Summary (Bitter); Wilderness Protocol 2 — Wound Cleaning + Debridement (CWS/WMS)

### 5a. Povidone-Iodine (Betadine)

**Standard commercial concentration:** 10% solution (contains 1% available iodine, approximately 1 ppm free iodine)

**WMS Recommended Field Use:**
- When clean irrigation water is unavailable or insufficient, rinse wound with a **1% povidone-iodine solution** (dilute standard 10% Betadine 1:10 with clean water)
- Also useful for water treatment in dire circumstances (not primary role)

**Available formulations:**
- 10% aqueous solution (most common; standard Betadine)
- 7.5% surgical scrub (detergent base — do NOT use directly in wounds)
- 2.5% dry powder spray
- 10% ointment
- Note: The scrub/soap formulation is for skin preparation only — the detergent component is cytotoxic to wound tissue

**Paradoxical Dilution Effect (Important):**
Free iodine actually increases as solution is diluted from 10% down to approximately 0.1% (1:100 dilution). Maximum antimicrobial activity occurs around the 0.1% concentration. For wound irrigation, the 1% solution is both safe and effective.

**Do NOT combine with:**
- Hydrogen peroxide (chemical inactivation of both agents)
- Silver-based products
- Taurolidine
- Enzymes/proteins

**Contraindications:**
- Known iodine allergy
- Thyroid disease (prolonged use can affect thyroid function)
- Pregnancy (iodine absorption concerns with prolonged use)
- Premature infants (do not use)
- Do not use 10% undiluted solution directly in open wound tissue

---

### 5b. Hydrogen Peroxide — The Debate

**Current WMS/wound care consensus:** Hydrogen peroxide (3% standard OTC formulation) is **not recommended** as a primary wound treatment in current wilderness medicine practice.

**Reasons:**
- Cytotoxic to healthy fibroblasts and granulation tissue needed for healing
- Can impair wound healing with repeated use
- Less effective than proper irrigation
- More cytotoxic than povidone-iodine in comparative testing
- Chemical reaction with iodine renders both agents ineffective

**Acceptable limited uses:** Initial superficial cleaning of visibly contaminated skin surface surrounding a wound (not within wound bed); can be used once to loosen debris in a very dirty wound if nothing else is available — with understanding that healing tissue is also affected.

**Bottom line:** High-pressure irrigation with potable water is far superior to hydrogen peroxide for wound decontamination.

---

### 5c. Antibiotic Ointments (Triple Antibiotic / Bacitracin / Mupirocin)

**Per WMS Wound Care Guidelines:** "Topical antibiotics are possibly useful, and there is little risk to their use, assuming the patient is not allergic to the agent."

**Common agents:**
- **Triple Antibiotic Ointment (bacitracin + neomycin + polymyxin B):** Apply thin layer to wound surface after irrigation; cover with sterile dressing; broad Gram+ and Gram- coverage
- **Bacitracin alone:** Gram-positive coverage; use when neomycin allergy present (neomycin is a common contact allergen)
- **Mupirocin (Bactroban) — Rx:** Superior S. aureus/MRSA coverage; useful for impetigo, infected wounds

**Application:** Thin layer to wound surface after thorough irrigation; change dressing daily or when saturated/soiled

**Contraindications:** Known allergy to any component (neomycin contact allergy is common — may cause contact dermatitis appearing as worsening redness around wound)

## 6. ANAPHYLAXIS TREATMENT PROTOCOL

**Source:** WMS Clinical Practice Guidelines on Anaphylaxis (Gaudio et al., 2022, *Wilderness & Environmental Medicine*); WMS Practice Guidelines for Use of Epinephrine in Outdoor Education and Wilderness Settings (2014 Update); CWS Wilderness Protocol 1

> **WARNING:** Anaphylaxis is immediately life-threatening. Epinephrine is the ONLY first-line treatment. Antihistamines and corticosteroids are secondary adjuncts only — they must NEVER delay or substitute for epinephrine.

### Recognition of Anaphylaxis

Anaphylaxis is likely when ANY TWO of the following occur within minutes to hours of exposure to a potential allergen:
- Skin/mucosal symptoms (hives, itching, flushing, swelling of lips/tongue/throat)
- Respiratory compromise (dyspnea, wheeze, stridor, reduced peak flow)
- Reduced blood pressure or symptoms of end-organ dysfunction (syncope, incontinence)
- Persistent GI symptoms after exposure (cramping, vomiting)

Additional warning signs: sense of impending doom, voice change/hoarseness (laryngeal edema), difficulty swallowing

**Onset:** Typically within minutes of sting/injection; 30–60 minutes after ingestion

---

### TREATMENT ALGORITHM (Per WMS 2022 Guidelines)

#### STEP 1 — EPINEPHRINE (IMMEDIATE — First Priority)
- Administer as soon as anaphylaxis is identified, especially with airway involvement, respiratory compromise, or cardiovascular symptoms
- **In the context of possible anaphylaxis, there are NO absolute contraindications to epinephrine**

**Auto-Injector Doses:**
- **Adult / Child >25 kg (>55 lbs):** EpiPen 0.3 mg IM (anterolateral mid-thigh preferred; may inject through clothing)
- **Child <25 kg (<55 lbs):** EpiPen Jr. 0.15 mg IM (or 0.01 mg/kg)

**Manual Injection (WFR/WEMT scope):**
- Epinephrine 1:1,000 concentration (1 mg/mL): **0.3–0.5 mg IM** for adults
- Injection site: Anterolateral mid-thigh (achieves highest serum concentration vs. deltoid)

**Repeat Dose:**
- May repeat in 5–15 minutes if no improvement
- Up to 25–35% of anaphylaxis cases require a second dose
- Biphasic reactions can occur 4–24 hours later (rarely up to 72 hours)

#### STEP 2 — POSITION PATIENT
- Position of comfort, generally supine with legs elevated if hypotensive
- Do NOT sit upright if the patient is hypotensive (risk of cardiovascular collapse)
- Separate from allergen; remove insect stingers by scraping (not squeezing); do NOT induce vomiting

#### STEP 3 — ADJUNCTIVE TREATMENTS (Secondary — After Epinephrine)
**Diphenhydramine (Benadryl):**
- **50 mg orally every 4–6 hours** (adult; patient must be conscious and able to swallow)
- Treats urticaria and pruritus; does NOT reverse airway compromise
- Pediatric: **1 mg/kg** per dose (maximum 50 mg)

**Corticosteroids (Prednisone):**
- **Prednisone 60 mg orally daily** (or equivalent)
- Purpose: Reduce risk of biphasic reaction; treats persistent inflammation
- Pediatric: **1 mg/kg** (maximum 60 mg)
- Note: Takes 4–6 hours for anti-inflammatory effect; does not help acute anaphylaxis

**Bronchodilator (if respiratory symptoms persist):**
- Albuterol metered-dose inhaler (MDI) for bronchospasm/wheeze
- Does not replace epinephrine

#### STEP 4 — EVACUATION (All Anaphylaxis Patients)
- ALL anaphylaxis patients must be evacuated for medical evaluation due to biphasic reaction risk
- Monitor closely for 24–72 hours
- Treat any recurrence with same protocol

---

### WHO CAN ADMINISTER EPINEPHRINE IN THE FIELD?
Per WMS guidelines, non-medical outdoor professionals (guides, instructors, park rangers, camp directors) should be trained to administer epinephrine via auto-injector. WFR and WEMT certifications permit manual syringe administration. Always check jurisdiction-specific laws and organizational standing medical orders.

## 7. PAIN MANAGEMENT IN AUSTERE ENVIRONMENTS

**Source:** WMS Clinical Practice Guidelines for Treatment of Acute Pain in Austere Environments: 2024 Update (Fink et al., *Wilderness & Environmental Medicine*); WMS 2014 Pain Guidelines (Russell et al.); EMRA Wilderness Pain Management

### Principles of Wilderness Pain Management
1. Manage scene safety and immediate threats to life first
2. Use **multimodal analgesia** — combine agents working by different mechanisms for superior pain control
3. Start with lowest-risk medications; escalate as needed
4. An ideal field analgesic is: compact, lightweight, durable against extremes, non-sedating, large therapeutic index, low abuse potential

---

### First-Line: Multimodal OTC Combination (WMS 2024)

**Preferred starting regimen (per WMS Clinical Practice Guidelines):**
> "Ibuprofen 400 mg oral with acetaminophen 1,000 mg oral can produce the analgesic effect of commonly used oral opioids/acetaminophen combinations. In the absence of contraindications, this should be the starting point in the treatment of almost every painful condition, with re-dosing every 6 hours as needed."

- Combined ibuprofen + acetaminophen was demonstrated in a systematic review of 21 studies to be **superior to either drug alone** and comparable to narcotic combinations
- U.S. military "combat pill pack" contains acetaminophen, an NSAID, and an antibiotic for immediate use after penetrating extremity wounds

---

### NSAID Rotation in Field Settings

Short-term NSAID use (up to 10 days at standard OTC doses) is extremely safe and well tolerated. Options include:

| NSAID | Dose | Frequency | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ibuprofen | 400–800 mg | Every 4–8h | Most common; good anti-inflammatory |
| Naproxen (Aleve) | 220–440 mg | Every 8–12h | Longer duration; twice daily dosing convenient |
| Meloxicam | 15 mg | Once daily | Preferred battlefield NSAID (Rx only); convenient |
| Diclofenac gel | 1% gel | Every 6h topically | Topical option for localized musculoskeletal pain |
| Diclofenac patch | 1.3% | Every 12h topically | Topical; good for joint/soft tissue injuries |

**Risk threshold per WMS/published literature:**
- Ibuprofen ≤1,200 mg/day: No greater GI bleeding risk than placebo
- Naproxen ≤660 mg/day: No greater GI bleeding risk than placebo

> **NSAID Rotation Note:** There is limited evidence that rotating between different NSAIDs reduces side effects. The primary benefit of having multiple NSAIDs available is logistical — each provides similar coverage. The key is to remain within safe daily dose limits.

---

### Acetaminophen Dosing Limits

- **Standard adult dose:** 650–1,000 mg every 4–6 hours
- **Maximum: 4,000 mg (4 grams) per 24-hour period**
- **High-risk populations (liver disease, heavy alcohol use):** Maximum 2,000 mg/day
- **Toxicity threshold:** 7.5–10 g/day (adult); 150 mg/kg (child)
- Track total acetaminophen from all sources (combination products)

---

### Topical Anesthetics for Field Use (WMS Recommendation: Strong, Moderate-Quality Evidence)

The WMS recommends locally instilled and topically applied local anesthetic agents for burns, stings, and soft tissue injuries in austere environments.

| Agent | Formulation | Application |
|---|---|---|
| Lidocaine | 2% viscous gel, 4–5% cream/ointment, 5% patch | Direct wound/burn application |
| LET (Lidocaine/Epinephrine/Tetracaine) | Combination solution/gel | Wound edges (especially facial lacerations); apply to gauze |
| EMLA (Lidocaine/Prilocaine eutectic) | 2.5%/2.5% cream | Skin surface prior to needle procedures |
| Bupivacaine | 0.25–0.5% solution | Wound infiltration; longer duration than lidocaine |

**Notes:**
- Viscous lidocaine also useful for oral mucosal pain (ulcers, esophagitis) when NSAIDs are contraindicated
- **IV lidocaine should NOT be used in the austere environment** (WMS: Strong recommendation, low-quality evidence)
- Efficacy of topical anesthetics outside recommended storage range (0–30°C) is not established — monitor for effectiveness in extreme temperature conditions

## 8. ALTITUDE SICKNESS MEDICATIONS

**Source:** WMS Clinical Practice Guidelines for the Prevention, Diagnosis, and Treatment of Acute Altitude Illness: 2024 Update (Luks, Beidleman, Freer, Grissom, Keyes, McIntosh, Rodway, Schoene, Zafren, Hackett, *Wilderness & Environmental Medicine*, 2024); AAFP summary (AFP, 2020); WMS 2019 Update (Luks et al.)

### Altitude Illness Classifications
- **AMS (Acute Mountain Sickness):** Headache with one or more of: fatigue, dizziness, nausea/vomiting, sleep disturbance; at altitude ≥2,500 m (8,200 ft)
- **HACE (High Altitude Cerebral Edema):** Severe AMS with ataxia and/or altered consciousness — **MEDICAL EMERGENCY; descend immediately**
- **HAPE (High Altitude Pulmonary Edema):** Dyspnea at rest, cough (initially dry, later productive/bloody), decreased exercise tolerance, cyanosis — **MEDICAL EMERGENCY; descend immediately**

**General Rule:** Descent is the definitive treatment for all altitude illness. Medications are adjuncts, not substitutes for descent.

---

### 8a. Acetazolamide (Diamox)

**Classification:** Carbonic anhydrase inhibitor; diuretic; Rx

**Mechanism:** Causes mild metabolic acidosis, stimulating increased respiratory rate, accelerating acclimatization; does NOT mask symptoms like dexamethasone

**Prevention of AMS (WMS 2024 — Recommendation Grade 1B):**
- **Adults:** 125 mg orally every 12 hours
- **Children:** 2.5 mg/kg (maximum 125 mg) every 12 hours
- Start: Day before ascent (can still be started day of ascent with benefit)
- Duration: Continue for 2–4 days after reaching target altitude

**Treatment of AMS:**
- Acetazolamide 250 mg orally every 12 hours (WMS: Recommendation Grade 1C)
- Can be used as adjunct to dexamethasone in HACE treatment

**NOT recommended for HAPE treatment** (per WMS 2024)

**Contraindications:**
- Prior anaphylactic reaction to any sulfonamide drug (NOT just cross-reactive allergy)
- History of Stevens-Johnson syndrome from sulfonamide
- Sulfa allergy: risk is extremely low; discuss with physician if uncertain
- Pregnancy (teratogenic in animal studies; use only if benefit outweighs risk)
- Renal failure
- Severe hepatic disease
- Hyponatremia or hypokalemia

**Common Side Effects:** Paresthesias (tingling in hands/feet; very common, benign), increased urination, altered taste of carbonated beverages, polyuria

---

### 8b. Dexamethasone

**Classification:** Corticosteroid; Rx

**Mechanism:** Reduces cerebral edema; does NOT facilitate acclimatization (rebound illness possible when stopped)

**AMS Prevention (WMS 2024):**
- **4 mg orally every 6 hours** (standard prevention dose)
- Higher doses (>4 mg q6h) only for extreme high-risk situations (military/SAR airlifted above 3,500 m with immediate physical activity)
- **NOT recommended for AMS prevention in children**
- Start: Day before ascent or day of ascent

**HACE Treatment (WMS 2024 — Recommendation Grade 1B):**
- **Dexamethasone 8 mg IM/IV initial dose**, then **4 mg every 6 hours**
- Oral route acceptable if patient can swallow
- Primary pharmacologic treatment for HACE; more reliable than acetazolamide for moderate-to-severe AMS/HACE
- Initiate descent simultaneously

**Duration Caution:**
- If used >5–7 days, taper over one week rather than abrupt discontinuation (adrenal suppression risk)
- Do not use as long-term acclimatization substitute — AMS rebounds when stopped

**HAPE:** Not primary treatment; may be used if nifedipine unavailable and tadalafil not available. Dexamethasone can prevent HAPE in known susceptible individuals who cannot use nifedipine (WMS: Strong recommendation, low-quality evidence)

---

### 8c. Nifedipine

**Classification:** Calcium channel blocker (dihydropyridine); Rx

**Mechanism:** Pulmonary vasodilator; reduces pulmonary artery pressure; preferred agent for HAPE

**HAPE Prevention (for individuals with prior HAPE history — WMS 2024):**
- **Extended-release nifedipine 30 mg every 12 hours** (preferred)
- Start: Day before ascent
- Duration: Continue until descent is initiated or 4–7 days at target altitude (longer if faster-than-recommended ascent rate)
- Preferred over other agents (tadalafil, dexamethasone) for HAPE prevention per WMS

**HAPE Treatment (WMS 2024):**
- When oxygen/hyperbaric chamber unavailable:
  - **10 mg immediate-release nifedipine orally**, followed by
  - **20 mg extended-release nifedipine every 6 hours**
- Initiate descent immediately while administering
- If nifedipine unavailable: tadalafil and sildenafil are unproven alternatives

**Concurrent HAPE + HACE:**
- Nifedipine may be used, but monitor carefully to avoid lowering systemic blood pressure excessively

**Contraindications:**
- Hypotension (may cause significant vasodilation and blood pressure drop)
- Concurrent use of other antihypertensives, PDE-5 inhibitors (sildenafil, tadalafil) — significant hypotension risk
- Aortic stenosis
- Active myocardial infarction within 30 days

---

### Quick Reference — Altitude Medications (WMS 2024)

| Drug | Indication | Dose | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Acetazolamide | AMS prevention | 125 mg PO q12h | Start day before ascent |
| Acetazolamide | AMS treatment | 250 mg PO q12h | Facilitates acclimatization |
| Dexamethasone | AMS/HACE prevention | 4 mg PO q6h | Does NOT facilitate acclimatization |
| Dexamethasone | HACE treatment | 8 mg IM, then 4 mg q6h | Grade 1B recommendation; descend simultaneously |
| Nifedipine ER | HAPE prevention | 30 mg PO q12h | History of HAPE only |
| Nifedipine IR | HAPE treatment | 10 mg PO, then 20 mg ER q6h | Descend simultaneously |

## 9. HERBAL & NATURAL ALTERNATIVES WITH DOCUMENTED EFFICACY

**Source:** PMC10046143 (Evidence for Natural Products as Alternative Wound-Healing Therapies); PMC9455684 (Natural Compounds for Wound Healing); PMC3839002 (Phytochemicals for Wound Healing); MDLinx evidence-based natural remedies review

> **IMPORTANT CAVEAT:** The natural agents listed here have varying levels of scientific evidence. None should replace standard first aid, conventional wound care, or prescribed medications when available. These are documented as adjuncts or last-resort alternatives for emergency preparedness only. "Natural" does not mean safe — allergic reactions, toxicity, and drug interactions are possible.

---

### 9a. Honey — Wound Care

**Evidence Level:** STRONG (multiple clinical trials; WHO and standard wound care literature support)

**Active components:** Hydrogen peroxide (from glucose oxidase), methylglyoxal (Manuka-specific), defensin-1 (bee-derived), high osmolarity, low pH

**Documented mechanisms:**
- Broad-spectrum antimicrobial activity (including MRSA — methicillin-resistant *Staphylococcus aureus*)
- Does not appear to induce antibiotic resistance in bacteria
- Creates moisture-retentive, hyperosmotic wound environment that inhibits bacterial growth
- Stimulates immune response and tissue regeneration
- Autolytic debridement (enzyme-mediated)
- Anti-inflammatory effect

**Best form:** Manuka honey (UMF-certified); medical-grade honey (e.g., Medihoney) preferred. Standard raw honey has activity but less standardized potency.

**Field application:**
- Irrigate wound first (see Section 5; Section 14)
- Apply honey directly to wound surface or to dressing, then apply to wound
- Cover with sterile dressing; change every 24–48 hours
- Suitable for chronic/infected wounds, burns (minor), abrasions

**Contraindications:** Known bee/pollen allergy (rare cross-reaction); do not use on deep puncture wounds or wounds requiring surgical debridement; **never give honey (any form) to infants under 12 months** (botulism risk — systemic, not topical)

---

### 9b. Garlic — Antimicrobial

**Evidence Level:** MODERATE (animal studies, in vitro; limited human trials)

**Active component:** Allicin (diallyl thiosulfinate) — released when raw garlic is crushed/minced

**Documented mechanisms:**
- Antimicrobial against Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria
- Antifungal properties
- Anti-inflammatory via inhibition of inflammatory cytokines
- Animal studies show enhanced wound healing and infection suppression

**Field application:** Crushed fresh garlic applied to wound area as poultice (not inside deep wounds); short contact time only

> **CAUTION:** Direct garlic application to skin for extended periods causes chemical burns and dermatitis. Apply for 10–15 minutes maximum, then remove and rinse. Do not use garlic inside open wounds.

---

### 9c. Willow Bark — Salicylates (Pain & Inflammation)

**Evidence Level:** MODERATE (historical strong; modern clinical trials limited but supporting)

**Active component:** Salicin (precursor to salicylic acid; related to aspirin mechanism)

**Documented mechanisms:**
- Analgesic and anti-inflammatory (similar to aspirin mechanism)
- Antipyretic (fever reduction)

**Field preparation:** Brew inner bark of willow (*Salix alba, S. purpurea*, or similar species) as tea; approximately 1–2 tablespoons of bark per cup of water, simmer 10–15 minutes

**Considerations:**
- Less potent than pharmaceutical aspirin
- Slower onset than aspirin
- Useful when no pharmaceutical options available

> **CAUTION:** Contains salicylates — share all contraindications with aspirin: aspirin allergy, aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease, anticoagulant use, peptic ulcer disease, renal impairment, pregnancy (third trimester), children/teenagers with viral illness (Reye's syndrome risk). Do not use in someone with aspirin allergy.

---

### 9d. Activated Charcoal — Poisoning & Wound Cleansing

**Evidence Level:** STRONG for oral poisoning (emergency toxicology); LIMITED but documented for wound topical use

**Oral use — Poisoning/Overdose:**
- **Standard dose:** 1 g/kg body weight (adult: typically 25–100 g); mixed in water as slurry
- Adsorbs many toxins in GI tract before absorption
- Effective within 1 hour of ingestion; less effective after 1–2 hours
- **Does NOT work for:** alcohols (ethanol, methanol, isopropanol), strong acids/bases, iron, lithium, potassium, hydrocarbons
- Patient must be conscious and have intact airway
- Contraindicated with caustic ingestion, ileus, or unprotected airway

> **CRITICAL WARNING:** Do NOT administer activated charcoal if patient has altered mental status or compromised airway (aspiration risk). Contact Poison Control (1-800-222-1222 in USA) before administering when communication is possible.

**Topical wound use (field, limited evidence):**
- Activated charcoal poultice (mixed with distilled/clean water) applied to infected wound surface
- Theoretical mechanism: adsorbs bacteria and debris from wound bed
- Apply, cover with sterile dressing, change every few hours
- Published wilderness medicine sources (NewTarget, preparedness literature) support this use
- Limited formal clinical trial data for topical wound application

---

### 9e. Ginger — Nausea & Antimicrobial

**Evidence Level:** MODERATE for nausea; LIMITED for wound care

**Active components:** Gingerols, shogaols, zingerone

**Documented mechanisms:**
- Antiemetic (nausea/vomiting relief): Multiple clinical trials support ginger for nausea from motion sickness, chemotherapy, pregnancy
- Antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory: In vitro data; tincture formulations used in wound management in traditional medicine
- GI motility effects

**Field preparation (nausea):** Ginger tea from fresh ginger root (slice 1-inch piece, steep in hot water 5–10 minutes); ginger candies, ginger capsules (250–500 mg standardized extract, up to 4 times daily)

**Contraindications:** Anticoagulant use (antiplatelet effect at high doses); known allergy; excessive doses may cause GI upset

---

### 9f. Bark Extracts (Oak, Willow) — Antimicrobial/Astringent

**Active components:** Polyphenols, tannins — antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and astringent properties

**Evidence:** A randomized controlled trial found that *Quercus rubra* (red oak bark) extract ointment was superior to silver sulfadiazine in healing diabetic ulcers and demonstrated clearance of *S. aureus* in wounds with initial bacterial growth.

**Field use:** Bark tea as wound rinse or poultice; tannins provide astringent/antimicrobial effect

---

## 10. DRUG STORAGE IN FIELD CONDITIONS

**Source:** WMS Magazine — Temperature Stability article; MSF Medical Guidelines — Drug Quality and Storage; Drug Storage and Stability (*EM Consulte*); Baystate Health Medication Storage Temperature Guidelines; Impact of Storage Conditions on Drug Shelf Life (*Precision Stability Storage*)

### Standard Temperature Categories

| Category | Temperature Range | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Refrigerated | 2–8°C (36–46°F) | Vaccines, biologics, certain insulin formulations, some antibiotics (amoxicillin suspension) |
| Controlled room temperature | 15–25°C (59–77°F) | Most oral tablets and capsules |
| Permitted excursion range | 15–30°C (59–86°F) | Labeled "excursions permitted" |
| Freezer | -20°C (-4°F) | Certain vaccines |

**Unless refrigeration is specified, most medications should be stored between 15–25°C (59–77°F), in a cool, dry place, away from sunlight.**

---

### Temperature Extremes in Field Settings

**Heat:**
- Vehicle interiors can reach 50–60°C during transport — temperatures at which stability is no longer guaranteed
- Epinephrine is particularly heat-sensitive: **repetitive heating and cooling cycles can cause loss of up to 64% of efficacy**
- EpiPen storage: 20–25°C (68–77°F); excursions permitted to 15–30°C (59–86°F); **do NOT refrigerate**; protect from light in carrier tube
- Tetracyclines, nitroglycerine, and certain biologics are especially heat-labile
- Gels (suppositories) may melt, altering physical form even if still chemically active

**Cold:**
- Freezing can cause precipitation and loss of potency in solutions
- Parenteral drugs (pre-filled syringes) may develop hairline plastic cracks from freezing — losing sterility even if contents remain potent
- ORS powder: must remain dry white powder; humidity or freezing/thawing that causes clumping/discoloration renders unusable

---

### Humidity Effects

- Maintain relative humidity below 65% for stored medications
- Aspirin undergoes hydrolysis in humid conditions, breaking down to salicylic and acetic acid — reduced efficacy and increased GI irritation
- Hygroscopic medications (absorb moisture) require moisture-proof containers
- ORS that has turned from white powder to compact brownish/insoluble mass should be discarded
- Blister packaging and dessicant packets provide best humidity protection in field conditions

---

### Light Sensitivity

Photodegradation (UV-triggered chemical breakdown) is a significant concern for:
- Nitroglycerin (critical — use amber bottles only)
- Doxycycline and all tetracyclines (loses effectiveness in sunlight)
- Epinephrine solutions (protect in opaque carrier)
- Certain biologics and vaccines
- Furosemide, metronidazole, ciprofloxacin (moderate sensitivity)

**Field storage principle:** Medications in clear bottles or blister packs should be stored inside dark bags, pouches, or containers. Never leave medications in direct sunlight.

---

### Physical Integrity

- Monitor tablets for crumbling, stickiness, or color change
- Monitor solutions for cloudiness, precipitation, or color change
- Plastic containers may leach compounds into solutions over time
- Parenteral drug packaging cracks from freezing: if a pre-filled syringe has been frozen, assume sterility is compromised even if contents look normal

---

### Detecting Compromised Medications

| Sign | Implication |
|---|---|
| Tablets stuck together | Moisture exposure; assess usability |
| Color change | Possible degradation |
| Unusual/absent odor | Possible degradation |
| Liquid turned cloudy or separated | Do not use; precipitation indicates degradation |
| ORS powder clumped/discolored | Discard |
| Gel capsule melted | May still be active but dosing unreliable |
| Blister pack cracked/compromised | Moisture/contamination risk |

**After extended expeditions:** Replace all medication stocks with fresh supply. Even if medications appear intact, cumulative heat/humidity/light exposure may have reduced efficacy.

---

### Best Practices for Field Drug Storage
1. Use waterproof, crushproof containers (Pelican cases, waterproof bags)
2. Use desiccant packets for humidity-sensitive medications
3. Store epinephrine in opaque carrier; avoid extreme temperatures
4. Separate light-sensitive medications into inner opaque bags or amber containers
5. Keep medications away from vehicle engine areas, tent walls in sun
6. In cold environments, keep medications next to body to prevent freezing (especially solutions)
7. Label all medications with expiration date and special storage requirements
8. Maintain a complete inventory list with expiration dates; check before each expedition

---

## 11. IV FLUID THERAPY BASICS

**Source:** StatPearls — Fluid Management (NBK532305); Crystalloid Solutions (NBK500033); AMBOSS Intravenous Fluid Therapy; WHO/pediatric guidelines; EMCrit fluid selection reference

> **CRITICAL WARNING:** IV fluid therapy in the field requires training, sterile technique, and appropriate equipment. Establishing IV access without proper training carries significant risks including infection, air embolism, extravasation, and incorrect fluid selection. This section provides knowledge-base information — field IV therapy should only be performed by trained personnel (Combat Lifesaver/CLS, Wilderness EMT, Paramedic, or higher).

---

### Oral vs. IV Rehydration: Decision Framework

**Use oral rehydration (ORS) when:**
- Patient is conscious and alert
- Patient can swallow and tolerate fluids without vomiting
- Dehydration is mild to moderate (not in shock)
- No signs of bowel obstruction (no ileus)

**Transition to IV when:**
- Patient cannot tolerate oral intake (persistent vomiting)
- Patient in shock (altered mental status, rapid/weak pulse, delayed capillary refill)
- Severe dehydration (>10% body weight loss)
- Rapid deterioration despite oral efforts
- Pediatric patient with severe dehydration

**Evidence:** Studies show oral rehydration is as effective as IV for mild-to-moderate dehydration when oral fluids are tolerated. ORT reduces need for IV fluids by approximately 33%.

---

### IV Fluid Types

#### Normal Saline (NS / 0.9% NaCl)
- **Composition:** Sodium 154 mEq/L, Chloride 154 mEq/L
- **Osmolarity:** 308 mOsm/L (isotonic)
- **Uses:** General dehydration, gastric fluid losses, medication diluent, TBI (avoids lactate)
- **Caution:** Supraphysiologic chloride — large volumes cause **hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis**, increased acute kidney injury risk, and potentially increased mortality in some patient groups. Use cautiously for large-volume resuscitation.

#### Lactated Ringer's Solution (LR / Hartmann's Solution)
- **Composition:** Sodium 130 mEq/L, Chloride 109 mEq/L, Potassium 4 mEq/L, Calcium 3 mEq/L, Lactate 28 mEq/L
- **Osmolarity:** 273 mOsm/L (near-isotonic)
- **Uses:** Trauma resuscitation, shock, burns, surgical fluid replacement, biliary/pancreatic losses
- **Advantage:** Balanced electrolyte profile; liver converts lactate to bicarbonate (buffering)
- **Preferred over NS for:** Most resuscitation scenarios, severe hypovolemia
- **Caution:** Large volumes may cause metabolic alkalosis; contains calcium (incompatible with certain medications, blood products)

#### D5W (5% Dextrose in Water)
- **Composition:** Glucose 50 g/L; no significant electrolytes
- **Osmolarity:** 252 mOsm/L (initially isotonic; becomes hypotonic as glucose is metabolized)
- **Uses:** Hypernatremia (provides free water to dilute sodium), hypoglycemia, medication diluent, caloric supplementation (170 cal/L)
- **NOT for:** Primary rehydration in dehydration (no electrolytes; can cause hyponatremia)
- **Caution:** In trauma/TBI patients — glucose can worsen neurological outcomes

---

### IV Resuscitation Dose Calculation

**Bolus for Shock/Severe Dehydration:**
- **Adults:** 500 mL – 1,000 mL bolus of NS or LR over 15–30 minutes; reassess after each bolus
- **Pediatric:** **20 mL/kg** bolus of isotonic crystalloid (NS or LR) over 20 minutes; reassess perfusion, mental status, vital signs; repeat as needed

**Maintenance Rate (Holliday-Segar "4-2-1" Rule):**
- First 10 kg: 4 mL/kg/hour
- Next 10 kg: 2 mL/kg/hour
- Each additional kg: 1 mL/kg/hour
- Example: 70 kg adult = (4×10) + (2×10) + (1×50) = 40 + 20 + 50 = **110 mL/hour**
- Maximum suggested rate: 120 mL/hour

**Fluid Stewardship Principle:** Administer the minimum volume needed to restore perfusion. Transition to oral hydration as soon as patient can tolerate it.

## 12. ANTIPARASITIC MEDICATIONS

**Source:** StatPearls — Antiparasitic Drugs (NBK544251); U.S. Pharmacist — Drug Therapy for Common Parasitic Infections; PMC8255490 (Albendazole/Mebendazole review); PMC10507222 (Ivermectin evidence-based indications); WHO Essential Medicines

> **Note:** All antiparasitic medications listed below require physician prescription. Field diagnosis of intestinal parasites is based on symptom pattern, epidemiological exposure history, and clinical judgment. Formal stool microscopy/PCR is the diagnostic standard — unavailable in the field.

---

### 12a. Metronidazole (Flagyl) — Giardia and Anaerobes

**Giardiasis (most common wilderness waterborne protozoan):**
- Exposure: Contaminated water (Giardia found in nearly every US waterway)
- Symptoms: Foul-smelling diarrhea, flatulence, bloating, abdominal cramps, nausea; onset 1–3 weeks after exposure; can become chronic
- **Dose:** 250 mg three times daily (TID) for 5–7 days

**Amoebiasis (E. histolytica):**
- Symptoms: Bloody diarrhea, abdominal pain; may disseminate (liver abscess)
- **Dose:** 500–750 mg three times daily for 5–10 days; follow with luminal agent (paromomycin)

**Anaerobic bacterial infections:**
- **Dose:** 500 mg every 8 hours for 7–10 days

**Side effects:** Metallic taste, nausea, headache, dizziness, peripheral neuropathy (prolonged use)

**Absolute rule: NO alcohol** during treatment and 48 hours after (disulfiram-like reaction)

---

### 12b. Tinidazole — Preferred Giardia Alternative

**Giardiasis:**
- **Dose:** 2 g as a single dose orally
- Higher cure rate than metronidazole; fewer side effects; shorter course
- Preferred when available

**Amoebiasis:** 2 g/day for 3 days

**Same alcohol prohibition** applies as metronidazole

---

### 12c. Mebendazole — Intestinal Helminths

**Coverage:** Pinworm (*Enterobius*), roundworm (*Ascaris*), hookworm (*Ancylostoma, Necator*), whipworm (*Trichuris*)

**Dosing:**
- **All susceptible intestinal helminths:** 100 mg twice daily for 3 days
- **Pinworm:** 100 mg single dose; repeat in 2 weeks (re-infection common from environment)

> **Note:** Mebendazole availability has been limited in the U.S. in recent years. Albendazole (below) is the primary alternative.

**Side effects:** Generally well tolerated; mild GI upset; avoid in first trimester pregnancy

---

### 12d. Albendazole — Broad-Spectrum Antihelminthic (Primary U.S. Alternative)

**Coverage:** Similar to mebendazole; also effective for some protozoa and larvae

**Dosing:**
- **Single-dose treatment (hookworm, roundworm, pinworm):** 400 mg orally as a single dose
- **Whipworm:** 400 mg daily for 3 days
- **Giardia (off-label):** 400 mg daily for 5 days
- **Strongyloides:** 400 mg twice daily for 3–7 days (ivermectin preferred)

**Take with a fatty meal** to improve absorption

**Contraindications:** First trimester pregnancy (teratogenic); liver disease

---

### 12e. Ivermectin — Nematodes, Strongyloides, Ectoparasites

**FDA-approved indications:**
- Strongyloidiasis (intestinal threadworm)
- Onchocerciasis (*Onchocerca volvulus*)
- Common off-label: Scabies, head lice, enterobiasis (pinworms)

**Dosing:**
- **Strongyloidiasis:** 200 µg/kg (0.2 mg/kg) as a single dose; may repeat in 2 weeks
- **Onchocerciasis:** 150–200 µg/kg as a single dose (annual dosing in endemic areas)
- **Scabies:** 200 µg/kg; repeat in 1–2 weeks
- **Pinworm (off-label):** 200 µg/kg single dose; repeat in 2 weeks

**Onset:** Starts killing parasites within hours; most patients feel relief within 24–48 hours

**Side effects:** Generally well tolerated; fatigue, dizziness, abdominal pain, rash; Mazzotti reaction (fever, pruritus, rash, arthralgias) in heavily infected onchocerciasis patients

**Contraindications:** Do not administer within 3 months of Loa loa exposure (severe neurologic reactions); pregnancy and breastfeeding (caution); concurrent warfarin (increases anticoagulant effect)

---

|---|---|---|

| *Giardia lamblia* | Metronidazole | 250 mg TID × 5–7 days |

## 13. IMPROVISED IV SETUP

**Source:** FM 4-25.11 (US Army First Aid Field Manual); Brookside Associates Medical Education Division — "Initiate an Intravenous Infusion in a Field Environment" (originally U.S. Army, 1995); EMRA — Improvised Medicine: Lessons from the Wilderness; Global Rescue — Improvised Emergency Medicine in the Wilderness

> **SCOPE OF PRACTICE NOTE:** IV cannulation and fluid administration are NOT within the scope of practice for basic WFR certification. This is a Wilderness EMT (WEMT), Combat Lifesaver (CLS), Paramedic, or licensed medical provider skill. FM 4-25.11 provides individual first aid guidance; IV procedures are covered under Combat Lifesaver training (STP 8-68W13-SM-TG for 68W Combat Medics).

### Components Required (Standard Field IV)
1. IV catheter (14–18 gauge for resuscitation; 20–22 gauge for maintenance)
2. IV tubing/administration set
3. IV fluid bag (NS or LR preferred — see Section 11)
4. Tourniquet or improvised constricting band (for vein visualization)
5. Alcohol prep pads / skin antiseptic
6. Tape or occlusive dressing (Tegaderm)
7. Hanging point for fluid bag (must hang ~18–24 inches above IV site for gravity flow)

### Improvised Fluid Bag Hanging
- Hang from tree branch, tent pole, pack frame, or improvised IV pole
- Commercial military IV bags incorporate snap buttons for alternative hanging configurations
- Fluid bag must remain above patient's IV site for gravity drip to function

### Alternative When IV Access Not Achievable: Rectal Fluid Administration
Published wilderness medicine sources (EMRA) note that **rectal fluid administration using hydration system tubing** has been used as a temporary measure when IV access cannot be established:
- Insert tubing 6–8 inches rectally
- Administer small volumes of clean water or diluted ORS
- Absorption is limited but can provide some fluid in extremis
- Use only in life-threatening dehydration when all other options are exhausted

### Intraosseous (IO) Access
- Military trauma protocols (TC 8-800 series) include IO access for when IV access fails in shock
- IO access at tibial plateau or humeral head using commercial IO devices (EZ-IO, FAST1)
- NOT an improvised technique — requires specific training and commercial devices
- Provides equivalent drug and fluid delivery as IV access

## 14. WOUND CLOSURE ALTERNATIVES

**Source:** WMS Practice Guidelines for Basic Wound Management (Quinn et al., 2014); PMC3884880 (Cutaneous Wound Closure Materials); PMC9728503 (Comparative Study of Sutures, Staples, and Adhesive Glue); Wilderness Protocol 2 — Wound Cleaning + Debridement (CWS)

### Wound Closure Timing (Per WMS Guidelines)
- Most clean wounds: Can close within **4–6 hours** of injury
- Head and facial wounds: Can extend to **10–12 hours** (excellent blood supply)
- Grossly contaminated wounds: Do NOT close primarily — pack open, irrigate, allow to heal by secondary intention or delayed primary closure

### Pre-Closure Steps (Always Required)
1. **Control hemorrhage** (direct pressure, tourniquet if necessary)
2. **Irrigate thoroughly** (see Section 15 — Wound Irrigation)
3. **Debride** obvious devitalized tissue and foreign material (within skill level)
4. Assess depth, contamination, and tension on wound edges

---

### 14a. Steri-Strips (Adhesive Wound Closure Strips / Butterfly Closures)

**Best for:** Minor to moderate lacerations with low tension; flat skin surfaces; facial lacerations (cosmetically good result); wounds in patients who cannot tolerate wound adhesive

**Application technique:**
1. Ensure wound edges are dry
2. Optionally apply benzoin tincture to skin adjacent to wound (NOT in wound) to improve adhesion
3. Attach one end of strip to skin 5 mm from wound edge
4. Approximate wound edges, then attach strip to opposite side
5. Apply strips every 3–5 mm along wound length
6. Apply cross-strips perpendicular to wound for additional reinforcement if available
7. Leave 3–5 mm gaps between strips to allow drainage

**Limitations:**
- Lowest tensile strength of closure methods — not suitable for high-tension wounds
- Poor adhesion in moist/hairy areas
- Risk of premature detachment in wet wilderness environments

---

### 14b. Dermabond (Octyl Cyanoacrylate Tissue Adhesive / Surgical Glue)

**Best for:** Facial lacerations, irregular surfaces, lacerations up to moderate length, clean linear wounds; excellent cosmetic results

**Application technique:**
1. Irrigate and dry wound thoroughly — adhesive does NOT work on wet surfaces
2. Approximate wound edges with fingers or forceps
3. Apply thin layer of adhesive across wound surface (not into wound)
4. Hold edges together for 30–60 seconds while adhesive cures
5. Apply 2–3 additional thin layers for strength
6. Do NOT cover with dressing (adhesive serves as protective layer)

**Properties:**
- Antimicrobial film provides some bacterial barrier (active against Gram-positive and Gram-negative organisms)
- Contains plasticizer allowing flexibility over moving surfaces
- Effective even over joints if properly splinted

**Critical Caution:**
- **Do NOT allow adhesive to enter the wound interior** — causes acute inflammatory reaction
- Do NOT combine with povidone-iodine or hydrogen peroxide
- Avoid mucosal surfaces, axilla, perineum (adhesive failure)
- Not suitable for large, deep, or heavily contaminated wounds

**Improvised alternative (last resort):** Superglue (cyanoacrylate, e.g., Krazy Glue) has been used in extreme wilderness emergencies. Less flexible, more tissue-reactive, and not sterile — significant infection and inflammatory reaction risk. Use only in true extremis when nothing else is available and wound would be life-threatening if open.

---

### 14c. Staples

**Best for:** Scalp lacerations (hair covers cosmetic concern); trunk wounds; high-tension wounds where strong closure needed; long lacerations requiring rapid closure

**Application technique:**
1. Irrigate wound
2. Approximate wound edges with non-dominant hand (or second rescuer)
3. Center stapler over wound
4. Gently press stapler against skin before activating trigger (prevents over-driving)
5. Apply at 5–7 mm intervals along wound length
6. Staple removal: requires staple remover (standard equipment for any kit including staples)

**Advantages:** Faster application than sutures; less skill required; strong; low tissue reactivity vs. sutures in some studies

**Limitations:**
- "Train track" scarring — not for cosmetically sensitive areas
- Higher patient discomfort during wear and removal
- Contraindicated for face

---

### 14d. Sutures (Traditional Standard; Advanced Skill)

- WFR curriculum includes basic wound closure techniques
- WEMT and higher training includes suturing
- For field use: Simple interrupted sutures using monofilament (nylon/prolene) are most practical
- Always close dead space with absorbable sutures before skin closure when depth warrants
- Typically requires 4-0 or 5-0 suture for face; 3-0 or 4-0 for trunk/extremities; 2-0 for scalp

---

### Quick Method Selection Guide

| Situation | Preferred Method |
|---|---|
| Small, clean, low-tension wound | Steri-Strips |
| Facial laceration, clean | Dermabond or Steri-Strips |
| Scalp laceration | Staples (or hair tie technique for small scalp wounds) |
| Long laceration, trunk | Staples or sutures |
| High-tension wound (over joint) | Sutures; splint joint after closure |
| Contaminated wound | Pack open; do NOT close primarily |
| Deep wound with dead space | Sutures (layered); beyond basic WFR scope |

## 15. IMPROVISED SPLINTS AND TRACTION DEVICES

**Source:** WMS — Improvised Femur Traction (WMS Magazine); PMC8027528 — A Guide to Improvised Femoral Traction Splint in a Resource-Limited Setting; PubMed 22441092 — Improvised Traction Splints: A Wilderness Medicine Tool or Hindrance? (*Wilderness & Environmental Medicine*, 2012); Wilderness Emergency Care — Splinting Fractured Femurs

---

### 15a. General Splinting Principles

**Indications:** Any suspected fracture or severe sprain; unstable joint injury; suspected dislocation (after reduction if applicable)

**Rules of splinting:**
1. Splint in position of function (slight flexion at joints) or in position found if movement causes significant pain
2. Immobilize the joint ABOVE and BELOW the fracture
3. Pad bony prominences to prevent pressure injury
4. Check neurovascular status (circulation, sensation, movement) before and after splinting
5. Elevate extremity when possible to reduce swelling

**Improvised splinting materials:**
- Trekking poles, tent poles, branches (rigid support)
- Sleeping pad foam (padding and rigidity)
- SAM splint (standard kit item — moldable aluminum/foam)
- Clothing, pack straps (securing material)
- Sleeping bag padding
- Cardboard (emergency; poor water resistance)

**General technique:**
1. Gather materials to span joint above and below fracture
2. Pad rigid materials to protect skin
3. Apply in anatomical position or position of comfort
4. Secure with straps/bandaging material at minimum 3 points (above, at, and below fracture)
5. Straps tight enough to hold position but allowing 2-finger space between strap and skin (neurovascular check)

---

### 15b. Femur Fracture and Traction Splinting

**Why traction splinting matters:**
- Femur fractures can cause blood loss of **up to 1.5 liters** into the thigh
- Untreated femur fractures carry mortality up to 54% in austere environments
- Traction reduces pain, limits ongoing blood loss by preventing hematoma expansion, reduces soft tissue injury, and prevents progression to open fracture

**Historical evidence:** Traction splinting in WWI reduced femur fracture mortality from 80% to 15.6%.

**Contraindications to traction splinting:**
- Pelvic fracture (do NOT apply traction)
- Patellar fracture or significant ligamentous knee injury
- Tibia/fibula fracture concurrent with femur fracture
- Distal femur fracture near knee (risk of popliteal artery/nerve injury)
- Hip dislocation

#### Improvised Traction Splint from Trekking Poles (Thomas-Style)
Published wilderness medicine literature supports improvisation from trekking poles:

1. **Preparation:** Two trekking poles extended longer than the injured leg; ankle hitch from webbing or bandana; padded ischial cushion from clothing
2. **Ankle hitch:** Loop webbing around ankle (not foot) in a figure-eight or clove hitch pattern; leave tails for traction
3. **Apply manual traction** to the leg (steady, firm pull along long axis of femur) — maintain throughout
4. **Position poles** on medial and lateral sides of the leg from groin to beyond foot
5. **Secure proximal end** with padded wrap against ischial tuberosity/perineum
6. **Attach ankle hitch tails** to the distal cross member of the pole arrangement
7. **Apply tension** until pain is reduced and muscle spasm eases (typically 10–15 lb traction)
8. **Secure** with straps at thigh and proximal leg (not over fracture site)
9. **Recheck** pulse, sensation, and capillary refill every 10–15 minutes

> **Research note (Weichenthal et al., 2012, *Wilderness & Environmental Medicine*):** Improvised traction splint was "not inferior" to commercially available devices in a pilot study.

**Commercial alternatives when available:** Sager, KTD, Slishman (no ischial pad designs preferred for mid-shaft and proximal fractures), Hare splint, CT-6

---

### 15c. Upper Extremity Splints

**Wrist/Forearm:** SAM splint or padded branch; volar or sugar-tong configuration; sling for elevation

**Elbow (position of function):** 90° flexion if vascular exam intact; 10–20° from full extension if neurovascular compromise present (prevents cephalic/basilic vein compression)

**Shoulder/Clavicle (sling and swathe):**
1. Sling: Triangle bandage supporting forearm with wrist slightly above elbow height
2. Swathe: Wrap around torso to immobilize arm against body

## 16. WOUND IRRIGATION DEVICES (IMPROVISED)

**Source:** Luck et al., "Pressures of Wilderness Improvised Wound Irrigation Techniques: How Do They Compare?" *Wilderness & Environmental Medicine*, 2016 (PubMed 27793443); StatPearls — Wound Irrigation (NBK538522); WMS Practice Guidelines for Basic Wound Management

### Why Irrigation Pressure Matters

High-pressure irrigation mechanically disrupts bacterial adhesion to wound surfaces, reducing infection risk. The clinically effective threshold is **7 pounds per square inch (psi)**.

**WMS Recommendation:** High-pressure irrigation of **6–15 psi** is recommended, especially for open fractures and high-risk wounds.

### Standard Medical Device
**35 mL syringe + 18–19 gauge needle or angiocatheter** = approximately **7–8 psi** (standard reference device)

### Improvised Devices — Evidence-Based Results (Luck et al., 2016)

| Device | Pressure Achieved | Meets Threshold? |
|---|---|---|
| 10 mL syringe + 14g angiocatheter | 16–49 psi | YES (exceeds) |
| 50 mL Sawyer syringe | 7–11 psi | YES |
| Punctured water bottle cap (14g needle hole) | 7–25 psi | YES |
| Water bottle with sports cap | 3–7 psi | MARGINAL |
| Commercial wound irrigation device | 4–5 psi | Reference device |
| Bladder hydration system (e.g., Camelbak) | 1–2 psi | NO |
| Punctured plastic bag + 14g needle | 2–3 psi | NO |

**Key finding (Luck et al.):** "All systems using compressible water bottles and all syringe-based systems provided pressures meeting or exceeding those measured with a commercial wound irrigation device."

### Improvised Irrigation Technique

**Using water bottle with punctured cap:**
1. Fill clean water bottle with clean (potable) water or 1% povidone-iodine solution
2. Puncture cap with 18-gauge needle (3–4 holes) OR use a sports top for lower pressure
3. Hold wound 2–3 cm from water source
4. Squeeze bottle firmly in short bursts, directing stream across wound surface
5. Volume: Irrigate until water runs clear; minimum 200–500 mL for small wounds; 1,000+ mL for contaminated wounds

**Using improvised syringe (if available):**
1. Fill 30–60 mL syringe with clean water
2. Attach 18-gauge needle, angiocatheter, or IV catheter
3. Direct at 45° angle, 2–3 cm from wound surface
4. Press plunger firmly in steady strokes
5. Do NOT direct stream directly perpendicular into wound (splatter/contamination risk)

**Irrigation fluid:**
- Preferred: Clean potable water (tap or filtered/treated)
- Acceptable: 1% povidone-iodine solution (dilute standard 10% Betadine 1:10)
- NOT recommended: Hydrogen peroxide, alcohol, bleach solutions (cytotoxic to wound tissue)

> **CAUTION:** High-pressure irrigation (above 70 psi) can damage tissue. The 16–49 psi achieved by syringe + angiocatheter is safe and effective, well below the tissue damage threshold.

## 17. IMPROVISED OXYGEN DELIVERY

**Source:** StatPearls — Oxygen Administration (NBK551617); Emergency Oxygen Administration (*Clinical Gate*, *Anesthesia Key*); JEMS — Consider Wilderness Medicine Techniques for MCIs; HSI Emergency Oxygen Administration; PMC6111205 — Oxygen Treatment in Intensive Care

> **NOTE:** Medical oxygen cylinders are rarely carried in wilderness settings due to weight and regulatory requirements. This section covers both standard emergency oxygen administration principles and improvised alternatives relevant to field medicine. Supplemental oxygen is standard treatment for HACE, HAPE, and altitude-related illness.

### Indications for Supplemental Oxygen in the Field
- SpO2 (pulse oximeter reading) ≤94%
- Signs/symptoms of hypoxia: cyanosis, altered mental status, respiratory distress
- HACE (High Altitude Cerebral Edema) — mandatory
- HAPE (High Altitude Pulmonary Edema) — mandatory; primary treatment
- Severe anaphylaxis with bronchospasm
- Carbon monoxide poisoning
- Cardiac arrest / respiratory arrest

**Administration of oxygen is NEVER contraindicated in emergency situations.** The risk of withholding oxygen far outweighs the theoretical risk of blunting respiratory drive (which is not a clinical concern in short-term field use).

---

### Standard Delivery Devices

| Device | FiO2 Delivered | Flow Rate | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nasal cannula | 24–44% | 1–6 L/min | Well-tolerated; good for mild-moderate hypoxia |
| Simple face mask | 35–50% | 5–10 L/min | Moderate hypoxia |
| Non-rebreather mask | 60–80% | 10–15 L/min | High-flow O2; most common high-concentration device |
| Bag-Valve-Mask (BVM) | ~100% | 15 L/min | Two-rescuer preferred; also functions without O2 source for ventilation |
| Demand delivery system | Near 100% | Breath-triggered | Conserves O2; ideal for extended field transport |

---

### O2 Conservation in Field Settings

A major challenge in wilderness oxygen administration is **O2 supply duration**. Standard flow masks waste oxygen continuously.

**Demand Oxygen Delivery Systems (DODS):** Deliver O2 only during inhalation (breath-triggered). Significantly conserve O2 supply. Valuable when supply is limited and evacuation transport will be delayed.

**Rebreather principle:** By removing CO2 from exhaled gas (using soda lime or similar absorbent) and recycling exhaled oxygen, rebreather systems maximize duration of oxygen supply — used in some rescue operations and hyperbaric chambers.

### Portable Hyperbaric Chambers (Gamow Bag / PAC)

**Altitude illness applications (HACE/HAPE):** A portable hyperbaric chamber (Gamow Bag) simulates descent by increasing atmospheric pressure around the patient. Effective when:
- Descent is not immediately possible
- Used simultaneously with oxygen and medications
- Inflated to 2–15 psi above ambient pressure (simulates descent of 1,500–2,400 m)

Operation requires continuous pumping to maintain pressure and vent CO2.

### Improvised Oxygen Delivery (True Last-Resort Measures)

**BVM without oxygen source:** BVM functions as a manual ventilation device without an O2 source, delivering room air (21% O2) — still preferable to no ventilation in respiratory arrest.

**Pocket mask:** Available with or without oxygen inlet port; provides rescue breathing with less exhaled gas dilution than mouth-to-mouth; lightweight and appropriate for all first aid kits.

**Improvised cricothyrotomy:** Published wilderness medicine literature (EMRA) notes that **hydration bladder tubing has been used to perform emergency airway access** on the side of a mountain. This is the most extreme improvised airway technique — for complete airway obstruction when all other maneuvers fail. Requires identifying the cricothyroid membrane, making a controlled incision, and inserting tubing for air passage. Only performed when airway is completely obstructed and patient is dying.

**Positioning for oxygen optimization (without supplemental O2):**
- Sitting upright (tripod position) optimizes respiratory mechanics in dyspnea
- In HAPE: minimize exertion (O2 consumption increases dramatically with movement)
- Descent: Every meter of descent increases atmospheric PO2 — even 300 m descent can produce meaningful improvement

# APPENDIX: GENERAL SAFETY WARNINGS

1. **This document does not replace training.** WFR, WEMT, or equivalent certification is strongly recommended for anyone who may need to apply these techniques.

2. **Scope of practice varies by jurisdiction.** Know your state/regional regulations for administering medications and performing procedures.

3. **Always evacuate.** Field treatment is a bridge to definitive medical care — not a substitute.

4. **Prescription medications require physician authorization.** Do not stockpile or administer prescription medications without appropriate medical oversight.

5. **Check for allergies.** Before any medication administration, assess for known allergies. Many wilderness emergencies have been worsened by anaphylactic reactions to medications administered in the field.

6. **Document everything.** Record medications administered (name, dose, time, route), procedures performed, vital signs, and patient responses. This information is critical for receiving medical teams.

7. **Expired medications:** Per FDA/DoD SLEP data, many medications retain efficacy beyond their labeled date when stored properly — but use is at your own risk, and some drugs (tetracyclines, cardiac medications, insulin) should never be used expired.

8. **Herbal remedies are not substitutes for conventional medicine** when conventional medicine is available.

9. **Update your kit.** Check expiration dates before every expedition. Replace expired items. Rotate stock.

10. **When in doubt, do not administer.** The risk of the wrong treatment can exceed the risk of no treatment in many field situations.
