## PART 1: PHARMACOLOGY FUNDAMENTALS

### Mechanisms of Drug Action

**Receptor Pharmacology**:
- **Agonist**: binds receptor AND activates it (mimics endogenous substance)
- **Antagonist**: binds receptor WITHOUT activating it (blocks endogenous substance)
- **Partial agonist**: binds and activates receptor but to lesser degree than full agonist
  - Example: buprenorphine (partial opioid agonist) — analgesic but "ceiling effect" on respiratory depression
- **Inverse agonist**: binds receptor and produces opposite effect of agonist

**Dose-Response Relationships**:
- **ED50**: dose effective in 50% of population
- **LD50**: lethal dose in 50% of population
- **Therapeutic index (TI)**: LD50/ED50 — higher = safer drug
  - Narrow TI drugs (require monitoring): digoxin, warfarin, lithium, aminoglycosides, vancomycin, phenytoin, theophylline
  - Wide TI drugs (relatively safe): acetaminophen (until overdose threshold), most antibiotics

**Enzyme Inhibition vs Induction**:
- **Enzyme inhibitors** (increase drug levels of substrate):
  - Cimetidine: inhibits CYP1A2, CYP2C9, CYP2D6, CYP3A4
  - Fluconazole: potent CYP2C9 and CYP3A4 inhibitor
  - Ciprofloxacin: CYP1A2 inhibitor
  - Grapefruit juice: CYP3A4 inhibitor (avoid with many medications)
  - Erythromycin, clarithromycin: CYP3A4 inhibitors

- **Enzyme inducers** (decrease drug levels of substrate):
  - Rifampin: powerful inducer of most CYP enzymes (dramatically reduces many drug levels)
  - Carbamazepine, phenobarbital, phenytoin: CYP inducers
  - St. John's Wort: CYP3A4 inducer (reduces hormonal contraceptive efficacy!)
  - Chronic alcohol use: induces CYP2E1 (increases acetaminophen toxicity risk)

### Critical Drug Interactions

**High-Risk Combinations**:

| Drug 1 | Drug 2 | Interaction | Risk |
|--------|--------|-------------|------|
| Warfarin | NSAIDs | Additive bleeding | High |
| Warfarin | Antibiotics (many) | Altered gut flora → increased warfarin effect | High |
| Warfarin | Fluconazole | CYP2C9 inhibition → 2-3× warfarin levels | High |
| MAOIs | SSRIs | Serotonin syndrome (life-threatening) | Absolute |
| MAOIs | Meperidine | Serotonin syndrome | Absolute |
| MAOIs | Tyramine foods | Hypertensive crisis | High |
| Metformin | IV contrast | Acute kidney injury → metformin accumulation → lactic acidosis | High |
| ACE inhibitors | Potassium-sparing diuretics | Hyperkalemia | High |
| ACE inhibitors | NSAIDs | Reduced antihypertensive effect; acute kidney injury | High |
| Beta-blockers | Verapamil/Diltiazem | Bradycardia, AV block, heart failure | High |
| Statins | Macrolide antibiotics | CYP3A4 inhibition → myopathy | Moderate |
| Fluoroquinolones | Antacids/Ca/Mg/Fe | Chelation → reduced antibiotic absorption | Important |
| Oral antibiotics | OCPs | Possibly reduced contraceptive efficacy (debated) | Low-moderate |

**Serotonin Syndrome** (memorize — life-threatening):
- Cause: excess serotonin activity
- Triggers: SSRIs + MAOIs, tramadol + SSRIs, linezolid + SSRIs, methylene blue + SSRIs
- Triad: cognitive changes + neuromuscular abnormalities + autonomic instability
- Specific findings: clonus (rhythmic jerking elicited by brisk dorsiflexion), hyperreflexia
- Treatment: stop offending agents, cyproheptadine (serotonin antagonist), supportive

**Prolonged QT and Torsades de Pointes**:
- QT interval: measure of ventricular repolarization (corrected for HR = QTc)
- Normal QTc: <440ms men, <450ms women; concerning >500ms
- Torsades: dangerous arrhythmia triggered by long QT
- QT-prolonging drugs (additive risk!): azithromycin, clarithromycin, fluoroquinolones (especially moxifloxacin), haloperidol, methadone, ondansetron (high doses), chloroquine, hydroxychloroquine
- Risk factors: female sex, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, bradycardia, structural heart disease
- Field management: correct electrolytes, magnesium 2g IV push for acute torsades

### Autonomic Pharmacology

**Sympathetic Nervous System** (fight or flight):
- Neurotransmitters: norepinephrine (NE) at effectors; epinephrine from adrenal medulla
- Alpha-1 receptors: vasoconstriction, pupil dilation
- Alpha-2 receptors: reduced NE release (presynaptic); reduced sympathetic outflow
- Beta-1 receptors: increased HR, increased contractility (cardiac)
- Beta-2 receptors: bronchodilation, vasodilation, uterine relaxation

**Sympathomimetics** (activate sympathetic):
- Epinephrine: alpha + beta; anaphylaxis treatment; vasoconstriction + bronchodilation
- Norepinephrine: primarily alpha; vasopressor in septic shock
- Dopamine: dose-dependent; low dose: D1 receptors (renal vasodilation); medium: beta-1 (cardiac); high: alpha (vasoconstriction)
- Albuterol: beta-2 selective; bronchodilation; tocolysis (relaxes uterus)
- Phenylephrine: alpha-1 selective; nasal decongestant; vasopressor when tachycardia is concern

**Sympatholytics** (block sympathetic):
- Beta-blockers: block beta-1 (and beta-2 for non-selective); reduce HR, BP, cardiac work
  - Selective: metoprolol, atenolol (cardiac, but at high doses lose selectivity)
  - Non-selective: propranolol, nadolol (also block beta-2 → bronchospasm, contraindicated in asthma)
  - Beta-blockers contraindicated in: decompensated heart failure (acutely), high-degree AV block, asthma
- Alpha-blockers: prazosin, doxazosin; reduce BP; used in BPH; orthostatic hypotension common

**Parasympathetic Nervous System** (rest and digest):
- Neurotransmitter: acetylcholine (ACh)
- Muscarinic receptors: M1 (CNS, gastric acid), M2 (cardiac), M3 (smooth muscle, glands)
- Mnemonic for muscarinic effects: DUMBBELLS or SLUDGE
  - **S**alivation, **L**acrimation, **U**rination, **D**efecation, **G**I distress, **E**mesis
  - + Bradycardia, Bronchoconstriction, Miosis

**Anticholinergics** (block parasympathetic/muscarinic):
- Atropine: tachycardia, mydriasis, dry mouth, urinary retention, reduced secretions
  - Uses: bradycardia, organophosphate poisoning, drying secretions before surgery
  - Mnemonic for toxicity: "Blind as a bat, mad as a hatter, red as a beet, hot as Hades, dry as a bone"
- Ipratropium: inhaled anticholinergic for COPD/bronchospasm (not well absorbed systemically)
- Diphenhydramine: antihistamine with anticholinergic properties; sedating; urinary retention risk

**Cholinergics/Anticholinesterases** (enhance parasympathetic):
- Neostigmine, pyridostigmine: block acetylcholinesterase → more ACh available
  - Uses: myasthenia gravis, reversal of neuromuscular blockade
- Organophosphates (insecticides, nerve agents): irreversible AChE inhibition
  - Causes: SLUDGE + bradycardia + seizures + respiratory failure
  - Treatment: atropine (large doses, titrate to dry secretions — NOT heart rate); pralidoxime (2-PAM) within 24 hours; benzodiazepines for seizures

## PART 2: CRITICAL MEDICATIONS REFERENCE

### Antimicrobials

**Antibiotic Classes and Mechanisms**:

| Class | Mechanism | Examples | Coverage |
|-------|---------|---------|---------|
| Penicillins | Cell wall synthesis (beta-lactam ring) | Amoxicillin, ampicillin | Gram+ (strep, staph non-MRSA) |
| Cephalosporins | Cell wall synthesis | Cephalexin, ceftriaxone, cefepime | Varies by generation |
| Carbapenems | Cell wall synthesis | Imipenem, meropenem | Broad spectrum; last resort |
| Fluoroquinolones | DNA gyrase/topoisomerase | Ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin | Gram-negative; atypicals |
| Macrolides | 50S ribosome (protein) | Azithromycin, erythromycin | Gram+, atypicals |
| Tetracyclines | 30S ribosome (protein) | Doxycycline, minocycline | Broad; atypicals, rickettsial |
| Aminoglycosides | 30S ribosome | Gentamicin, tobramycin | Gram-negative (synergy) |
| Sulfonamides/TMP | Folic acid synthesis | TMP-SMX | Broad; UTI, PCP |
| Metronidazole | DNA damage (anaerobes) | Flagyl | Anaerobes, C. diff, Giardia |
| Vancomycin | Cell wall (glycopeptide) | Vancomycin | MRSA, Gram+; IV for serious |
| Clindamycin | 50S ribosome | Clindamycin | Gram+; anaerobes; MRSA skin |
| Linezolid | 50S ribosome (unique) | Zyvox | MRSA; VRE; no IM/IV needed |

**Beta-Lactam Allergy Considerations**:
- True penicillin allergy (anaphylaxis): 1–2% cross-reactivity with cephalosporins
- Reported penicillin allergy: often not true allergy; skin testing preferred if available
- Carbapenems: <1% cross-reactivity with penicillin allergy
- If true severe penicillin allergy: use azithromycin (respiratory), clindamycin (soft tissue), vancomycin (serious infections), fluoroquinolone (GI/UTI)

**Empiric Treatment by Infection Site**:

*Skin/Soft Tissue (mild/moderate)*:
- Cellulitis without MRSA risk: cephalexin 500mg QID × 5–7 days
- Cellulitis with MRSA risk: TMP-SMX DS 2 tabs BID + cephalexin; or doxycycline 100mg BID
- Abscess: drainage is primary treatment; antibiotics if cellulitis >2cm extending from abscess

*Respiratory (Community-acquired Pneumonia)*:
- Outpatient, no comorbidities: azithromycin 500mg day 1 then 250mg × 4 days; or doxycycline 100mg BID × 5 days
- Outpatient, comorbidities: amoxicillin-clavulanate + azithromycin; or respiratory fluoroquinolone

*Urinary Tract Infection*:
- Uncomplicated UTI (female): TMP-SMX DS BID × 3 days; or nitrofurantoin 100mg BID × 5 days
- Complicated UTI/pyelonephritis: ciprofloxacin 500mg BID × 7–14 days; or TMP-SMX × 14 days

*Intra-abdominal*:
- Metronidazole + ciprofloxacin (covers anaerobes + Gram-negatives)
- Or amoxicillin-clavulanate alone for mild cases

*Typhoid fever (Salmonella typhi)*:
- Azithromycin 500mg QD × 7 days; or ciprofloxacin × 10–14 days

*Malaria treatment*:
- Chloroquine-sensitive: chloroquine 600mg base then 300mg at 6h, 24h, 48h
- Chloroquine-resistant (most of world): artemether-lumefantrine (Coartem) or atovaquone-proguanil
- Severe malaria: IV artesunate (preferred) or IV quinine + doxycycline

### Cardiovascular Medications

**Anticoagulants**:

*Heparin (unfractionated)*:
- Mechanism: enhances antithrombin III; prevents clot extension
- DVT/PE treatment: 80 units/kg IV bolus then 18 units/kg/hr infusion; adjust by PTT
- Monitor: aPTT (goal 60–100 sec); platelets (HIT risk after day 4)
- Reversal: protamine sulfate (1 mg per 100 units heparin given in last 4 hours)

*Warfarin*:
- Mechanism: blocks vitamin K epoxide reductase → reduced clotting factors II, VII, IX, X
- Onset: 3–7 days (existing factors must be cleared)
- Monitor: INR (goal 2–3 for most indications; 2.5–3.5 for mechanical heart valve)
- Reversal: vitamin K 5–10mg PO/IV; fresh frozen plasma (FFP) for urgent reversal; 4-factor PCC for life-threatening bleeding
- Drug interactions: highly variable; many antibiotics, NSAIDs, herbals affect INR

*Aspirin*:
- Irreversibly inhibits COX-1 → reduced thromboxane A2 → platelet aggregation impaired
- Effect lasts platelet lifetime (7–10 days)
- Low-dose (81–100mg): antiplatelet; used in CAD, stroke, TIA prevention
- High-dose (325–650mg): anti-inflammatory, analgesic, antipyretic

**Cardiac Medications**:

*Digoxin*:
- Mechanism: inhibits Na/K-ATPase → increases intracellular Ca → increased contractility; slows AV node
- Uses: heart failure with reduced EF; rate control in atrial fibrillation
- Narrow therapeutic index: toxicity is common
- Toxicity signs: nausea/vomiting, visual disturbances (yellow-green halos), arrhythmias (any arrhythmia possible)
- Toxic level: >2 ng/mL; dig-specific antibody fragments (DigiFab) for severe toxicity
- Caution: levels increase with hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, renal failure

*Amiodarone*:
- Class III antiarrhythmic; multiple mechanisms; used for many arrhythmias
- IV: loading dose 150mg over 10 min, then infusion for pulseless VT/VF
- Oral: loading then 200mg QD maintenance; multiple weeks to reach steady state
- Adverse effects: pulmonary toxicity, thyroid dysfunction (both hypo and hyper), corneal deposits, liver toxicity, photosensitivity, blue-gray skin discoloration
- Half-life: 40–55 DAYS (extremely long; effects persist long after stopping)
- Prolongs QT; monitor thyroid, LFTs, chest x-ray annually

### Endocrine Medications

**Insulin Types** (memorize for diabetic emergencies):
| Type | Onset | Peak | Duration | Use |
|------|-------|------|---------|-----|
| Lispro (Humalog) | 15 min | 30–90 min | 3–5 hr | Mealtime |
| Aspart (NovoLog) | 15 min | 40–50 min | 3–5 hr | Mealtime |
| Regular | 30–60 min | 2–4 hr | 6–8 hr | Mealtime; IV drip |
| NPH | 1–2 hr | 4–10 hr | 12–16 hr | Intermediate |
| Glargine (Lantus) | 2–4 hr | Peakless | 20–24 hr | Basal |
| Detemir (Levemir) | 1–2 hr | Peakless | 16–24 hr | Basal |
| Degludec (Tresiba) | 1 hr | Peakless | >42 hr | Ultra-long basal |

**Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) Management**:
1. Fluids: normal saline 1L/hr × 2 hours, then 250–500 mL/hr
2. Insulin: regular insulin 0.1 units/kg/hr IV infusion (NO bolus initially)
3. Potassium replacement: if K <3.5, replace before starting insulin (insulin drives K into cells → dangerous hypokalemia)
4. Glucose target: drop by 50–75 mg/dL/hr; don't drop too fast
5. Add D5 to fluids when glucose <250 mg/dL (but continue insulin to close anion gap)
6. Monitor: glucose hourly, electrolytes every 2–4 hours, anion gap

**Hypoglycemia Treatment**:
- Mild (<70 mg/dL, conscious): "Rule of 15" — 15g carbohydrate, wait 15 min, recheck
  - 4 oz juice; 3–4 glucose tablets; 8 oz milk; 1 tbsp honey; 6–8 Lifesavers
- Moderate (altered consciousness): dextrose 25–50g IV (D50W); or glucagon 1mg IM/SC
- Severe (unconscious, no IV access): glucagon 1mg IM/SC; honey/sugar inside cheeks if airway protected
- Never give oral glucose to unconscious patients (aspiration risk)

## PART 3: TOXICOLOGY AND POISONING

### Toxic Syndromes (Toxidromes)

**Opioid Toxidrome**:
- Classic triad: altered mental status (stupor/coma), respiratory depression, miosis
- Additional: bradycardia, hypotension, hypothermia
- Reversal: naloxone; titrate to breathing, not consciousness
- Common opioids: heroin, morphine, oxycodone, fentanyl, methadone, codeine, tramadol

**Anticholinergic Toxidrome**:
- "Blind as bat" (blurred vision, mydriasis), "Mad as hatter" (delirium), "Red as beet" (flushing), "Hot as Hades" (hyperthermia, no sweating), "Dry as bone" (dry skin, dry mucous membranes)
- Also: tachycardia, urinary retention, decreased bowel sounds
- Causes: diphenhydramine, tricyclic antidepressants, scopolamine, atropine, jimsonweed (Datura), deadly nightshade
- Treatment: physostigmine (specific reversal for severe cases); cooling; benzodiazepines for agitation

**Cholinergic Toxidrome**:
- SLUDGE + bradycardia + bronchospasm + seizures
- Causes: organophosphate insecticides, nerve agents (sarin, VX), carbamates, pilocarpine
- Treatment: atropine 2–4mg IV q5 min until secretions dry; pralidoxime (2-PAM); benzos

**Sympathomimetic Toxidrome**:
- Tachycardia, hypertension, hyperthermia, mydriasis, diaphoresis, agitation
- Causes: cocaine, methamphetamine, MDMA, ephedrine, pseudoephedrine
- Treatment: benzodiazepines (first-line); avoid beta-blockers (unopposed alpha stimulation)

**Sedative-Hypnotic Toxidrome**:
- CNS depression; slurred speech, ataxia, stupor → coma
- Relatively PRESERVED pupil and lung sounds (unlike opioids)
- Causes: benzodiazepines, barbiturates, alcohol, GHB, zolpidem
- Treatment: supportive; flumazenil reverses benzodiazepines but NEVER in chronic users (precipitates withdrawal seizures)

**Serotonin Syndrome vs NMS**:
| Feature | Serotonin Syndrome | NMS |
|---------|-------------------|-----|
| Onset | Hours after drug | Days after antipsychotic |
| Temperature | Elevated | Very elevated |
| Muscle findings | Clonus, hyperreflexia | Lead-pipe rigidity |
| Pupils | Dilated | Normal/variable |
| Cause | Serotonergic excess | Dopamine blockade |
| Treatment | Cyproheptadine, benzos | Dantrolene, bromocriptine |

### Plant Toxins

**Highly Toxic Plants**:

*Digitalis (Foxglove)*:
- Toxicity: same as digoxin toxicity (AV block, bradycardia, ventricular arrhythmias, nausea)
- ALL parts toxic; relatively small amount dangerous
- Treatment: DigiFab (digoxin-specific antibody fragments); atropine for bradycardia; avoid calcium

*Hemlock (Conium maculatum)*:
- Smooth stem with purple spots; umbrella flower clusters; musty smell
- Toxin: coniine (nicotinic agonist) → paralysis; death by respiratory failure
- Distinguish from edible Queen Anne's Lace: hemlock stem is smooth and purple-spotted; QAL has hairy stem
- No antidote; respiratory support

*Water Hemlock (Cicuta)*:
- Most violently toxic plant in North America
- Causes intense convulsions (status epilepticus) within minutes of ingestion
- Roots look edible; grow near water
- Treatment: benzos for seizures; aggressive supportive care

*Atropa belladonna (Deadly Nightshade)*:
- Anticholinergic toxicity (see above)
- Black berries, sweet; extremely dangerous in children

*Amanita phalloides (Death Cap mushroom)*:
- Responsible for 90% of mushroom fatalities worldwide
- Amatoxins: inhibit RNA polymerase → progressive liver and kidney failure
- Onset delayed 6–24 hours after ingestion (misleadingly asymptomatic initially)
- Treatment: activated charcoal if within 1 hour; silibinin (milk thistle extract — IV form available in Europe); NAC; liver transplant for fulminant hepatic failure

*Castor Bean (Ricinus communis)*:
- Contains ricin (one of most toxic natural substances known)
- 1 mg/kg is lethal dose; symptoms: GI then multi-organ failure 2–5 days post-ingestion
- No antidote; supportive care

**Venomous Animals**:

*Pit Viper Envenomation (Rattlesnake, Copperhead, Cottonmouth)*:
- Cytotoxic venom: tissue destruction, coagulopathy (clotting factor consumption)
- Assessment: fang marks, local swelling/ecchymosis, lab abnormalities (coagulation studies)
- Severity grading: dry bite → mild (local swelling) → moderate (swelling past ankle/wrist) → severe (systemic coagulopathy, shock)
- Treatment: CroFab (crotalid polyvalent immune Fab) — gold standard; give 4–6 vials IV; repeat if symptoms recur
- Do NOT: apply tourniquet; incise and suck; apply ice; use electric shock

*Coral Snake (Mojave, Eastern)*:
- Neurotoxic: delayed onset 8–12 hours; then respiratory paralysis
- "Red on yellow kill a fellow, red on black venom lack" — eastern US only (unreliable in other countries)
- Treatment: antivenom (limited availability); respiratory support; onset of symptoms warrants intubation

*Black Widow Spider*:
- Alpha-latrotoxin: causes massive neurotransmitter release
- Symptoms: intense muscle cramping/pain especially abdomen; hypertension; diaphoresis
- Treatment: pain control (opioids, benzodiazepines); antivenom for severe cases/elderly/children

*Brown Recluse Spider*:
- Sphingomyelinase D: local tissue necrosis; occasionally hemolysis
- Initial bite may be painless; develops over 2–8 hours
- No evidence-based treatment for local wound; excision no longer recommended
- Dapsone (controversial); supportive

## PART 4: IV DRUG RECONSTITUTION AND ADMINISTRATION

### Sterile Compounding Basics

**Aseptic Technique Principles**:
1. Hand washing: surgical scrub technique; 30-60 seconds
2. Gloves: sterile (not just clean); change if contaminated
3. Surface decontamination: 70% isopropyl alcohol; wipe, allow to dry
4. Horizontal laminar flow: not achievable in field; minimize airborne contamination
5. No touch technique: never touch critical sites (vial top, syringe tip, needle)
6. All manipulations: within inches of critical zone

**Beyond Use Dating** (BUD) — *from memory, do not rely on for formal practice*:
- Opened single-dose vials: discard within 1 hour
- Opened multi-dose vials: 28 days (must be labeled with date)
- Reconstituted drugs: check manufacturer insert; typically 24 hours refrigerated, 4 hours room temp
- IV admixtures: facility policy; generally 24–72 hours refrigerated, 4–12 hours room temp

**Reconstitution Steps**:
1. Verify drug, dose, concentration
2. Calculate volume to add and final concentration
3. Swab vial top with 70% alcohol; let dry
4. Draw calculated volume of diluent into syringe
5. Inject diluent into powder vial; don't touch needle to powder (prevents coring)
6. Swirl gently (don't shake unless specified); ensure complete dissolution
7. Withdraw required volume; check for particulates
8. Label: drug name, concentration, date/time, initials, BUD

**Calculation Template**:
```
Desired dose (mg) ÷ Concentration (mg/mL) = Volume to administer (mL)

Concentration (mg/mL) = Total drug (mg) ÷ Total volume (mL)

IV infusion rate: Volume (mL) ÷ Time (hours) = mL/hr
Drops/min = (Volume × drop factor) ÷ Time (minutes)
  Standard tubing: 15–20 gtt/mL
  Microdrip: 60 gtt/mL
```

**Common Reconstitution Examples**:

*Ceftriaxone 1g IV*:
- Add 9.6 mL NS → 100 mg/mL
- Or dilute in 100 mL NS for infusion over 30 minutes

*Vancomycin 1000mg IV*:
- Reconstitute with 20 mL sterile water → 50 mg/mL
- Dilute in 200–500 mL NS or D5W
- Infuse over minimum 60 minutes (faster → "red man syndrome" — histamine release)
- Rate: never faster than 10 mg/min

*Ampicillin 2g IV*:
- Reconstitute with 10 mL → 200 mg/mL
- Dilute in 100 mL NS
- Infuse over 30 minutes
- Stability: stable only 1 hour at room temperature after dilution

*Morphine 2mg/mL infusion*:
- Take 20mg morphine (10 mL of 2mg/mL), dilute in 240 mL NS = 250 mL total at 0.08 mg/mL
- Or: take 50mg morphine, add to 250 mL NS = 0.2 mg/mL; 10 mL/hr = 2 mg/hr

### Compatibility and Stability

**Y-Site Compatibility** (common incompatibilities):
- Phenytoin: precipitates in almost anything; give in NS only, flush before and after
- Calcium gluconate: incompatible with phosphate-containing solutions → precipitate
- Amphotericin B: only D5W (NS causes precipitation)
- Furosemide: incompatible with many acidic drugs
- Bicarbonate: incompatible with calcium, magnesium, dopamine

**Do NOT Mix** (common combinations):
- Calcium + Phosphate (at high concentrations) → calcium phosphate precipitation
- Penicillin + Aminoglycoside (in same syringe) — inactivate each other
- TPN (2-in-1) + lipids → oil in water emulsion destabilization

## PART 5: ANTIPARASITIC AND TROPICAL MEDICINE

### Parasitic Infections

**Malaria Prophylaxis**:
| Drug | Dose | Start/Stop | Notes |
|------|------|-----------|-------|
| Chloroquine | 500mg weekly | 1 week before, 4 weeks after | Only where sensitive |
| Atovaquone-proguanil (Malarone) | 1 tab daily | 1-2 days before, 7 days after | Expensive; few SE |
| Doxycycline | 100mg daily | 1-2 days before, 4 weeks after | Cheap; photosensitivity |
| Mefloquine | 250mg weekly | 2-3 weeks before, 4 weeks after | Neuro side effects |
| Primaquine | 30mg daily | 1-2 days before, 7 days after | Requires G6PD normal |

**G6PD Deficiency** (critical consideration):
- X-linked; affects ~400 million people worldwide; higher prevalence in malaria-endemic regions
- Oxidant drugs trigger hemolysis in G6PD-deficient patients
- Drugs to AVOID: primaquine, dapsone, rasburicase, high-dose aspirin, some antibiotics
- Test before prescribing primaquine (required for preventing P. vivax relapse)

**Intestinal Parasites Treatment**:
| Parasite | First Choice | Alternative |
|----------|-------------|------------|
| Giardia | Metronidazole 250mg TID × 5 days | Tinidazole 2g single dose |
| Entamoeba histolytica | Metronidazole × 10 days + luminal agent | — |
| Ascaris (roundworm) | Albendazole 400mg single dose | Mebendazole 500mg |
| Hookworm | Albendazole 400mg single dose | — |
| Strongyloides | Ivermectin 200 mcg/kg × 2 days | Albendazole × 7 days |
| Pinworm (Enterobius) | Albendazole 400mg; repeat in 2 weeks | Mebendazole |
| Tapeworm (Taenia) | Praziquantel 5-10 mg/kg single dose | Niclosamide |

**Ivermectin — Multiple Uses**:
- Strongyloides, Onchocerciasis (river blindness), Lymphatic filariasis, Lice, Scabies
- Very safe at standard doses; widely used in global health programs
- Dose: 150–200 mcg/kg (scabies/head lice: 200 mcg/kg × 1–2 doses)
- Widely available; over-the-counter in many countries
