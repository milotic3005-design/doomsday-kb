# Educational Survival & Navigation Supplement
## Open Educational Reference — Navigation, Wilderness Skills, Self-Reliance & Security

*Based on US Army Survival Manual FM 21-76, SERE (Survival, Evasion, Resistance, Escape) curriculum, National Outdoor Leadership School (NOLS) curriculum, and open-access wilderness education resources.*

## PART 1: LAND NAVIGATION

### Map Reading

**Map Types**:
- **Topographic (topo) map**: contour lines show elevation; USGS 7.5-minute series standard in US
- **Road/street map**: good for vehicle navigation; no terrain info
- **Satellite imagery**: excellent detail; no elevation data
- **Military grid maps**: UTM/MGRS coordinate system; 1:50,000 or 1:25,000 scale

**Contour Lines**:
- Lines connecting points of equal elevation
- Contour interval: elevation change between adjacent lines (shown in map legend)
- Close contours = steep terrain; wide contours = gentle terrain
- V-shape pointing uphill = valley/drainage
- V-shape pointing downhill = ridge/spur
- Closed circle: hilltop (higher if no tick marks; depression if inward-pointing ticks)
- Index contours: every 5th line is darker with elevation marked

**Map Scale**:
- 1:24,000 (USGS standard): 1 inch = 24,000 inches = 2000 feet; 2.64 inches = 1 mile
- 1:50,000 (military): 1 inch = 50,000 inches = ~0.79 miles; 1.25 inches = 1 mile
- 1:100,000: good for large-area planning; less terrain detail
- 1:250,000: strategic planning only; major features only

**UTM Coordinate System**:
- Divides world into 60 zones 6° longitude wide
- Northings: distance north from equator (or 10,000,000m south for Southern Hemisphere)
- Eastings: distance east within zone (false easting of 500,000m at zone center)
- Example: 17T 630000E 4830000N = zone 17T, 630km east of zone edge, 4830km north of equator
- MGRS extends UTM: adds 100km grid square letter codes

**Magnetic Declination**:
- Compass points to Magnetic North, not True North
- Declination: angle between magnetic and true north; varies by location and changes over time
- East declination: magnetic north is east of true north; subtract from magnetic to get true
- West declination: magnetic north is west of true north; add to magnetic to get true
- US: ranges from +20° (Washington state) to -20° (Maine)
- Rule: "East is least (subtract), West is best (add)" — for converting magnetic to true
- Always check current declination for your area (changes 0.1°/year)

### Compass Navigation

**Parts of a Compass**:
- Baseplate: transparent, with ruler edges and orienting lines
- Compass housing: rotating bezel with 360° markings (the azimuth ring)
- Magnetic needle: red end points magnetic north
- Orienting arrow: fixed in compass housing; aligns with magnetic north
- Direction of travel arrow: on baseplate; points in direction of travel

**Taking a Bearing to a Landmark**:
1. Point direction-of-travel arrow at the landmark
2. Rotate bezel until orienting arrow aligns with needle (red end in north)
3. Read bearing at direction-of-travel arrow
4. Account for declination

**Bearing from a Map**:
1. Draw line from current position to destination on map
2. Place compass along this line with direction-of-travel arrow pointing to destination
3. Rotate bezel until orienting lines align with map north-south grid lines (NOT magnetic needle)
4. Adjust for declination: East declination → subtract; West → add
5. This is your magnetic bearing to travel

**Dead Reckoning**:
- Track position by starting position + direction + distance traveled
- Pace counting: know your pace count (steps per 100m)
  - Average adult: ~62 double paces per 100m on flat terrain
  - Uphill: more paces per 100m; dense brush: more paces
  - Count double paces (every time left foot hits) to reduce mental load
- Tracking tool: ranger beads (9 beads first section = 900m; 4 beads second section = 4km)

**Terrain Association**:
- Match terrain features seen to map features
- Move from one identifiable feature to next: hilltops, streams, valleys, ridgelines
- Triangulation: take bearings to 3+ known features → intersection = your position

### Celestial Navigation

**Sun Navigation**:
- In Northern Hemisphere: sun rises roughly east, transits roughly south, sets roughly west
- Accuracy depends on latitude and time of year — east/west are approximate
- Shadow stick method:
  1. Place stick vertically; mark tip of shadow (this is a west point in morning, east in afternoon)
  2. Wait 10–15 minutes; mark new shadow tip
  3. Line from first to second mark: first mark → second mark = west → east
  4. Perpendicular to this line: north-south axis

**Star Navigation**:
- **Polaris (North Star)**: within 1° of true north; always visible in Northern Hemisphere
  - Find: locate Big Dipper; follow line from outer bowl stars (Merak to Dubhe) 5× distance → Polaris
  - OR: find Cassiopeia (W-shape); Polaris is on the "open" side midway between constellations
  - Altitude of Polaris above horizon ≈ your latitude

**Southern Hemisphere Navigation**:
- No equivalent to Polaris in Southern Hemisphere
- Southern Cross (Crux): identify; extend long axis 4.5× → south celestial pole
- Two bright stars (Pointer Stars: Alpha and Beta Centauri): draw line between them, bisect perpendicularly → south

**Time from Sun**:
- Local noon: sun at highest point (due south in Northern Hemisphere)
- Use to verify/set watch, determine location (longitude calculations)
- Watch method: hold watch horizontally; point hour hand at sun; south is midway between hour hand and 12 (Northern Hemisphere)

### Route Planning and Cross-Country Travel

**Route Selection Priorities**:
1. Safety (avalanche zones, flash flood areas, unstable terrain)
2. Water sources (stay within day's travel of water in dry country)
3. Terrain efficiency (ridgelines and valleys often easier than bushwhacking)
4. Visibility/concealment (as tactical situation requires)
5. Resupply points

**Energy Expenditure Estimation**:
- Flat ground: 2.5 mph comfortable pace; 3.5 mph fast
- Add 30 min per 1000 feet of elevation gain (Naismith's Rule)
- Heavy pack (>40 lbs): reduce estimates by 20%
- Bushwhacking: reduce to 1 mph or less through dense brush
- Snow travel without snowshoes: 0.5 mph or less in deep snow

**Crossing Water**:
- Moving water: assess: depth, speed, footing, hazards downstream
- Safe crossing if: depth × speed² < 0.5 (in feet and feet/sec)
- Use walking stick for stability (upstream, forms triangle with feet)
- Unbuckle hip belt and sternum strap (be ready to ditch pack if swept)
- Cross at wide, shallow sections (valley floor, not outside of bends)
- Rope assists: only if you can maintain rope tension; rope can become trap

**Mountain Terrain**:
- Afternoon thunderstorms in mountains: be below treeline by noon
- Scree: descend by plunging heels in, short shuffling steps; ascending: use zigzag
- Talus: step on top of larger rocks; test before committing weight
- Ridge travel: more exposed to weather and lightning; excellent visibility; better footing
- Valley travel: better shelter; water access; easier walking; poor visibility; flash flood risk

## PART 2: WILDERNESS SURVIVAL SKILLS

### Survival Priorities (Rule of Threes)

- 3 minutes without air (or in icy water)
- 3 hours without shelter in harsh weather
- 3 days without water
- 3 weeks without food
- 3 months without hope (psychological element)

**STOP Protocol**:
- **S**top: don't panic; sit down
- **T**hink: assess situation, resources, priorities
- **O**bserve: terrain, weather, materials available
- **P**lan: decide course of action

### Shelter Building

**Selecting a Shelter Site**:
- Avoid: valley bottoms (cold air settles, flooding), exposed ridges (wind, lightning), south-facing slopes in summer (too hot), north-facing slopes in winter (cold, wet)
- Choose: slight slope for drainage, natural windbreak, access to water (but above flood plain)
- Tree: examine for widow-makers (dead branches overhead); check soil stability

**Emergency Debris Hut**:
- Most effective emergency shelter requiring no tools
- Find downed log or lean branch against tree fork at roughly 45° angle
- Create rib structure along main support beam
- Layer debris (leaves, bark, pine needles): start at base, work toward top like shingles
- Thickness: arm's-length of debris minimum (18–24 inches); more in wet/cold
- Entrance: just large enough to enter; close with debris pile
- Rule: if you can compress debris to the thickness of your insulating layer and it springs back, you have enough

**Tarp Shelter Systems**:
- A-frame: fold tarp over ridgeline; stake out corners; add extra stake for pitch
- Lean-to: tie one edge to ridgeline; stake out other two corners; faces away from wind
- Plow point: complex fold; excellent rain shedding; requires single ridgeline
- Guy lines: tension at 30–45° angle; equalize tension around perimeter
- Seam sealing: new tarps need seam treatment (silicon sealant or seam sealer) for waterproofing

**Insulation Principles**:
- Dead air space = insulation; thickness of material matters more than material type
- Minimize conduction to ground: 2 inches of insulation under body more important than over (you compress insulation above you with your weight)
- Vapor barrier: in extreme cold, moisture barrier prevents insulation saturation but creates condensation issue
- Wind = major heat loss; windproofing is almost as important as insulation

### Water Procurement and Purification

**Finding Water**:
- Follow valleys downhill (water flows down)
- Look for vegetation changes: willows, cottonwood, cattails indicate water
- Watch for animal trails (may converge at water sources)
- Dig in dry stream beds: often find water 1–2 feet below surface at lowest point
- Dew collection: early morning; run cloth over vegetation; wring out into container
- Solar still: dig hole, place vegetation inside, cover with plastic, weight center — effectiveness is poor (may produce less than you sweat digging)
- Transpiration bag: plastic bag over leafy branch in sun; condensation collects

**Water Purification Methods**:

*Boiling* (most reliable):
- Kills all biological threats
- 1 minute vigorous boil (3 minutes above 6500 feet altitude)
- Does NOT remove chemical contamination or heavy metals

*Chemical treatment*:
- Bleach (sodium hypochlorite, 6–8.25%): 8 drops per gallon clear water; 16 drops cloudy
  - Wait 30 minutes; should smell faintly of chlorine
  - Effective against bacteria, most viruses; NOT against Cryptosporidium
- Iodine (2% tincture): 5 drops per liter clear water; 10 drops cloudy
  - Wait 30 minutes; longer if water cold
  - NOT recommended for pregnant women or thyroid conditions
  - NOT effective against Cryptosporidium
- Water purification tablets: follow manufacturer (usually 1 tablet/liter)

*Filtration*:
- Ceramic filter: 0.2–0.5 micron; removes bacteria and protozoa; NOT viruses
- Hollow fiber (LifeStraw, Sawyer): similar to ceramic
- Ultrafiltration: 0.01 micron; removes viruses
- Reverse osmosis: removes everything including dissolved salts (needed for seawater)
- Pre-filter turbid water through cloth before using fine filters (extends filter life)

*Combined approach for maximum safety*:
Filter (removes particles, extends chemical effectiveness) → Chemical treatment (kills viruses) → Boil if available resources allow

**DIY Gravity Filter**:
1. Layer: cotton cloth (course filter) → fine sand → coarse sand → small gravel → coarse gravel
2. In container with hole at base
3. Slow drip rate through layers
4. MUST follow with chemical treatment — only removes turbidity and some large organisms

### Fire Making

**Fire Triangle**: Fuel + Heat + Oxygen (remove any one = no fire)

**Tinder Selection and Preparation**:
- Dry, fine, fibrous material
- Best: dried grass, dry leaves, bark fibers, cattail fluff, birch bark, fatwood shavings
- Process tinder: shred, fluff, create "bird's nest" shape
- Test: should light with a single match in ideal conditions

**Friction Fire — Bow Drill** (most reliable primitive method):
- Materials needed: straight, dry, soft wood spindle; slightly harder fire board; notch cut in board; bark tinder catch
- Wood selection: cottonwood, willow, cedar, mullein for spindle; poplar, basswood, cottonwood for board
  - Woods must have similar hardness; both must be DRY (moisture content <10%)
  - Test: if spindle blackens same color as board, wood pairing works
- Bow: flexible wood ~24"; cordage: 1/4" diameter, non-stretchy (rawhide, paracord inner strands)
- Technique: apply downward pressure with handhold; bow strokes long and even; rhythm not speed

**Friction Fire — Hand Drill** (hardest; requires proper wood and conditions):
- No bow needed — spin spindle between palms
- Works best in dry, hot conditions; very difficult in humidity

**Flint and Steel** (charred cloth):
- True flint, chert, quartzite, or obsidian as striker
- High-carbon steel as striker (spine of knife)
- Char cloth: cotton fabric charred in tin with small hole; catches sparks at low heat
- Strike steel against edge of flint; direct spark onto char cloth; fold into tinder bundle; blow

**Fire Lay Types**:
- Teepee: good for starting; focuses heat; suitable for cooking on; burns fast
- Log cabin: burns slower; better for sustained fire; good bed of coals
- Star: push logs inward as they burn; fuel-efficient; good in desert
- Trench: dig trench; creates chimney effect; efficient; hidden at night

**Fire in Wet Conditions**:
- Elevated fire lay: keep fuel off wet ground (use green logs as base)
- Dry tinder: source from center of standing dead wood, inner bark, underside of raised logs
- Start small, build gradually; add larger wet wood to outer ring to dry it
- Wax-based fire starters (candles, wax cotton) extremely effective in wet conditions

### Food Procurement in the Field

**Caloric Needs vs Investment**:
- Average active adult: 2500–3500 calories/day
- Hunting calories may cost more than gained (large effort for uncertain return)
- Priorities: starchy roots and plants first (predictable calories), then invertebrates, then fish/small game, then large game

**Edible Plant Identification Framework**:
**Universal Edibility Test** (time-consuming but systematic):
1. Separate plant into parts: roots, leaves, stems, flowers, seeds
2. Smell: avoid rotten, musty smell (not reliable indicator alone)
3. Skin contact test: rub small amount on wrist; wait 15 minutes for reaction
4. Lip test: touch small amount to lips; wait 3 minutes
5. Tongue test: hold small amount on tongue tip; wait 15 minutes
6. Chew test: chew small amount; hold in mouth 15 minutes; spit out
7. Wait 8 hours with nothing else eaten; watch for reaction
8. Eat small portion; wait 8 hours
9. If no reaction, plant is likely safe to eat in this form

**Definite Avoids** (not worth testing):
- Milky sap (latex) — often toxic (exception: dandelion, which is safe)
- Umbrella-shaped (Apiaceae family) — includes deadly hemlock; need expert ID
- Plants with three-part leaves + white/yellow berries (poison ivy family)
- Bitter/burning taste that persists
- Any mushroom unless absolutely certain of identification (no "safe" test)

**Reliably Edible and Identifiable**:
- Cattail (Typha): pollen (yellow), young shoots, roots; starchy and nutritious; throughout North America
- Acorns: ALL oaks produce edible acorns (leach tannins in cold water changes 3-4×); high calories
- Dandelion: ALL parts edible; roots (roast for coffee), leaves (salad/cooked), flowers (fritters)
- Pine inner bark (cambium): white layer just under outer bark; raw or dried/ground
- Pine needles: high in Vitamin C; make tea (avoid Yew — needles toxic)
- Clover: leaves and flowers edible; common and easily identified
- Plantain (Plantago): flat rosette with parallel veins; widespread; leaves cooked or raw

**Trapping and Snaring** (food investment vs return):
- Small animals (squirrel, rabbit): best caloric return for effort
- Snare location: runs (narrow paths), near water, burrow entrances
- Number of sets: run many snares simultaneously (10+ minimum for reliable catch)
- Check frequently: dead animal spoils; live animal escapes or gets eaten by predator

**Simple Snare Construction**:
1. Wire loop (28–24 gauge wire for small game): 6–8 inch diameter for rabbits
2. Fixed anchor to stick/root
3. Position: center of run at head height of target animal (fist height for rabbits)
4. Support loop with small twigs to keep it open and positioned

## PART 3: COMMUNITY SECURITY AND SITUATIONAL AWARENESS

### Threat Assessment

**CARVER Matrix** (prioritization framework):
- **C**riticality: how important is the target to mission?
- **A**ccessibility: how easy to reach?
- **R**ecuperability: how long to recover?
- **V**ulnerability: how susceptible to attack?
- **E**ffect: what is the impact?
- **R**ecognizability: how easy to identify?
- Score each 1–5; highest total = highest priority threat or defensive priority

**Threat Indicators** (observation):
- **Surveillance behavior**: repeated presence in area without clear purpose; photographing infrastructure
- **Elicitation**: unusual questions about numbers, schedules, procedures
- **Testing security**: probing responses, checking timing
- **Acquiring supplies**: unusual purchases of materials with dual-use potential
- **Anomalies**: things that don't fit the normal pattern

**Cooper's Color Code** (awareness levels):
- **White**: unaware; relaxed; sleep
- **Yellow**: relaxed alertness; aware of environment; normal for trained individual in public
- **Orange**: specific alert; something has attracted attention; preparing to respond
- **Red**: action; responding to confirmed threat

### Perimeter and Access Control

**Layers of Defense** (defense in depth):
1. **Outer perimeter**: maximum warning distance; early detection
2. **Inner perimeter**: working/living space boundary; access control
3. **Core protection**: immediate protection of critical assets/people
4. Each layer slows threat and triggers alarm/response at outer layers

**Early Warning Systems** (low-tech):
- **Tripwires**: monofilament fishing line at ankle height; connect to noise-maker (cans with pebbles)
- **Crushed stone paths**: difficult to walk silently on gravel
- **Animal sentinels**: geese and guinea fowl excellent alarm animals; dogs most reliable
- **Watch schedule**: two-person watch; 2-hour rotations; log all activity

**Access Control Principles**:
- Single point of entry when possible (easier to control/log)
- Graduated access: not everyone has access to everything
- Visitor escorting: no unescorted visitors in sensitive areas
- Authentication: challenge/password; known face recognition; verification code
- Deny access by limiting knowledge: need-to-know basis

**Vehicle Barriers**:
- Vehicle stopping force: mass × deceleration
- Concrete Jersey barrier: stops vehicles; movable with heavy equipment
- Earth berm: 3 feet tall prevents car entry; 6+ feet stops trucks
- Bollards: steel posts in concrete; 6–10 inch diameter steel; set 3 feet deep
- Ditch/HESCO: filled earth containers; quick to establish; effective vehicle barriers

### Communication Security

**OPSEC (Operational Security)**:
1. **Identify critical information**: what would harm operations if enemy knew?
2. **Analyze threats**: who wants that information? What are their capabilities?
3. **Analyze vulnerabilities**: how could they get it?
4. **Assess risk**: probability × impact
5. **Apply countermeasures**: reduce vulnerabilities cost-effectively

**Radio Emission Security**:
- Transmitting reveals your location (direction finding in <30 seconds with good DF equipment)
- Minimize transmissions: brevity reduces exposure time
- Frequency changes: pre-arranged schedule changes
- Authentication: verify identity of all stations before sharing sensitive information
- Low-intercept modes: spread spectrum, burst transmission (advanced, requires specialized equipment)

**Information Hygiene**:
- Separation: what goes on radio vs written vs spoken only to trusted individuals
- Need-to-know: share minimum information necessary
- Compartmentalization: different groups know different pieces (no one person knows everything)

## PART 4: LONG-TERM SURVIVAL PSYCHOLOGY

### The Will to Survive

**Maslow's Hierarchy Applied to Survival**:
1. Physiological: air, water, food, shelter, sleep
2. Safety: security from threat
3. Love/belonging: social connection with group members
4. Esteem: contributing to the group; feeling competent and useful
5. Self-actualization: meaning-making; hope for future

**Common Psychological Pitfalls**:
- **Normalcy bias**: refusing to believe situation is serious; underestimating threat
- **Panic**: emotion overrides rational thought; spreads in groups
- **Resignation**: "I'm going to die anyway" → stops taking survival actions
- **Tunnel vision**: fixated on one problem, missing bigger picture
- **Group polarization**: groups make riskier decisions than individuals

**Building Mental Resilience**:
- **Competence**: trained skills reduce fear; practice before you need it
- **Focus on what you can control**: ignore what you cannot
- **Short-term goals**: focus on next hour, next meal, next task — not "forever"
- **Routine**: structure provides psychological comfort in chaos
- **Humor**: proven stress buffer in military and survival research
- **Spirituality/meaning**: connection to larger purpose improves survival outcomes

**Group Leadership in Survival**:
- Establish clear leadership early (prevents conflict when under stress)
- Decision-making: gather input from knowledgeable members; leader decides; commit fully
- Morale management: distribute tasks, celebrate small wins, address conflicts quickly
- Information control: share enough for function; shield from information that causes panic without enabling action

### Medical and Psychological Triage in Mass Casualty

**SALT Triage**:
- **S**ort: global sorting → move if can; wave hand if minimal; obvious death
- **A**ssess: individual assessment → breathing? Major bleeding?
- **L**ifesaving interventions: before tagging → stop major bleeding, open airway
- **T**reat/transport: tag and prioritize for treatment

**Triage Tags** (START color system):
- **Black**: expectant (unsurvivable given resources, or dead)
- **Red**: immediate (life-threatening, treatable, will die without rapid intervention)
- **Yellow**: delayed (serious but can wait 30+ minutes)
- **Green**: minor (walking wounded; can self-care)

**Expectant Category** (ethically difficult):
- When resources insufficient for all casualties, triage means prioritizing survivable patients
- This is not abandonment — resources reallocated to those with better outcomes
- Black-tagged patients receive comfort care if possible
