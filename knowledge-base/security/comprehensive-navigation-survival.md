## 1. LAND NAVIGATION

### 1.1 Map and Compass Fundamentals

**The Baseplate Compass (e.g., Silva, Suunto, Brunton)**

Components:
- Baseplate: transparent, with direction-of-travel arrow
- Rotating bezel: marked 0–360 degrees in 2-degree increments
- Magnetic needle: red end points to magnetic north
- Orienting arrow: fixed inside bezel, aligns with north on compass housing
- Orienting lines: parallel lines inside bezel housing

**Taking a Field Bearing**

1. Point the direction-of-travel arrow toward your target or destination.
2. Rotate the bezel until the orienting arrow aligns under the red (north) end of the magnetic needle — "red in the shed."
3. Read the bearing from the index line at the top of the baseplate.
4. Walk on that bearing: periodically re-check that the needle remains in the orienting arrow.

**Transferring a Map Bearing to the Field**

1. Place the baseplate edge between your current location and destination on the map.
2. Rotate the bezel until the orienting lines run parallel to the north-south grid lines, with the orienting arrow pointing toward map north.
3. Read the bearing — this is a grid bearing.
4. Apply declination correction (see 1.2) to convert to a magnetic bearing.
5. Follow the bearing in the field.

---

### 1.2 Declination Adjustment

**Definitions**
- True North (TN): geographic North Pole — the direction maps are oriented to.
- Magnetic North (MN): where compass needles point — the magnetic pole, which shifts each year.
- Grid North (GN): the direction of the map's vertical grid lines — very close to true north, but slightly off in many projections.

**Declination** = the angle between true north and magnetic north at a given location.

- East declination (positive): magnetic north is east of true north. Your compass reads higher than your map bearing. To convert magnetic to true, SUBTRACT east declination.
- West declination (positive): magnetic north is west of true north. To convert magnetic to true, ADD west declination.

**Memory Aid: "East is Least, West is Best"**
- East declination: subtract from compass reading to get true/grid bearing.
- West declination: add to compass reading to get true/grid bearing.

**Practical Workflow**

If your map shows 12° East declination:
- You want to travel on a TRUE bearing of 045°.
- Magnetic bearing = 045° - 12° = 033°.
- Set your compass to 033° and walk.

Alternatively, if you took a MAGNETIC bearing of 270° and want to plot it on a map (true):
- True = 270° + 12° (east declination is subtracted when going M→T) — wait, apply the rule: East is Least, so true bearing is LESS than magnetic. Therefore: True = Magnetic - East Decl = 270° - 12° = 258°.

**Annual Drift:** Declination shifts approximately 0.1°–0.2° per year depending on location. Always use a current declination value from the NOAA Magnetic Declination Calculator (www.ngdc.noaa.gov/geomag/calculators/) or the declination diagram on your map (check the map's publication date and add annual correction if map is old).

**Adjustable Compass Method:** Many compasses (e.g., Suunto A-10, Silva Ranger) have an adjustable declination scale. Set the declination value, and the compass automatically accounts for it — you work directly with true/grid bearings without mental arithmetic.

### 1.3 Triangulation (Intersection)

Used to locate an unknown point (e.g., where something is) when you are at a known position.

**Procedure:**
1. From your known position A, take a bearing to the target (the unknown point).
2. Move to a second known position B, separated from A by a significant distance (the farther, the better the accuracy — ideally at least 200–500 m apart).
3. Take a second bearing to the target from B.
4. On the map, draw both back-bearings (add 180° to each bearing, or draw lines in the direction of travel away from the target toward your positions — the reciprocal lines cross at the target).
5. The intersection of the two lines is the target's location.

**Error Triangle:** In practice, three bearings create a small triangle of uncertainty. The target is within that triangle. Use the center of the triangle.

---

### 1.4 Resection

Used to determine YOUR location when you can see identifiable landmarks but don't know where you are.

**Procedure:**
1. Identify two or three landmarks visible from your position that also appear on your map (hilltop, tower, distinctive bend in a road, etc.).
2. Take a magnetic bearing to Landmark A. Apply declination to get a grid/map bearing.
3. Convert to a back-bearing: add or subtract 180°. Example: bearing to A is 060°, back-bearing is 240°.
4. On the map, place the compass at the symbol for Landmark A, align the orienting lines with grid north, and draw a line from A along the back-bearing (240° in this example). You are somewhere on this line.
5. Repeat for Landmark B (and optionally C).
6. The intersection of the back-bearing lines is your position.

**Tips:**
- Use landmarks at roughly 60°–120° apart from each other (from your position) for best accuracy.
- A third landmark dramatically reduces error.
- If lines don't intersect at a point, you have a triangle of error — you are within that triangle.
- In mountainous terrain, use ridgeline saddles, prominent peaks, and valley junctions as they are easy to identify.

### 1.5 Pace Counting

Used to estimate distance traveled on the ground.

**Calibrating Your Pace:**
1. Measure a 100-meter baseline on flat ground using a tape measure.
2. Walk the 100 meters at a natural field pace, counting every time your LEFT foot (or right foot, choose one and be consistent) strikes the ground.
3. Repeat 3–5 times and average the count. This is your "100-meter pace count."

**Typical Values:**
- Average adult: 62–66 double paces per 100 meters on flat, open ground.
- Uphill: paces shorten, you may need ~10% more paces per 100 m.
- Downhill: paces lengthen, may need ~5% fewer.
- Dense vegetation: increase pace count 20–40%.
- Darkness: increase pace count 15–25%.
- Sand or mud: increase pace count 20–40%.
- Snow (packed): increase pace count 20–50%.

**Counting Method:**
- "Ranger Beads" (pace beads): String of 9 beads + 4 beads on a cord. After each 100 m (your full pace count), slide one bead down on the lower 9-bead section. After 9 × 100 m = 900 m, slide one bead on the upper 4-bead section. After all 4 upper beads are down = 4,000 m = 4 km, reset all.
- Alternatively, use a tally counter or simply count in groups memorized mentally.

**Distance Formula:**
Distance (meters) = (Actual Pace Count / Your 100-m Pace Count) × 100

### 1.6 Azimuth Calculation

An azimuth is a horizontal angle measured clockwise from north (0°–360°). It is functionally equivalent to a bearing in compass navigation.

**Types:**
- True azimuth: from true north.
- Magnetic azimuth: from magnetic north (what a compass gives you).
- Grid azimuth: from grid north on the map.

**Computing Azimuth from Coordinates (Grid)**

If you know your grid coordinates and the destination grid coordinates:

1. Calculate delta East (dE) = Destination Easting - Your Easting.
2. Calculate delta North (dN) = Destination Northing - Your Northing.
3. Compute angle from north: θ = arctan(dE / dN) [use a calculator or trig table].
4. Apply quadrant rules:
   - NE quadrant (dN > 0, dE > 0): azimuth = θ (as calculated, 0°–90°).
   - SE quadrant (dN < 0, dE > 0): azimuth = 180° - θ (where θ is the absolute value).
   - SW quadrant (dN < 0, dE < 0): azimuth = 180° + θ.
   - NW quadrant (dN > 0, dE < 0): azimuth = 360° - θ.

**Back Azimuth:** Add or subtract 180°. If azimuth < 180°, add 180°. If azimuth ≥ 180°, subtract 180°.

**Declination Conversion:**
- Grid to Magnetic: Subtract the G-M angle (grid-magnetic angle, shown on most military topo maps).
- Magnetic to Grid: Add the G-M angle.

**G-M Angle** is printed in the declination diagram in the margin of military topographic maps (1:25,000 and 1:50,000 scale sheets).

## 2. CELESTIAL NAVIGATION

### 2.1 Polaris (North Star) — Northern Hemisphere

Polaris is located within ~1° of true north celestial pole. Finding it gives you a true north bearing accurate to approximately 1°.

**Locating Polaris:**

Method A — Big Dipper (Ursa Major):
1. Find the Big Dipper (7 stars in a "ladle" shape).
2. Identify the two "pointer stars" forming the outer edge of the dipper cup: Merak (bottom) and Dubhe (top).
3. Draw an imaginary line through Merak and Dubhe, extending upward (away from the cup) approximately 5× the distance between those two stars.
4. The bright star at that point is Polaris.

Method B — Cassiopeia:
1. Find Cassiopeia — a "W" or "M" shape of 5 stars on the opposite side of Polaris from the Big Dipper.
2. The middle star of the W points roughly toward Polaris (the open side of the W faces Polaris).
3. Polaris is approximately the same distance from Cassiopeia as from the Big Dipper.

**Using Polaris for Navigation:**
- Face Polaris: you are facing true north.
- Right = East, Left = West, Behind you = South.
- Mark north on the ground with a stick, then navigate by that reference.
- Polaris altitude above horizon approximately equals your latitude (Polaris at 35° above horizon → you are at ~35°N latitude).

**Limitations:** Polaris is only visible in the Northern Hemisphere. Below about 5°S latitude it disappears below the horizon.

---

### 2.2 Southern Cross (Crux) — Southern Hemisphere

The Southern Cross (Crux) is a compact cross of four bright stars visible south of about 25°N latitude. It is used to find the South Celestial Pole, which has no bright star (unlike Polaris).

**Locating South Using Crux:**

1. Identify the Southern Cross: four stars forming a cross, with the long axis pointing toward the South Celestial Pole. Two bright "pointer stars" (Alpha and Beta Centauri) lie nearby and help confirm the Cross's identity.
2. Draw a line through the long axis of the Cross (Gacrux at top, Acrux at bottom), extending beyond Acrux in the direction it points.
3. Separately, find the two Pointer Stars (Alpha and Beta Centauri). Draw a perpendicular bisector between these two stars.
4. The point where the extended long axis of the Cross intersects the perpendicular bisector of the Pointers indicates the South Celestial Pole.
5. Drop a vertical line from that point to the horizon — that is due south.

**Simpler Approximation:**
- Extend the long axis of the Cross by 4.5× the length of the Cross itself beyond the foot star (Acrux). That point is approximately the South Celestial Pole.
- Drop a vertical from that point to the horizon for south.

---

### 2.3 Shadow-Stick Sun Compass

Exploits the sun's east-to-west arc across the sky, which is accurate to within a few degrees of true east/west.

**Classic Two-Stick Method (Watch Method Alternative):**

1. Place a straight stick (60–90 cm, as vertical as possible) in flat, level ground in an open area.
2. Mark the tip of the shadow with a small stone or stick. Note the time.
3. Wait 15–30 minutes. Mark the new shadow tip.
4. Draw a line between the two marks (or lay a stick between them).
5. This line runs approximately west (first mark) to east (second mark) — the shadow moves west to east as the sun moves east to west.
6. Stand perpendicular to this line: north is ahead of you (Northern Hemisphere) or south is ahead of you (Southern Hemisphere).

**Accuracy Notes:**
- Most accurate near the equinoxes (March, September) and near solar noon.
- Least accurate within 5° of the equator (sun nearly overhead) and near the solstices at high latitudes.
- Works any time the shadow moves discernibly — works even under partly cloudy conditions if you can take two shadow readings.

**Watch-Based Sun Compass (Northern Hemisphere, Analog Watch):**

1. Hold the watch horizontal.
2. Point the hour hand at the sun.
3. South lies halfway between the hour hand and the 12 o'clock position (bisect the shorter arc).
4. Example: 4:00 PM — hour hand at 4, 12 is at 12. Bisect: 2 o'clock position on the watch face points south.
5. Adjust for Daylight Saving Time: use 1 o'clock instead of 12 o'clock as the reference.

**Southern Hemisphere:** Point the 12 o'clock at the sun. South is the hour hand direction; north is halfway between 12 and the hour hand (reverse the bisection).

**Accuracy:** Varies with season and latitude. Use as a general bearing only — reliable to within ~15°–25° in mid-latitudes.

---

### 2.4 Moon Direction

**Full Moon:** rises approximately in the east, is due south at midnight (Northern Hemisphere), sets approximately in the west.

**Crescent Moon Method:**
1. Draw an imaginary line between the tips (horns) of a crescent moon.
2. Extend that line down to the horizon.
3. In the Northern Hemisphere, this point on the horizon approximates south (it varies by season, more accurately south in autumn/winter crescents).
4. Accuracy: roughly ±30°, useful only for general orientation.

**Half Moon — Precise Method:**
- At first quarter (right half lit): the moon is roughly due south at sunset, due west at midnight.
- At last quarter (left half lit): the moon is roughly due east at midnight, due south at sunrise.

---

### 2.5 Star Azimuths and Rise/Set Bearings

**Key Stars and Their Approximate Rise Bearings:**

Stars rise in the east and set in the west, but the exact azimuth of rise/set depends on their declination (celestial latitude):

| Star | Approximate Rise Azimuth (from East) |
|------|--------------------------------------|
| Orion's Belt (Mintaka) | Very close to due east (within 1°) |
| Vega (summer) | NNE (~40°N lat: rises ~38°) |
| Arcturus | ENE (~55°N lat: rises ~60°) |
| Sirius | ESE (~55°N lat: rises ~105°) |
| Canopus | SSE (rises ~148° at 30°N lat) |

**Mintaka Rule:** The westernmost star of Orion's Belt (Mintaka) rises within 1° of due east regardless of latitude (because its declination is near 0°). When Mintaka clears the horizon, you are looking due east.

**Southern Cross Transit:** When the long axis of the Southern Cross is vertical (the Cross is "standing upright"), the foot star (Acrux) points very close to due south.

## 3. NATURAL NAVIGATION INDICATORS

*Based substantially on Tristan Gooley's research in "The Natural Navigator" and related works.*

### 3.1 What Actually Works

**Tree Growth and Wind:**
- In consistent prevailing wind areas, tree branches on the leeward side of isolated trees are denser and longer. On the British coast, this reliably indicates prevailing SW wind. In the US Great Plains, trees lean toward the SE-E due to prevailing W winds.
- This works best in open, exposed areas with consistent winds. Unreliable in complex terrain (valleys, canyons, forests).

**Snow and Ice:**
- Snow melts faster on south-facing slopes (Northern Hemisphere) because they receive more solar radiation. South-facing slopes are generally drier, have less snow cover, and may have shorter/sparser vegetation.
- Ice on the south side of rocks and trees melts first.
- Sastrugi (wind-sculpted snow ridges in polar areas) align with prevailing wind direction — direction varies by region, but consistent within a region.

**Tree Rings (Cut Stumps):**
- Rings are wider on the south side (Northern Hemisphere) in open-grown trees, due to more sunlight. However, this is only reliable when the tree grew in full sun exposure throughout its life. Wind patterns, soil, and competition from other trees override this signal. Use cautiously — it is a weak indicator.

**Moss:**
- The oft-cited "moss grows on the north side" is unreliable. Moss grows wherever it's moist — in shadowy gullies, on the wet side of rocks regardless of compass direction, and on all sides of trees in humid climates.
- More reliable version: in exposed, open rocky terrain, moss and lichen grow predominantly on the north side of large rocks (Northern Hemisphere), where shade is most persistent. This is a weak single indicator; use only to confirm other findings.

**Ant Hills:**
- Ant mounds in the UK and Northern Europe (e.g., wood ants — Formica rufa) are frequently built on the south side of trees, with the south face of the mound more gently sloped to receive maximum solar warmth. This indicator is geographically inconsistent; it applies mainly to certain species in certain climates.

**Plant Heliotropism:**
- Compass plant (Silphium laciniatum) in the American prairie orients its leaf edges north-south. This is a documented, reliable indicator within its range (eastern US prairie).
- Sunflowers (young, growing plants) track the sun during the day and return to face east at night. Mature sunflowers mostly face east.

**River and Terrain Signals:**
- Vegetation is often denser on north-facing slopes (Northern Hemisphere) because they retain more moisture.
- Rivers in the Northern Hemisphere frequently undercut their west-facing banks due to Coriolis effect at the macro scale and local solar exposure — but this is a weak, inconsistent indicator at the local scale.

---

### 3.2 What Does NOT Work (Common Myths)

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "Moss grows only on the north side of trees" | Moss grows wherever moisture accumulates — all sides of trees in humid environments. |
| "Birds always migrate south for winter" | Many species migrate in directions other than south, depending on their breeding range. Swallows flying south tells you it is autumn, not a navigation bearing. |
| "Stars don't move" | All stars (except Polaris, which is within ~1°) rotate through the sky. Using a random star as a heading for more than a few minutes will send you off course. |
| "The sun always rises exactly in the east and sets exactly in the west" | Only true at the equinoxes. In mid-summer (Northern Hemisphere, 45°N), the sun rises ~32° north of east and sets ~32° north of west. Significant error if used without seasonal adjustment. |
| "You walk in circles when lost" | Partially true under disorientation, but purposeful use of landmarks, terrain, and compass fully overrides this tendency. |
| "Dead reckoning without tools is useless" | Skilled practitioners using pace count, time, and terrain association can achieve 90%+ accuracy over several kilometers. |
| "Prevailing wind from the west always in the US" | Prevailing mid-latitude winds are westerly, but local topography, sea breezes, and weather systems can override this substantially. |

## 4. TOPOGRAPHIC MAP READING

### 4.1 Contour Lines — Fundamentals

A contour line connects all points of equal elevation. Every point on a single contour line is at the same altitude.

**Contour Interval (CI):** The elevation difference between adjacent contour lines. Common values:
- 1:24,000 scale USGS: 10 ft, 20 ft, or 40 ft depending on terrain relief.
- 1:25,000 military (US): 10 m, 20 m, or 25 m.
- 1:50,000 military: 20 m or 25 m.
- The CI is printed in the map margin.

**Index Contours:** Every 5th contour line is an "index contour" — drawn thicker and labeled with the elevation. Intermediate contours between index contours are thinner and unlabeled.

**Supplemental Contours:** Dashed contour lines at half the normal contour interval, used in areas of low relief where standard contours would be too far apart to be useful.

**Rules:**
1. Contour lines never cross (two elevations cannot occupy the same point).
2. Contour lines always close — they form complete loops (though the loop may be off the edge of the map).
3. The closer the contour lines, the steeper the slope.
4. Uniformly spaced contours = uniform slope.
5. Contour lines crossing a stream form a "V" pointing upstream (uphill) — water flows toward the open end of the V.

---

### 4.2 Terrain Features

**Hills / Summits:**
- Concentric closed contour rings, each inner ring at higher elevation.
- A dot or X marks the highest point (the summit).

**Depressions:**
- Concentric closed contour rings where each inner ring is at LOWER elevation than the outer.
- Marked with small tick marks (hachures) pointing inward toward the bottom of the depression.
- Sinkhole, volcanic crater, or any sunken area.

**Ridgelines:**
- A line of high ground extending from a hilltop.
- Contours form a series of U-shapes or V-shapes pointing away from the high ground (downhill direction).
- Drainage flows away from a ridge on both sides.

**Spurs:**
- A short ridgeline extending from a main ridge, dropping away on three sides.
- Contours form U or V shapes pointing downslope.
- Spurs separate adjacent draws/valleys.

**Draws / Re-entrants:**
- A small V-shaped valley draining into a larger valley.
- Contours form V or U shapes pointing uphill (same as a valley but smaller).
- Intermittent streams form in draws after rainfall.

**Saddles / Passes / Cols:**
- A low point between two hilltops along a ridgeline.
- Contours form an hourglass or figure-eight shape — two sets of closed rings joined at a narrow point.
- Water drains to both sides of a saddle (in opposite directions along the ridgeline).
- Saddles are natural travel routes over ridges.

**Valleys:**
- Elongated low area between ridges.
- Contours form wide, open U-shapes pointing uphill.
- Streams flow through valleys — the contour V always points upstream.

**Cliffs and Escarpments:**
- Contour lines converge so closely they appear to touch or merge.
- Cliff symbol: specialized hatching of horizontal lines on the downslope side of the cliff edge.
- Overhanging cliffs may show contours "reversing" — a feature that requires special depiction.

**Gentle vs. Steep Slopes:**
- Gentle slope: wide spacing between contours.
- Steep slope: narrow spacing.
- Concave slope: contours widely spaced near the top, closely spaced at the bottom (slope gets steeper as you descend).
- Convex slope: contours closely spaced near the top, widely spaced at the bottom (slope gets gentler as you descend). Convex slopes can hide terrain below — you may not see the bottom or what's ahead.

---

### 4.3 Reading Relief — Practical Tips

**Identifying Your Location on a Topo Map:**
1. Identify your visible terrain features (hilltop, stream, saddle, cliff).
2. Match those to the map's contour pattern.
3. Confirm with additional features (another hilltop, the direction of stream flow, a road or trail).

**Estimating Elevation:**
- Count contour lines from the nearest labeled index contour, multiply by CI.
- Example: Nearest index contour = 500 m. You count 3 intermediate contours above it, CI = 20 m. Your elevation ≈ 500 + (3 × 20) = 560 m.

**Estimating Route Difficulty:**
- Calculate total elevation gain: count the number of contour lines you cross going uphill, multiply by CI.
- A route crossing 20 contour lines at 20 m CI = 400 m of elevation gain.
- In general, 300 m of elevation gain per kilometer is steep and slow hiking; under 100 m/km is gentle.

## 5. GPS COORDINATE SYSTEMS

### 5.1 Geographic Coordinates — Latitude and Longitude

**Format Types:**

| Format | Example (Eiffel Tower) | Notes |
|--------|------------------------|-------|
| Degrees, Minutes, Seconds (DMS) | 48°51'30.12"N, 2°17'40.20"E | Traditional survey format |
| Degrees Decimal Minutes (DDM) | 48°51.502'N, 2°17.670'E | Common in nautical GPS |
| Decimal Degrees (DD) | 48.85837°N, 2.29450°E | Used in most digital systems, Google Maps |

**Conversion: DMS to DD**
DD = Degrees + (Minutes / 60) + (Seconds / 3600)

Example: 48°51'30.12"N
= 48 + (51/60) + (30.12/3600)
= 48 + 0.85 + 0.008367
= 48.858367°N

**Conversion: DD to DMS**
1. Degrees = integer part (48)
2. Remaining decimal × 60 = minutes (0.858367 × 60 = 51.502')
3. Decimal minutes × 60 = seconds (0.502 × 60 = 30.12")
Result: 48°51'30.12"N

---

### 5.2 UTM (Universal Transverse Mercator)

UTM divides the world into 60 vertical zones, each 6° of longitude wide. Each zone is projected onto a flat plane, giving a Cartesian (X-Y) coordinate system with metric units.

**Components of a UTM Coordinate:**
- Zone Number: 1–60 (increases eastward from 180° W longitude). Zone 1 starts at 180°W, Zone 60 ends at 180°E.
- Zone Letter (MGRS band): C–X (excluding I and O), indicates latitude band.
- Easting: distance in meters east of the zone's central meridian, with a 500,000 m false easting added (so all values are positive, ranging from ~100,000 to ~900,000 m within a zone).
- Northing: distance in meters north of the equator (Northern Hemisphere) or south from the equator with a 10,000,000 m false northing (Southern Hemisphere, so all values are positive).

**Example UTM Coordinate:**
17T 630084E 4833438N
- Zone 17T
- 630,084 meters east of the zone 17 central meridian (offset by 500,000)
- 4,833,438 meters north of the equator

**Precision:**
- 6-digit easting + 6-digit northing = 1-meter precision.
- Coordinates are typically given to 1 m, 10 m, or 100 m depending on need.

**Using UTM on a Paper Topo Map:**
- UTM grid lines are printed (or can be added) to USGS and military topo maps.
- Grid square spacing is 1,000 m × 1,000 m at 1:50,000 scale (each grid square = 1 km × 1 km).
- Use the map's printed grid labels to read eastings and northings.
- Use a romer (corner scale) or the ruler on your compass baseplate to interpolate within a grid square.

---

### 5.3 MGRS (Military Grid Reference System)

MGRS is built on top of UTM, adding a grid zone designator (GZD) and a 100,000-meter square identifier.

**Structure:**
`[Zone Number][Zone Letter][100km Square ID][Easting][Northing]`

Example: `17TME3008433438`
- `17T` = UTM zone 17, latitude band T
- `ME` = 100,000-meter square identifier within zone 17T
- `30084` = 5-digit easting (within the 100km square, relative)
- `33438` = 5-digit northing

**Common Precision Levels:**
| Digits (each axis) | Grid Square | Typical Use |
|--------------------|-------------|-------------|
| 2 digits each (4 total) | 1,000 m × 1,000 m | General area |
| 3 digits each (6 total) | 100 m × 100 m | Tactical, general |
| 4 digits each (8 total) | 10 m × 10 m | Precise planning |
| 5 digits each (10 total) | 1 m × 1 m | Survey precision |

**Reading MGRS:** Always read RIGHT then UP (Easting then Northing): "Read right, then up."

---

### 5.4 WGS84 vs. NAD83

**WGS84 (World Geodetic System 1984):** The datum used by GPS satellites and most civilian GPS receivers. It defines the Earth's size, shape (an oblate spheroid), and the prime meridian. Used globally.

**NAD83 (North American Datum 1983):** A datum optimized for North America. Closely aligned with WGS84 but not identical. Horizontal difference between WGS84 and NAD83 is typically less than 1 meter in the continental US, and up to ~2 m in Alaska and Hawaii.

**Practical Impact:**
- For most field use and hiking, the difference is negligible.
- For precision survey work or when using older US government maps: always note the map's stated datum.
- USGS 7.5-minute topo maps produced before ~2000 are frequently on NAD27 (North American Datum 1927), which can differ from WGS84 by up to 100–200 meters in some areas.
- Convert NAD27 → WGS84 using GIS software or online calculators (NOAA's NGS Coordinate Conversion is authoritative).

**Setting Your GPS Datum:**
- Always match your GPS receiver's datum to the datum of the map you are using.
- Menu path varies by device, but typically: Setup → Units → Map Datum.
- Default for most modern GPS units: WGS84.

---

### 5.5 Hand Conversion Between Formats (No Calculator)

**Decimal Degrees to Degrees-Minutes:** The integer part is degrees. Multiply the decimal by 60 for minutes.
- 48.858° → 48° + (0.858 × 60)' = 48°51.48' → 48°51'(0.48 × 60)" = 48°51'28.8"

**Estimating Distance from Coordinate Difference:**
- 1° of latitude ≈ 111 km (constant everywhere).
- 1' of latitude ≈ 1.85 km ≈ 1 nautical mile.
- 1" of latitude ≈ 31 m.
- 1° of longitude = 111 km × cos(latitude). At 45°N: 111 × cos(45°) ≈ 111 × 0.707 ≈ 78.5 km.

**Quick UTM grid reading in the field:**
1. Locate your position between grid lines.
2. Estimate how far (in tenths) you are from the nearest westward grid line (easting offset).
3. Estimate how far (in tenths) you are from the nearest southward grid line (northing offset).
4. Add those tenths to the full grid line values to obtain a 6-digit coordinate.

## 6. DEAD RECKONING

Dead reckoning (DR) is estimating your current position based on a known starting position, a traveled bearing, an estimated speed or pace count, and elapsed time — without reference to external landmarks or GPS.

### 6.1 The Navigation Log

A navigation log is a written record maintained as you travel. At minimum, record:

| Column | Contents |
|--------|----------|
| Leg # | Sequential number for each segment |
| Start Position | Coordinates or description |
| Bearing | Magnetic or grid bearing traveled |
| Distance | Measured in meters or kilometers (from pace count, time × speed, or odometer) |
| Elapsed Time | Time departed to time arrived at end of leg |
| End Position | Calculated or identified |
| Notes | Terrain, hazards, landmarks observed |

**Running Fix:** Each time you identify a landmark and can pinpoint your position, you update your DR position with a "fix," resetting accumulated error.

---

### 6.2 Speed-Time-Distance (STD)

**Formula:** Distance = Speed × Time
- Rearranged: Speed = Distance / Time; Time = Distance / Speed.

**Common Field Rates:**
- Road walking, flat: 5 km/h (or 1 km per 12 minutes).
- Trail hiking with pack: 3.5–4 km/h.
- Cross-country bush: 2–3 km/h.
- Elevation gain: subtract time for vertical: Naismith's Rule — add 1 hour per 600 m of ascent.
- Snow travel (snowshoes): 2–3 km/h.
- River following (indirect route): effectively 1.5–2.5 km/h toward destination.

**Example DR Calculation:**
- Started at Position A (known) at 09:00.
- Traveled bearing 045° at estimated 3 km/h for 2 hours.
- Distance traveled: 3 × 2 = 6 km.
- On the map, measure 6 km on bearing 045° from A to get estimated position B.
- Confirmed by sighting a hilltop that matches the map — update fix.

---

### 6.3 Drift Correction

In wind, water, or on a slope traverse, lateral drift moves you off your intended bearing. Dead reckoning must account for this.

**Wind Drift (Overland):** In strong crosswind, lean into the wind and aim slightly upwind of your intended direction. Drift is difficult to quantify without instruments; observe your shadow or landmarks frequently.

**Current Drift (Water):** When crossing or paddling on a current, you must point upstream of your destination. The required upstream angle is:

Ferry Angle = arcsin(Current Speed / Paddling Speed)

Example: Paddling at 4 km/h, current is 2 km/h crosswise.
Ferry angle = arcsin(2/4) = arcsin(0.5) = 30°.
Point 30° upstream of your destination bearing.

**Track Made Good:** The actual path across the ground, combining your boat/paddling direction with the current. Draw it on the navigation log.

**Leeway (Sailing/Paddling):** Lateral drift caused by wind on the vessel's hull/sail. Leeway is estimated (typically 3°–10° depending on vessel and wind) and added to your compass course to compensate. Record the estimated leeway correction in the navigation log.

---

### 6.4 DR Accuracy and Error Management

- DR error accumulates with distance: typical overland DR error is 5%–10% of distance traveled.
- After 10 km of DR, expect to be within 500 m–1,000 m of true position.
- Reduce error by: taking frequent fixes when landmarks are visible, maintaining accurate pace counts, minimizing bearing errors (check compass every 100 m).
- In thick brush or poor visibility: shorten legs to 200–300 m between checks.
- Plan routes to "hit" large linear features (roads, rivers, ridgelines) that you cannot miss, then follow them to your destination — the "aim-off" technique. Deliberately aim upwind/upstream of your target, so when you hit the linear feature you know which direction to follow.

## 7. RIVER CROSSING

### 7.1 Reading Current and Flow

**Before Approaching the Bank:**
- Survey from a high point if possible — observe bends, braiding, color changes.
- Turbulent white water = rocks, shallow areas, fast current.
- Smooth, dark water = deeper, faster flow in the main channel.
- Eddies (areas of slack or reverse current) form behind boulders and on the inside of bends — use these for rest or staging.
- Pillow water: water piling up on the upstream face of a submerged rock (dome of water) — indicates a significant obstacle just below.

**Selecting a Crossing Point:**
- Braided sections (multiple channels) are shallower and slower — often the best crossing.
- Inside of a river bend: water is slower and shallower.
- Outside of a bend: water is faster and deeper, bank is often eroded and undercut — avoid.
- Look for riffles (shallow, rocky sections): noisy, choppy, white water over a wide area. Typically 0.3–0.8 m deep and crossable.
- Avoid: deep pools, main current channels, areas above waterfalls or rapids downstream.

**Depth Assessment from Shore:**
- Throw a stick in: observe speed and direction of current.
- Probe with a sturdy pole before committing weight.
- Clear, cold, fast-moving water in mountain rivers is highly dangerous — even knee-deep fast water can knock you down.
- "If in doubt, don't": hypothermia, entrapment, and drowning are major causes of wilderness death.

---

### 7.2 Crossing Techniques

**Solo Crossing — Diagonal Technique:**
1. Face slightly upstream at a 30°–45° angle to the current.
2. Use a sturdy pole (1.8–2.5 m long, 5–7 cm diameter) on the upstream side for a third point of contact. Lean into the pole.
3. Shuffle sideways — do NOT cross your feet (loss of balance).
4. Move the pole first, then one foot, then the other — always maintain two points of contact.
5. Move diagonally downstream, letting the current assist your lateral movement.
6. Keep pack straps accessible (loosen waist belt so you can ditch the pack if you fall — a submerged pack will drag you down).

**Two-Person Crossing:**
- Stand side by side, upstream person slightly leading. Hold each other's packs or shoulders.
- Face upstream. Move together in synchronized shuffle steps.

**Line Crossing:**
1. Set up a fixed rope ("lifeline") across the river if possible — anchor securely on both banks.
2. Crosser clips a carabiner or wraps their hand around the line (do NOT tie yourself to a fixed line — if you fall, you cannot release).
3. Cross diagonally, holding the rope for balance, letting the line take some load.
4. Rope should be taut enough to hold but not so taut it's above the surface — a rope at waist height that goes taut can trap you underwater if you fall.

**If You Fall In:**
1. Do not fight the current.
2. Get on your back, feet downstream, toes up.
3. Angle your body to use the current to ferry you toward the bank (like a rudder).
4. Target an eddy behind a rock or inside a bend.
5. Only attempt to stand when you feel bottom and the current is manageable.
6. Abandon your pack if necessary for survival.

---

### 7.3 Cold Water Considerations

- Water at 10°C (50°F): swimming ability begins to fail within 5–10 minutes.
- Water at 4°C (39°F): cold shock, gasp reflex, incapacitation within 3–5 minutes.
- Wet clothing after crossing is an immediate hypothermia risk: change, insulate, and rewarm before proceeding.
- Strip to minimal clothing if the crossing is unavoidable in cold conditions — wool or synthetics retain warmth when wet, cotton does not.

## 8. URBAN NAVIGATION

### 8.1 Sun Tracking in Cities

- Morning: sun is in the east — face a building's east-facing side to determine its orientation.
- Midday (solar noon): sun is due south (Northern Hemisphere). Check by observing shadows — shortest shadow of the day points due north at solar noon.
- Afternoon: sun is in the west.
- Use building shadows to establish direction: a building casting a shadow to the north at noon is facing south.
- Glass-faced buildings reflect the sun — look at a reflective building across a street to determine the sun's position without looking directly at it.

**Solar Noon Calculation:**
- Solar noon is when the sun is highest and shadows are shortest. It is NOT always at 12:00 PM clock time.
- Solar noon = 12:00 + (4 minutes × degrees of longitude from your time zone's central meridian) ± daylight saving time offset.
- Example: You are at 90°W, in the CST zone (central meridian at 90°W). Solar noon = approximately 12:00 PM CST (no adjustment needed). If at 95°W, solar noon is about 12:00 + (4 × 5) = 12:20 PM CST.

---

### 8.2 Landmark Triangulation in Urban Areas

1. Identify two prominent landmarks (tall building, radio tower, distinctive spire) visible from your position.
2. Estimate the bearing to each by shadow/sun or magnetic compass if available.
3. If you have a local map (downloaded screenshot, tourist map, etc.), apply the same resection technique as for wilderness: draw back-bearings from each landmark to find your position.
4. If no map: use the relative angle between landmarks to estimate your position along a street grid.

**Building and Block Reference:**
- In cities with numbered grids (Chicago, New York, Denver): address numbers reveal location.
  - Manhattan: numbers increase moving away from 5th Avenue (east or west) for street cross-section; building numbers on avenues increase going north.
  - Chicago: 0 is at State and Madison; numbers increase 800 per mile in each direction (800 = 1 mile from center).
  - Denver: 100 block per block from the city center grid.
- Even/Odd sides: In the US, typically even numbers on one side of a street, odd on the other (varies by city — e.g., odd on the north side in most grid cities, even on south).

---

### 8.3 Building Numbering Conventions

**United States (Grid Systems):**
- Streets perpendicular to the baseline (usually a major avenue) carry one numbering system.
- Streets parallel to the baseline carry another.
- Even numbers frequently on the east or south side of streets; odd on the west or north side — but this varies significantly by city. Learn the local system.

**European Cities:**
- Most do not use grid systems. Buildings are numbered sequentially along a street — odd on one side, even on the other, OR clockwise around a loop from a fixed starting point.
- House numbers can jump or be non-sequential if buildings were added later.
- Navigate by named streets, plazas, and landmarks rather than numbers.

**Grid Navigation Strategy:**
1. Determine the city's cardinal orientation (usually a map is necessary, but the sun can confirm N-S axis).
2. Find the nearest street sign — name + any number tells you which block you're on.
3. Walk toward higher or lower numbers based on your destination.
4. Count blocks — most US grids have uniform block lengths (80–100 m for interior blocks, 200–400 m on arterials).

## 9. SHELTER CONSTRUCTION

### 9.1 Site Selection — Universal Principles

Before building ANY shelter:
- **Avoid low ground:** Cold air sinks at night — a hollow or valley bottom can be 10–15°C colder than surrounding terrain. Frost settles in low areas first.
- **Avoid flood risk:** Above the high water mark of streams. Check for water stains, debris lines.
- **Avoid dead standing trees (widow-makers):** Branches and entire trees can fall in wind.
- **Avoid ridgetops in storms:** High wind exposure and lightning risk.
- **Ideal location:** Leeward side of a hill or ridge, slightly elevated from the valley floor, near (but not next to) a water source, with available building materials.
- **Proximity to materials:** Building a shelter costs energy — site near abundant branches, leaves, bark, or snow.

---

### 9.2 Lean-To Shelter

**Best for:** Temperate conditions, one person or small group, available wood.

**Construction:**

Materials needed: 2 trees or upright poles ~2–2.5 m apart; 1 ridgepole ~2.5 m long; multiple rafters; leafy branches, bark, or debris for covering.

Dimensions:
- Width: 1.8–2.4 m (shoulder-to-shoulder sleeping width + margins).
- Depth: 1.2–1.5 m (enough for one person lying perpendicular to the ridgepole).
- Height at front (ridgepole): 1.2–1.5 m.
- Roof angle: 45°–60° (steeper sheds rain better).

Steps:
1. Lash or wedge the ridgepole between two trees at approximately shoulder height (1.2–1.5 m).
2. Lean rafter poles against the ridgepole at 45°–60° angle, spaced 20–30 cm apart.
3. Lash horizontal cross-members across the rafters every 30 cm to hold covering material.
4. Layer bark, large leaves, or debris from the bottom up, like shingles (overlapping, bottom layer first so each layer sheds water onto the one below).
5. Layer should be at least 30 cm thick for effective rain protection; 60+ cm for cold weather insulation.
6. Build a side wall on the upwind side.
7. Insulate the floor: lay 20–30 cm of dry leaves, pine needles, or grass before sleeping on it. Ground contact is the greatest heat loss in a lean-to.

**Limitation:** Open-fronted lean-to loses heat rapidly in cold. Use a fire in front for warmth — position yourself between the fire and the lean-to to reflect heat.

---

### 9.3 Debris Hut

**Best for:** Cold or rainy temperate conditions, single person, no tools needed.

**Principle:** An extremely well-insulated cocoon. The debris (leaves, grass, pine needles) traps dead air, providing insulation equivalent to a sleeping bag. Historically used for cold-weather emergencies without blankets.

**Dimensions:**
- Length: just long enough for the occupant to fit lying down with feet at the closed end — typically 2.0–2.2 m for an average adult. Smaller is warmer.
- Interior width at shoulder: 45–60 cm (just wide enough to roll over).
- Interior height: approximately 45–60 cm at the highest point — barely enough to squeeze in.

**Construction:**

1. Find or create a ridgepole: one straight pole 2.5–3.0 m long, 7–10 cm diameter.
2. Prop one end in the fork of a tree or on a forked stick lashed between two trees, at about 1 m height. The other end rests on the ground. This forms the spine of the shelter.
3. Lean ribbing poles along both sides of the ridgepole at roughly 30–45° angle, creating a tent-like framework. Ribs spaced 30 cm apart.
4. Pile debris on top and sides: leaves, needles, bark, grass, ferns. Pile it thick — minimum 60 cm on all sides, 90 cm is better. The deeper the better for insulation. A useful test: if your forearm buried in the debris disappears up to your elbow (about 30 cm), the layer is barely adequate.
5. Fill the interior with dry debris (leaves, grass) — this is your "sleeping bag." Pack it loosely enough to burrow into but densely enough that it holds body heat.
6. Leave an entrance just large enough to crawl through. Plug it with a debris-stuffed bag, a bundle of leaves, or your pack once you are inside.
7. Mark the shelter with a stick visible from above for rescue.

**Performance:** A well-built debris hut with good insulation fill can maintain survivable interior temperatures even when exterior is near 0°C (32°F), relying solely on body heat.

---

### 9.4 Quinzee (Quinzhee)

**Best for:** Deep snow conditions, 1–4 people, all-season use in snow country.

**Principle:** Unlike an igloo (built from cut snow blocks), a quinzee is hollowed from a mounded pile of snow that has been allowed to sinter (snow crystals bond together, making the structure rigid).

**Dimensions:**
- Mound diameter: 2.5–3.5 m for a 2-person quinzee; 3.5–5.0 m for 4 people.
- Mound height: 1.5–2.0 m.
- Wall thickness: 20–25 cm minimum (use sticks as thickness guides — see below).
- Interior height: 1.0–1.2 m (enough to sit up).

**Construction:**

1. Tramp snow and pile it into a large mound of the desired diameter and height.
2. Before hollowing: insert 20–25 cm sticks radially through the mound at various points — these serve as thickness gauges during excavation.
3. Allow the mound to sinter for at least 2 hours (4–6 hours is better, longer is ideal). During sintering, the crystals bond and the mound solidifies enough to be hollowed without collapse.
4. Dig an entrance from the downhill side (cold air drains out through the low entrance, warm air stays inside). The entrance should be narrow (60 cm wide, 75 cm tall) and angled so it curves upward inside — a "cold trap."
5. Hollow the interior: work from the entrance inward and upward. Stop excavating when you hit the stick tips — that is your minimum wall thickness.
6. Punch a ventilation hole through the roof with a ski pole or stick (~2–3 cm diameter). This is critical — without ventilation, CO2 accumulates or melting ice can seal the hole.
7. Smooth the interior ceiling to reduce dripping: moisture condenses and runs down smooth curved surfaces rather than dripping.

**Interior Temperature:** Even at -30°C outside, interior maintains -5°C to 0°C from body heat alone — survivable with proper clothing and sleeping system.

**Warning:** Check ventilation hole frequently — heavy snow or melt can block it. Never sleep in a snow shelter without ventilation.

---

### 9.5 Snow Trench

**Best for:** Emergency overnight, minimal energy and time, any snow depth over 60 cm.

**Construction:**

1. Dig a trench in the snow just wide enough to lie in (60–75 cm wide), long enough for the occupant (2.0–2.2 m), and deep enough to stand in (1.0–1.5 m).
2. Trench depth should shelter you from wind — the wind should pass over the top.
3. Roof the trench: lay skis, tree branches, a tarp, or snow blocks across the top.
4. Cover the roof with loose snow for insulation.
5. Leave a small entrance and close it with your pack or snow blocks.
6. Insulate the floor with 10–15 cm of evergreen boughs, sleeping pad, clothing, or other available material — the snow floor will suck heat from your body rapidly.

**Time:** 20–40 minutes to construct. Significantly faster than a quinzee.
**Performance:** Less insulative than a quinzee, but the snow walls still protect from wind and maintain a livable temperature well above ambient air temperature.

---

### 9.6 A-Frame Shelter

**Best for:** Temperate forest, 1–2 people, moderate rain, available poles and cordage.

**Dimensions:**
- Ridgepole length: 3.0–3.5 m.
- Ridgepole height at peak: 1.0–1.2 m (at center, supported on two A-frames or poles).
- Floor length: 2.0–2.5 m.
- Width at base: 1.5–2.0 m.

**Construction:**

1. Cut or find two Y-shaped forked poles approximately 1.0–1.2 m tall for the peak supports. Drive their bases firmly into the ground 2.0–2.5 m apart.
2. Lay the ridgepole in the forks.
3. Lean rafters from ridgepole to ground on both sides — spacing 20–30 cm. Creates a symmetrical tent shape.
4. Add horizontal stringers (cross-bars) lashed to rafters every 30–40 cm to hold covering material.
5. Cover with bark, large leaves, or debris, shingling from the bottom up.
6. Close both ends (gables) with debris or a woven lattice of sticks and debris.
7. Fill floor with insulating debris: 20–30 cm minimum.

**Advantage over lean-to:** Enclosed on all sides — better thermal retention and rain protection.

## 10. FIRE STARTING

### 10.1 Bow Drill — Friction Fire

Bow drill is the most reliable primitive fire-starting method and teachable with practice. Success depends on matching the correct wood species, dimensions, proper technique, and dry conditions.

**Components:**
1. **Spindle:** Straight, smooth, dry stick.
   - Length: 28–35 cm.
   - Diameter: 1.5–2 cm (pencil-thick to thumb-thick).
   - Top end: rounded/rounded-taper to reduce friction at handhold socket.
   - Bottom end: tapered to a blunt point that contacts the fireboard.

2. **Fireboard (hearthboard):**
   - Flat board, 30–50 cm long, 5–8 cm wide, 1.5–2.5 cm thick.
   - Must be dry and flat.
   - A notch is carved: V-shaped, with the point of the V about 1/8 the diameter of the socket hole from the edge of the hole.
   - Socket hole: carved depression where the spindle base rotates — begins as a small indentation burned in by the first few strokes, then notched.

3. **Bow:**
   - Length: 50–75 cm.
   - A slightly curved, rigid branch.
   - Cord: paracord, rawhide, natural cordage twisted from plant fibers, or boot lace.
   - Cord should be taut enough to grip the spindle without slipping but not so tight it binds rotation.

4. **Handhold (socket):**
   - A rock with a smooth concave depression, or a hardwood block with a slight recess.
   - Must be much harder than the spindle (to minimize friction at top and maximize friction at base).
   - Lubricate with pine pitch, lip balm, fat, or green leaves to reduce friction at the top.

5. **Tinder bundle:**
   - A bird's nest shape of very fine, dry, fluffy material.
   - Materials: dried grass, cattail down, inner bark fibers (cedar, basswood), dry lichen, dry moss, dried leaves crumbled fine.
   - Must be bone-dry. Pre-dry inside clothing next to skin in damp conditions.

**Wood Species Pairings (North America):**

| Spindle | Fireboard | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| Cottonwood | Cottonwood | Excellent; soft and produces coal readily |
| Willow | Willow | Very good; widely available near water |
| Cedar (western red) | Cedar | Classic pairing; aromatic smoke |
| Basswood | Basswood | Excellent; readily available in eastern US |
| Mullein (stalk) | Mullein or cottonwood | Common roadside plant; works very well |
| Sotol | Agave (heart wood) | Desert Southwest |
| Yucca | Yucca | Desert; spindle from stalk, board from stem |
| Sycamore | Cottonwood | Works; less ideal |

**DO NOT USE:** Green (live) wood, resinous woods (pine, fir, spruce) — too sticky and smear the surfaces; very hard woods (oak, hickory) — spindle and board must be similar hardness (softish wood like cottonwood, willow, cedar); wet wood.

**Rule of Thumb:** Spindle wood and fireboard should be similar in hardness and must be bone-dry. If you can dent the wood with your thumbnail, it may be suitable.

**Technique — Step by Step:**

1. Kneel or sit: dominant-side knee up, non-dominant knee down. Pin the fireboard with your non-dominant foot.
2. Place a dry leaf or flat piece of bark under the notch to catch the coal (a piece of knife blade or flat stone works).
3. Wrap the bow cord around the spindle one loop — spindle should be outside the bow cord.
4. Place the spindle bottom into the socket hole on the fireboard. Press the handhold on top of the spindle with your non-dominant hand — lock your non-dominant wrist against your non-dominant shin for stability (prevents wobbling).
5. Begin long, smooth, horizontal strokes with the bow — use the full length of the bow cord on each stroke. Do not rush at first: establish smooth, consistent strokes. Build cadence.
6. Gradually increase speed and downward pressure. The goal is to generate a fine black powder (char) that collects in the notch and on the catch leaf.
7. After 30–60 seconds of sustained effort, the powder will smoke and hold a glow — this is your coal.
8. Carefully remove the spindle and set it aside. Tap the fireboard to ensure the coal falls onto the catch leaf.
9. Gently transfer the coal to the center of your tinder bundle. Fold the tinder bundle around it gently.
10. Lift the bundle and blow gently — a steady, gentle stream of air directed at the coal. Gradually increase breath as the bundle ignites.
11. When flame erupts from the bundle, place it into the prepared fire lay.

**Failure Troubleshooting:**

| Problem | Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------|-----|
| White smoke, no coal | Moist wood | Dry the wood; try indoor-stored wood |
| Powder is light tan | Wrong wood or too slow | Use darker, softer wood; increase speed |
| Spindle slips in cord | Loose bow cord | Tighten cord; re-wrap spindle |
| Coal falls apart | Powder too dry or coal too small | Keep pressure until larger coal forms |
| Burning at top, not bottom | Handhold not lubricated | Add lubricant to handhold socket |
| Coal won't light tinder | Tinder too coarse or damp | Finer, drier material; pre-dry in clothing |

---

### 10.2 Flint and Steel

**Materials:**
- **Striker:** A carbon-steel striker (C-shaped "fire steel" striker), or the spine of a carbon-steel knife (NOT stainless steel — stainless does not produce sparks). File a flat facet on the knife spine if necessary.
- **Stone:** Flint, chert, quartz, jasper, or other cryptocrystalline silica-based rock. Must have a fresh, sharp edge (strike the rock against another to create a new edge if needed). Pyrite (fool's gold) also works for the stone role.
- **Char cloth:** Charred cotton, linen, or other plant-fiber cloth kept in a small tin. Char cloth catches a spark and holds a slow-burning coal reliably. To make: heat small squares of cotton fabric in a sealed tin with a small hole until they stop smoking; cool before opening.
- **Tinder:** As described above — fine, fluffy, bone-dry material in a nest shape.

**Technique:**
1. Hold char cloth under your thumb against the sharp edge of the flint/chert.
2. Strike the carbon-steel striker downward in a firm, glancing blow against the flint edge — the sparks should fly onto the char cloth.
3. One spark catching on char cloth produces a slow orange glow spreading across the cloth.
4. Fold the glowing char cloth into the center of the tinder bundle and blow gently.
5. Transfer resulting flame to fire lay.

---

### 10.3 Fire by Lens

**Works when:** Direct sunlight is available and strong.

**Materials:**
- Convex lens: magnifying glass, reading glasses lens (any convex lens), binocular lens (removed), water-filled clear plastic bag, clear water-filled balloon, piece of ice shaped convex on both sides.
- Tinder as described above.

**Technique:**
1. Focus the lens to the smallest possible bright spot on the tinder.
2. Keep the lens still — movement reduces intensity.
3. The hot spot should be approximately 1–3 mm in diameter (smaller = hotter).
4. Thin, dark tinder chars fastest: charred cloth, dark fungus (Fomes fomentarius — horse hoof fungus), dark dried leaves.
5. Hold steady until the tinder begins to glow, then proceed to tinder bundle.

**Ice lens:** Shape a piece of clear ice into a convex disk, 15–20 cm diameter, 3–4 cm thick at center. Polish with warm hands. Works at high altitude where UV is intense; marginal at sea level.

---

### 10.4 Fire Lays

**Teepee (cone):**
- Best for: Quick ignition, boiling water, signal fires.
- Build tinder bundle in center; lean small kindling over it in a cone; lean progressively larger sticks around those.
- Fire burns hot quickly; collapses inward; not self-feeding.

**Log Cabin:**
- Best for: Sustained cooking fire, warmth.
- Place two parallel logs on either side of tinder pile. Place two more perpendicular on top (like a log cabin). Build upward, decreasing size. Tinder ignites center and fire burns from inside out.
- Long-burning, stable structure.

**Star Fire:**
- Best for: Long-term fire with minimal fuel processing (no cutting necessary).
- Place 4–6 large logs in a star pattern, tips meeting at center. Burn only the tips; push logs inward as they burn.
- Ideal for long cold nights with limited tools.

**Long Fire (Altar Fire / Platform Fire):**
- Best for: Ground insulation in snow or wet conditions; cooking.
- Build a raised platform of green (non-burning) logs as a base. Build fire on top.
- In snow: prevents the fire from sinking into a hole as it melts snow beneath.

**Upside-Down Fire (Top-Down Fire):**
- Best for: Sustained, low-maintenance fire.
- Largest logs on bottom, medium in middle, small kindling and tinder on top.
- Ignite from the top. Fire burns downward through progressively larger fuel.
- Produces less smoke, burns longer between tending.

## 11. WATER PROCUREMENT AND PURIFICATION

### 11.1 Water Priority

The human body loses approximately 2–3 liters of water per day at rest in temperate conditions; 4–6+ liters per day in hot conditions or under physical exertion. Dehydration begins to impair judgment at 1–2% body weight loss; becomes severe at 5–8%.

**Priority order of water sources (cleanliness ranking, most to least likely to be safe before treatment):**
1. Rainwater caught off clean surfaces (highest quality).
2. Moving springs (outlet, not pool).
3. Fast-moving streams (above human habitation).
4. Still/slow water from ponds, lakes.
5. Murky/turbid water (requires pre-filtering).
6. All field water should be purified regardless of apparent clarity.

**Purification Methods (effectiveness):**

| Method | Effective Against | Notes |
|--------|-------------------|-------|
| Boiling (1 min at altitude, 3 min over 2,000 m) | Bacteria, protozoa, viruses | 100% effective; fuel required |
| Chemical treatment (iodine, chlorine dioxide) | Bacteria, most protozoa, viruses | Less effective against Cryptosporidium; chemical taste |
| Filtration (pump, gravity, squeeze) | Bacteria, protozoa | Most filters do NOT remove viruses |
| UV treatment (SteriPen type) | Bacteria, protozoa, viruses | Requires batteries; doesn't work in turbid water |
| Combined filtration + chemical | Bacteria, protozoa, viruses | Most reliable field method |

---

### 11.2 Solar Still

**Principle:** Solar radiation heats the soil, evaporating moisture. Vapor condenses on the underside of a clear plastic sheet and drips into a collection vessel.

**Materials required:**
- 1 sheet clear plastic, minimum 1.8 m × 1.8 m (2 m × 2 m preferred).
- 1 collection container (cup, pot, canteen).
- 1 length of flexible tubing (drinking straw diameter works), 1–1.5 m.
- Digging tool.

**Construction:**

1. Select a site that maximizes solar gain: south-facing slope in Northern Hemisphere, unshaded, preferably in a low area where soil moisture collects (dry stream bed, base of a slope).
2. Dig a bowl-shaped pit 90–100 cm in diameter, 45–60 cm deep. The sides should slope inward.
3. If green vegetation is available: place cut vegetation (cactus pads, leaves, plant matter) into the pit — enhances moisture output from plant transpiration.
4. Place the collection container in the center-bottom of the pit. Run the tubing from inside the container up over the pit edge (for drinking without dismantling the still).
5. Drape the clear plastic sheeting over the pit. Anchor all edges with soil, rocks, or packed dirt — an airtight seal around the perimeter is critical.
6. Place a small stone (fist-sized) in the center of the plastic directly above the collection container. This creates a depression so condensed water drips to the center and into the container.

**Dimensions Summary:**
- Pit diameter: 90–100 cm.
- Pit depth: 45–60 cm.
- Plastic overhang (anchor zone): 20–30 cm beyond pit edge on all sides.
- Cone depth (plastic sag below rim): 40–50 cm.
- Stone weight: just enough to create the cone without touching the collection container.

**Expected Output:** 0.2–0.6 liters per day in favorable conditions (moist soil, hot sun, green vegetation added). This is barely enough to sustain life but better than nothing. Multiple stills are needed for a reliable supply.

**Limitations:**
- Requires full sunlight.
- Low output — far less than often portrayed in popular media.
- Works best in areas with some soil moisture or fleshy vegetation.
- Dismantling to drink (if no tube) loses efficiency.

---

### 11.3 Transpiration Bag

**Principle:** A clear plastic bag placed over living vegetation traps transpired water vapor from the leaves. The sun drives evaporation from the leaves; water condenses on the bag interior and collects at the lowest point.

**Procedure:**
1. Select a leafy branch on a non-toxic plant (avoid toxic plants — see edible plant section). Larger, sunnier branches produce more water.
2. Slip a clear plastic bag over the branch and as many leaves as will fit.
3. Seal the open end tightly around the branch with a twist tie, cord, or tape.
4. Weight the bottom corner of the bag (small rock inside or drop the branch downward) so water collects at a low point.
5. Leave for 4–8 hours. Collect 0.1–0.5 liters per bag in good conditions.
6. Move the bag to a new branch periodically as the leaves dehydrate.

**Output:** Highly variable. Leafy deciduous trees in direct sun on a warm day are best. Conifers, non-leafy plants, shade — significantly less.

---

### 11.4 Seep Well

Used when a water source is murky, stagnant, or heavily sedimented — dig a small well adjacent to the water to allow filtering through the soil.

**Procedure:**
1. Identify the edge of a pond, stream, or marshy area.
2. Dig a hole 30–60 cm from the water's edge, 30–60 cm deep, 30 cm diameter.
3. Allow water to seep in from the surrounding soil. This natural filtration removes most sediment and many microorganisms.
4. Let the seep well fill and settle (30–60 minutes for initial filling).
5. Collect from the seep well, not the main water body.
6. Purify collected water by boiling or chemical treatment before drinking — seep wells do NOT sterilize.

---

### 11.5 Dew Collection

**Method:**
1. In the early morning (before sunrise to ~1 hour after sunrise), dry grass and leaves are heavily coated with dew.
2. Tie absorbent cloths (bandana, cotton shirt, socks) around your legs and walk through tall dewy grass — the fabric absorbs dew rapidly.
3. Wring the fabric into a container. Repeat.
4. In good dew conditions, 0.5–1.0 liters can be collected per hour.
5. Alternatively, lay a plastic sheet or tarp at night: dew condenses on it. Tilt to drain into a container.

**Dew conditions:** Best in clear nights with low humidity early, followed by radiative cooling; coastal areas; areas near bodies of water.

**Safety:** Dew collected from most vegetation or clean tarps is safe (no purification needed if collected off non-toxic surfaces, as it is distilled water). Dew collected from soil or potentially contaminated vegetation should be boiled.

---

### 11.6 Improvised Water Filter

An improvised filter removes sediment, particulates, and many microorganisms. It does NOT make water biologically safe — boiling or chemical treatment is still required after filtering.

**Materials:**
- Container with a hole in the bottom (plastic bottle, can, bamboo section, bark cone).
- Collecting container.

**Filter Layers (from top to bottom — water enters from the top):**

| Layer | Material | Depth | Purpose |
|-------|----------|-------|---------|
| 1 (top) | Coarse grass or cloth | 3–5 cm | Pre-filter for large debris |
| 2 | Fine sand or crushed rock | 8–10 cm | Primary particulate removal |
| 3 | Crushed charcoal (from burned hardwood) | 8–10 cm | Chemical absorption, odor, taste |
| 4 | Fine sand | 5–8 cm | Secondary particulate removal |
| 5 (bottom) | Gravel or coarse sand | 5–8 cm | Final mechanical filter; prevents clogging of lower layers |
| 6 | Coarse grass or cloth | 2–3 cm | Exit filter; keeps layers in place |

Total filter depth: approximately 30–45 cm.

**Charcoal Preparation:** Use charcoal from a wood fire — not ash. Break hardwood charcoal into small pieces (5–10 mm) or crush to coarse granules. Rinse briefly before placing in filter to remove soot.

**Operation:**
1. Pre-assemble filter. Pour turbid water into the top slowly.
2. Filtered water drips out the bottom into the collection container.
3. Pass water through the filter 2–3 times if still turbid.
4. Boil the filtered water or treat with chemicals before drinking.
5. Replace charcoal and sand layers when the filter becomes clogged or water runs through without filtering (usually after several uses).

## 12. SIGNALING FOR RESCUE

### 12.1 Signal Mirror Technique

A signal mirror produces a flash visible up to 16 km (10 miles) in good conditions. Even an improvised mirror (any reflective surface: polished metal, glass, a CD) is effective.

**Commercial Signal Mirror (with sighting hole):**
1. Hold the mirror with the sighting hole at eye level.
2. Reflect sunlight through the sighting hole onto your hand or a nearby surface. You will see a bright spot.
3. Aim this spot toward the target (aircraft, ship, distant person).
4. The bright spot on your hand must align with the sighting hole as you look through it.
5. Sweep the flash slowly across the target. For aircraft: flash repeatedly even if you are unsure the aircraft sees you.

**Improvised Mirror (no sighting hole):**
1. Hold the mirror to reflect sunlight onto your open hand held at arm's length.
2. Focus the reflected spot in the "V" formed between two fingers.
3. Aim the V and the mirror at the target — keep the bright spot visible in the V while the mirror faces the target.

**Best conditions:** Bright sun. Works at sunrise and sunset if you can angle the mirror.

**Without sunlight:** Signal mirrors are useless in overcast conditions. Switch to smoke signal or whistle.

**Flash Pattern:** No universally required pattern for a signal mirror. Just flash repeatedly toward any aircraft or potential observer.

---

### 12.2 Signal Fire

**Three Fires (Universal Distress Signal):**
- Build three fires in a triangle or straight line, spaced approximately 25–30 meters apart.
- Three evenly spaced fires (or three of any distress signal) is the internationally recognized signal for distress.
- A single fire is ambiguous; three fires in a pattern is unmistakable.

**Fire Spacing:** 25–30 m apart is the standard recommended spacing, visible from the air as a clearly defined pattern.

**Smoke Signal:**
- White smoke (visible against dark forest): add green vegetation, fresh leaves, grass, rubber.
- Black smoke (visible against sky, snow, desert): add rubber, plastic, oil-soaked rags, tire rubber.
- Smoky fire: smother a fire with green leaves or wet material to produce heavy smoke rapidly.

**Signal Fire Pre-Building:**
- Prepare three fire lay sites in advance (tinder and kindling ready, covered from rain with a bark lean-to or tarp).
- Maintain a "signal fire watch" — be ready to ignite instantly when an aircraft is heard or seen.

---

### 12.3 Ground-to-Air Signals (ICAO Standard)

Ground symbols for air rescue follow the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) standards. Symbols should be:
- **Size:** Minimum 2.5 m wide, 10 m long for each element (larger is better — 3 m × 10 m is ideal).
- **Color contrast:** Maximum contrast with background. White on dark ground; black or dark material on snow or light ground.
- **Materials:** Rocks, logs, trampled vegetation, colored fabric, rescue blanket, spray paint.

**Standard ICAO Ground Symbols:**

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| V | Require assistance |
| X | Require medical assistance |
| → (arrow) | Traveling in this direction |
| F | Need food and water |
| LL | All is well |
| N | No or negative |
| Y | Yes or affirmative |
| □ (rectangle) | I do not understand |
| Δ (triangle) | Safe to land here |

**Construction Tips:**
- Use logs, rocks, or mounded soil for the symbol's outline; fill with contrasting material.
- Create shadow by using 3D elements (logs on edge) where possible — visible from higher altitude.
- Clear surrounding area of similar-looking natural features that might confuse the pattern.
- A simple large SOS built prominently is universally recognized (though not in the ICAO standard symbol set specifically).

---

### 12.4 Whistle Protocol

The standard distress signal: **Three short blasts, pause, repeat.**

International distress signal (also used in maritime): Three short, three long, three short (SOS in Morse). More commonly, simply three blasts is recognized.

**Whistle Selection:**
- A pealess whistle (Fox 40, Storm, etc.) functions when wet and at extreme cold without a ball bearing that can freeze. Significantly louder than a pea whistle.
- Audible range: 1–3 km in open terrain; much less in dense forest.
- Carry always when in wilderness: it has a better ratio of weight to signaling ability than almost any other device.

**Pattern:** Blow three blasts, listen for 30 seconds, blow again. Repeat.

## 13. COLD WEATHER SURVIVAL

### 13.1 Hypothermia Prevention

**Definition:** Core body temperature drops below 35°C (95°F). Life-threatening below 32°C (90°F); cardiac arrest risk below 28°C (82°F).

**Stages:**

| Stage | Core Temp | Symptoms |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| Mild | 35–32°C (95–90°F) | Shivering, impaired coordination, confusion beginning |
| Moderate | 32–28°C (90–82°F) | Violent shivering stops (dangerous sign), muscle rigidity, slurred speech, poor judgment |
| Severe | Below 28°C (82°F) | No shivering, unconsciousness, cardiac arrhythmia, appears dead |

**STOP Protocol for Prevention:** See Section 15.

**Core Prevention Principles:**
- Keep dry: water conducts heat ~25× faster than air. Wet clothing = rapid heat loss.
- Cover the head: 30–40% of body heat loss is from the head (though this percentage depends on what else is exposed — the principle is that the head is often uncovered and should be insulated).
- Stay out of wind: wind chill dramatically accelerates convective heat loss.
- Insulate from ground: ground contact is extremely effective at conducting heat away from the body.
- Caloric intake: shivering burns approximately 500–1,000 kcal/hour — maintain high-calorie intake in cold.
- Stay active to generate heat, but stop before sweating heavily (sweating then stopping in cold = rapid cooling).

**Field Treatment of Hypothermia:**
1. Remove from cold, wind, wet conditions. Shelter the victim.
2. Replace wet clothing with dry insulation if available.
3. Insulate from ground: place victim on leaves, packs, branches — anything dry.
4. Gentle rewarming: warm (not hot) water on armpits and groin (major blood vessel areas), warm drink if conscious.
5. Avoid: vigorous rubbing of extremities (can cause cold blood to return to core suddenly — "afterdrop").
6. Victim should be handled gently: jarring can trigger ventricular fibrillation in severe hypothermia.
7. Evacuate — severe hypothermia is a medical emergency.

---

### 13.2 Wet Clothing Management

**Cotton:** Loses virtually all insulative value when wet. Wets quickly, dries slowly. The phrase "Cotton kills" exists for this reason. Avoid cotton as a base layer or insulation in wet/cold conditions.

**Wool:** Retains approximately 70–80% of insulative value when wet. Continues to insulate. Dries slowly but maintains warmth throughout. Merino wool is the preferred base layer material for cold wet environments.

**Synthetic (polyester, polypropylene):** Retains 60–80% insulative value when wet. Wicks moisture from skin. Dries faster than wool. Vulnerable to degradation from campfire sparks.

**Down insulation:** Loses nearly all insulative value when wet. Treated (hydrophobic) down retains somewhat more, but wet down is a survival liability in sustained wet conditions. Down is an excellent insulator in dry cold.

**Field Management:**
- Layer system: base layer wicks moisture from skin; mid-layer insulates; outer layer repels wind and rain.
- Ventilate during exertion to prevent soaking the insulating layer with sweat.
- Remove the outer shell during heavy exertion if it traps sweat; put it back on immediately when stopping.
- Store extra clothing in a dry bag or waterproof stuff sack.
- If clothing gets wet: wring out as much water as possible, hang near fire (not so close it burns or melts), use body heat to dry by wearing the damp layer close to skin if no alternative exists.

---

### 13.3 Vapor Barrier (VB) Technique

**Principle:** A waterproof layer (plastic bag, trash bag, VBL sock or liner) placed between the skin/base layer and the insulation layer traps body moisture before it can saturate the insulation.

**Application:** Primarily used in extreme cold (below -20°C) or when insulation will be in use for many days without drying opportunity.

**Method:**
1. Wear a thin base layer (wool or synthetic) against the skin.
2. Over the base layer: slip on plastic bags (bread bags, contractor bags, purpose-made VBL liners) over the feet, hands, and/or torso.
3. Over the vapor barrier: wear insulation (sleeping bag, down jacket, wool sweaters).

**Result:** The insulation stays dry because moisture cannot pass through the VBL to reach it. The skin feels clammy (the moisture stays on the skin/base layer), but the critical insulation remains dry and functional.

**Use case:** Long-term shelter situations in extreme cold where you cannot dry gear; wet environments where the outer insulation would otherwise become saturated. Not comfortable for active travel.

---

### 13.4 Improvised Sleeping System

When no sleeping bag is available:

1. **Debris nest:** Gather dry leaves, pine needles, grass, cattail down. Fill a large trash bag or make a mound. Burrow inside. The principle is the same as a debris hut interior — dead air space provides insulation. Need approximately 0.6–0.9 m³ of loosely packed debris to equal a light sleeping bag.

2. **Layering clothing:** Wear ALL available clothing in layers. Synthetic and wool next to skin. Stuff additional dry leaves inside clothing (especially the torso layer).

3. **Reflective/emergency blanket:** A Mylar emergency blanket reflects approximately 90% of radiated body heat back. It does not insulate (no dead air space) but dramatically reduces radiative loss. Wrap over other insulation for best effect. Crinkle it before use — the wrinkles trap small air pockets.

4. **Ground insulation:** Before any other sleeping system component, ensure adequate ground insulation. Minimum 10–15 cm of dry debris between you and the ground. Without this, body heat drains into the earth regardless of how many blankets are above you.

5. **Hot rocks:** Heat smooth (non-porous) rocks next to the fire for 30–60 minutes. Wrap in clothing or place in a bag. Tuck alongside the torso or feet in the sleeping system. Warning: avoid wet rocks or flaky rock (shale, slate) — they can explode when heated.

## 14. HOT WEATHER SURVIVAL

### 14.1 Heat Illness

**Heat Cramps:** Muscle cramps from salt/fluid loss during exertion. Treatment: rest, cool water, salty food.

**Heat Exhaustion:**
- Core temp: 37–40°C (99–104°F).
- Symptoms: heavy sweating, weakness, cold/pale/clammy skin, weak pulse, nausea, possible fainting.
- Treatment: move to shade, lie flat with feet elevated, cool with wet cloths, small sips of water.

**Heat Stroke:**
- Core temp: above 40°C (104°F).
- Symptoms: hot/red/dry or damp skin, rapid strong pulse, confusion, loss of consciousness.
- Medical emergency. Treatment: rapidly cool the victim by any means — immersion in cool water, ice packs to neck/armpits/groin, wet clothing with fanning. Evacuate immediately.

---

### 14.2 Shade Priority and Activity Scheduling

**The Sun's Effect:**

Radiant heat from direct sun in desert/tropical environments can add 300–600 W/m² of heat load. Shade reduces this to near zero.

**Activity Scheduling (Desert Survival):**
- 06:00–10:00: Maximum activity window. Temperatures are lowest; accomplish travel, water procurement, signaling.
- 10:00–16:00: Rest in shade. Do NOT travel. This is the period of maximum heat and solar radiation.
- 16:00–19:00: Secondary activity window. Temperatures begin falling.
- Night: If you must travel in extreme heat, nighttime travel is far more efficient (cooler, lower water requirement, less heat stress). Requires navigation ability.

**Shade Construction:**
- Minimum shade for desert survival: roof of any material elevated 30+ cm above the body to allow air circulation.
- Two-layer shade: most effective — outer layer reflects radiation, inner layer blocks convected heat from the hot first layer. A gap of 10–15 cm between layers significantly reduces heat transfer.
- A simple poncho stretched between two poles provides acceptable shade.
- Ground surface can be 15–30°C hotter than air temperature in direct sun. Shade your body AND the ground you rest on, or elevate yourself above the ground surface.

---

### 14.3 Desert Water Sources — What Works vs. Myths

**What Works:**

- **Following animal trails:** Game trails converge at water sources. Converging trails heading downhill often lead to water. Bird flight at dawn and dusk often toward water (doves, pigeons, finches fly directly to water in morning).
- **Rock outcroppings and canyons:** Rock pools (tinajas) collect rainwater in crevices and potholes. Look for waterstain marks (green algae, mineral deposits) below overhangs.
- **Vegetation concentration:** Dense green vegetation in an otherwise dry landscape often indicates subsurface water. Cottonwood, willow, sycamore, cattail are phreatophytes — deep-rooted plants that grow only where groundwater is accessible.
- **Valley floors and dry streambeds:** Water often flows below the surface of a dry riverbed (arroyo). Digging in the outer bend of the inside of a dry bend, or in the lowest elevation point of the channel, may reach water within 0.5–1.5 m.
- **Morning dew:** Exists even in hot deserts on clear nights. Collect from rocks and vegetation.

**What Does NOT Work:**

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| Drinking cactus juice solves desert dehydration | Most cacti (except barrel cactus, Ferocactus species) produce a highly acidic, alkaline, or toxic juice that causes vomiting and accelerates dehydration. Only the Fishhook Barrel Cactus (Ferocactus wislizeni) in the SW US provides limited drinkable moisture. Still only as emergency measure. |
| Digging at the lowest point of the desert will find water | Only works in specific geological contexts (valley floors, near mountains). Digging in the middle of a sand flat will not find water. |
| Sucking moisture from pebbles reduces thirst | Increases saliva production slightly but provides no hydration. Marginally useful for oral comfort, not actual water replacement. |
| Animals know where water is and you can follow any animal | Partially true for mammals (especially at dawn and dusk). But many desert insects and reptiles get all water from food and do not lead to surface water. Follow hoofed mammals, birds (especially doves), and bees. |
| You won't sweat in dry desert air | False. Sweat evaporates immediately in dry air, giving the false impression you're not sweating. Dehydration progresses without the subjective sensation of sweat. |
| Drinking urine saves you | Urine contains concentrated waste products. Drinking it adds to the solute load the kidneys must process, accelerating dehydration over multiple cycles. Drink urine only as a very last resort in the first cycle (if highly dilute — pale yellow) and never subsequently. |

## 15. SURVIVAL PSYCHOLOGY

### 15.1 Rule of Threes

A practical framework for survival priority setting:

| Time Frame | Without... | You can survive approximately... |
|------------|------------|----------------------------------|
| 3 minutes | Air (oxygen, or in icy water) | 3 minutes |
| 3 hours | Shelter in extreme conditions | 3 hours |
| 3 days | Water | 3 days |
| 3 weeks | Food | 3 weeks |

**Application:** Use the Rule of Threes to set priorities in any survival situation.
1. Address immediate life-threatening hazards (fire, gas, drowning, wounds).
2. Build shelter if exposed to lethal weather.
3. Find water.
4. Find food last — food is almost never the immediate priority.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Attempting to find food before shelter or water is secured.
- Moving when shelter should be built (expends energy and water, reduces signaling opportunity).
- Delaying shelter construction until conditions become extreme.

---

### 15.2 STOP Method

The most important survival technique is mental discipline. When lost or in a crisis:

**S — Stop**
- Halt all movement immediately. Sit down if possible.
- Panic, aimless movement, and running consume energy, water, and body heat.
- A person who is "lost" becomes statistically more likely to be found if they stop and signal rather than wander.

**T — Think**
- What do I actually know? Last known position. Time elapsed. Direction traveled.
- What resources do I have? Water, food, shelter materials, tools, signaling equipment.
- What is the immediate hazard? Weather, injury, darkness approaching?
- What are my priorities (apply the Rule of Threes)?

**O — Observe**
- Observe the terrain: landmarks, water features, trails, high ground.
- Observe the weather: is it deteriorating? Wind direction, cloud types.
- Observe your party: who is injured, cold, dehydrated?
- Observe your resources: how much water, food, daylight, fire-starting materials?

**P — Plan**
- Form a concrete plan based on the information gathered.
- Set decision criteria: "If we do not reach the ridge by 15:00, we will build camp here."
- Assign tasks if in a group.
- Communicate the plan to all members.
- Act on the plan deliberately — not reactively.

---

### 15.3 Priority Management

**Decision Framework:**
1. Immediate threats to life → address first regardless of other factors.
2. Maintain body core temperature → shelter, fire, dry clothing.
3. Hydration → find and purify water.
4. Signal for rescue → mirrors, smoke, whistles, ground signals. The sooner, the better.
5. Navigation and self-rescue → only if rescue is unlikely or delayed; moving while injured/dehydrated/disoriented is dangerous.
6. Food → very last priority.

**Self-Rescue Decision Criteria:**
- Self-rescue is appropriate when: you know exactly where you are and where safety is; you are physically capable; the route is known and manageable; your current situation will not be improved by waiting.
- Staying in place is appropriate when: you are near a known search corridor; your shelter, water, and signaling capability are adequate; you or a group member is injured; you cannot navigate reliably.

**Group Dynamics:**
- Panic and groupthink are survival killers. One decisive, calm leader significantly improves outcomes.
- Assign specific tasks: someone gathers fuel, someone builds shelter, someone works on water, someone watches for aircraft.
- Check in frequently: dehydration and hypothermia impair judgment — monitor group members' mental state.
- Maintain morale: tell realistic stories of rescue, keep everyone active and purposeful, acknowledge difficulty while maintaining focus on actionable steps.

**Cognitive Traps to Avoid:**
- "Normalization of deviance": gradually accepting a worsening situation as normal. Set clear tripwires ("If X happens, we take Y action").
- Completion bias: feeling compelled to finish the planned route even when conditions have changed.
- Expert halo: following one person's judgment without critique even if that judgment is incorrect.
- Underestimating the situation: most wilderness deaths involve individuals who were not taking the situation seriously.

## 16. FOOD PROCUREMENT — CONCEPTUAL OVERVIEW

*This section provides educational overviews only. For detailed trap construction diagrams and specifications, see the resource note at the end of this document.*

### 16.1 Trapping — Overview of Concepts

Trapping exploits animal behavior patterns: movement along established trails, curiosity about disturbances, food-seeking behavior, and shelter-seeking behavior.

**Core Principles:**
- Animals follow predictable paths (runs, trails) between food, water, and shelter.
- Traps are most effective placed on these established travel routes, not in open areas.
- Human scent is a powerful deterrent — minimize scent on traps and approach from downwind.
- Multiple traps placed simultaneously ("trapping out a line") dramatically increase success probability compared to one trap.
- Check traps frequently — animals die in traps and become unfit for consumption; some animals escape or are taken by other predators.

**Trap Types (Conceptual):**
- Snares: loop of cord placed to catch an animal around the neck or body as it passes through. Set on trails, in gaps in brush, near burrow entrances.
- Deadfalls: a weighted object triggered to fall and pin or kill the animal. Requires a trigger mechanism. Set near food bait or on runways.
- Pit traps: a concealed hole into which animals fall. Effective for small mammals in the right soil.

**Legal Note:** Trapping wild animals is regulated by state and federal law. In a true survival emergency (imminent threat to life), regulations may be superseded by necessity. In all other circumstances, applicable trapping licenses and regulations apply.

**For complete trap construction diagrams, trigger mechanisms, and snare configurations:** See the US Army FM 21-76 Survival Manual, Chapter 8. This is public domain and freely available.

---

### 16.2 Fishing — Basics

**Reading Water:**

- Fish congregate where food is available and where they can rest from current: behind rocks, in eddies, at the confluence of streams, at the edges of current (where fast and slow water meet), under overhanging banks, in pools below riffles.
- Temperature stratification: in warm weather, fish seek cooler, deeper water during midday; active near the surface at dawn and dusk.
- Feeding lanes: in rivers, fish hold in positions where current brings food to them. The seam between fast and slow water is prime territory.

**Improvised Fishing:**

- **Line:** Paracord inner strands (very thin and flexible), plant fiber twisted cord, thread from clothing, natural materials (vines, stinging nettle fiber).
- **Hook:** A gorge hook is a simple alternative to a barbed hook — a straight sharp piece of wood, bone, or metal (sharpened at both ends) tied at the middle. When a fish swallows the bait, the gorge hook turns crosswise inside the fish.
- **Hook from safety pin, button hook:** Shape metal into a hook if tools are available.
- **Bait:** Worms, grubs (found under logs, in soil), insects, grasshoppers, small fish parts, berries that fall into the water naturally.
- **Fish trap / weir:** A V-shaped arrangement of sticks driven into a stream bed, open end facing downstream, closed end with a funnel opening. Fish moving downstream swim into the funnel and cannot easily reverse direction.

---

### 16.3 Edible Plants — Identification Principles

**Critical Caution:** Misidentification of plants is potentially lethal. Many edible plants have toxic lookalikes. Always use the Universal Edibility Test before consuming an unfamiliar plant, and positively identify plants with multiple characteristics (not just one feature).

**Widely Recognized Safe Plants (North America):**

| Plant | Edible Parts | Notes |
|-------|-------------|-------|
| Cattail (Typha spp.) | Young shoots, pollen, rhizomes | Near water; all parts edible at various seasons |
| Dandelion (Taraxacum officinale) | Leaves, flowers, roots | Widespread; leaves bitter when mature |
| Chickweed (Stellaria media) | Leaves, stems, flowers | Common in disturbed ground; mild flavor |
| Wood sorrel (Oxalis spp.) | Leaves, flowers | Clover-like with heart-shaped leaflets; tart flavor; moderate consumption only |
| Purslane (Portulaca oleracea) | Leaves, stems | Succulent; common garden weed |
| Pine (Pinus spp.) | Inner bark, young needles, pine nuts | Inner bark (cambium) can be eaten raw; needles make tea rich in Vitamin C |
| Acorns (Quercus spp.) | Nuts after leaching | Must leach tannins with repeated water soakings before eating |
| Wild berries | Fruit | Positive identification essential; red/white/yellow berries require extra caution |

**Universal Edibility Test (UET) Procedure:**

Use ONLY when positive identification is not possible and starvation is a real risk. Takes up to 8+ hours for a single plant test.

1. Separate the plant into components: root, stem, leaves, buds, flowers, fruit.
2. Test only one component at a time.
3. Fast for 8 hours before beginning.
4. Smell: if the component smells strongly of almonds (cyanide) or soap — do not test, discard.
5. Contact test: rub the component on the inner wrist or inner elbow. Wait 15 minutes. If burning, itching, swelling, or rash occurs — discard.
6. Lip test: hold a small piece to the lip for 3 minutes. Observe for numbness, tingling, burning.
7. Tongue test: place on the tongue for 15 minutes. Observe for reactions.
8. Chew test: chew a small amount and hold in the mouth for 15 minutes. Do NOT swallow. Observe for reactions.
9. Swallow a small amount. Wait 8 hours. Observe for vomiting, diarrhea, cramps, dizziness.
10. If no reaction after 8 hours: eat a slightly larger portion (a handful). Wait 8 more hours.
11. If still no reaction: the tested component of that plant is likely safe.

**UET does NOT work for:** Mushrooms (reactions can be delayed 24–48+ hours and still fatal), plants that cause reactions only from skin contact, or neurological toxins that may not cause immediate symptoms.

**Poisonous Plant Caution — "Deadly Eight" groups in North America:**
- Hemlock (Conium maculatum) and Water Hemlock (Cicuta spp.): look similar to many edible species; extremely toxic.
- Nightshade family (Solanaceae): many toxic members.
- Lily family: many toxic species mixed with edible.
- White-berried plants: avoid until positively identified.
- Plants with milky sap: often (not always) toxic.
- Plants with three-part leaves in woodland: may be Poison Ivy/Oak/Sumac.

### 16.4 Tracking — Animal Sign Identification

**Deer (Whitetail — Odocoileus virginianus):**
- Track: Heart-shaped, two-pointed toes (cleaves), approximately 6–8 cm long, 5–6 cm wide for mature doe; 8–10 cm for mature buck.
- Dewclaws (small rounded impressions behind and above main track) appear in deep substrate when running.
- Gait: Walking = straddle-step pattern; trotting = diagonal two-print pattern; running/bounding = four prints in a cluster with large gaps between clusters. Stride length at walk: 35–50 cm.
- Sign: Rubs (antler scrapes on saplings), scrapes (pawed-up ground with urine), droppings (pellets 1–1.5 cm, football-shaped, clustered).

**Rabbit (Eastern Cottontail — Sylvilagus floridanus):**
- Track: Front feet small, oval, approximately 3 × 2.5 cm. Hind feet long and narrow, approximately 8–10 cm long, 3 cm wide.
- Bounding gait: Front feet land first (small), then large hind feet land ahead of the front feet (this is the characteristic bounding pattern). Tracks appear as a "Y" or chevron pattern.
- Stride: 30–60 cm between bounding groups.
- Sign: Round droppings (0.5–1 cm pellets, in scatters), gnawed plant stems at 45° angle near ground level.

**Squirrel (Fox or Gray Squirrel — Sciurus spp.):**
- Track: Bounding pattern like rabbit but smaller. Front feet: 2–3 cm. Hind feet: 4–6 cm.
- Five toes on hind foot (four visible usually), four toes on front.
- Bounding groups: hind feet land in front of or alongside front feet.
- Sign: Caches (small excavations where nuts are buried), stripped pinecones (remove scales to get seeds), gnawed nuts with round or irregular holes.

**Track Aging:**

| Factor | Fresh Track | Old Track |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| Edges | Sharp, crisp | Rounded, crumbled |
| Moisture | Moist soil still damp | Soil may be dry, track has dried |
| Debris | No debris in track | Leaves, seeds may have blown in |
| Surface | Clear detail, toe impressions sharp | Fine detail lost |
| Frost/dew | None in track if made after frost | Frost/dew crystals inside old track |
| Insect activity | Absent | Spider webs, insect tracks crossing |

Estimating age is difficult and context-dependent. The key question: is the animal likely still nearby? If tracks are within 30 minutes to 2 hours (by humidity, sun exposure, and temperature assessment), the animal may still be in the immediate area.

**Reading Movement:**
- Slow, deliberate tracks with regular gait = unhurried, feeding or traveling.
- Sudden direction change = startled; look for what caused the alarm.
- Runs and bounds indicate the animal was moving fast — frightened or chasing prey.
- Well-worn trails (multiple track layers, compressed vegetation) = regular route used over days to weeks.

### 17.1 US Army FM 21-76 Survival Manual

The US Army Field Manual 21-76, "Survival," is the foundational military survival reference document. It is in the public domain and available free of charge from official sources.

**Content includes:**
- Chapter 8: Obtaining Food — includes detailed diagrams and step-by-step construction instructions for primitive traps (figure-4 deadfall, Paiute deadfall, snares, fish traps, bird traps) and hunting tools (spears, bows, clubs).
- Chapter 7: Water Procurement.
- Chapter 9: Survival Use of Plants.
- Chapter 15: Desert, Arctic, Jungle, Sea, and Tropical Survival.
- And much more.
