# Perimeter Security and Situational Awareness

## Situational Awareness

### Cooper Color Code System
- **White**: Unaware. Not paying attention to surroundings. AVOID this state.
- **Yellow**: Relaxed alert. General awareness of surroundings, people, exits. DEFAULT state.
- **Orange**: Focused alert. Specific potential threat identified. Evaluating, planning response.
- **Red**: Action. Threat confirmed. Executing response plan (fight, flight, or de-escalation).

### OODA Loop (Decision Cycle)
1. **Observe**: What is happening? What has changed?
2. **Orient**: What does it mean? How does it affect me?
3. **Decide**: What should I do? What are my options?
4. **Act**: Execute the decision. Then loop back to Observe.

Faster OODA loops = better response to threats. Practice makes this automatic.

### Baseline Awareness
- Learn the "normal" for your area: typical traffic patterns, animal behavior, noise levels.
- Anomalies to watch for: unusual vehicle presence, out-of-place objects, changes in animal behavior (birds going silent), unexpected light or noise at night.

## Perimeter Security

### Layered Defense
Create multiple concentric security rings:

1. **Outer ring (observation zone)**: 200-500 meters. Early warning only. Trip wires, noise makers, game cameras, observation posts.
2. **Middle ring (delay zone)**: 50-200 meters. Physical barriers that slow approach: fencing, dense brush, cleared fields (deny cover), lighting.
3. **Inner ring (defense zone)**: 0-50 meters. Hardened positions, strong lighting, communication hub.

### Early Warning Systems

**Low-Tech Methods**:
- **Trip wire alarms**: String monofilament (fishing line) at ankle height across approach routes. Attach to tin cans with pebbles or bells.
- **Gravel paths**: Crunching gravel is difficult to move across quietly.
- **Geese/Guinea fowl**: Excellent natural alarm systems — extremely alert and loud.
- **Dogs**: Best alarm and deterrent. Multiple dogs cover each other's blind spots.

**Entry Point Hardening**:
- Reinforce door frames with 3-inch screws into studs (replace standard 3/4-inch screws).
- Install secondary barriers: security bars, cross-bars, furniture barricades.
- Windows: security film slows breach by 30-60 seconds. Plywood backup.
- Single point of entry is easier to defend than multiple access points.

## Navigation Without GPS

### Compass Navigation
- Hold compass level, let needle settle.
- Magnetic north ≠ true north. Declination varies by location (in US: 0-20°).
- To follow a bearing: set desired degree on compass, rotate body until needle aligns with N, walk toward direction-of-travel arrow.
- **Back bearing**: Your return bearing = original bearing ± 180°.

### Natural Navigation

**Finding North (Northern Hemisphere)**:
- **North Star (Polaris)**: Follow the "pointer stars" of the Big Dipper. Polaris is at the end of the Little Dipper's handle. It indicates true north.
- **Watch method**: Point the hour hand at the sun. Halfway between the hour hand and 12 o'clock is approximately south.
- **Shadow stick**: Place a stick vertically in the ground. Mark the shadow tip. Wait 15-20 minutes, mark again. A line between the two marks runs approximately east-west (first mark is west).

**Estimating Distance**:
- Average walking pace: ~2.5 feet per step.
- Pace count: Count every left foot step over 100 meters on flat ground (typically 62-68 paces). Use this as your personal calibration.
- Terrain adjustment: Add 10% for hills, 20% for dense vegetation.

## Fire Making

### Fire Triangle
Three elements required: **Heat + Fuel + Oxygen**. Remove any one and the fire goes out.

### Fire Lay Methods

**Tinder (catches spark/ember)**:
- Dry grass, bark shavings (birch bark is excellent), cotton balls with petroleum jelly, dryer lint, char cloth, fine wood shavings.

**Kindling (catches from tinder)**:
- Twigs pencil-thickness and smaller, dry. Dead branches still attached to trees (off the ground = drier).

**Fuel (sustains the fire)**:
- Wrist-thickness and larger wood. Hardwoods burn longer and hotter. Softwoods ignite easier.

### Ignition Methods

**Ferro Rod (Ferrocerium)**:
- Strike spine of knife along rod to create sparks (3000°F).
- Direct sparks into tinder bundle. Reliable in all weather.

**Bow Drill (Primitive)**:
1. Fireboard: flat piece of dry softwood (cedar, willow, cottonwood).
2. Spindle: straight, dry, round stick. Same wood as fireboard.
3. Bow: curved stick with cordage tied end to end.
4. Bearing block: hardwood or stone with socket for top of spindle.
5. Notch: Cut a V-shaped notch in the fireboard to collect hot dust.
6. Spin spindle with bow until black dust fills notch and begins to smoke.
7. Transfer coal to tinder bundle and gently blow into flame.

**Flint and Steel**:
- Strike steel against sharp flint edge to create sparks.
- Direct sparks onto char cloth or fungus tinder.

### Fire Safety
- Clear area 10 feet around fire of flammable materials.
- Never leave fire unattended.
- Keep water or dirt nearby for extinguishing.
- In survival, fire provides: warmth, water purification, cooking, signaling, morale, insect deterrence.
