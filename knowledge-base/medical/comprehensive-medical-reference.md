## 1. MARCH Protocol - Tactical Combat Casualty Care

**Source:** Committee on Tactical Combat Casualty Care (CoTCCC), Joint Trauma System (JTS), TCCC Guidelines January 2024.

MARCH is the prioritized treatment mnemonic used in Tactical Combat Casualty Care. The expanded version, MARCH-PAWS, stands for: **M**assive Hemorrhage, **A**irway, **R**espirations, **C**irculation, **H**ypothermia/Head Injury, **P**ain, **A**ntibiotics, **W**ounds, **S**plinting.

### M - Massive Hemorrhage

Massive hemorrhage is the #1 cause of preventable death on the battlefield. Uncontrolled hemorrhage causes death through hypovolemia, and can lead to hypothermia, metabolic acidosis, and coagulopathy (the "lethal triad").

**Actions:**
1. Apply a CoTCCC-recommended limb tourniquet for hemorrhage amenable to tourniquet use
2. Place tourniquet over the uniform, clearly proximal to the bleeding site
3. If bleeding site is not readily apparent, apply tourniquet "high and tight" (as proximal as possible)
4. For junctional hemorrhage (axilla, groin, neck): apply junctional tourniquet or pack wound with Combat Gauze (CoTCCC hemostatic dressing of choice) with at least 3 minutes of direct pressure
5. Alternative hemostatic adjuncts: Celox Gauze, ChitoGauze, XStat (for deep narrow-tract junctional wounds), iTClamp

### A - Airway

**Actions:**
1. If casualty has impaired ventilation and uncorrectable hypoxia (SpO2 < 90%), insert a properly sized nasopharyngeal airway
2. Ventilate using a 1000mL bag-valve-mask resuscitator
3. Monitor continuously with EtCO2 and SpO2

### R - Respirations

**Actions:**
1. Be alert for tension pneumothorax
2. If casualty develops increasing hypoxia, respiratory distress, or hypotension with suspected tension pneumothorax: burp or remove the chest seal dressing, or perform needle decompression
3. All open and/or sucking chest wounds should be treated by applying a vented chest seal

### C - Circulation

**Actions:**
1. Reassess prior tourniquet application; expose wound and confirm tourniquet necessity
2. Replace any tourniquet placed over uniform with one applied directly to skin, 2-3 inches above bleeding site
3. Check for distal pulse (unless traumatic amputation); if bleeding persists or distal pulse present, tighten or add second tourniquet side-by-side
4. Prevent hypotension; maintain systolic BP 100-110 mmHg
5. Preferred resuscitation fluids: whole blood or plasma
6. Mark all tourniquets with time of application using permanent marker

**Tourniquet Conversion Criteria (all three must be met):**
- Casualty is NOT in shock
- Wound can be closely monitored for bleeding
- Tourniquet is NOT controlling bleeding from an amputation

Convert tourniquets in less than 2 hours if possible. Do NOT remove a tourniquet in place >6 hours unless close monitoring and lab capability are available.

### H - Hypothermia / Head Injury

**Actions:**
1. Use hypothermia enclosure systems
2. Minimize exposure to cold ground, wind, and air temperature
3. Keep protective gear on or with casualty
4. Replace wet clothing with dry clothing
5. Use battery-powered warming device for IV/IO fluids (38 degrees C output, up to 150 mL/min)
6. Burn casualties are particularly susceptible to hypothermia

## 2. Tourniquet Application & Hemorrhage Control

**Sources:** Stop the Bleed (ACS/DHA), American Red Cross, CoTCCC.

### The ABCs of Bleeding Control (Stop the Bleed)

The number one cause of preventable death after injury is bleeding. A person can die from severe blood loss in less than 5 minutes.

### Decision Tree: Which Technique to Use

```
LIFE-THREATENING BLEEDING IDENTIFIED
            |
    Where is the bleeding?
           / \
         /     \
   ON A LIMB    NOT ON A LIMB (scalp, neck,
       |         shoulder, groin, back)
       |              |
  TOURNIQUET     WOUND PACKING
  + Direct       + Direct Pressure
  Pressure
       |
  Still bleeding?
       |
  Apply 2nd tourniquet
  ABOVE the first
```

### Tourniquet Application Steps (Red Cross / Stop the Bleed)

1. **Position:** Place tourniquet 2-3 inches above the wound, between the wound and the heart
2. **Avoid:** Do NOT place on top of the wound or on a joint (elbow, knee)
3. **Warn:** Tell the person it will be painful but necessary
4. **Apply:** Pass strap through buckle, then PULL - TWIST - CLIP
   - PULL the strap tight
   - TWIST the windlass rod until bleeding stops
   - CLIP/secure the windlass rod in the clip
5. **Second tourniquet:** If first tourniquet is maximally tightened but bleeding continues, apply a second tourniquet above the first (closer to heart)
6. **Do NOT remove:** Only a medical professional should loosen or remove a tourniquet
7. **Mark time:** Note the time of application on the tourniquet with permanent marker

### Wound Packing Technique

**When:** Life-threatening bleeding from scalp, neck, shoulder, groin, back, or limb when no tourniquet is available.

1. Expose the wound - remove or cut away clothing
2. Using hemostatic gauze (preferred) or plain gauze, pack the wound tightly
3. Start packing deep in the wound, filling the entire wound cavity
4. Apply at least 3 minutes of firm, direct pressure over the packed wound
5. Apply pressure dressing over the packing
6. If one hemostatic dressing fails, remove and try a fresh dressing (same or different type)

### Direct Pressure

1. Apply direct pressure using hands, gauze, or any clean cloth
2. Press firmly and do not release
3. Do not remove original gauze pad; do not stack multiple pads
4. If bleeding soaks through, apply additional pressure

### Embedded Objects

- Do NOT remove embedded objects
- Pack dressings around the object
- Bandage in place
- If tourniquet needed, apply above the object

## 3. Wound Care & Irrigation

**Sources:** Wilderness Medical Society (WMS) Wound Care Practice Guidelines, NOLS Wilderness Medicine, WMA International Field Protocols.

### Priority Order

1. **Control bleeding first** - hemorrhage control always takes priority over wound cleaning
2. **Irrigate the wound** - reduce infection risk
3. **Assess for closure vs. open packing**
4. **Dress and monitor**

### Wound Irrigation Protocol

**Purpose:** Remove foreign materials, decrease bacterial contamination, clear cellular debris.

**Technique:**
1. Remove visible foreign matter with disinfected tweezers or gentle brushing
2. Irrigate with clean water using a syringe (12cc syringe recommended) to generate adequate pressure
3. Use at least 1 liter of water for wound irrigation (more for heavily contaminated wounds)
4. Direct the irrigation stream across the wound, not straight into deep tissue
5. Cover with a clean bandage

**Irrigation Pressure:** A 12cc syringe provides approximately 8-12 PSI, which is the recommended range for effective wound irrigation - enough to dislodge debris without driving bacteria deeper into tissue.

### Water Quality for Irrigation

In order of reliability:
1. **Boiled water** - most reliable method for eliminating microorganisms
2. **Filtered water** - next most effective for reducing turbidity
3. **Chemically treated water** (e.g., Aquamira drops)
4. **UV-treated water**

Recommendation: Carry 2-3 water decontamination options.

**Note:** Potable (drinkable) water is acceptable for wound irrigation in wilderness settings. Sterile water is ideal but not required when unavailable.

### Wound Closure Decision Tree

```
WOUND ASSESSMENT
       |
  Is wound grossly contaminated?
      / \
    YES   NO
     |      |
  Clean,    Is it within closure window?
  irrigate,    |
  pack OPEN   Clean wound: 4-6 hours
  (do NOT      Head/face wound: 10-12 hours
  close)          |
              Close in field with
              wound closure strips
              or sutures (if trained)
```

### Wounds Requiring Evacuation

Rapidly evacuate any patient with:
- Heavily contaminated wound
- Wound that opens a joint space
- Wound involving underlying tendons or ligaments
- Animal bite wound
- Facial wound
- Impaled/embedded object
- Amputation
- Crushing mechanism injury
- Signs of serious infection (spreading redness, red streaking, fever, pus)

### Infection Monitoring

Watch for signs of infection:
- Increasing pain after 24 hours
- Redness spreading beyond wound margins
- Red streaks tracking from wound (lymphangitis)
- Warmth and swelling increasing
- Purulent drainage (pus)
- Fever
- Foul odor

## 4. Splinting Techniques

**Sources:** WFR curricula (NOLS, WMA International), WMS, U.S. Army Field Manual.

### General Splinting Principles

1. **Immobilize joints above AND below the fracture site**
2. **For joint injuries:** Splint in position found or midrange position; immobilize bones above and below the joint
3. **Pad all splints** - use compact padding (not loose/fluffy) to prevent pressure points and discomfort
4. **Splint snugly but not tight** - must not restrict circulation
5. **Multidimensional splinting** - apply splint material to two or more sides for best stability
6. **Check CSM before AND after splinting** - then continue monitoring

### CSM Checks (Circulation, Sensation, Motion)

Perform before and after every splint application, and at regular intervals:

- **Circulation:** Check radial pulse (wrist) or pedal pulse (ankle) distal to injury. Check skin color, temperature, capillary refill
- **Sensation:** Gently squeeze fingers/toes; ask patient to identify which digit. Check for numbness or tingling
- **Motion:** Ask patient to wiggle fingers/toes; test grip strength

**If CSM is compromised after splinting:** loosen the splint and reassess.

### Improvised Splint Materials

Wilderness splinting emphasizes improvisation with available materials:
- Ski poles, trekking poles, tent poles
- Sleeping pads (SAM splint substitute)
- Backpack frame stays (aluminum)
- Paddles, snowshoes
- Sticks, branches, boughs
- Rolled magazines, cardboard
- Duct tape (structural and securing)
- Clothing (compressed for padding)
- Belts, boot laces, webbing, parachute cord
- Cargo/accessory straps
- Life jackets

### Traction-in-Position (TIP)

In wilderness settings, repositioning a fracture to anatomical position is often appropriate (differs from urban first aid "splint as found" teaching):

**Steps:**
1. Grasp the limb above (proximal to) the fracture and stabilize
2. With your other hand, grasp the limb below (distal to) the fracture
3. Apply steady, gentle traction (pulling) along the long axis of the bone
4. While maintaining traction, slowly guide the limb toward anatomical position

**Stop TIP if:**
- It causes considerably more pain
- You encounter resistance to movement
- New neurological symptoms develop

If TIP cannot be completed, splint in position found.

### Femur Traction Splint

A mid-shaft femur fracture is a serious injury with potential for 1000-1500cc blood loss in 15-20 minutes.

**Traction splint purpose:**
- Immobilize the fracture
- Relax muscle spasm (pain control)
- Minimize internal blood loss when wrapped with compression

**Improvised traction splint principle (Thomas ring concept):**
1. One end of splint positioned against the hip, pushing against the pelvic ischium
2. A strap around the foot/ankle connects to the other end
3. Strap is tightened (often with an improvised windlass)
4. Traction counteracts thigh muscle spasm
5. Wrap entire leg and splint with 6-inch elastic bandages from ankle to hip for compression
6. Monitor for shock; check distal CSM regularly

### Pelvic Splint (Improvised)

For suspected pelvic fractures, commercial devices may not be available in the field. Improvised methods include wrapping with a sheet, sleeping pad, or SAM splint around the pelvis and securing snugly. The goal is circumferential compression to stabilize the pelvic ring.

### Specific Splinting Applications

| Injury | Joints to Immobilize | Splint Type |
|--------|---------------------|-------------|
| Forearm fracture | Wrist + Elbow | Rigid splint (two sides) |
| Wrist fracture | Hand + Forearm | Rigid splint, position of function |
| Upper arm (humerus) | Shoulder + Elbow | Sling and swathe |
| Lower leg (tib/fib) | Knee + Ankle | Rigid splint (two sides) |
| Femur (mid-shaft) | Hip + Knee | Traction splint |
| Ankle | Foot + Lower leg | Pillow splint or rigid |
| Knee | Femur + Lower leg | Long rigid splint |
| Finger | Adjacent finger buddy tape | Buddy tape to adjacent finger |

## 5. Burn Assessment - Rule of Nines

**Sources:** PHTLS, ATLS, StatPearls (NIH/NLM), American Burn Association, JEMS.

### Burn Classification by Depth

| Degree | Also Called | Depth | Appearance | Sensation | Healing |
|--------|-----------|-------|------------|-----------|---------|
| 1st | Superficial | Epidermis only | Red, dry, no blisters | Painful | 3-5 days, no scarring |
| 2nd | Partial thickness | Epidermis + dermis | Red, wet, blisters | Very painful | 2-3 weeks (superficial) to months (deep) |
| 3rd | Full thickness | Through all skin layers | White/brown/black, leathery, dry | Painless (nerves destroyed) | Requires grafting |

**Important:** Only 2nd and 3rd degree burns are counted in Total Body Surface Area (TBSA) calculations.

### Rule of Nines - Adult Body Surface Area

```
         HEAD
        9% (total)
    (4.5% front, 4.5% back)

   ----TRUNK----
   Front: 18%    Back: 18%
   (chest 9%     (upper back 9%
    abdomen 9%)   lower back 9%)

  LEFT ARM    RIGHT ARM
    9%           9%
  (each: 4.5%   (each: 4.5%
   front, 4.5%    front, 4.5%
   back)          back)

  LEFT LEG    RIGHT LEG
    18%          18%
  (each: 9%     (each: 9%
   front, 9%      front, 9%
   back)          back)

     GROIN: 1%

  TOTAL = 100%
```

### Pediatric Modifications

Children have proportionally larger heads and smaller legs. The standard Rule of Nines is inaccurate for pediatric patients.

**Rule of Eights (patients under 10 kg):**
- Head: 20%
- Trunk: 32%
- Each leg: 16%
- Each arm: 8%

**Lund-Browder Chart:** Most accurate for pediatric burns - accounts for changing body proportions with age. Use when available.

### Palmar Method

For smaller or scattered burns, use the patient's palm (with fingers closed/adducted) as approximately 1% TBSA. Useful for estimating irregular burn patterns.

Note: Actual palm size is closer to 0.8% in men and 0.7% in women, so the palmar method slightly overestimates.

### Parkland Formula for Fluid Resuscitation

**Formula:** 4 mL x body weight (kg) x %TBSA burned = total crystalloid (Lactated Ringer's) for first 24 hours

**Administration:**
- First HALF of calculated volume over the first 8 hours post-burn
- Second HALF over the remaining 16 hours
- Goal: urine output > 0.5 mL/kg/hour in adults

**Modified Brooke Formula:** Uses 2 mL/kg/% TBSA (reduces over-resuscitation risk).

**Example:** 80 kg patient with 30% TBSA burn:
- Parkland: 4 x 80 x 30 = 9,600 mL in 24 hours
- First 8 hours: 4,800 mL (600 mL/hour)
- Next 16 hours: 4,800 mL (300 mL/hour)

### Prehospital Burn Management

1. **Scene safety** - ensure the burning process has stopped
2. **Remove clothing and jewelry** (if clothing adheres to skin, leave it)
3. **Cool the burn** - soak in cool (not ice) water for 20 minutes (standard of early care)
4. **Evaluate airway** - look for coughing, stridor, soot around nostrils/lips, singed nasal/facial hair. If ALS available, consider early intubation before swelling worsens
5. **Do NOT apply** ice, butter, ointments, or adhesive bandages to burns
6. **Cover with loose, non-adherent dressing**
7. **Prevent hypothermia** - maintain core temperature above 36.5 degrees C (97.7 degrees F). Burn patients lose heat rapidly
8. **Initiate IV fluids** if available and burn >20% TBSA
9. **Elevate burned extremities** above heart level

### Burn Center Referral Criteria (American Burn Association)

Transfer to a burn center for:
- 2nd and 3rd degree burns >10% TBSA in patients <10 or >50 years old
- 2nd and 3rd degree burns >20% TBSA in all other age groups
- Burns involving face, hands, feet, genitalia, perineum, or major joints
- 3rd degree burns of any size
- Electrical burns (including lightning)
- Chemical burns
- Inhalation injury
- Burns with pre-existing medical conditions
- Burns with associated trauma (if burn is greater threat)
- Circumferential burns of extremity or chest

## 6. Shock Recognition & Management

**Sources:** PHTLS, ATLS (hemorrhagic shock classification), StatPearls (NIH/NLM), NAEMT.

### Definition

Shock is inadequate tissue perfusion resulting in insufficient oxygen delivery to meet cellular metabolic demands. It is a clinical syndrome, not a single disease.

### Hemorrhagic Shock Classification (ATLS)

Based on a healthy 70 kg adult with ~5L circulating blood volume:

| Parameter | Class I | Class II | Class III | Class IV |
|-----------|---------|----------|-----------|----------|
| **Blood Loss** | <750 mL | 750-1500 mL | 1500-2000 mL | >2000 mL |
| **% Blood Volume** | <15% | 15-30% | 30-40% | >40% |
| **Heart Rate** | <100 | 100-120 | >120 | >120 |
| **Blood Pressure** | Normal | Normal to slightly decreased | Decreased | Significantly decreased |
| **Pulse Pressure** | Normal | Narrowed | Narrowed | Very narrow (<25 mmHg) |
| **Respiratory Rate** | 14-20 | 20-30 | 30-40 | >35 |
| **Urine Output** | >30 mL/hr | 20-30 mL/hr | 5-15 mL/hr | Negligible |
| **Mental Status** | Slightly anxious | Mildly anxious | Anxious, confused | Confused, lethargic |
| **Fluid Replacement** | Crystalloid | Crystalloid | Crystalloid + blood | Crystalloid + blood |

### Shock Index

**Shock Index = Heart Rate / Systolic Blood Pressure**

- Normal: 0.5 - 0.7
- **>1.0 = reliable indicator of hemodynamic instability** (hypovolemic or septic shock)
- Useful prehospital tool when individual vital signs may appear within normal range

### Types of Shock - Recognition Table

| Type | Cause | HR | BP | Skin | SVR | Key Sign |
|------|-------|----|----|------|-----|----------|
| **Hypovolemic** | Hemorrhage, dehydration, burns | Up | Down | Cool, pale, clammy | Up | Obvious fluid/blood loss |
| **Cardiogenic** | MI, heart failure, dysrhythmia | Up | Down | Cool, pale, clammy | Up | JVD, pulmonary edema |
| **Neurogenic** | Spinal cord injury, brain injury | DOWN (bradycardia) | Down | Warm, dry | Down | Bradycardia + hypotension |
| **Septic** | Infection | Up | Down | Warm, flushed (early) | Down | Fever, known infection |
| **Anaphylactic** | Allergen exposure | Up | Down | Flushed, hives, edema | Down | Allergen exposure, urticaria |
| **Obstructive** | Tension pneumo, cardiac tamponade, PE | Up | Down | Cool, pale | Up | JVD, muffled heart sounds |

### General Shock Management (Prehospital / Wilderness)

**Decision Tree:**
```
SUSPECT SHOCK (altered mental status, tachycardia,
hypotension, pale/cool/clammy skin, weak pulses)
              |
     1. Ensure scene safety
     2. Manage ABCs (airway, breathing, circulation)
     3. Control obvious hemorrhage
              |
     4. Position patient:
        - Supine with legs elevated 8-12 inches
          (modified Trendelenburg)
        - EXCEPTION: suspected spinal injury (supine, flat)
        - EXCEPTION: difficulty breathing (semi-sitting)
        - EXCEPTION: pregnant (left lateral position)
              |
     5. Prevent heat loss (cover with blankets)
     6. Do NOT give oral fluids if surgery likely
     7. IV fluid resuscitation if available:
        - 20 mL/kg isotonic crystalloid bolus
          (Normal Saline or Lactated Ringer's)
        - Repeat as needed; reassess after each bolus
        - Hemorrhagic shock: 3 mL fluid per 1 mL
          estimated blood loss (3:1 ratio)
              |
     8. If unresponsive to fluids:
        - Consider blood products (if available)
        - Consider vasopressors (ALS/hospital)
              |
     9. Rapid transport to definitive care
```

### Key Principles

- Hypovolemia must be corrected BEFORE administering vasopressors
- Shock is progressive - early recognition and treatment are critical
- Reassess frequently - shock is a dynamic condition
- In wilderness settings, rapid evacuation is essential when shock is identified

## 7. Patient Assessment System

**Sources:** NOLS Wilderness Medicine, WMA International, WFR curricula.

### Overview

The Patient Assessment System (PAS) is the systematic approach used in wilderness medicine to evaluate patients. It is visualized as a pyramid:

```
         /\
        /  \        SCENE SIZE-UP
       /    \
      /------\
     /        \     PRIMARY SURVEY (ABCDE)
    /----------\
   /            \   SECONDARY SURVEY
  /   (Patient   \  (Exam + History + Vitals)
 /    Assessment)  \
/------------------\
     SOAP NOTE       PLAN (Treatment + Evacuation)
```

### Step 1: Scene Size-Up

Before approaching any patient:
- **BSI (Body Substance Isolation):** Gloves, eye protection, masks as needed
- **Scene Safety:** Is it safe to approach? Hazards?
- **Mechanism of Injury (MOI):** What happened? How did it happen?
- **Number of patients:** Mass casualty considerations?
- **Additional resources needed?**

### Step 2: Primary Survey (ABCDE)

**Before ABCDE:**
- Establish responsiveness: introduce yourself, ask if you may help
- This simultaneously obtains consent to treat and assesses consciousness
- Unresponsive patient = implied consent
- If MOI suggests possible spine injury: manually stabilize the head/neck

**A - Airway**
- Is the airway open and clear?
- Look for obstructions (blood, vomit, foreign body, tongue, swelling)
- Open airway: head-tilt/chin-lift (or jaw thrust if spine injury suspected)
- Suction or sweep if obstructed
- Consider nasopharyngeal airway if needed

**B - Breathing**
- Is the patient breathing? What is the rate and quality?
- Ask the patient to take a deep breath
- Look, listen, feel: chest rise, breath sounds, air movement
- If labored or painful, expose the chest and assess for life-threatening injuries
- Treat immediately: tension pneumothorax, open chest wound, flail chest

**C - Circulation**
- Check for pulse (radial first; carotid if no radial)
- Look and sweep for severe bleeding (run gloved hands under patient)
- Control life-threatening hemorrhage immediately
- Assess skin signs (color, temperature, moisture) as quick perfusion check

**D - Disability (Neurological Status)**
- Assess Level of Responsiveness (LOR) using AVPU:
  - **A** = Alert (awake, oriented)
  - **V** = Responds to Verbal stimuli
  - **P** = Responds to Painful stimuli
  - **U** = Unresponsive

**E - Environment / Expose**
- Expose injuries as needed for assessment
- Protect from environmental hazards (cold, heat, rain, sun)
- Consider removing from dangerous environment if safe to do so

### Step 3: Secondary Survey

Performed only AFTER primary survey is complete and life threats are managed.

#### Head-to-Toe Physical Exam

One person performs the entire exam for consistency. Designate a separate notetaker.

**Systematic examination:**
- **Head:** Palpate skull, check ears (blood/fluid = CSF leak), eyes (pupils equal and reactive - PERRL), nose, mouth, jaw
- **Neck:** Palpate cervical spine (midline tenderness?), check tracheal position, JVD
- **Chest:** Palpate clavicles, ribs, sternum. Auscultate lung sounds bilaterally. Check for crepitus, paradoxical motion, pain
- **Abdomen:** Palpate all four quadrants gently. Assess for rigidity, guarding, distention, tenderness
- **Pelvis:** Gentle compression (do NOT rock). Pain = stop and stabilize
- **Extremities:** Palpate all long bones. Check CSM in all four extremities
- **Back:** Log-roll (with spinal precautions if indicated) and palpate entire spine

#### CSM Check (Circulation, Sensation, Motion)

- **Circulation:** Check radial pulse in each hand, pedal pulse on each ankle. Should be equal bilaterally
- **Sensation:** Squeeze individual fingers and toes on medial and lateral sides. Patient identifies which digit
- **Motion:** Ask patient to wiggle fingers/toes. Test grip strength bilaterally (squeeze your hands)

#### Patient History (SAMPLE)

- **S**ymptoms: What is the chief complaint? What bothers you?
- **A**llergies: Medications, environmental, food, insects
- **M**edications: Prescription, OTC, supplements, recreational
- **P**ertinent medical history: Relevant past medical conditions, surgeries
- **L**ast intake/output: Last food, water, urination, bowel movement
- **E**vents: What happened leading up to this? What were you doing?

#### OPQRST (for pain complaints)

- **O**nset: When did it start? What were you doing?
- **P**rovocation/Palliation: What makes it worse? What makes it better?
- **Q**uality: Describe the pain (sharp, dull, burning, pressure, tearing)
- **R**adiation: Does the pain move or spread?
- **S**everity: Rate 1-10
- **T**ime: How long has it lasted? Is it constant or intermittent?

#### Vital Signs (SCTM + LOR)

Record with time stamps. Repeat every 5-15 minutes depending on patient stability.

- **Skin Color, Temperature, Moisture (SCTM):** Normal = warm, pink, dry. Assess at forehead, hands, trunk
- **Level of Responsiveness (LOR):** Oriented to Person, Place, Time, Events (Ox4)
- **Heart Rate:** Rate, rhythm, quality (strong/weak)
- **Respiratory Rate:** Rate, rhythm, depth, effort
- **Blood Pressure:** If equipment available
- **Pupils:** Equal, round, reactive to light (PERRL); note size
- **Temperature:** If thermometer available

### Step 4: SOAP Note Documentation

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| **S** - Subjective | Chief complaint, patient's description of events, SAMPLE history, OPQRST |
| **O** - Objective | Physical exam findings, vital signs with times, CSM checks |
| **A** - Assessment | Problem list (prioritized), working diagnosis |
| **P** - Plan | Treatment provided, anticipated problems, evacuation plan, monitoring schedule |

### AEIOU-TIPS (Altered Mental Status Differential)

When a patient has altered mental status, consider:
- **A** - Alcohol / Acidosis
- **E** - Epilepsy / Endocrine (diabetes)
- **I** - Insulin (hypo/hyperglycemia)
- **O** - Overdose / Oxygen deprivation
- **U** - Uremia (kidney failure)
- **T** - Trauma / Temperature
- **I** - Infection
- **P** - Psychiatric / Poisoning
- **S** - Stroke / Seizure / Shock

## 8. Spine Assessment & Spinal Cord Protection

**Sources:** Wilderness Medical Society (WMS) 2024 Practice Guidelines, NOLS Wilderness Medicine, NEXUS, Canadian C-Spine Rule.

### Key Philosophy Shift (WMS 2024)

The WMS has moved from technique-based "spinal immobilization" to goal-based **Spinal Cord Protection (SCP)**. Key findings:

- No well-documented case of out-of-hospital neurologic deterioration from inadequate immobilization has been identified
- Many cases of severe morbidity and mortality secondary to immobilization itself have been documented
- Rigid cervical collars have no demonstrated benefit and carry documented risks (aspiration, respiratory compromise, pressure ulcers)
- Only 1-2% of trauma patients have a clinically significant spinal injury
- WMS suggests NOT using the term "clearing the spine" in out-of-hospital care

### Spine Assessment Decision Tree

```
MECHANISM OF INJURY PRESENT?
(Fall, MVC, diving, axial load, blunt force)
              |
         YES  |  NO --> No spine precautions needed
              |
    DOES PATIENT HAVE ANY OF THESE?
    - Neurologic deficit (weakness, numbness, tingling)
    - Midline spinal tenderness on palpation
    - Altered mental status (drugs, alcohol, head injury)
    - Distracting injury (painful injury elsewhere)
              |
         YES  |  NO
              |      |
    IMPLEMENT SCP    FOCUSED SPINE ASSESSMENT
    (Spinal Cord     (WFR scope - requires training)
     Protection)           |
              |       Is patient:
              |       - Alert, reliable, cooperative?
              |       - Sober (no drugs/alcohol)?
              |       - No distracting injuries?
              |       - No midline tenderness?
              |       - No neurologic deficits?
              |       - Can actively rotate neck
              |         45 degrees L and R without pain?
              |            |
              |       ALL YES --> Patient may be
              |                   allowed to self-mobilize
              |                   with instructions to
              |                   minimize painful movement
              |
              |       ANY NO --> Implement SCP
```

### Spinal Cord Protection (SCP) Principles

SCP is NOT rigid immobilization. It is motion restriction using the least harmful method:

1. **Neutral alignment:** Restore and maintain using nonrigid tools during extrication, UNLESS met with resistance, increased pain, or new/worsening neurologic deficit
2. **Self-extrication:** Alert, reliable patients may self-extricate with clear instructions
3. **Passive restriction:** Padding, verbal encouragement not to move in a painful way - this is sufficient
4. **Airway takes priority:** Use jaw thrust (not head-tilt/chin-lift) for suspected spinal injury

### Equipment Recommendations (WMS 2024)

| Equipment | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Rigid cervical collar | NOT recommended (documented risks, no proven benefit) |
| Soft collar | May assist motion reduction if it doesn't compromise care |
| Vacuum splint | Preferred over rigid collar |
| Vacuum mattress | Superior to backboard for motion restriction and comfort |
| Backboard | For extrication ONLY, not for transport. Remove ASAP |

### Transfer Technique

The **lift and slide transfer with trap squeeze** is preferred over the log-roll when motion restriction is needed.

### Penetrating Trauma Exception

Spinal immobilization should NOT be performed for isolated penetrating trauma (evidence-based).

### Important Notes

- The Focused Spine Assessment is within WFR scope of practice but NOT within WFA (16-hour course) scope
- There are no studies validating WFRs performing selective spine assessment, though EMTs and paramedics have been validated
- When in doubt, implement SCP and evacuate

## 9. Wilderness Water Treatment

**Sources:** CDC, EPA, Wilderness Medical Society (WMS) 2024 Clinical Practice Guidelines.

### Method Comparison Table

| Method | Bacteria | Viruses | Protozoa (Giardia) | Crypto-sporidium | Notes |
|--------|----------|---------|-------------------|------------------|-------|
| **Boiling** | YES | YES | YES | YES | Most reliable single method |
| **Chlorine bleach** | YES | YES | Limited | NO | Must pre-filter for parasites |
| **Iodine** | YES | YES | YES (long contact) | NO | Time-limited use only |
| **Chlorine dioxide** | YES | YES | YES | PARTIAL | Requires hours for Crypto |
| **Filter (0.3 micron)** | YES | NO | YES | YES | Does not remove viruses |
| **Filter (0.1 micron)** | YES | SOME | YES | YES | Depends on filter type |
| **Reverse osmosis** | YES | YES | YES | YES | Also removes salt |
| **UV light** | YES | YES | YES | YES | Requires clear water |

### Boiling

**The single most reliable method for killing all waterborne pathogens.**

- Bring water to a full **rolling boil for 1 minute**
- At elevations **above 6,500 feet (2,000 m): boil for 3 minutes**
- Water does not need to reach 100 degrees C to kill pathogens; pasteurization occurs at 60 degrees C (140 degrees F) for 30 minutes
- At 4,900 m (16,000 ft), water boils at approximately 83 degrees C (182 degrees F) - still sufficient for pathogen inactivation
- Allow water to cool naturally; do not add ice

### Chemical Disinfection: Chlorine Bleach

**Use only regular, unscented household chlorine bleach.**

| Bleach Concentration | Dosage per Gallon | Dosage per Liter |
|---------------------|-------------------|------------------|
| 6% sodium hypochlorite | 8 drops | ~2 drops |
| 8.25% sodium hypochlorite | 6 drops | ~1.5 drops |

- **Double the dose** if water is cloudy, colored, or very cold
- **Contact time:** Minimum 30 minutes
- After 30 minutes, water should smell faintly of chlorine; if not, repeat the dose and wait another 30 minutes
- Effective against bacteria and viruses; limited against parasites

### Chemical Disinfection: Iodine

**2% Tincture of Iodine:**
- **5 drops per quart/liter** of clear water
- **10 drops per quart/liter** of cloudy or colored water
- **Contact time:** At least 30 minutes (longer in cold water)
- Stir thoroughly and let stand

**Safety Restrictions - Do NOT use iodine if:**
- Pregnant (risk of neonatal goiter)
- Thyroid disease (even if controlled on medication)
- Known iodine hypersensitivity
- Strong family history of thyroid disease
- From country with chronic iodine deficiency
- **Do not use iodine for water treatment for more than a few weeks** (limit to 1 month maximum)

### Chemical Disinfection: Chlorine Dioxide (ClO2)

- Available as tablets (e.g., Aquamira, Katadyn Micropur) or liquid drops
- More potent than equivalent doses of chlorine
- **Can kill Giardia** at practical doses and contact times
- **Partially effective against Cryptosporidium** (requires several hours of contact time)
- Follow manufacturer instructions for dosage and contact time
- Simple to use; good option when boiling is not feasible

### Filtration

| Filter Type | Pore Size | Removes |
|-------------|-----------|---------|
| Standard portable | ~1 micron | Parasites only |
| NSF 53 or 58 certified | Varies | Parasites (cyst reduction) |
| Fine portable | 0.3 micron or smaller | Bacteria + parasites (NOT viruses) |
| Reverse osmosis | Molecular | All pathogens + salt |

- Filters require maintenance; check flow rate for clogging
- Pre-filter turbid water through a cloth or coffee filter before chemical treatment

### UV Light Treatment

- Portable UV devices (e.g., SteriPEN) kill parasites, bacteria, and viruses
- Works only in **clear water** - particles block UV penetration
- Pre-filter cloudy water before UV treatment
- Effective for small quantities of water
- Requires batteries; not suitable for large volumes

### Best Practice: Combination Treatment

When boiling is not feasible, the most effective approach combines:
1. **Filtration** or coagulation-flocculation (removes turbidity, parasites, and some bacteria)
2. **Followed by chemical disinfection** (kills bacteria and viruses)

### Improving Taste of Chemically Treated Water

- Run through a granular activated carbon (GAC) filter
- Add a pinch of powdered ascorbic acid (vitamin C)
- Add 5-10 drops of 3% hydrogen peroxide per quart/liter
- Pour between containers to aerate

### Key Factors Affecting Chemical Disinfection

1. **Concentration** of disinfectant (primary)
2. **Contact time** (primary)
3. **Temperature** (secondary) - cold water requires longer contact time
4. **Turbidity** (secondary) - cloudy water requires higher dose; pre-filter when possible

## 10. Blister Care, Hypothermia, Heat Stroke

**Sources:** WMS Clinical Practice Guidelines, NOLS Wilderness Medicine (6th Edition), WFR curricula.

### 10A. Blister Prevention & Treatment

#### Prevention

- **Properly fitted footwear** is the #1 preventive measure
- Wear a thin liner sock under hiking socks to reduce shear
- Use advanced lacing techniques to lock the heel in place
- Pre-tape known problem areas before hiking
- Keep feet dry: remove shoes/socks at rest breaks; carry spare dry socks
- **Treat hot spots immediately** - stopping to address a hot spot prevents blisters

#### Assessment Stages

| Stage | Description | Treatment |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| **Hot spot** | Red, warm, irritated area (pre-blister) | Tape immediately (Leukotape or duct tape) |
| **Intact blister** | Fluid-filled, roof intact | Protect with moleskin donut; do NOT pop unless necessary |
| **Torn blister** | Roof partially torn | Keep roof in place; clean, apply antibiotic ointment, cover |
| **Deroofed blister** | Skin completely removed | Hydrocolloid dressing (do NOT use on intact blisters) |

#### Moleskin Donut Technique

1. Cut a piece of moleskin (or mole foam) 1/2 inch larger than the blister on all sides
2. Cut a hole in the center slightly larger than the blister ("donut" shape)
3. Place the donut around the blister - this creates a pressure-free pocket
4. Cover the entire donut with a second piece of moleskin
5. Secure with duct tape or athletic tape

#### When to Drain a Blister

**Drain only if:**
- Blister is large, tense, and painful
- Blister is likely to rupture on its own during activity
- Pain prevents continued travel

**Draining procedure:**
1. Clean the blister and surrounding skin with antiseptic
2. Sterilize a needle or safety pin (flame or alcohol)
3. Pierce the blister at the base (low point)
4. Gently massage fluid out
5. **Keep the roof (skin) intact** - this protects the underlying tissue
6. Apply antibiotic ointment
7. Cover with adhesive bandage or moleskin pad
8. Monitor for signs of infection (redness, swelling, pus, warmth)

### 10B. Hypothermia

**Source:** WMS Practice Guidelines for Out-of-Hospital Accidental Hypothermia (2024 update).

#### Definition

Accidental hypothermia = unintentional drop in core temperature to 35 degrees C (95 degrees F) or below, caused by net heat loss through conduction, convection, radiation, or evaporation.

#### Classification

| Severity | Core Temp | Key Signs | Shivering |
|----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| **Mild** | 32-35 C (90-95 F) | Alert, shivering, poor coordination, slurred speech, poor judgment | Vigorous |
| **Moderate** | 28-32 C (82-90 F) | Decreased consciousness, loss of shivering, muscle rigidity, slow pulse, slow breathing | Decreasing to absent |
| **Severe** | <28 C (<82 F) | Unresponsive, fixed dilated pupils, very slow/absent pulse, rigid, appears dead | Absent |

#### Field Assessment Decision Tree

```
SUSPECT HYPOTHERMIA
(cold exposure + mental status changes or shivering)
              |
    Can the patient shiver?
           / \
         YES   NO
          |      |
       MILD    Is patient conscious?
    Hypothermia    / \
          |      YES   NO
          |       |      |
          |   MODERATE  SEVERE
          |   Hypothermia  Hypothermia
          |       |      |
    Active    Active    Handle EXTREMELY
    rewarming  rewarming   gently (cardiac
    in field   + evacuate  irritability)
                         + evacuate ASAP
```

#### Treatment by Severity

**All stages:**
1. Remove from cold environment / provide shelter
2. Remove wet clothing; replace with dry
3. Insulate from ground (sleeping pad, branches, pack)
4. Protect from wind
5. Apply vapor barrier (plastic/tarp layer)

**Mild hypothermia (shivering patient):**
- Active external rewarming: warm drinks (no alcohol), calorie-dense food
- Exercise if able (generates body heat)
- Body-to-body warming in sleeping bag
- Heat packs to trunk (armpits, groin, neck) - avoid extremities
- Most patients can be rewarmed in the field

**Moderate hypothermia:**
- Handle gently - rough handling can trigger cardiac dysrhythmia
- Active external rewarming with large heat pads/hot water bottles to trunk
- Forced air warming if available
- Warm IV fluids if available (38 degrees C / 100 degrees F)
- Keep ambient temperature 24-28 degrees C (75-82 degrees F) during transport
- EVACUATE to hospital

**Severe hypothermia:**
- Handle with extreme care - the heart is very irritable
- Check for pulse for at least 60 seconds before starting CPR
- Do NOT start compressions if ANY pulse is detectable (regardless of rate)
- If NO pulse: begin CPR
- Do NOT administer resuscitation medications until core temp >30 degrees C; then double the intervals between doses
- Insulate and evacuate to hospital with rewarming capability (ideally ECMO/cardiopulmonary bypass)
- **"A patient is not dead until warm and dead"** - prolonged resuscitation may be warranted

#### Do NOT Start Resuscitation If:

- Fixed, dilated pupils AND obvious fatal injuries
- Rigor mortis
- Note: Lividity is an unreliable indicator in hypothermic patients - do NOT use as sign of death

#### Afterdrop Warning

Core temperature may continue to fall during initial rewarming as cold blood from the extremities returns to the core. This is called "afterdrop." It can cause cardiac arrest. This is why:
- Rewarm the core FIRST (trunk, not extremities)
- Handle the patient gently
- Avoid vigorous movement or exercise in moderate/severe cases

### 10C. Heat Illness

**Source:** WMS Clinical Practice Guidelines for Heat Illness (2024 update).

#### Classification

| Condition | Core Temp | Mental Status | Key Features |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|--------------|
| **Heat cramps** | Normal | Normal | Involuntary muscle contractions during exertion in heat |
| **Heat exhaustion** | <40 C | Normal (may be fatigued) | Weakness, fatigue, thirst, headache, nausea, dizziness, muscle aches |
| **Heat stroke** | >40 C (104 F) | ALTERED (confusion, seizures, coma) | Medical emergency; can occur without preceding heat exhaustion |

**Two types of heat stroke:**
- **Exertional (EHS):** From strenuous exercise; typically younger, fit individuals
- **Non-exertional (NEHS/classic):** From passive exposure to high ambient temperature; elderly, chronically ill

#### Recognition Decision Tree

```
PERSON IN HOT ENVIRONMENT WITH SYMPTOMS
              |
    Is mental status ALTERED?
    (confusion, disorientation, irritability,
     ataxia, drowsiness, seizure, coma)
           / \
         NO    YES
          |       |
    HEAT           HEAT STROKE
    EXHAUSTION     (EMERGENCY - cool
    (or cramps)     immediately)
          |
    - Move to shade/cool area
    - Remove excess clothing
    - Oral rehydration (drink to thirst)
    - Rest
    - Monitor for progression
```

**Field note:** Since core temperature often cannot be measured in the field, any person with heat stress signs AND altered mental status should be treated as heat stroke.

#### Heat Stroke Treatment Protocol

**Cooling is the PRIORITY. Cool first, transport second.**

**Cooling methods (in order of effectiveness):**

1. **Cold Water Immersion (CWI)** - GOLD STANDARD
   - Immerse patient in cold/ice water up to the neck
   - Protect the airway
   - This is the fastest cooling method available
   - Use the coldest water available

2. **Evaporative Cooling** (if immersion not available)
   - Remove/loosen all clothing
   - Douse patient completely with cold water
   - Fan continuously to maximize evaporation
   - Repeat dousing as water evaporates

3. **Ice Packs** (if only option)
   - Cover the ENTIRE body (not just neck/axilla/groin)
   - **Updated guidance:** Ice packs on cheeks, palms, and soles provide TWICE the cooling rate compared to traditional neck/axilla/groin placement
   - Traditional groin/axilla/neck placement shows no benefit over whole-body coverage

**Target cooling:** 38.3-38.8 degrees C (101-102 degrees F). Monitor temperature to avoid overcooling into hypothermia.

**Hydration:**
- Oral fluids for mild/moderate cases
- IV fluids for heat stroke
- Monitor for exercise-associated hyponatremia (low sodium from overhydration)

**Disposition:**
- EHS patients who return to baseline mental status and mobility within 2 hours may be discharged
- ALL NEHS (classic heat stroke) patients require hospital transfer

#### Prevention

- **Acclimatization:** 10-14 days of successive heat exposure reduces physiologic strain by up to 20%
- **Hydration:** Drink to thirst; even 2-3% body weight loss from dehydration elevates core temperature
- **Environmental monitoring:** Use WBGT (Wet-Bulb Globe Temperature) or heat index to guide activity
- **Screening:** Identify risk factors (elevated BMI, prior heat injury, medications affecting thermoregulation)
- **Work/rest cycles:** Modify activity intensity and duration based on environmental conditions

## 11. Emergency Childbirth

**Sources:** Canadian Red Cross Emergency Care Workbook, American Red Cross (Responding to Emergencies), StatPearls - EMS Prehospital Deliveries, EMT protocols.

### When to Prepare for Field Delivery

Delivery at the scene should be considered when:
- Delivery is imminent (within minutes) - crowning is visible
- Natural disaster, weather, or environment prevents transport to hospital
- Contractions are less than 2 minutes apart
- Mother reports urge to push or feeling of the baby coming

**In all other cases:** Transport to hospital with obstetric capability.

### Three Stages of Labor

| Stage | What Happens | Duration |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Stage 1** | Regular contractions begin; cervix dilates to 10 cm | Hours (variable) |
| **Stage 2** | Full dilation to delivery of the baby | Minutes to 1-2 hours |
| **Stage 3** | Delivery of the placenta (afterbirth) | 5-30 minutes |

### Preparation

1. Call 911 / activate EMS
2. Wash hands, don gloves and PPE (amniotic fluid and blood exposure risk is high)
3. Position mother on her back with knees drawn up and apart
4. Elevate buttocks with blankets or pillow
5. Gather supplies: clean towels, blankets, clamps or clean shoelaces, bulb syringe, plastic bag (for placenta)

### Delivery Protocol

**Step 1: Control the Head**
- When the baby's head crowns (visible at vaginal opening), place palm gently over the occiput (back of head)
- Apply gentle counter-pressure to prevent explosive delivery
- Allow the head to deliver slowly and in a controlled manner
- Do NOT pull on the baby

**Step 2: Check for Nuchal Cord**
- Immediately after head delivers, slide fingers along the baby's neck
- Check for umbilical cord wrapped around the neck
- If cord is loose: gently slip it over the baby's head
- If cord is tight and prevents delivery: double clamp and cut between the clamps

**Step 3: Suction (if needed)**
- Keep baby's head slightly lower to aid fluid drainage
- Suction mouth first, then nose, using a bulb syringe
- Only suction if there is visible obstruction (routine suctioning no longer recommended)

**Step 4: Deliver the Shoulders**
- Place hands on either side of the baby's head (thumbs toward face)
- Guide head gently DOWNWARD to deliver the anterior (top) shoulder
- Then guide gently UPWARD to deliver the posterior (bottom) shoulder
- **Do NOT pull** - pulling can damage spinal nerves (brachial plexus injury)

**Step 5: Deliver the Body**
- The body will follow quickly after the shoulders
- Support the baby as it emerges - the infant will be very slippery
- **Record time of birth**

### Immediate Newborn Care

1. **Dry the baby** immediately and vigorously with clean towels (stimulates breathing)
2. **Wrap in warm blanket** and cover the head (greatest risk = hypothermia and hypoglycemia)
3. **Assess breathing:** If not breathing within 10-15 seconds of stimulation, provide gentle ventilation with infant BVM (just enough to see chest rise)
4. **Rescue breathing rate:** 40-60 breaths per minute
5. **If CPR needed:** Compression-to-ventilation ratio = 3:1 (90 compressions + 30 ventilations per minute)
6. **APGAR scores** at 1 and 5 minutes after delivery

### APGAR Score

| Sign | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|------|---|---|---|
| **A**ppearance (color) | Blue/pale all over | Blue extremities, pink body | Completely pink |
| **P**ulse (heart rate) | Absent | <100 bpm | >100 bpm |
| **G**rimace (reflex) | No response | Grimace | Cry, cough, sneeze |
| **A**ctivity (muscle tone) | Limp | Some flexion | Active movement |
| **R**espiration | Absent | Slow, irregular | Good cry |

Score 7-10 = Normal. Score 4-6 = Moderately depressed, needs assistance. Score 0-3 = Severely depressed, needs immediate resuscitation.

### Umbilical Cord Management

**Delayed Cord Clamping (DCC):** Current evidence supports waiting until the cord stops pulsating (approximately 30-60 seconds) before clamping, except for infants requiring immediate CPR.

**Clamping and Cutting:**
1. Wait until cord stops pulsating (~30-60 seconds)
2. Place first clamp **6 inches (15 cm)** from the baby's body
3. Place second clamp **2-4 inches further** along the cord (toward placenta)
4. Cut between the two clamps ONLY if sterile equipment is available
5. If sterile equipment is not available, leave the cord intact

### Placenta Delivery

- The placenta will typically deliver on its own within 5-30 minutes
- **Do NOT pull on the umbilical cord** - this can cause uterine inversion or tear the placenta, causing severe hemorrhage
- When the placenta delivers, transport it in a plastic bag to the hospital
- If placenta has not delivered within 30 minutes, transport urgently (high bleeding risk)

### Post-Delivery Maternal Care

1. Place baby on mother's chest (skin-to-skin) and encourage breastfeeding (stimulates uterine contractions)
2. Feel for the uterine fundus (firm mass at or below the navel)
3. Massage the fundus in firm circular motions every 5 minutes to keep it firm and minimize bleeding
4. Monitor for postpartum hemorrhage

### Complications

**Shoulder Dystocia (shoulder stuck behind pubic bone):**
- McRoberts maneuver: flex mother's thighs sharply against abdomen
- Apply suprapubic pressure (press above pubic bone toward baby)
- Do NOT apply pressure to the uterine fundus (risk of uterine rupture)
- If unresolved: rapid transport

**Prolapsed Cord (cord delivers before baby):**
- **Do NOT push the cord back in**
- Position mother with hips elevated or in knee-chest position
- Keep cord moist with warm saline-soaked cloth
- Rapid transport

**Breech Presentation (buttocks or feet first):**
- Do NOT attempt to deliver in the field unless no alternative
- Place your fingers in a "V" position around the baby's mouth/nose to maintain airway
- Transport immediately if possible

**Postpartum Hemorrhage:**
- Usually caused by uterine atony (uterus fails to contract)
- Massage uterine fundus firmly in circular motions
- Keep mother warm; monitor for shock
- Rapid transport

**Pre-eclampsia / Eclampsia:**
- Signs: headaches, vision problems, abdominal pain, sudden swelling of hands/feet/face, hypertension
- Eclampsia: seizures (can occur up to 1-2 months post-delivery)
- Place seizing patient on side; protect from injury
- Transport immediately

## 12. Dental First Aid

**Sources:** American Dental Association (ADA), MouthHealthy.org (ADA public resource), ADA Pain Management Guidelines.

### Toothache

1. Rinse mouth with warm water to clean the area
2. Gently use dental floss to remove any food caught between teeth
3. **Do NOT place aspirin on the tooth or gums** (causes chemical burn of tissue)
4. For pain management: NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) are first-line, alone or combined with acetaminophen
5. ADA recommends nonopioid medications for acute dental pain
6. See dentist as soon as possible

### Knocked-Out Permanent (Adult) Tooth

**Time is critical. Act fast.**

1. Pick up the tooth by the **crown** (top) - do NOT touch the root
2. If dirty, rinse the root gently with water - do NOT scrub or remove attached tissue fragments
3. **Try to reinsert** the tooth into the socket:
   - Hold by the crown
   - Gently push into socket
   - Bite down on gauze or cloth to hold in place
4. **If cannot reinsert**, keep the tooth moist at all times:
   - Place between cheek and gums
   - Place in milk
   - Use an ADA-approved tooth preservation kit (Save-a-Tooth)
   - **Do NOT store in water**
5. **Get to a dentist immediately** - best outcomes within 30 minutes

### Knocked-Out Baby (Primary) Tooth

- Find the tooth and keep it moist
- **Do NOT try to reinsert** a baby tooth (risk of damaging developing permanent tooth)
- See dentist to confirm whether entire tooth came out and assess for damage to permanent tooth bud

### Cracked or Broken Tooth

1. Rinse mouth immediately with warm water
2. Apply cold compress to the face to control swelling
3. Save any tooth fragments if possible
4. See dentist as soon as possible

### Broken Jaw

1. Apply cold compresses to control swelling
2. **Do NOT attempt to realign the jaw**
3. Stabilize jaw by wrapping a bandage over the top of the head and under the jaw
4. Go to dentist or hospital emergency department immediately

### Object Stuck Between Teeth

1. Try to gently remove with dental floss
2. **Do NOT use sharp or pointed instruments** (risk of infection and tissue damage)
3. If cannot remove, see dentist

### Bitten Tongue or Lip

1. Clean the area gently with a clean cloth
2. Apply cold compress to reduce swelling
3. If bleeding is excessive or does not stop, go to dentist or emergency department

### Dental First Aid Kit Additions

- Dental floss
- Temporary filling material (available at pharmacies)
- Oil of cloves / eugenol (temporary toothache relief)
- Save-a-Tooth emergency tooth preservation kit
- Dental wax (for broken braces/wires)
- Ibuprofen and acetaminophen
- Small mirror

### Prevention

- Wear a mouthguard during sports and recreational activities with mouth injury risk
- Avoid chewing ice, popcorn kernels, and hard candy (can crack teeth)
- Do not use teeth as tools (opening bottles, tearing packages)

## Quick Reference: Key Decision Points

### "Big Three" Killers in Trauma (Preventable Deaths)

1. **Massive hemorrhage** - Stop the bleeding (tourniquet, packing, direct pressure)
2. **Airway obstruction** - Open and maintain the airway
3. **Tension pneumothorax** - Decompress (needle or burp chest seal)

### When to Evacuate (Wilderness Setting)

**Evacuate IMMEDIATELY (life threat):**
- Airway compromise not resolved by basic maneuvers
- Difficulty breathing / respiratory distress
- Signs of shock that do not improve
- Altered mental status (not improving)
- Chest pain suggesting cardiac event
- Severe allergic reaction / anaphylaxis
- Suspected stroke (FAST: Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call)
- Femur fracture
- Suspected spinal cord injury with deficits
- Heat stroke
- Severe hypothermia
- Major burns (>20% TBSA, or face/hands/feet/genitalia)
- Signs of internal bleeding

**Evacuate (urgent but may not be immediate):**
- Open fractures
- Dislocations that cannot be reduced
- Wounds requiring surgical repair
- Signs of wound infection spreading
- Moderate hypothermia not responding to field rewarming
- Abdominal pain with rigidity or guarding
- Pregnancy complications
- Dental avulsion (within 30 min for best outcome)

### Universal Patient Approach Checklist

1. Scene safety / BSI
2. Primary Survey (ABCDE) - treat life threats immediately
3. Secondary Survey (head-to-toe exam + SAMPLE history + vitals)
4. Document (SOAP note)
5. Plan (treatment + evacuation decision)
6. Monitor (reassess vitals every 5-15 minutes)
7. Communicate (SOAP note to incoming EMS / hospital)
