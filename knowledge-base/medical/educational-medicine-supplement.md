## PART 1: HUMAN ANATOMY ESSENTIALS FOR EMERGENCY CARE

### The Cardiovascular System

**Heart Anatomy**
The heart is a four-chambered muscular pump:
- **Right atrium** → receives deoxygenated blood from body via superior/inferior vena cava
- **Right ventricle** → pumps blood to lungs via pulmonary artery
- **Left atrium** → receives oxygenated blood from lungs via pulmonary veins
- **Left ventricle** → pumps oxygenated blood to body via aorta (thickest wall — highest pressure)

**Cardiac Output**: Heart Rate × Stroke Volume = ~5 L/min at rest
- Normal HR: 60–100 bpm
- Stroke Volume: ~70 mL per beat
- Increases 4-5x during vigorous exercise

**Blood Pressure Physiology**
- Systolic (top number): pressure when heart contracts — normal 90–120 mmHg
- Diastolic (bottom number): pressure when heart relaxes — normal 60–80 mmHg
- Pulse pressure = Systolic − Diastolic (normally ~40 mmHg)
- Mean Arterial Pressure (MAP) = Diastolic + (Pulse pressure/3); minimum 60 mmHg needed for organ perfusion

**Shock Physiology** (critical for field care)
Shock = inadequate tissue perfusion. Types:
1. **Hypovolemic**: blood/fluid loss → tachycardia, weak pulse, pale/cool skin, decreased urine output
2. **Distributive (Septic)**: vasodilation from infection → warm flushed skin early, then cold collapse
3. **Cardiogenic**: pump failure → dyspnea, pulmonary edema, JVD
4. **Obstructive**: tamponade or tension pneumothorax → Beck's triad (JVD, muffled heart sounds, hypotension) for tamponade

**Shock Stages**:
- Class I: <750 mL lost, HR <100, BP normal, mild anxiety
- Class II: 750–1500 mL, HR 100–120, decreased pulse pressure, moderate anxiety
- Class III: 1500–2000 mL, HR 120–140, decreased BP, confused
- Class IV: >2000 mL, HR >140, BP very low, lethargic — life-threatening

### The Respiratory System

**Anatomy**
- **Upper airway**: nasopharynx, oropharynx, larynx
- **Lower airway**: trachea → bronchi → bronchioles → alveoli
- **Alveoli**: ~300 million; surface area ~70 m²; gas exchange site
- **Diaphragm**: primary muscle of respiration (C3,4,5 "keeps the diaphragm alive")

**Normal Values**
- Respiratory Rate: 12–20 breaths/min (adults); 20–30 (children); 30–60 (infants)
- Tidal Volume: ~500 mL per breath
- Minute Ventilation: RR × TV = ~7–8 L/min at rest
- Oxygen saturation (SpO2): 95–100% normal; <90% hypoxia; <80% severe

**Oxygenation vs Ventilation**
- **Oxygenation**: O2 getting into blood (assessed by SpO2 or PaO2)
- **Ventilation**: CO2 removal (assessed by RR and breath depth)
- Can have good O2 but poor CO2 removal (slow breathing rate)

**Lung Sounds** (auscultation — place stethoscope on back/sides)
- Normal vesicular: soft, rustling
- Crackles (rales): fluid in alveoli → pneumonia, CHF
- Wheezes: narrowed airways → asthma, COPD
- Rhonchi: mucus in larger airways → clear with cough
- Absent: pneumothorax or hemothorax on affected side
- Stridor: upper airway obstruction — EMERGENCY

**Pneumothorax Field Management**
- Simple: oxygen, observe
- Tension (life-threatening): tracheal deviation AWAY from injury, absent breath sounds, hypotension, JVD
  - Needle decompression: 2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line, 14-16g needle
  - Then chest seal with flutter valve or improvised (plastic wrapper sealed 3 sides)

### The Nervous System

**Brain Regions and Function**
- Frontal lobe: executive function, motor control, personality
- Parietal lobe: sensory processing, spatial awareness
- Temporal lobe: memory, language comprehension, hearing
- Occipital lobe: vision
- Cerebellum: coordination, balance
- Brainstem (medulla/pons/midbrain): breathing, heart rate, consciousness — VITAL

**Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)**
| Component | Score | Description |
|-----------|-------|-------------|
| Eye Opening | 4 | Spontaneous |
| | 3 | To voice |
| | 2 | To pain |
| | 1 | None |
| Verbal | 5 | Oriented |
| | 4 | Confused |
| | 3 | Words only |
| | 2 | Sounds only |
| | 1 | None |
| Motor | 6 | Follows commands |
| | 5 | Localizes pain |
| | 4 | Withdraws |
| | 3 | Flexion (decorticate) |
| | 2 | Extension (decerebrate) |
| | 1 | None |

- **GCS 13–15**: Minor TBI
- **GCS 9–12**: Moderate TBI
- **GCS ≤8**: Severe TBI — intubation generally indicated; "GCS of 8, intubate"

**Pupil Assessment**
- PERRL: Pupils Equal, Round, Reactive to Light — normal
- Unequal pupils (anisocoria >1mm): herniation, CN III palsy, trauma
- Blown (fixed dilated) pupil: brainstem herniation — critical
- Pinpoint pupils: opioid toxicity, pontine lesion

**Dermatomes** (key for spinal injury assessment)
- C5: lateral arm
- C6: thumb
- C7: middle finger
- C8: little finger
- T4: nipple line
- T10: umbilicus
- L1: inguinal ligament
- L4: medial knee
- L5: great toe
- S1: lateral foot

## PART 2: PATHOPHYSIOLOGY — UNDERSTANDING DISEASE IN THE FIELD

### Infection and Sepsis

**The Infection Cascade**
1. Pathogen entry (bacteria, virus, fungi, parasite)
2. Local inflammatory response (redness, swelling, heat, pain)
3. Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome (SIRS) if not contained
4. Sepsis = infection + organ dysfunction
5. Septic shock = sepsis + vasodilation + hypotension despite fluids

**SIRS Criteria** (2 or more = SIRS):
- Temp >38°C or <36°C
- HR >90 bpm
- RR >20 or PaCO2 <32
- WBC >12,000 or <4,000 or >10% bands

**Sepsis Recognition in the Field** (qSOFA — score ≥2 = high risk):
- Altered mental status (GCS <15)
- RR ≥22
- Systolic BP ≤100

**Wound Infection Progression Timeline**:
- Day 1–2: Normal inflammatory response (redness, swelling) — acceptable
- Day 3–5: Increasing pain, purulent discharge, wound edges separating = INFECTION
- Day 5–7: Cellulitis spreading (red streaks), fever = SYSTEMIC spread
- >Day 7 untreated: Bacteremia, sepsis possible

**Wound Care Principles (Evidence-Based)**:
1. Irrigation is the most important intervention: 200-500 mL normal saline or clean water under pressure
2. Debridement: remove devitalized tissue
3. Leave contaminated wounds open or loosely closed
4. Clean wounds closed within 6-8 hours (golden period) — hands/feet 4 hours
5. Puncture wounds: never close primarily, high infection risk

**Antibiotic Selection for Common Field Infections**:
| Infection | First Choice | Alternative |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Skin/soft tissue | Amoxicillin-clavulanate | Clindamycin |
| Deep wound | Amoxicillin-clavulanate + Metronidazole | Clindamycin |
| UTI | Trimethoprim-Sulfamethoxazole | Ciprofloxacin |
| Respiratory | Amoxicillin | Azithromycin |
| Diarrhea (bacterial) | Azithromycin | Ciprofloxacin |
| Abscess drainage | Drainage is primary treatment | TMP-SMX if cellulitis |

**Tetanus Prophylaxis**:
- All puncture wounds, soil contamination, animal bites
- If vaccinated (<5 years): nothing needed
- If vaccinated (5–10 years): booster if dirty wound
- If vaccinated (>10 years): booster always
- Unknown/unvaccinated: TIG (tetanus immune globulin) + begin series

### Trauma Physiology

**Hemorrhage Control Priority (MARCH protocol)**:
- **M**assive hemorrhage: tourniquet, wound packing, pressure
- **A**irway: position, jaw thrust, airway adjunct
- **R**espiration: seal chest wounds, needle decompression
- **C**irculation: IV access, fluids
- **H**ypothermia/Head injury: keep warm, monitor neuro

**Tourniquet Application**:
- Apply 2–3 inches proximal to wound
- Tighten until bleeding stops (not just "tight")
- Note time applied
- Do NOT remove in field — reassess in hospital
- Limb ischemia: effective tourniquet causes significant pain — expected
- Time limits: 2 hours arterial (safe limit commonly cited), though cases up to 6 hours documented with limb salvage

**Wound Packing Technique**:
1. Expose wound completely
2. Apply hemostatic gauze (Combat Gauze, QuikClot) into deepest part of wound
3. Pack firmly — fill dead space
4. Apply direct pressure for 3 minutes minimum
5. Do NOT remove packing in field

**Burns Assessment**:
- **Rule of Nines** (Adult):
  - Head: 9%
  - Each arm: 9%
  - Chest anterior: 9%, posterior: 9%
  - Abdomen anterior: 9%, posterior: 9%
  - Each leg: 18% (anterior 9%, posterior 9%)
  - Genitalia: 1%
  - Total = 100%

- **Burn Depth**:
  - Superficial (1st degree): red, no blisters, painful — sunburn
  - Partial thickness (2nd degree): blisters, very painful, moist
  - Full thickness (3rd degree): leathery, white/black, painless (nerves destroyed)

- **Parkland Formula** for fluid resuscitation: 4 mL × kg × %TBSA burned
  - Give half in first 8 hours, half in next 16 hours
  - Use Lactated Ringer's preferred
  - Watch urine output: target 0.5 mL/kg/hr (adult) or 1 mL/kg/hr (child)

- **Burns Requiring Evacuation**:
  - >10% TBSA partial or full thickness
  - Any full thickness burn
  - Burns to face, hands, feet, genitalia, joints
  - Circumferential burns
  - Inhalation injury (singed nasal hair, carbonaceous sputum, hoarse voice)

### Environmental Emergencies

**Heat Illness Spectrum**:

**Heat Cramps**: Painful muscle spasms, usually calves/abdomen
- Cause: sodium depletion from sweat
- Tx: rest, oral hydration with electrolytes, stretch

**Heat Exhaustion**: Profuse sweating, weakness, headache, nausea, cool/pale skin
- Core temp usually <40°C (104°F)
- Normal mental status (distinguishes from heat stroke)
- Tx: move to cool environment, oral/IV fluids, cool cloths

**Heat Stroke** (EMERGENCY): Core temp >40°C + altered mental status
- Classic: elderly, no sweating, insidious onset
- Exertional: young athletes, may still sweat
- Tx: IMMEDIATE cooling — cold water immersion most effective, ice packs to neck/axilla/groin, fan with misting
- Target: reduce temp to <39°C within 30 minutes
- Mortality 10–50% — brain damage starts at >41°C

**Hypothermia Stages**:
| Temp | Stage | Findings |
|------|-------|---------|
| 32–35°C (90–95°F) | Mild | Shivering, impaired coordination |
| 28–32°C (82–90°F) | Moderate | Shivering stops, confusion, bradycardia |
| 20–28°C (68–82°F) | Severe | Unconscious, arrhythmias, rigid |
| <20°C (<68°F) | Profound | Apparent death, cardiac arrest |

**Hypothermia Rewarming**:
- Mild: passive rewarming (warm environment, dry insulation)
- Moderate: active external rewarming (warm blankets, heat packs to groin/axilla, NOT directly on skin)
- Severe: active internal rewarming (warm IV fluids, warmed humidified O2)
- "Not dead until warm and dead" — CPR and rewarm before pronouncing death
- Avoid rough movement (can precipitate V-fib)

**Frostbite Management**:
- Do NOT rewarm if refreezing possible — walking on thawed feet = massive tissue damage
- Thaw in 40–42°C water (warm, not hot) — takes 30–60 min
- Expect significant pain — pain = blood returning = good
- Blisters: leave intact unless blood-filled
- Do NOT rub, break blisters, use dry heat

**Drowning Physiology**:
- Wet drowning: laryngospasm relaxes, water enters lungs
- Outcomes depend on time submerged, water temp (cold = protective)
- Cold water drowning in children: successful resuscitation after 20+ min documented
- Begin CPR immediately for any submersion victim
- Rescue breath first — victims need O2 before compressions usually

**Altitude Illness**:
- Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS): headache + one of (nausea, fatigue, dizziness) at altitude
  - Tx: stop ascent, descend if not improving, ibuprofen, acetazolamide 250 mg BID
- High Altitude Pulmonary Edema (HAPE): cough, dyspnea at rest, cyanosis
  - Tx: IMMEDIATE descent, nifedipine 10 mg PO if available, supplemental O2
- High Altitude Cerebral Edema (HACE): AMS + ataxia or confusion
  - Tx: IMMEDIATE descent, dexamethasone 8 mg initial then 4 mg q6h

## PART 3: CLINICAL ASSESSMENT SKILLS

### Systematic Patient Assessment

**Primary Survey (ABCDE)**:
- **A**irway: open? Patent? Position
- **B**reathing: rate, depth, bilateral equal, O2 sat
- **C**irculation: pulse rate/quality, BP, skin color/temp/moisture, bleeding
- **D**isability: GCS, pupils, grip strength, sensation
- **E**xposure: undress fully to assess, prevent hypothermia

**Secondary Survey** (after life threats addressed):
- Head-to-toe physical exam
- History: SAMPLE (Symptoms, Allergies, Medications, Past history, Last meal, Events)
- OPQRST for pain: Onset, Provocation/Palliation, Quality, Radiation, Severity 0-10, Time

**Vital Sign Normal Values by Age**:
| Age | HR | RR | Systolic BP |
|-----|----|----|-------------|
| Infant (0–1 yr) | 100–160 | 30–60 | 70–90 |
| Toddler (1–3) | 90–150 | 24–40 | 80–100 |
| School-age (6–12) | 70–120 | 18–30 | 90–110 |
| Adolescent (12–18) | 60–100 | 12–16 | 100–120 |
| Adult | 60–100 | 12–20 | 110–130 |

**Pediatric Assessment Triangle**:
1. Appearance (TICLS): Tone, Interactability, Consolability, Look/gaze, Speech/cry
2. Work of Breathing: retractions, nasal flaring, head bobbing, abnormal sounds
3. Circulation to skin: pallor, mottling, cyanosis

**Shock Recognition in Children**:
- Tachycardia is FIRST sign (hypotension is LATE — children compensate well until decompensation)
- Capillary refill >2 sec, pale/mottled skin, altered mental status
- When BP drops in child = IMMINENT cardiovascular collapse

### Neurological Examination

**Mental Status Assessment**:
- Orientation: person, place, time, situation (O×4)
- Immediate recall: repeat 3 objects
- Short-term memory: recall 3 objects after 5 minutes
- Language: name objects, follow 3-step command
- Calculation: serial 7s (100-7-7-7...)

**Cranial Nerve Quick Assessment**:
- CN II: vision, pupil light reflex
- CN III, IV, VI: extraocular movements (follow finger H-pattern)
- CN V: facial sensation (forehead, cheek, jaw)
- CN VII: facial symmetry (raise brows, smile, puff cheeks)
- CN VIII: hearing (finger rub at ear)
- CN IX/X: palate elevation ("aah"), gag reflex
- CN XII: tongue protrusion (deviates toward lesion)

**Cerebellar Assessment**:
- Finger-nose test: touch nose then examiner finger, repeat
- Rapid alternating movements: supinate/pronate hand rapidly
- Heel-shin test: place heel on opposite knee, slide down shin
- Romberg: stand feet together eyes open (pass), then eyes closed (fail = proprioception problem)

### Abdominal Assessment

**Peritoneal Signs** (indicate surgical emergency):
- Guarding: voluntary/involuntary muscle tension over painful area
- Rigidity: board-like abdomen
- Rebound tenderness: pain WORSE when pressure RELEASED (Blumberg sign)
- Rovsing's sign: RLQ pain when LLQ pressed → appendicitis
- Psoas sign: pain with hip extension → retrocecal appendicitis
- Obturator sign: pain with internal hip rotation → pelvic appendicitis

**Abdominal Quadrant Pain Differential**:
| Location | Consider |
|----------|---------|
| RUQ | Liver, gallbladder, right kidney |
| LUQ | Spleen, stomach, left kidney |
| RLQ | Appendix, ovary (female), hernia |
| LLQ | Sigmoid colon, diverticulitis, ovary |
| Epigastric | Stomach, pancreas, heart (referred) |
| Periumbilical | Early appendicitis, small bowel |
| Suprapubic | Bladder, uterus |

## PART 4: FIELD PHARMACOLOGY BASICS

### Pharmacokinetics (How Drugs Work in the Body)

**ADME Principles**:
- **Absorption**: How drug gets into bloodstream (IV=100%, IM~85%, PO varies)
- **Distribution**: How drug spreads through body (volume of distribution)
- **Metabolism**: Primarily liver (cytochrome P450 enzymes)
- **Excretion**: Primarily kidneys (dose-adjust in renal failure)

**Routes of Administration** (field context):
- **IV** (intravenous): fastest, 100% bioavailability, requires access
- **IO** (intraosseous): nearly equivalent to IV, any drug can be given; insert into proximal tibia or humeral head with EZ-IO drill; all ages including adults
- **IM** (intramuscular): onset 15–30 min; thigh (vastus lateralis) or deltoid
- **SC** (subcutaneous): slower than IM; insulin, epinephrine
- **SL** (sublingual): rapid absorption bypasses first-pass; nitroglycerin, buprenorphine
- **PR** (rectal): useful when unable to take oral; diazepam, ondansetron
- **IN** (intranasal): rapid CNS delivery; naloxone, midazolam, fentanyl (mucosal atomizer)
- **Inhalation**: fastest delivery to lungs; albuterol, volatile anesthetics
- **Topical/Transdermal**: local or systemic through skin; fentanyl patches, lidocaine gel

**Half-Life and Dosing**:
- Half-life (t½): time for plasma concentration to decrease by 50%
- After 4-5 half-lives: drug is essentially eliminated (~97%)
- Steady state achieved after 4-5 half-lives of regular dosing
- Example: Amoxicillin t½ = 1 hour → dosing q8h maintains therapeutic levels
- Example: Fluconazole t½ = 30 hours → once daily dosing adequate

**Renally Cleared Drugs** (reduce dose in kidney failure):
- Aminoglycosides, vancomycin (monitor levels)
- Many antibiotics: ciprofloxacin, TMP-SMX, cephalosporins
- Digoxin, gabapentin, metformin (STOP in severe renal failure — lactic acidosis risk)

**Hepatically Cleared Drugs** (caution in liver failure):
- Most benzodiazepines
- Opioids (accumulate, use lower doses)
- Warfarin (dose requirements decrease significantly)

### Pain Management in the Field

**Pain Assessment**:
- Numeric Rating Scale: 0–10 (0=none, 10=worst imaginable)
- FACES scale (children, altered mental status)
- Behavioral indicators: guarding, grimacing, tachycardia, diaphoresis

**WHO Pain Ladder**:
1. Non-opioid: NSAID/acetaminophen for mild pain
2. Weak opioid + non-opioid: tramadol, codeine for moderate pain
3. Strong opioid: morphine, oxycodone for severe pain
- Always add adjuvant as needed (antispasmodics, anti-anxiolytics)

**NSAID vs Acetaminophen**:
| Property | NSAIDs | Acetaminophen |
|----------|--------|---------------|
| Anti-inflammatory | Yes | No |
| Antipyretic | Yes | Yes |
| Analgesic | Yes | Yes |
| GI risk | Yes | No |
| Renal risk | Yes (especially dehydrated) | No |
| Hepatic risk | No | Yes (>3g/day or alcohol use) |
| Use in bleeding | Avoid | OK |
| Use in fever | Good (+ anti-inflammatory) | Good |

**Opioid Dosing Reference** (adult, opioid-naive):
| Drug | Route | Dose | Interval |
|------|-------|------|----------|
| Morphine | IV | 2–4 mg | q4h PRN |
| Morphine | PO | 5–15 mg | q4h PRN |
| Oxycodone | PO | 5–10 mg | q4-6h PRN |
| Hydromorphone | IV | 0.5–1 mg | q4h PRN |
| Fentanyl | IN | 1.5–2 mcg/kg | q30min PRN |
| Tramadol | PO | 50–100 mg | q6–8h PRN |

**Opioid Overdose Recognition and Treatment**:
- Triad: decreased LOC, respiratory depression (<10/min), miosis (pinpoint pupils)
- Naloxone: 0.4–0.8 mg IV/IM/SC/IN — repeat q2–3 min up to 10 mg
- IN route: 4 mg in 0.1 mL per nostril (1 mg/0.1 mL concentration)
- Half-life of naloxone (30–90 min) < most opioids — may need repeat doses or infusion
- Re-sedation risk — observe 4+ hours after last naloxone dose

### Antibiotic Stewardship in Resource-Limited Settings

**Principles**:
1. Treat the most likely pathogen based on infection site
2. Use narrowest effective spectrum
3. Know local resistance patterns (if available)
4. Complete course — stopping early selects resistant organisms
5. Viral infections: antibiotics are USELESS and cause harm (antibiotic-associated diarrhea, C. diff)

**Viral vs Bacterial Infection Clues**:
| Feature | Viral | Bacterial |
|---------|-------|----------|
| Onset | Gradual | Often abrupt |
| Fever | Usually low-grade | Often higher |
| Cough | Dry, gradual | Often productive |
| Exudate | Absent | Common (throat, wound) |
| Myalgias | Prominent | Less common |
| Duration | Self-limited 5–14 days | Progressive without treatment |

**Antibiotic Duration Evidence**:
- Strep throat: 10 days (penicillin), 5 days (azithromycin)
- Uncomplicated UTI: 3–7 days
- Skin infection (mild cellulitis): 5–7 days
- Pneumonia (community-acquired): 5–7 days
- Ear infection (otitis media): 5–7 days adults, 10 days young children
- Longer courses often not superior and increase resistance/side effects

## PART 5: CHILDBIRTH AND OBSTETRIC EMERGENCIES

### Normal Labor and Delivery

**Stages of Labor**:
- **Stage 1** (Early): Contractions every 5–30 min, cervix 0–6 cm; can last 8–12+ hours (nullipara)
- **Stage 1** (Active): Contractions q2–5 min, 60-90 sec duration, cervix 6–10 cm; ~1 cm/hr
- **Stage 2**: Fully dilated to delivery; pushing phase; usually <2 hours
- **Stage 3**: Delivery of placenta; usually within 30 min

**Normal Delivery Sequence**:
1. Head crowns at vaginal opening
2. Restitution: head rotates to face thigh after delivery
3. External rotation: head aligns with fetal shoulders
4. Anterior shoulder delivers first (gentle downward traction)
5. Posterior shoulder delivers (upward traction)
6. Body delivers rapidly — support, don't pull

**Immediate Newborn Care**:
1. Note time of delivery
2. Dry and stimulate (rub back/feet vigorously)
3. Assess: tone, breathing, color, heart rate
4. Suction only if visible secretions obstructing airway
5. Warmth — wet newborns lose heat rapidly (kangaroo care or blankets)
6. Delayed cord clamping: wait 30–60 sec minimum before clamping (improves iron stores)
7. Cord: clamp 3-4 cm from umbilicus, cut between clamps

**APGAR Score** (at 1 and 5 minutes):
| Sign | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|------|---|---|---|
| Appearance | Blue/pale | Body pink, extremities blue | All pink |
| Pulse | Absent | <100 | ≥100 |
| Grimace | No response | Grimace | Cry/cough |
| Activity | Limp | Some flexion | Active motion |
| Respiration | Absent | Weak/irregular | Strong cry |

- 7–10: Normal
- 4–6: Moderate depression, stimulate
- 0–3: Severe depression, begin resuscitation

**Newborn Resuscitation** (NRP):
1. Dry, stimulate, reposition airway
2. If HR <100 or apneic: positive pressure ventilation (PPV) 40–60 breaths/min
3. If HR <60 despite 30 sec PPV: chest compressions 3:1 ratio
4. Epinephrine if HR <60 despite CPR: 0.01–0.03 mg/kg IV/IO

### Obstetric Emergencies

**Postpartum Hemorrhage** (most common cause of maternal death worldwide):
- Definition: >500 mL blood loss vaginal delivery, >1000 mL cesarean
- 4 Ts: Tone (uterine atony 80%), Tissue (retained placenta), Trauma (lacerations), Thrombin (coagulopathy)

**Uterine Massage** (primary maneuver for atony):
- Bimanual compression: one hand on abdomen over fundus, other hand vaginal
- Massage firmly and continuously
- Normal fundus should be firm as a grapefruit at level of umbilicus after delivery

**Medications for PPH**:
- **Oxytocin** (first line): 10 units IM immediately after delivery; prevents 60% of PPH
- **Misoprostol**: 600–800 mcg sublingual; used when oxytocin unavailable; oral/sublingual only
- **Tranexamic acid** (TXA): 1g IV over 10 min, repeat in 30 min if needed; reduces mortality if given within 3 hours of delivery

**Shoulder Dystocia** (fetal shoulder impacted behind pubic bone):
- HELPERR mnemonic:
  - **H**elp: call for assistance
  - **E**pisiotomy: if needed for maneuvers
  - **L**egs (McRoberts): flex maternal thighs to abdomen
  - **P**ressure (suprapubic): firm downward pressure on pubic bone (not fundal)
  - **E**nter (Rubin/Woods): rotate posterior shoulder
  - **R**emove posterior arm
  - **R**oll patient to all-fours position

**Eclampsia** (seizures in pregnancy + hypertension):
- Magnesium sulfate: 4–6 g IV over 20 min, then 2 g/hr infusion
- Monitor: respiratory rate (>12), urine output (>25 mL/hr), DTRs (must be present)
- Antidote for Mg toxicity: Calcium gluconate 1g IV over 10 min
- Hydralazine or labetalol for BP control if >160/110

## PART 6: MENTAL HEALTH FIRST AID IN CRISIS

### Psychological First Aid (PFA)

**Core Principles** (WHO/Red Cross framework):
1. **Look**: observe for safety, distress, basic needs
2. **Listen**: give calm, non-judgmental attention; don't force talking
3. **Link**: connect to information, services, social supports

**What NOT to Do**:
- Don't pressure people to share their story
- Don't judge their reactions ("you should be grateful")
- Don't make promises you can't keep
- Don't tell people how they are/should be feeling
- Don't force assistance on those who clearly don't want it

**Acute Stress Reactions** (normal responses to abnormal events):
- Shock, disbelief, numbness
- Crying, grief, sadness
- Anxiety, fear, hypervigilance
- Anger, irritability
- Guilt (survivor guilt)
- Physical symptoms: fatigue, sleep disturbance, appetite changes
- These are NORMAL — not pathological — in first weeks after trauma

**When to Suspect PTSD** (>1 month after trauma):
- Intrusive memories/nightmares/flashbacks
- Avoidance of reminders
- Negative mood/cognitions
- Hyperarousal (startle, sleep disturbance, anger)
- Significant functional impairment

**Crisis Intervention — SAFER-R Model**:
- **S**tabilize
- **A**cknowledge the crisis
- **F**acilitate understanding
- **E**ncourage coping
- **R**estore functioning or **R**efer

### Suicide Risk Assessment

**Risk Factors** (SAD PERSONS mnemonic):
- S: Sex (male higher completion rate)
- A: Age (adolescent, elderly)
- D: Depression
- P: Previous attempt (strongest predictor)
- E: Ethanol use
- R: Rational thinking loss (psychosis)
- S: Social support lacking
- O: Organized plan
- N: No spouse/partner
- S: Sickness/illness

**Safety Planning Elements**:
1. Warning signs to watch for
2. Internal coping strategies (what I can do alone)
3. Social contacts who can distract (no disclosure needed)
4. People to ask for help
5. Mental health professionals/crisis lines
6. Means restriction (remove access to lethal means)

**Crisis Resource**: National Suicide Prevention Lifeline — 988 (when phones available)
