# Wound Care and Infection Prevention

## Wound Classification

### By Cleanliness
- **Clean**: Surgical or fresh wounds with minimal contamination (<6 hours old).
- **Clean-contaminated**: Wounds with minor contamination, treated within 6-12 hours.
- **Contaminated**: Wounds with significant contamination, soil, foreign bodies, or >12 hours old.
- **Dirty/Infected**: Wounds with visible infection, pus, dead tissue, or >24 hours old.

### By Mechanism
- **Lacerations**: Irregular cuts from sharp objects. Bleed freely (helps clean).
- **Puncture wounds**: Small entry, deep penetration. HIGH infection risk (anaerobic environment).
- **Abrasions**: Skin scrapes. Painful but usually superficial.
- **Avulsions**: Tissue torn away. May need surgical repair.
- **Crush injuries**: Tissue damage from compression. Watch for compartment syndrome.

## Wound Cleaning

### Irrigation
The single most important step in wound care is thorough irrigation.

1. Use clean water (boiled and cooled, or purified) or normal saline (0.9%).
2. Make normal saline: 1 teaspoon (5g) salt per 1 liter of boiled water. Cool before use.
3. Irrigate under pressure: use a syringe with an 18-gauge needle or a squeezable bottle with a small hole.
4. Target pressure: 5-8 PSI. A 30-60mL syringe with an 18ga needle achieves this.
5. Use at least 500mL for small wounds, 1-2 liters for contaminated wounds.
6. Irrigate away from yourself and bystanders.

### Debridement
Remove dead tissue and foreign material:
1. Remove all visible debris with sterile tweezers.
2. Trim clearly dead (black, gray) tissue with sterile scissors.
3. Leave questionable tissue — it may recover.
4. Be aggressive with contaminated wound debridement — infection kills more than tissue loss.

## Wound Closure

### When to Close
- Clean wounds less than 6-8 hours old (12 hours for face/scalp due to good blood supply).
- After thorough irrigation and debridement.

### When NOT to Close
- Contaminated wounds or wounds >12 hours old (except face).
- Puncture wounds.
- Animal or human bites.
- Wounds with retained foreign bodies.
- Signs of infection already present.
- Leave these wounds open — pack with moist sterile gauze and allow healing by secondary intention.

### Closure Methods

**Adhesive Strips (Steri-Strips / Butterfly Bandages)**
- Best for: clean, superficial, low-tension wounds.
- Apply perpendicular to wound edges, pulling them together.
- Apply tincture of benzoin on surrounding skin first for better adhesion.

**Suturing (Field Suturing)**
- Use nylon suture material (3-0 or 4-0 for skin).
- Simple interrupted sutures, 5mm from wound edge, 5mm apart.
- Evert wound edges slightly for best healing.
- Remove sutures: face 5 days, scalp 7 days, torso 7-10 days, extremities 10-14 days.

**Staples**
- Fast closure for scalp and trunk wounds.
- Evert edges, apply staples 5-8mm apart.

## Signs of Wound Infection

Watch for these signs starting 24-48 hours after injury:

- **Increasing redness** spreading beyond wound edges.
- **Warmth** around the wound.
- **Swelling** that worsens over time.
- **Pain** that increases rather than decreases.
- **Purulent drainage** (pus — yellow, green, or foul-smelling).
- **Red streaks** extending from wound (lymphangitis — URGENT).
- **Fever** (temperature >38°C / 100.4°F).
- **Systemic signs**: chills, malaise, rapid pulse.

## Treating Wound Infections

### Mild Infection (Localized redness, minor swelling)
1. Open the wound — remove sutures/staples if closed.
2. Express pus gently.
3. Irrigate thoroughly with saline.
4. Pack wound open with moist gauze.
5. Change dressing 2-3 times daily.
6. Start oral antibiotics if available.

### Antibiotic Selection for Wound Infections
- **First choice**: Amoxicillin-clavulanate (Augmentin) 875/125mg twice daily.
- **Penicillin allergy**: Clindamycin 300mg four times daily.
- **Bite wounds**: Must cover anaerobes — Augmentin is ideal.
- **MRSA suspected**: Add Trimethoprim-Sulfamethoxazole (Bactrim) or Doxycycline.
- Duration: 5-7 days for mild infection, 10-14 days for severe.

### Severe Infection / Sepsis Signs
- Fever >38.5°C, heart rate >90, respiratory rate >20.
- Confusion, extreme weakness.
- **This is a life-threatening emergency.** Requires IV antibiotics and surgical debridement.

## Tetanus Considerations

Tetanus is a lethal risk from contaminated wounds, especially punctures and crush injuries.

- **High-risk wounds**: puncture wounds, crush injuries, burns, wounds contaminated with soil/feces/saliva.
- If immunization status unknown or >5 years since last booster: give tetanus toxoid booster (if available).
- Wound cleaning is the best prevention when vaccine is unavailable.
- Tetanus symptoms appear 3-21 days post-injury: jaw stiffness ("lockjaw"), muscle spasms, difficulty swallowing.
