# First Aid Essentials

## Primary Assessment (ABCDE)

When encountering a casualty, follow the ABCDE approach:

### A — Airway
- Check if the airway is clear. Look for obstructions (food, blood, teeth, vomit).
- If unconscious but breathing: place in recovery position (on their side).
- If obstructed: perform head-tilt chin-lift maneuver. For suspected spinal injury, use jaw-thrust without head tilt.

### B — Breathing
- Look, listen, and feel for breathing for up to 10 seconds.
- If not breathing: begin rescue breathing — 2 breaths every 30 compressions.
- Normal breathing rate: 12-20 breaths/min (adult), 20-30 (child), 30-50 (infant).

### C — Circulation
- Check for a pulse at the carotid artery (neck) for adults, brachial (inner arm) for infants.
- Check for signs of shock: pale/clammy skin, rapid weak pulse, confusion.
- If no pulse: begin CPR immediately.

### D — Disability (Neurological)
- AVPU scale: Alert, Voice responsive, Pain responsive, Unresponsive.
- Check pupils: equal and reactive to light = normal. Unequal = potential head injury.

### E — Exposure
- Expose the body to check for hidden injuries (cuts, fractures, burns) while maintaining warmth and dignity.

## CPR (Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation)

### Adult CPR
1. Place the person on a firm, flat surface.
2. Kneel beside the chest. Place the heel of one hand on the center of the chest (lower half of sternum), other hand on top.
3. Push hard and fast: at least 2 inches deep, 100-120 compressions per minute.
4. After 30 compressions, give 2 rescue breaths (tilt head back, lift chin, pinch nose, seal mouth).
5. Continue 30:2 ratio until help arrives or person recovers.

### Child CPR (Age 1-8)
- Use one hand for compressions, about 1/3 the depth of the chest.
- Same 30:2 ratio.

### Infant CPR (Under 1 year)
- Use two fingers on the breastbone just below the nipple line.
- Compress about 1.5 inches deep.
- Cover both mouth and nose when giving breaths.

## Severe Bleeding Control

### Direct Pressure
1. Apply firm, direct pressure with a clean cloth or bandage.
2. Do NOT remove the first cloth if it soaks through — add more on top.
3. Maintain pressure for at least 15 minutes continuously.

### Tourniquet Application
Use a tourniquet for life-threatening limb bleeding that cannot be controlled by direct pressure.

1. Place the tourniquet 2-3 inches above the wound (never on a joint).
2. Tighten until bleeding stops.
3. Note the time of application.
4. **Never loosen a tourniquet once applied** — this can cause reperfusion injury and cardiac arrest.
5. Improvised tourniquets: use a belt, strip of cloth, or triangular bandage with a windlass (stick to twist and tighten).

### Wound Packing
For deep wounds in areas where a tourniquet cannot be applied (groin, armpit, neck):
1. Pack sterile gauze tightly into the wound cavity.
2. Apply firm direct pressure on top.
3. Hemostatic gauze (QuikClot, Celox) is ideal if available.

## Shock Management

Signs: rapid weak pulse, cold clammy pale skin, rapid shallow breathing, confusion, anxiety, thirst.

### Treatment
1. Lay the person flat. Elevate legs 12 inches (if no spinal, head, or leg injury).
2. Keep warm with blankets.
3. Do NOT give food or water (aspiration risk if surgery needed).
4. Monitor breathing and pulse continuously.
5. Treat the underlying cause (stop bleeding, splint fractures, etc.).

## Fracture Management

### Signs of Fracture
- Pain, swelling, deformity, inability to move the limb, grinding sensation (crepitus).
- Open fracture: bone visible through skin — high infection risk.

### Splinting
1. Immobilize the joint above and below the fracture.
2. Use rigid material: boards, sticks, rolled newspapers, cardboard.
3. Pad between splint and skin.
4. Secure with bandages, strips of cloth, or tape.
5. Check circulation beyond the splint every 15 minutes (pulse, sensation, color).
6. For open fractures: cover wound with sterile dressing first, then splint. Do NOT push bone back in.

## Burns

### Classification
- **First degree**: Red, painful, no blisters (sunburn). Treat with cool water.
- **Second degree**: Blisters, severe pain, red/mottled skin. Cool water, sterile dressing.
- **Third degree**: White, charred, or leathery skin. May be painless (nerve damage). CRITICAL — needs advanced care.

### Treatment
1. Remove from heat source. Remove clothing/jewelry near burn (unless stuck to skin).
2. Cool with running cool (not cold) water for 10-20 minutes.
3. Do NOT apply ice, butter, or ointments.
4. Cover with sterile non-stick dressing or clean cling wrap.
5. For chemical burns: flush with large amounts of water for 20+ minutes.
6. Watch for shock in burns covering >15% body surface area.

### Rule of Nines (Adult Body Surface Area)
- Head and neck: 9%
- Each arm: 9%
- Front torso: 18%
- Back torso: 18%
- Each leg: 18%
- Groin: 1%
- Patient's palm = approximately 1% BSA
