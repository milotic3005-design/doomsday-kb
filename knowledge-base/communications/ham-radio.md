# Ham Radio and Emergency Communications

## Radio Basics

### Frequency Bands
- **HF (High Frequency): 3-30 MHz** — Long distance via skywave propagation (bounces off ionosphere). Can communicate worldwide. Best for regional and global emergency comms.
- **VHF (Very High Frequency): 30-300 MHz** — Line of sight + some terrain bending. Typical range 10-50 miles. 2-meter band (144-148 MHz) is most popular for local emergency use.
- **UHF (Ultra High Frequency): 300 MHz-3 GHz** — Strictly line of sight. Better building penetration. 70cm band (420-450 MHz). Good for urban comms.

### Key Ham Radio Bands
| Band | Frequency | Typical Range | Best For |
|------|-----------|---------------|----------|
| 80m | 3.5-4.0 MHz | 100-500 miles (night) | Regional nighttime comms |
| 40m | 7.0-7.3 MHz | 50-500 miles | Reliable day/night regional |
| 20m | 14.0-14.35 MHz | 500-5000 miles | Worldwide daytime comms |
| 2m | 144-148 MHz | 10-50 miles | Local emergency, repeaters |
| 70cm | 420-450 MHz | 5-30 miles | Local, building penetration |

### Propagation
- **Ground wave**: Follows earth's surface. Useful below 2 MHz. Range: 100+ miles.
- **Skywave**: HF signals bounce off ionosphere. Range: worldwide. Varies by time of day, season, solar cycle.
- **Line of sight**: VHF/UHF. Range depends on antenna height and terrain.
- **Formula for VHF/UHF range**: Distance (miles) ≈ 1.42 × √(height in feet). A 100-foot antenna can reach ~14 miles to the horizon.

## Emergency Frequencies

### Calling Frequencies (Always Monitor These)
- **2m simplex**: 146.520 MHz FM — National calling frequency
- **70cm simplex**: 446.000 MHz FM
- **HF emergency**: 7.030 MHz CW (morse code), 7.290 MHz voice (SSB)
- **International distress**: 14.300 MHz (HF), 243.0 MHz (military UHF)

### FRS/GMRS (No License Required for FRS)
- Channel 1: 462.5625 MHz — Most common for groups
- Channel 20: 462.6625 MHz — Common emergency channel
- Range: 0.5-2 miles typical (FRS), up to 5+ miles (GMRS with better antenna)

## Antenna Building

### Dipole Antenna (Simplest HF Antenna)
The best general-purpose wire antenna. Formula: **Total length (feet) = 468 ÷ frequency (MHz)**.

Example for 40m band (7.15 MHz): 468 ÷ 7.15 = 65.4 feet total, each leg = 32.7 feet.

**Construction**:
1. Cut two equal lengths of wire (insulated or bare copper).
2. Connect a center insulator or solder to coax feed point.
3. Connect coaxial cable (RG-58 or RG-8) to center: one conductor to each leg.
4. String between two trees or poles, center at highest point possible.
5. Minimum height: 1/4 wavelength above ground for best performance.

### Ground Plane Antenna (VHF/UHF)
For 2m band (146 MHz): main element = 19.5 inches. Four ground radials = 19.5 inches each, angled 45° down.

### Emergency Antenna (Field Expedient)
- Random wire: Any length of wire, as high as possible. Connect one end to antenna port, other end as high/far as you can. Performance varies but better than nothing.
- Vehicle whip: A telescoping whip antenna on a vehicle can work for VHF/UHF.

### Critical Morse Signals
- **SOS**: ... --- ... (distress signal — universally recognized)
- **CQ**: -.-. --.- (calling any station)
- **QRZ**: Who is calling me?
- **QTH**: My location is...
- **73**: Best regards / goodbye

## Signaling Without Radio

### Visual Signals
- **Signal mirror**: Aim reflected sunlight at target. Can be seen 10+ miles. Improvise from any reflective surface.
- **Signal fire**: Three fires in a triangle = international distress signal. Add green vegetation for white smoke (daytime) or burn dry wood for bright flame (night).
- **Ground signals**: For aircraft — large letters in open ground.
  - V = Need assistance
  - X = Need medical help
  - → = Traveling this direction
  - Minimum size: 10 feet tall, 3 feet wide.

### Audio Signals
- **Three of anything** is a universal distress signal: three whistle blasts, three gunshots, three fires.
- Whistle carries further than shouting and requires less energy.
- Pattern: three blasts, pause, repeat.
