## 1. CALORIC DENSITY OF STORED FOODS

### 1.1 Why Caloric Density Matters

In a long-term survival scenario, the primary goal is maintaining sufficient caloric intake to support daily activity, immune function, and cognitive performance. The minimum caloric intake to prevent starvation and maintain basic function for an average adult is approximately **1,800–2,200 calories/day** at sedentary activity; manual farm labor or survival activities increase requirements to **2,500–3,500+ calories/day**.

**Key principle:** Not all stored food is equal. Some foods provide many calories per unit of weight and storage space; others provide minimal calories but important micronutrients. A sustainable food storage plan balances both.

### 1.4 Practical Food Storage Math

**Calories needed per person:**
- Sedentary adult: 2,000 cal/day
- Active adult (manual labor): 2,500–3,000 cal/day
- Cold climate, working outdoors: 3,000–4,000+ cal/day

**One-year supply estimates per person (2,000 cal/day):**
- Total calories needed: 730,000 calories/year
- White rice alone (at 1,640 cal/lb): ~445 lbs
- Pinto beans alone (at 1,560 cal/lb): ~468 lbs
- Practical balanced approach:
  - 300 lbs grain (rice/wheat/oats)
  - 100 lbs dried legumes (beans/lentils)
  - 25 lbs fat/oil
  - 20 lbs sugar/honey
  - 20 lbs powdered milk
  - 10 lbs salt
  - Supplemental freeze-dried fruits/vegetables (to address nutrient gaps)

**Storage space calculation:**
- 1 gallon mylar bag or 5-gallon bucket of white rice ≈ 33 lbs ≈ 54,000 calories
- 5-gallon bucket of wheat berries ≈ 33 lbs ≈ 49,500 calories
- To store 730,000 calories of grain: ~22 standard 5-gallon buckets

## 2. NUTRITIONAL GAPS IN LONG-TERM FOOD STORAGE

### 2.1 The Problem

A calorie-sufficient diet of stored staples (rice, beans, wheat, salt, oil, sugar) will prevent starvation but will produce serious nutritional deficiencies within weeks to months. These deficiencies can cause disability, disease, and death even when caloric intake is adequate.

**Historical precedent:** Scurvy (vitamin C deficiency) killed more sailors and soldiers than combat for centuries. Beriberi (B1/thiamine deficiency) from polished white rice diets killed thousands in Asia. Pellagra (B3/niacin deficiency) from corn-only diets devastated the American South. These are entirely preventable.

---

### 2.2 Critical Nutrient Deficiencies in Storage-Based Diets

#### Vitamin C (Ascorbic Acid)
- **Daily requirement:** 75–90 mg/day adults; 120 mg during illness
- **Scurvy onset:** Within 4–6 weeks of zero intake; fatigue, weakness, bleeding gums; death within months
- **Problem with stored foods:** Vitamin C degrades rapidly in heat, light, and oxygen; canned foods retain 25–50% initially, then continue to decline; no vitamin C in grains, rice, or dried beans
- **Solutions:**
  - Grow sprouts (wheat, lentil, bean sprouts — high in vitamin C after 3–5 days sprouting, even in darkness)
  - Sprout 1 tablespoon seeds per day → 1–2 cups sprouts per day; provides significant vitamin C
  - Grow or forage wild edibles (see KB_Agriculture Section 12): Rose hips, elderberries, purslane, dandelion greens, stinging nettle
  - Dehydrate and store rose hips (Rosa spp.) — very high vitamin C, shelf-stable when dried
  - Pine needle tea: Simmer (do NOT boil) 1 tablespoon fresh pine needles per cup of water for 10 minutes; 3–5× the vitamin C of oranges; available year-round in most of North America
  - Vitamin C tablets (ascorbic acid powder): Buy in bulk; stable for 2–3+ years sealed
  - Fermented foods (lacto-fermentation): Sauerkraut, kimchi — B vitamins and some vitamin C preserved or increased by fermentation

---

#### Vitamin D
- **Daily requirement:** 600–800 IU/day; 1,000–2,000 IU recommended for optimal health
- **Deficiency:** Rickets in children; osteomalacia (soft bones) in adults; immune dysfunction; depression
- **Problem:** Virtually absent from stored foods; only oily fish and egg yolks significant sources; requires UV-B sunlight for synthesis in skin
- **Solutions:**
  - Sun exposure: 10–30 minutes of midday sun (skin exposed) provides 1,000–5,000 IU; best between 10 am–2 pm; more skin surface = more production; darker skin requires longer exposure
  - Canned sardines, salmon, tuna: Among the few food sources; eat regularly
  - Cod liver oil: Extremely high in D and A; shelf life 1–2 years; supplement stock
  - Dried mushrooms (UV-exposed): Gills-up in direct sunlight for 2 days → significant vitamin D created; store dried; rehydrate as needed. Even commercial mushrooms significantly increase D when sun-dried. This is a free, renewable source.
  - Vitamin D3 supplements: Inexpensive; store 2+ year supply; 1,000–2,000 IU/day

---

#### Vitamin A (and Beta-Carotene)
- **Daily requirement:** 700–900 mcg RAE/day
- **Deficiency:** Night blindness → total blindness; immune system failure; high mortality in children
- **Problem:** Low in grain/legume-focused diets; present in orange/yellow/dark green vegetables which are hard to store long-term
- **Solutions:**
  - Grow and dehydrate sweet potatoes, carrots, winter squash, pumpkin — excellent sources; dehydrate or store in root cellar
  - Forage dandelion greens, lambsquarters, purslane (all high in beta-carotene)
  - Dried red/orange vegetables: Dehydrated red peppers, carrots, sweet potatoes store 1–2 years and retain significant beta-carotene
  - Freeze-dried sweet potatoes: 25-year shelf life; excellent source
  - Cod liver oil: Contains preformed vitamin A (retinol); very high concentration
  - Liver from any butchered animal: Exceptionally rich in preformed vitamin A; process and dry or can

---

#### B Vitamins (Thiamine B1, Riboflavin B2, Niacin B3, B6, B12, Folate)
- **Deficiency diseases:**
  - B1/Thiamine: Beriberi (nerve damage; heart failure); caused by white rice–only diet
  - B3/Niacin: Pellagra (dermatitis, diarrhea, dementia, death); from corn-only diet (untreated)
  - B12: Anemia; nerve damage; primarily found in animal products
  - Folate: Anemia; neural tube defects in pregnancy
- **Solutions:**
  - Use WHOLE grains, not refined: Brown rice and whole wheat flour contain B vitamins; polished white rice does not contain significant B1
  - Nixtamalization for corn: Treat corn with wood ash lye or calcium hydroxide (slaked lime) before cooking; this process makes niacin bioavailable and was traditional in Mesoamerica; prevents pellagra. Process: Soak shelled corn in 1 tablespoon wood ash + 1 quart water 12–24 hours; rinse thoroughly; the resulting hominy has vastly superior nutritional profile
  - Nutritional yeast: Can be dried and stored; very high in B vitamins including B12 in fortified forms; add to foods
  - Sprouts: Sprouted grains have dramatically increased B vitamin content
  - Animal products: Eggs, dairy, meat, fish — prioritize in diet for B12
  - Fermented foods: Tempeh, miso, certain fermented vegetables generate or retain B vitamins
  - Sunflower seeds and peanuts: Good B1 and B3 sources; store well

---

#### Iron
- **Daily requirement:** 8 mg/day men; 18 mg/day women (premenopausal); 27 mg/day pregnant
- **Deficiency:** Iron-deficiency anemia; fatigue; immune impairment; high mortality in pregnant women
- **Problem:** Plant iron (non-heme) much less bioavailable than animal iron (heme); grains and legumes contain iron but also phytates that block absorption
- **Solutions:**
  - Reduce phytates: Soak legumes and grains 8–24 hours before cooking (reduces phytate content 25–50%); ferment grains (sourdough fermentation reduces phytates significantly)
  - Pair with vitamin C: Consuming any vitamin C source with iron-rich plant food dramatically increases absorption
  - Cast iron cooking: Cooking acidic foods in cast iron skillet adds dietary iron to food (up to 80% increase in iron content of some foods cooked in cast iron)
  - Animal sources: Liver is highest; red meat, dark poultry meat; dried blood (if butchering animals)
  - Dried dark beans (especially kidney, black, lentils): Plant iron sources; optimize absorption with vitamin C
  - Wild foraged curly dock: Very high plant iron

---

#### Calcium
- **Daily requirement:** 1,000–1,200 mg/day
- **Deficiency:** Osteoporosis; muscle cramps; nerve dysfunction; in extreme deficiency, tetany (muscle spasms)
- **Problem:** Primary sources (dairy) are difficult to store long-term; most grains low in calcium
- **Solutions:**
  - Powdered milk: Excellent calcium source; store in quantity (non-fat or whole)
  - Bone broth: When butchering animals, save all bones; simmer 12–24 hours in water with a splash of vinegar (acid leaches calcium from bones); drink broth
  - Canned sardines and salmon (eat the bones): Very high calcium; softened bones are safe and nutritious to eat
  - Lime (calcium hydroxide): Use in nixtamalization; also small amounts added to cooking water provide calcium
  - Sesame seeds: Very high calcium; store well
  - Dried dark leafy greens: Dehydrated kale, collards; reasonable calcium source
  - Lambs quarters, stinging nettle (fresh or cooked): Very high calcium
  - Wood ash water (traditional): Not recommended in volume but was historically used in calcium-deficient areas

---

#### Iodine
- **Daily requirement:** 150 mcg/day; 220 mcg pregnant; 290 mcg breastfeeding
- **Deficiency:** Goiter (enlarged thyroid); hypothyroidism; severe developmental delays in infants
- **Problem:** Present in iodized salt (depletes over time when stored; iodine is volatile); absent from most non-coastal foods; completely absent if using non-iodized sea salt or rock salt for preservation
- **Solutions:**
  - Stock iodized salt (replace every 2–3 years as iodine evaporates from opened containers)
  - Potassium iodide supplements: Very inexpensive; stock large supply; 150 mcg/day
  - Seaweed/kelp: Very high iodine; stock dried seaweed; add to soups and stews; OR kelp powder (1/4 teaspoon = several days' iodine needs). Caution: Too much iodine from seaweed can cause thyroid problems; don't overdo
  - Coastal foraging: Sea vegetables (nori, dulse, kelp) if near coast; dry for storage

---

#### Zinc
- **Daily requirement:** 8–11 mg/day
- **Deficiency:** Immune dysfunction; poor wound healing; growth failure; reproductive problems
- **Solutions:**
  - Pumpkin seeds: Extremely high in zinc; store well; stock in quantity
  - Red meat and poultry (when available)
  - Shellfish (oysters highest zinc source of any food)
  - Lentils and legumes (some zinc but poor bioavailability due to phytates — reduce by soaking)
  - Nuts and seeds generally

---

#### Magnesium
- **Daily requirement:** 310–420 mg/day
- **Deficiency:** Muscle cramps, anxiety, poor sleep, heart arrhythmias
- **Solutions:**
  - Pumpkin seeds: Very high
  - Dark chocolate (if stored)
  - Nuts and seeds generally
  - Whole grains (not refined)
  - Legumes
  - Epsom salt baths (magnesium sulfate absorbs through skin) — not dietary but supplemental

---

#### Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA/DHA)
- **Why important:** Brain function; inflammation control; cardiovascular health; joint health
- **Problem:** Almost entirely absent from stored grain/legume diet; oxidize quickly in stored nuts
- **Solutions:**
  - Canned sardines, salmon, tuna: Best long-term storage source; rotate stock
  - Flaxseeds: High ALA (plant omega-3); store whole seeds for 2+ years; grind fresh for use; ALA converts partially to EPA/DHA
  - Purslane (foraged): Highest plant source of omega-3 fatty acids
  - Walnuts: Reasonable ALA source; store frozen for best results

---

### 2.3 Anti-Nutrient Management (Phytates, Oxalic Acid, Lectins)

Many storable foods contain compounds that interfere with nutrient absorption or are mildly toxic when consumed raw or in large quantities.

| Anti-Nutrient | Found In | Problem | Solution |
|--------------|----------|---------|---------|
| Phytate (phytic acid) | Grains, legumes, seeds | Blocks absorption of iron, zinc, calcium | Soak 8–24 hrs; ferment (sourdough); sprout; cook |
| Oxalic acid | Spinach, dock, purslane, rhubarb (leaves) | Binds calcium; kidney stones in excess | Cook greens; variety; don't consume extremely large amounts |
| Phytohaemagglutinin (lectin) | Kidney beans, fava beans | Causes severe food poisoning if undercooked | Boil HARD at rolling boil for 10+ minutes; slow cookers may not reach adequate temperature |
| Tannins | Acorns, some nuts, dock seeds | Inhibit protein and mineral absorption | Leach acorns; roast nuts; cooking reduces in most foods |
| Saponins | Quinoa, soapwort | Digestive upset | Rinse quinoa thoroughly before cooking |

## 3. GRAIN GRINDING: HAND MILLS AND STONE GRINDING

### 3.1 Why Grain Grinding Matters

Stored whole grain berries (wheat, corn, rye, oats, barley, etc.) are dramatically more shelf-stable than flour:
- Whole wheat berries: 25–30 years in oxygen-free storage
- Whole wheat flour: 5–10 years sealed; 6–12 months at room temperature
- All-purpose white flour: 10–25 years sealed

Grinding your own flour means:
- Maximum nutrient retention (bran and germ intact until grinding)
- Optimal freshness (freshly ground flour has superior flavor and baking characteristics)
- True long-term self-sufficiency (seeds → grain → flour → bread)

---

### 3.2 Types of Grain Mills

**Three primary grinding mechanisms:**

**Stone Burr Mills**
- Traditional method; two stones (natural or synthetic) grinding grain between them
- Modern stone: Corundum (synthetic ceramic; very hard; self-sharpening)
- Produces full range of grind textures from fine flour to coarse cracked grain
- Cooler grinding = better nutrient retention
- Works with: Wheat (hard and soft), rye, oats, spelt, kamut, barley, corn, millet, teff, quinoa, sorghum, amaranth, buckwheat, rice
- Does NOT work with: Oily seeds (flax, sesame), fresh or moist grains, herbs

**Steel Burr (Plate) Mills**
- Two steel plates with serrated surfaces; grinds grain between them
- More durable than stone for tough grains and beans
- Slightly coarser output; excellent for cornmeal, legume flour
- Works for: Corn, beans (for flour), grains
- Most hand mills use steel burrs

**Impact Mills**
- Two spinning steel discs with teeth; grain is hammered rather than ground
- Very fast; very fine flour only (cannot produce coarse meal)
- Electric only — not viable for grid-down use
- Generally not appropriate for manual/off-grid use

### 3.4 Grinding Procedure and Flour Quality

**Preparation for best results:**
- Chill grains in freezer 1–2 hours before grinding (reduces heat buildup during grinding; heat destroys nutrients and degrades gluten-forming proteins)
- Start with coarser setting; grind twice (first pass = coarse; second = fine flour) — easier on mill and hands; better flour quality
- Run 1/4 cup dry white rice through mill before and after grinding sessions to clean burrs (cleans stone and steel burrs; rice absorbs residual moisture)

**Hard wheat vs. soft wheat:**
- Hard wheat (high protein, 12–14%): Best for yeast breads, sourdough; strong gluten network
- Soft wheat (low protein, 8–10%): Best for pastries, biscuits, cakes; weaker gluten

**Fresh-ground flour characteristics:**
- Higher in moisture than commercial flour (resting 24 hours allows moisture to even out)
- Refrigerate or freeze fresh-ground whole wheat flour immediately if not using within 2–3 days (oils in germ oxidize quickly; commercial flour has germ removed to extend shelf life)
- Fresh-ground flour absorbs liquid differently than commercial flour; recipes may need adjustment (often +10% more flour or less liquid)

---

### 3.5 DIY Stone Grinding Without a Mill

In absolute emergencies, grain can be ground without a commercial mill:

**Flat rock grinding (metate method):**
1. Find a flat, smooth, hard rock (granite or quartzite ideal; sandstone works but crumbles into flour)
2. Find a second smooth rock that fits comfortably in hand (mano)
3. Place grain on flat rock; rub with mano in circular, pressing motion
4. Collect flour at edges; repeat
5. Sift through cloth to remove coarse particles; regrind those

**Output:** Coarse cornmeal-like texture; can be used for flatbreads, porridge, and corn tortillas; not ideal for risen bread (gluten poorly developed)
**Rate:** Very slow; plan on 1–2 hours per pound of flour
**Best grains for this method:** Corn, dried beans (for flour), soft wheat

## 4. BEAN COOKING WITHOUT GAS: RETAINED HEAT AND FIRELESS COOKING

### 4.1 Why Beans Are Challenging

Dried beans and legumes require long cooking times:
- Pinto beans: 1.5–2 hours at rolling boil
- Kidney beans: 1.5–2 hours (MUST reach rolling boil for 10+ min to destroy lectin toxin)
- Black beans: 1–1.5 hours
- Chickpeas: 2–3 hours
- Lentils: 20–30 minutes (fastest, best for fuel conservation)
- Split peas: 30–45 minutes

At wood/biomass fire rates, this represents significant fuel consumption. Retained heat cooking can reduce fuel use by **60–80%** by using heat that is already generated.

---

### 4.2 Soaking — Essential Before Cooking

**Why soak:**
- Reduces cooking time by 25–50%
- Removes oligosaccharides (reduces gas)
- Reduces phytic acid (improves mineral absorption)
- Improves digestibility overall

**Cold soak:** Cover beans with 3× their volume of water; soak 8–24 hours at room temperature; drain and rinse; cook in fresh water
**Quick soak:** Cover with water; bring to boil; boil 2 minutes; turn off heat; soak 1 hour; drain; rinse; cook in fresh water

**Important:** ALWAYS discard soaking water and cook in fresh water. Soaking water contains the compounds you're trying to remove.

**Kidney bean warning:** Kidney beans (and fava beans) contain phytohaemagglutinin, a toxic lectin. You MUST bring to a hard rolling boil and boil for a minimum of 10 minutes. Slow cookers do NOT get hot enough to destroy this toxin; do not use a slow cooker for kidney beans. The toxin causes severe vomiting within 1–3 hours of ingestion.

---

### 4.3 Fireless Cooker / Haybox Cooker — Construction and Use

**Principle:** Bring food to a full boil; seal in insulated container; retained heat finishes the cooking over several hours without any additional fuel. Works for anything that normally requires prolonged simmering or boiling.

**Historical context:** Used for generations in Norway, Germany, and other countries as "hay boxes." USDA documented the technique as early as 1869. Reduces fuel consumption by 60–80%.

**Energy savings:** A fireless cooker can save **80% of cooking fuel** compared to simmering on a stove.

---

### 4.4 DIY Fireless Cooker Construction

#### Method A — Wooden Box (Best Performance)

**Materials:**
- Wooden box or crate large enough for your cooking pot + 4–6 inches of insulation on all sides
- Insulation material: Hay, straw, sawdust, crumpled newspaper, wool, old sleeping bags, insulating foam
- Old blankets or towels (exterior wrapping for lid)
- Your cooking pot (pot with tight-fitting lid is essential)

**Construction:**
1. Select or build a box 12–18 inches larger in each dimension than your cooking pot
2. Line the bottom with 4–6 inches of packed insulation
3. Create a "nest" in the center slightly smaller than your pot
4. Ensure the pot fits snugly in the nest
5. Create an insulating lid from same materials (old pillow, folded blankets packed into a lid-shaped box, or a thick layer stuffed into a bag)
6. Optional: Line interior walls with aluminum foil to reflect heat back to pot

#### Method B — Cooler / Ice Chest (Repurposed)

- An insulated camping cooler works excellently as a fireless cooker

- Works even better if you pre-warm the cooler with warm water before placing pot

### 4.5 Fireless Cooking Procedure — Step by Step

**Step 1 — Prepare beans:**
- Soak overnight; drain; rinse; cover with fresh water by 2 inches

**Step 2 — Heat on fire:**
- Bring to hard rolling boil over maximum heat
- For kidney or fava beans: Maintain rolling boil for minimum 10 minutes (lectin destruction)
- For other beans: Bring to boil; boil 5–10 minutes actively
- Fill pot approximately 80% full (fuller = more thermal mass = more heat retained)

**Step 3 — Transfer to fireless cooker:**
- Quickly place boiling pot in the insulated nest
- Do NOT remove lid to check during cooking
- Cover immediately with insulating lid/cushion
- Wrap entire assembly with additional blankets or coat

**Step 4 — Wait (do not disturb):**
- Most beans: 4–8 hours
- Harder beans (chickpeas): 8–12 hours
- Lentils: 1–2 hours (they're fast even in fireless cooker)
- Thick soups and stews: 4–6 hours
- Rice: 1–2 hours (bring to boil 10 minutes, then transfer; perfect every time)
- Porridge/oatmeal: Soak oats overnight; bring to boil; transfer immediately; ready in 2–4 hours

**Step 5 — Check and serve:**
- Beans should be fully tender
- If still hard: Return to fire briefly to reheat; transfer back to cooker for additional time

**Important notes:**
- Do NOT open the lid to check during cooking — releases heat rapidly
- Over-cooking is nearly impossible in a fireless cooker (unlike boiling, which turns vegetables to mush)
- Keep cook pot at least 80% full for best heat retention
- Cook multiple pots simultaneously to maximize efficiency of your fire

## 5. FERMENTATION FOR FOOD PRESERVATION AND NUTRITION

### 5.1 Why Fermentation Matters in Grid-Down Scenarios

Fermentation:
- Preserves perishable foods for weeks to months without refrigeration or pressure canning
- Increases nutritional value (creates B vitamins; increases vitamin C; improves mineral bioavailability)
- Improves digestibility (breaks down phytates; partially pre-digests proteins and starches)
- Creates probiotic organisms that support gut health and immune function
- Requires only salt, water, and the food itself — no electricity, no special equipment
- Is one of humanity's oldest food preservation technologies (estimated 10,000+ years of use)

---

### 5.2 Lacto-Fermentation (Lactic Acid Fermentation)

**Mechanism:** Naturally present *Lactobacillus* bacteria on vegetables and in the environment convert sugars to lactic acid in an anaerobic (oxygen-free) environment. The lactic acid acts as a natural preservative, dropping pH to 3.5–4.0, which inhibits all pathogenic bacteria.

**What you need:**
- Vegetables (any; cabbage is easiest/most reliable)
- Salt (non-iodized; iodine inhibits beneficial bacteria) — sea salt, pickling salt, or kosher salt
- Clean water (if making brine; chlorinated tap water can inhibit fermentation; let stand overnight first)
- Clean glass jars or ceramic crocks
- Something to keep vegetables submerged (zip-lock bag filled with brine, clean rock, wooden follower)

---

### 5.3 Basic Sauerkraut — Master Recipe

**Ingredients:**
- 1 medium head green cabbage (~3 lbs)
- 1.5–2 tablespoons non-iodized salt (approximately 2% of cabbage weight by weight)

**Procedure:**
1. Remove outer leaves; set aside 2–3 large outer leaves
2. Shred cabbage thinly (1/8–1/4 inch); discard core
3. Place shredded cabbage in large bowl
4. Add salt; massage vigorously with clean hands for 5–10 minutes until cabbage releases significant liquid (brine)
5. Pack tightly into clean quart jar (or wide-mouth jar); press firmly with fist after each handful; cabbage should be fully submerged under its own brine by the time jar is 80% full
6. If not enough brine: Mix 1 teaspoon salt per 1 cup water; add as needed to cover cabbage
7. Place a reserved cabbage leaf over the top; weigh down with a zip-lock bag filled with brine (prevents air exposure and mold growth)
8. Cover jar with cloth (not airtight lid — CO₂ must escape)
9. Leave at room temperature (65–75°F ideal)

**Timeline:**
- Days 1–2: Salt draws out more liquid; bubbling may begin
- Days 3–7: Active fermentation; significant bubbling; sour smell develops; taste daily
- Days 7–14: Bubbling slows; acidity increases; flavor develops
- Weeks 2–6: Continue fermenting for stronger, more sour flavor; or move to cold storage
- Move to cold storage (root cellar, 35–40°F) when desired sourness is achieved

**Storage in cold conditions:** Sauerkraut keeps 6+ months in a cold (32–40°F) environment when properly fermented and kept submerged.

**Signs of success:** Sour smell; bubbles; cabbage submerged under brine; acidic taste
**Signs of failure:** Pink, red, or black mold on surface (discard top layer if just white kahm yeast; discard entirely if colored mold); rotten smell (not sour); slimy texture throughout

---

### 5.4 Brine Ratios for Lacto-Fermentation

| Brine Concentration | Salt per Quart Water | Best For |
|--------------------|---------------------|---------|
| 2% (vegetable standard) | 1 teaspoon (loose, shredded veg) | Sauerkraut, kimchi (by weight method) |
| 3% (standard brine) | 1.5 teaspoons | Most vegetable ferments; cucumber pickles |
| 5% (medium brine) | 2.5 teaspoons | Whole or large vegetable pieces; garlic cloves; longer storage |
| 8–10% (heavy brine) | 4–5 teaspoons | Very long-term storage; traditional olive brine |

**Note:** The weight method is more accurate:
- 2% brine = 20g salt per 1,000g (1 kg) of total weight (salt + vegetables + water)

---

### 5.5 Other Lacto-Fermented Foods

**Fermented Vegetables (any combination):**
- Carrots, beets, radishes, turnips, green beans: Pack in jar; cover with 3% brine; weigh down; ferment 5–14 days at room temperature
- Garlic: Whole peeled cloves in 5% brine; ferment 2–4 weeks; incredible flavor
- Jalapeños or hot peppers: Slice or whole; 3% brine; 5–14 days
- Mixed vegetable pickles: Any combination; 3% brine; 7–21 days

**Fermented Beverages:**
- Kombucha: SCOBY (symbiotic culture of bacteria and yeast) + sweetened tea; ferments 7–14 days; tart, effervescent
- Kvass (traditional grain ferment): Stale bread + water; ferment 24–72 hours; mildly sour; nutritious
- Tepache (fermented pineapple): Pineapple rind + water + piloncillo/sugar; ferment 2–3 days; mildly alcoholic, probiotic

**Fermented Dairy (if milk available):**
- Kefir: Milk + kefir grains; ferment 24–48 hours at room temperature; thick, tart
- Yogurt: Milk heated to 180°F, cooled to 110°F; add 2 tablespoons live yogurt as starter; hold at 100–110°F for 8–12 hours (wrap in blankets; use haybox method)
- Sour cream: Heavy cream + cultured buttermilk starter; ferment 24 hours

---

### 5.6 Vinegar Making

Vinegar requires two fermentation steps: alcoholic fermentation (sugar → alcohol) followed by acetic acid fermentation (alcohol → acetic acid).

**Step 1 — Make an alcoholic base (or use existing wine/beer/cider):**
- Combine: 1 gallon apple juice or grape juice + 1/4 teaspoon wine yeast OR wild yeast (place in open jar, allow wild yeast to colonize flour-water paste, then add to juice)
- Ferment in loosely covered container at 65–75°F for 1–3 weeks until all bubbling stops
- Resulting liquid: Hard cider or wine (~5–7% alcohol) — the perfect base for vinegar

**Step 2 — Acetic acid fermentation:**
- Pour wine/cider into wide-mouth container (wide surface area speeds oxidation)
- Add 1–2 cups raw, unfiltered "live" vinegar as starter (contains *Acetobacter* bacteria) OR allow natural *Acetobacter* from air to colonize (slower; 1–3 months)
- Cover with cheesecloth (allows airflow while keeping insects out)
- Store at 65–80°F in dark place; do NOT disturb
- Stir gently every few days to oxygenate
- A "mother of vinegar" (SCOBY-like film) will form on the surface — normal; beneficial; can be used to start future batches
- Ready in 4–12 weeks; taste for acidity (tangy, sharp)
- pH should be below 4.6 when done

**Testing doneness:**
- Taste: Sharp, distinctly sour, no sweetness remaining
- pH strips: Should read 3.0–4.0 for table vinegar

**Important:** Vinegar must be minimum **5% acidity** to be safe for preserving (pickling). Homemade vinegar acidity is variable and difficult to verify without titration equipment. Use homemade vinegar for cooking and dressings; use commercial vinegar (known 5% acidity) for canning pickles.

---

### 5.7 Alcohol Production (for medicinal use, preservation, and fuel)

**Legal note:** Distillation of alcohol is regulated in the US and most countries; fermenting for personal use varies by jurisdiction. In genuine survival/grid-down scenarios, knowledge is valuable regardless.

**Simple Mash for Alcohol (grain or fruit):**

*Grain mash:*
1. Cook grain (corn, barley, wheat) in 2× its volume of water until fully soft
2. Cool to 150°F; add 1 teaspoon malted barley flour (or commercial amylase enzyme) per gallon — converts starches to sugars (1–2 hours at 150°F)
3. Cool to 75°F; add wine or distiller's yeast
4. Ferment in loosely covered container 5–14 days until bubbling stops
5. Result: 5–10% alcohol "beer" or "wash"

*Fruit mash:*
1. Crush ripe fruit; mix with equal volume water
2. Add sugar if desired (1–2 lbs per gallon increases alcohol yield)
3. Add yeast; ferment 1–3 weeks until bubbling stops

**Uses of fermented alcoholic beverages (without distillation):**
- As antiseptic rinse (wine vinegar/beer; limited effectiveness; better than nothing)
- For tincture preparation (herb extraction in alcohol)
- Vinegar production
- Barter item
- Morale and social use (in moderation)

## 6. WATER BATH CANNING VS. PRESSURE CANNING

### 6.1 The Fundamental Science

**The critical pH:** 4.6

*Clostridium botulinum* (botulism-causing bacteria) produces a deadly toxin that can form in sealed, low-oxygen (anaerobic) jars of food. It cannot grow or produce toxin at pH 4.6 or below. Its spores survive boiling water (212°F) — they are only killed at 240°F+.

**Two canning realities:**
- High-acid foods (pH ≤ 4.6): Botulism cannot grow; boiling water (212°F) sufficient; water bath canning is safe
- Low-acid foods (pH > 4.6): Botulism CAN grow; must reach 240°F; pressure canning REQUIRED

Botulism is particularly dangerous because contaminated food often looks, smells, and tastes normal. The toxin is among the most lethal substances known. There is no antidote available without medical care.

---

### 6.2 Water Bath Canning — Complete Guide

**Safe foods for water bath canning (pH ≤ 4.6):**

| Food Category | Examples | Notes |
|--------------|---------|-------|
| Fruits | Apples, peaches, pears, cherries, berries, plums, apricots | All naturally high-acid |
| Fruit juices | Apple cider, grape juice, berry juices | Natural acidity |
| Jams and jellies | Any fruit jam/jelly/preserve | Added sugar; naturally acidic fruit |
| Fruit butters | Apple butter, plum butter | Long-cooked; naturally acidic |
| Pickles (cucumber) | Dill pickles, bread and butter | Must use tested recipe with correct vinegar amount |
| Pickled vegetables | Pickled green beans, beets, okra, carrots | Vinegar MUST acidify adequately; use tested recipes only |
| Tomatoes | Tomatoes, tomato sauce, salsa, tomato juice | MUST add 1 tbsp bottled lemon juice per pint OR 2 tbsp per quart OR 1/4 tsp citric acid per pint |
| Sauerkraut | Fermented cabbage | Lactic acid drops pH adequately |
| Fruit salsas | Peach salsa, mango salsa | Tomato-based salsas require tested recipe |
| Marmalades | Citrus marmalade | Naturally acidic |
| Relishes | Corn relish, cucumber relish | Must follow tested recipe for acid balance |

**Altitude adjustments for water bath canning:**

| Altitude | Additional Processing Time |
|---------|--------------------------|
| 0–1,000 ft | None (use recipe time) |
| 1,001–3,000 ft | Add 5 minutes |
| 3,001–6,000 ft | Add 10 minutes |
| 6,001–8,000 ft | Add 15 minutes |
| 8,001–10,000 ft | Add 20 minutes |

**Step-by-step water bath canning procedure:**

1. **Inspect jars:** Discard any with chips, cracks, or nicks on rim; use only standard canning jars
2. **Wash jars:** Hot, soapy water; rinse well; keep hot in simmering water until use
3. **Prepare lids:** Wash; simmer in hot water (not boiling) 10 minutes to soften seal compound
4. **Prepare canner:** Fill water bath canner halfway with water; bring to simmer; add more water as needed
5. **Prepare food:** Follow specific tested recipe exactly — do not change proportions of acid or vegetables
6. **Fill jars:** Ladle hot food into hot jars; leave correct headspace:
   - Jams/jellies: 1/4 inch
   - Fruits and tomatoes: 1/2 inch
   - Pickles: 1/2 inch
7. **Remove air bubbles:** Insert thin spatula or table knife; slide around inside of jar; remove bubbles
8. **Wipe rims:** Use clean, damp cloth; any food on rim may prevent seal
9. **Apply lids and bands:** Center lid; apply band fingertip-tight (can feel resistance; not crank-tight)
10. **Load canner:** Lower jars on rack into canning water; water must cover jars by 1–2 inches; add boiling water if needed
11. **Bring to boil:** Increase heat; when full rolling boil begins, start timer
12. **Process:** Maintain gentle boil throughout entire processing time; adjust heat as needed
13. **Remove jars:** When time is up, turn off heat; remove lid; wait 5 minutes; remove jars with jar lifter; do not tilt; place on towel with 1-inch space between jars
14. **Cool:** Do not disturb 12–24 hours; you should hear the satisfying "pop" as lids seal
15. **Test seals:** Press center of lid — should be concave and not flex. If lid flexes, jar did not seal; refrigerate and use within 2 weeks; do not attempt to reprocess.
16. **Label and store:** Date; contents; store in cool, dark location

### 6.3 Complete USDA Pressure Canning Guide

**Essential equipment:**
- Pressure canner (NOT a regular pressure cooker; must hold at minimum 4 quarts; must have pressure gauge and safety valve)
- Rack to elevate jars off canner bottom
- Jar lifter
- Timer

**Pressure canning procedure:**

1. Place rack in canner; add 2–3 inches of hot water (some recipes specify specific amounts)
2. Fill and seal jars per recipe specifications; appropriate headspace is typically 1 inch for most vegetables and meats
3. Load filled jars onto rack in canner
4. Lock canner lid per manufacturer's instructions
5. Place on HIGH heat
6. **CRITICAL — Vent for 10 minutes:** Allow steam to vent freely through open vent/petcock for a full 10 minutes before closing. This exhausts all air from the canner (air pockets create cold spots; jars may not reach required temperature)
7. Close vent/petcock or place weight gauge
8. Allow pressure to build to required level (watch gauge)
9. Start timing when pressure gauge reaches required level
10. Maintain STEADY pressure (small, careful heat adjustments; pressure should fluctuate no more than ±0.5 psi for dial gauge canners)
11. If pressure drops below requirement: Bring back to pressure; restart timing from zero — do not add the remaining time to what you've already completed
12. When time is complete: Turn off heat; allow canner to cool and depressurize naturally — do NOT rush by removing weights or running under cold water
13. For weighted gauge: Wait until weight stops jiggling and no steam escapes
14. For dial gauge: Wait until pressure reads zero; then wait additional 10 minutes
15. Open lid tilting away from your face; allow steam to dissipate
16. Remove jars; cool 12–24 hours; test seals; label

**Complete processing times reference:**

*(Dial gauge at 11 lbs PSI, 0–2,000 ft elevation; weighted gauge at 10 lbs, 0–1,000 ft / 15 lbs above 1,000 ft)*

## 7. FOOD ROTATION: FIFO INVENTORY MANAGEMENT

### 7.1 The FIFO Principle

**First In, First Out (FIFO):** The first food items purchased or produced should be the first ones consumed. Newer stock goes to the back; older stock comes to the front for use.

**Why FIFO matters:**
- Prevents waste from expiration
- Ensures food is always at peak quality and nutritional value
- Maintains a continuously restocked "living" food supply rather than a static emergency pile
- Saves money by consuming before expiration rather than discarding
- Allows you to gradually build a large supply through regular purchases

---

### 7.2 Implementing FIFO — Complete System

**Step 1: Audit and Inventory**
1. Remove everything from storage
2. Group by category (grains, canned goods, dried legumes, fats, etc.)
3. Check all expiration or "best by" dates
4. Discard anything clearly past quality (bulging cans, off-smelling contents, moldy dry goods)
5. Create a master inventory list:
   - Food item and variety
   - Purchase date (or date stored)
   - Best-by/expiration date
   - Quantity and unit (lbs, cans, jars)
   - Location in storage

**Step 2: Organize Storage**
- Shelving rule: Oldest items FRONT; newest items BACK
- Group by category for easy access and rotation
- Items without expiration dates: Label with storage date using permanent marker on masking tape
- Bulk items (bins of rice, flour): Date them with a marker on the container; use from the front

**Step 3: Label All Items**

*Label contents:*
- Item name
- Date stored/purchased
- Date opened (for opened containers)
- Expiration/best-by date

*Label format suggestion:* ITEM / STORED: MM/YYYY / BEST BY: MM/YYYY

**Step 4: Create a Rotation Calendar**

Monthly check:
- Pull items with closest expiration dates
- Incorporate into daily cooking
- Replace consumed items with fresh stock (moving to back)
- Update inventory list

Quarterly check:
- Full review of all storage
- Redistribute items as needed
- Check for signs of pest intrusion, moisture, or other issues

---

### 7.3 Shelf Life Reference for Rotation Planning

**Important distinction:** "Best by" dates indicate QUALITY, not safety. Most properly stored foods remain safe beyond their "best by" date, though flavor and nutrient content may decline.

**Shorter Rotation Priority (rotate every 1–3 years):**

| Item | Shelf Life | Rotation Note |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| Cooking oils | 1–3 years | Check for rancidity (off smell); replace |
| Baking powder | 1–2 years | Test: 1 tsp in hot water should bubble vigorously |
| Flour (white, stored) | 1–2 years | Can extend in freezer; check for rancidity |
| Brown rice | 6–12 months | Oils in bran go rancid; store cool or freeze |
| Nuts and seeds (room temp) | 6–12 months | Freeze for 2–3 year storage |
| Whole grain flour | 1–3 months room temp | Store in fridge/freezer; buy small quantities |
| Crackers | 6–12 months | Reseal tightly after opening |
| Dried pasta (egg) | 2–3 years | Semolina (no egg) lasts longer |
| Canned goods (most) | 3–5 years | Quality degrades; still safe longer |

**Medium Rotation Priority (rotate every 5–10 years):**

| Item | Shelf Life | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| White rice (sealed) | 25–30 years | Rotate every 10 years to maintain quality |
| Dried pasta (plain) | 10–30 years | Sealed; no egg |
| Oats, rolled (sealed) | 10–25 years | O₂ absorbers critical |
| White sugar | Indefinite | Keep dry; no rotation needed |
| Salt | Indefinite | Keep dry; iodized salt loses iodine in 2–5 years |
| Baking soda | Indefinite | Test: Vinegar should bubble vigorously |
| Honey (raw) | Indefinite | May crystallize; reliquify by warming |

|------|-----------|-------|

### 7.4 Oxygen Absorbers and Mylar Bags

**For true long-term storage of grains and legumes:**

**Mylar bags:** Metalized polyester bags that block oxygen, light, and moisture.
- Sizes: 1-gallon (for variety packing), 5-gallon (for 5-gallon bucket inserts)
- Thickness: Minimum 4.5 mil; 7 mil preferred for long-term storage
- Heat seal with flat iron (dry, no steam) or hair straightener; press firmly; no gaps

**Oxygen absorbers:**
- Iron-based packets that absorb oxygen within sealed containers
- Measured in cc (cubic centimeters)
- 1-gallon bag: 300–400 cc absorber
- 5-gallon bag/bucket: 2,000–2,500 cc absorbers
- After opening package of absorbers: Use within 15 minutes; reseal remaining immediately in zip-lock bag
- Test: Freshly active absorbers feel soft; spent absorbers feel hard/rigid

**Process for maximum shelf life:**
1. Fill Mylar bag with dry grain or legumes; tap to settle; do not compress
2. Add oxygen absorbers on top of food
3. Fill to within 4 inches of top
4. Heat seal
5. Place inside 5-gallon bucket with gamma seal lid (protects Mylar; keeps out rodents)
6. Label with contents and date

---

### 7.5 Physical Organization of Food Storage

**Shelving design principles:**
- Store heaviest items on lowest shelves (canned goods, buckets)
- Store most frequently used items at eye level and easy reach
- Group by category, then by rotation date within category
- Leave walking room between shelves for easy access and rotation
- Keep storage area dry, cool (below 70°F ideal), and dark
- Monitor for rodent signs (droppings, gnaw marks); use hardware cloth to seal entry points
- Off the floor: Pallets or boards under all storage (moisture wicking; better air circulation; deters some pests)

**Digital vs. paper inventory:**
- Paper master list: Posted inside storage room door; updated with pencil each use
- Simple spreadsheet (printed copy): Lists all items with amounts and dates
- Either system works; choose one and maintain it consistently

## 8. WILD GAME PROCESSING: FIELD DRESSING AND PRESERVATION

### 8.1 Field Dressing Deer — Complete Protocol

**Essential tools:**
- Hunting/field knife (4–6 inch fixed blade; must be SHARP — dull knives are more dangerous)
- Rubber or latex gloves (minimum 2 pairs; prevent disease transmission; deer carry pathogenic bacteria)
- Small sharpening stone
- Rope (for dragging or hanging)
- Plastic bags (for heart/liver)
- Game bags (muslin or mesh; covers quarters after butchering to keep insects off)

**Step 1 — Act immediately:**
Field dress as soon as possible after the kill. Every minute at body temperature allows bacterial growth in gut. Work fast.

**Step 2 — Positioning:**
- Roll deer on its back
- On slopes: Head uphill allows gravity to keep body cavity contents from rolling toward clean meat
- Spread hind legs and hold open with your knee or have an assistant hold

**Step 3 — Initial cut (belly/abdominal):**
1. Pinch a fold of skin between the hind legs at the base of the ribcage
2. Make a small, shallow starting incision through just skin (not muscle)
3. Insert two fingers of non-knife hand in V shape, straddle the knife blade between fingers pointing up; this prevents cutting into intestines while cutting skin
4. Cut from that starting point down toward the hind legs to the pelvic area; keep cut shallow (just skin and muscle layers)
5. Continue cut upward from starting point toward breastbone/sternum

**Step 4 — Circling the vent:**
1. Make a circular cut completely around the anus, cutting 2–3 inches deep
2. Free the colon/rectum from surrounding tissue completely
3. Tie off the end firmly with a piece of string or zip tie to prevent contents spilling
4. Tuck it back into the body cavity

**Step 5 — Removing the organs:**
1. With belly open, identify the diaphragm (thin muscular wall between chest and abdominal cavity)
2. Cut diaphragm loose from rib cage on both sides
3. Reach up into chest cavity; locate windpipe (trachea) and esophagus at top of chest
4. Cut through both as far up toward head as you can reach
5. Pull downward — heart, lungs, and all abdominal organs should come out together
6. Guide the tied-off colon through the pelvic opening (may need to split the pelvis with a knife)
7. If you want the liver and heart: Separate from the organ mass now; place in plastic bags (excellent nutrition)

**Step 6 — Clean the cavity:**
- Remove any visible intestinal content that spilled
- Rinse with clean water or wipe with dry cloth (in cold conditions, prop open with a stick for air circulation)
- Avoid washing excessively — excess moisture promotes bacterial growth

**Step 7 — Cool the carcass immediately:**
- Temperature above 40°F: Critical — bacteria multiply rapidly
- If cold (below 40°F): Leave chest propped open; hang if possible; let cool naturally
- If warm: Pack chest cavity with ice bags from a cooler
- Do NOT allow carcass to remain above 40°F for more than 2–4 hours before being processed or refrigerated

**Step 8 — Get to shelter:**
- Transport to where you can hang and process
- In a vehicle: If possible, keep in a cooler with ice; otherwise, place in bed of truck with chest cavity propped open for airflow; cover to prevent road debris

**Safety notes:**
- Chronic Wasting Disease (CWD): Where CWD is present, do NOT cut through spine, brain, or spinal cord; avoid lymph nodes (white, round tissue); do NOT consume spinal cord, brain, or eyes; consider testing before eating in known CWD areas
- Tuberculosis: If you find unusual lesions on internal organs, lymph nodes, or inside the body cavity — yellow/white nodules or abscesses — consult wildlife authorities before consuming
- Always wash hands thoroughly after field dressing

### 8.2 Skinning and Quartering Deer

**Hanging for skinning:**
- Gambrel hooks through Achilles tendons of both hind legs
- Hang from a tree branch, vehicle engine hoist, or purpose-built pole
- Height: High enough that animal clears ground by 12+ inches

**Skinning procedure:**
1. Cut around each hind leg just below hock (ankle)
2. Slit inside of each hind leg from hock cut to the center (meeting at the crotch)
3. Begin peeling skin down; use fist to "fist" skin away from muscle where possible (reduces knife contact with meat)
4. Cut around tail
5. Continue pulling skin down over body toward front legs and head
6. Cut around front legs; continue pulling
7. Remove head at atlas joint (first neck vertebra) — feel for joint with fingertip; insert knife tip and work through joint
8. Skin comes completely free

**Quartering:**
1. Remove front shoulders: No ball-and-socket joint; slide knife blade along ribcage; shoulder lifts free
2. Remove hind quarters: Find ball-and-socket hip joint; cut through surrounding muscle; pop joint; cut through remaining tissue
3. Remove backstraps: Two long muscles running alongside spine; slice along spine from rump to shoulders; then cut underneath along rib bones; peel back
4. Remove tenderloins: Inside the body cavity, alongside the spine; small but extremely tender
5. Remove rib meat, neck meat: Trim from bones for stew meat or ground meat
6. Pack all cuts in game bags to protect from insects

**Yield estimate:**
- 150 lb live deer → approximately 75–80 lbs hanging weight (after field dressing)
- 75 lb hanging weight → approximately 50–55 lbs boneless venison (depending on trimming)
- Typical breakdown: ~10 lbs hindquarters each side, ~5 lbs backstrap each side, ~1 lb tenderloin each side, ~10 lbs stew/grind from front shoulders, neck, rib meat

### 8.3 Aging Venison

Aging breaks down proteins via natural enzymes (dry aging); improves tenderness and flavor. Optional for young deer; recommended for mature bucks.

**Requirements:**
- Temperature: 32–45°F (35–38°F ideal)
- Humidity: Moderate (not soggy conditions)
- Air circulation: Some airflow prevents mold; prevents "sour" smell

**Duration:**
| Animal | Minimum Aging | Optimal Aging |
|--------|--------------|--------------|
| Young deer (< 1.5 yrs) | 1–3 days | 3–5 days |
| Adult deer | 3–5 days | 7 days |
| Large, mature buck | 5–7 days | 10–14 days |

**Signs aging is complete:** Dry outer surface; dark color on exterior; no off-odor (slight gamey smell is normal)
**Signs aging went wrong:** Strong offensive odor; green tinge; heavy mold (light white bloom on exterior is normal dry-aging mold and can be trimmed; thick fuzzy mold = discard that portion)

### 8.4 Small Game Processing (Rabbit, Squirrel)

**Rabbit — Field Dressing:**
1. Make a shallow cut around the entire waist of the animal, through skin only, midway between ribs and pelvis
2. Grip the hindquarters firmly; pull with one hand pulling down while other hand holds upper body — skin inverts off both halves like pulling off a sweater; works on fresh-killed rabbits within an hour
3. Remove head; remove feet at joints
4. Open belly cavity; remove organs (intestines, stomach, kidneys, liver — liver is edible)
5. Rinse; keep cold

**Rabbit — Butchering:**
- Cut into parts: Front legs (2); saddle (loin section); rear legs (2)
- Refrigerate at 40°F; use within 3–5 days; OR freeze; OR pressure can (75 minutes pints, 90 minutes quarts)

**Squirrel:**
- Similar to rabbit; smaller; skin same way
- Cook long (older squirrels are tough); braise or pressure cook

### 8.5 Fish Processing and Preservation

**Immediate field care:**
- Kill immediately (spike through brain or use firm strike to head) — reduces stress hormones that affect flavor
- Remove gills immediately (they harbor bacteria and make fish bitter)
- Keep cold (in stream-cooled water, ice, or cold cooler)
- Clean as soon as possible

**Gutting fish:**
1. Insert knife tip into vent (anal opening)
2. Cut along belly to base of head; keep cut shallow
3. Remove organs; rinse cavity
4. Scale if needed: Scrape from tail toward head with back of knife or scaler
5. Rinse thoroughly

**Filleting (larger fish):**
1. Make cut behind head to backbone
2. Turn knife; cut along spine from head to tail; fillet separates from bones
3. Remove skin by pressing fillet skin-side down; cut between skin and flesh at tail end; slide knife forward while holding skin

### 8.6 Fish Preservation Methods

#### Salting (Dry Salt Cure)

**Best for:** Most fish species; especially fatty fish (salmon, trout, mackerel)

**Procedure:**
1. Fillet and dry fish (pat dry with cloth)
2. Coat ALL surfaces generously with non-iodized coarse salt (1 part salt to 3–4 parts fish by weight)
3. Stack fillets in layers in a wooden box or crock, skin-side down, alternating layers with additional salt
4. Cover; weight down with heavy stone
5. Liquid ("fish liquor") will accumulate; drain daily
6. Leave 2–4 weeks depending on fillet thickness

**After curing:**
- Rinse off excess salt
- Air dry 1–2 days (surface should feel firm and dry)
- Store in cool, dry location; wrapped in cloth; keeps months

**To use:** Soak in cold water 12–24 hours (changing water every 4–6 hours) to desalinate; cook as fresh

---

#### Drying (Jerky-Style)

**Best for:** Lean fish (cod, bass, catfish, perch) — fatty fish go rancid when dried without salting first

**Procedure:**
1. Fillet; slice 1/4-inch thick strips along the grain of the muscle
2. Brine in 25% salt solution (1/2 cup salt per quart water) for 30–60 minutes
3. Rinse; pat dry
4. Hang on drying racks in strong airflow (wind, or use fire's radiant heat without direct flame)
5. In sunny, warm, dry conditions: 2–5 days
6. In dehydrator: 160°F for 6–8 hours
7. Finish over smoke for better preservation and flavor

**Doneness:** Should be leathery, stiff; breaks rather than bends when dry

---

#### Hot Smoking

**Best for:** Trout, salmon, catfish, bass, perch

**Brine (pre-smoking):**
- 1 cup non-iodized salt + 1/2 cup sugar + 1 gallon water
- Submerge fish fillets for 4–12 hours (thin fillets 4 hrs; thick 12 hrs)
- Rinse; air dry 1–2 hours (develop a tacky surface "pellicle" — smoke adheres better)

**Smoking procedure:**
1. Build small fire with hardwood (NOT softwood/pine — too much resin and acrid smoke)
2. Best smoking woods: Alder (excellent for fish; traditional Pacific Northwest), apple, cherry, hickory, pecan, oak
3. Allow fire to burn to coals; add wood chips or chunks for smoke
4. Temperature target: 180–225°F air temperature in smoker
5. Place fish on grate; fish internal temperature must reach **180°F and hold for 30 minutes**
6. Total smoking time: 2–6 hours depending on thickness and fat content
7. Fish is done when flaky throughout, deeply colored, and internal temperature has been maintained

**Storage of hot-smoked fish:**
- Room temperature: 1–3 days (with good smoke penetration and low humidity)
- Refrigerated: 1–2 weeks
- Frozen: 6+ months
- Vacuum sealed + frozen: 1–2 years

---

#### Cold Smoking

**Temperature:** Below 80°F (the fish is NOT cooked; only smoke-cured)
- Requires more salt than hot smoking as the only preservation; safe practices are critical
- Traditional process for lox (cold-smoked salmon); gravlax; traditional stockfish
- Cold smoking without subsequent cooking or sufficient salting can allow bacterial growth
- Safest approach: Heavy salt cure (24+ hours) followed by cold smoking, then consume promptly OR hot smoke before serving

### 8.7 Jerky from Wild Game

**Safety protocol (USDA-recommended):**
1. Pre-heat meat to 160°F BEFORE dehydrating (oven at 325°F for 10–15 minutes; or simmer in marinade briefly) — kills E. coli and Salmonella even if dehydrator doesn't maintain that temp
2. Dehydrate at 145–165°F for 4–12 hours

**Simple no-marinade survival jerky:**
1. Slice venison or other game very thin (1/8–1/4 inch) against the grain (easier to chew when dry); freeze 30 minutes first for easier slicing
2. Coat both sides lightly with salt and black pepper
3. Optional: Add smoke flavor (a few minutes over open smoke before drying)
4. Dry in dehydrator at 160°F for 6–10 hours; OR over fire at low heat (120–160°F range) for 12–24 hours; OR air-dry in dry, hot, breezy conditions for 2–4 days (traditional method; less reliable safety)
5. Properly dried jerky bends without breaking; no moisture when bent

**Storage:**
- Room temperature: 1–2 months (if fully dried)
- Refrigerated: 3–6 months
- Frozen: 1+ year
