## 1. SOIL TESTING WITHOUT LAB EQUIPMENT

### The Jar Test (Soil Texture/Composition)
Determines sand/silt/clay ratios — critical for knowing drainage and amendment needs.

**Materials:** Clear glass jar, soil sample, water, 1 drop dish soap
**Procedure:**
1. Fill jar 1/3 with dry soil
2. Add water to nearly full, add one drop dish soap
3. Seal and shake vigorously for 2 minutes
4. Let settle undisturbed for 24–48 hours

**Reading layers (time-based):**
- After 1 minute: measure sediment = **SAND** (coarse, settles first)
- After 5 minutes total: new sediment above sand = **SILT**
- After 24 hours: finest top layer = **CLAY**

**Ideal loam soil:** 40% sand / 40% silt / 20% clay
**Heavy clay:** >40% clay — amend with sand and compost
**Sandy:** >60% sand — amend with compost and clay-rich soil

---

### Vinegar & Baking Soda pH Test (Free)
**Procedure:**
1. Take 2 tablespoons of soil into each of 2 separate jars
2. Add distilled water to make a slurry (important: use distilled, not tap)
3. To Jar 1: add 1/2 cup **white vinegar** → fizzing = **ALKALINE soil** (pH >7)
4. To Jar 2: add 1/2 cup water + 1/2 tsp **baking soda** → fizzing = **ACIDIC soil** (pH <7)
5. No reaction in either jar = **NEUTRAL** (pH ~7)

**Limitation:** No exact number — only indicates direction. Margin of error ±1.5 pH.

---

### Red Cabbage Juice pH Indicator
More nuanced than vinegar test — gives approximate color zones.

**Make the indicator:**
1. Chop 4–5 red cabbage leaves, boil in 2 cups **distilled** water for 10 minutes
2. Steep 30 minutes off heat, strain and reserve purple liquid

**Use it:**
- Add 2 tsp soil to a jar + 2 inches of cabbage water
- Shake, wait 30 minutes
- **Pink/Red** = acidic (pH 4–6)
- **Purple** = neutral (pH 7)
- **Blue-Green/Yellow-Green** = alkaline (pH 8–10)

---

### pH Test Strips
- Use universal strips (0–14) or soil-specific strips (4–9 range)
- Mix soil with distilled water, let settle, dip strip in clear liquid
- Read against color chart within 1 minute
- Cost: ~$12 for 100 strips; accuracy ±0.5 pH units
- **Best option** for precision without a meter

---

| Plant/Weed Present | Soil Indication |
|---|---|
| Dandelions | Alkaline, compacted |
| Sorrel, plantain | Acidic |

**Cultivated plant indicators:**
- Yellowing leaves (interveinal) = iron or manganese deficiency (usually alkaline soil)
- Yellow leaves overall = nitrogen deficiency
- Purple-tinged leaves = phosphorus deficiency
- Blossom end rot (tomatoes, peppers) = calcium deficiency or irregular watering

| Crop | Ideal pH |

| Blueberries | 4.5–5.5 |

## 2. COMPOSTING — RATIOS AND HOT COMPOSTING

### Carbon-to-Nitrogen (C:N) Ratio
- **Ideal C:N ratio:** 25:1 to 30:1
- Too much carbon (>35:1): decomposition stalls, pile cold and slow
- Too much nitrogen (<20:1): smells like ammonia, anaerobic pockets

**Practical ratio:** 3 parts BROWNS : 1 part GREENS by volume

---

### Browns (Carbon-Rich Materials) — C:N Ratios
| Material | C:N Ratio |
|---|---|
| Dry leaves | 60:1 |
| Straw | 75:1 |
| Wood chips/sawdust | 400:1 |
| Cardboard (shredded) | 350:1 |
| Newspaper | 170:1 |
| Corn stalks | 75:1 |

### Greens (Nitrogen-Rich Materials) — C:N Ratios
| Material | C:N Ratio |
|---|---|
| Fresh grass clippings | 20:1 |
| Vegetable scraps | 15:1 |
| Coffee grounds | 20:1 |
| Chicken manure | 7:1 |
| Cow manure | 20:1 |
| Alfalfa hay | 12:1 |

**Warning:** Manure is a "green" despite its color. Alfalfa hay is also a "green."

---

### Hot Composting (Rapid Method — 3 to 8 Weeks)

**Pile size minimum:** 3 ft × 3 ft × 3 ft (1 cubic yard) — smaller piles won't retain heat
**Pile size maximum:** 5 ft × 5 ft × 5 ft — larger piles lack airflow in center

**Temperature targets:**
- **130–160°F (55–71°C):** Active thermophilic zone — kills pathogens and weed seeds
- **131°F+ for 15 days:** USDA standard for pathogen destruction
- Below 110°F: pile is cold composting (slower, less effective pathogen kill)
- Above 170°F: pile overheats, beneficial microbes die — turn immediately

**Moisture:** 40–60% (squeeze a handful — it should feel like a wrung-out sponge, releasing only a drop or two)

**Oxygen:** Turn pile to maintain >10% oxygen concentration

---

### Hot Composting Turning Schedule

| Day | Action |
|---|---|
| Day 1 | Build pile, water if dry |
| Day 3–4 | Check temp; if >130°F, turn; if cold, add more greens |
| Day 5–6 | Turn again |
| Days 7–14 | Turn every 3–5 days |
| Week 3+ | Turning less frequent; pile begins cooling |
| Week 6–8 | Finished compost: dark, crumbly, smells like earth |

**Smell troubleshooting:**
- Ammonia smell = too much nitrogen → add browns and turn
- Rotten egg smell = anaerobic (too wet, no air) → turn and add dry browns
- No smell, no heat = too dry or too much carbon → add water and greens

**Signs of finished compost:**
- Dark brown, uniform, crumbly texture
- Earthy smell (no identifiable original materials)
- Cool to the touch
- Earthworms present (they move in as it cools)

---

### Cold Composting (Passive Method — 6 to 24 Months)
Simply pile materials and wait. No turning required. Does NOT reliably kill weed seeds or pathogens. Good for low-effort systems.

## 3. COMPANION PLANTING GUILDS

### The Three Sisters (Corn + Beans + Squash)
The most proven companion planting system, used by Indigenous peoples for thousands of years.

**Planting sequence:**
1. **Week 1:** Plant corn in mounds 18 inches across, 4 feet apart. Plant 4–6 corn seeds per mound. Wait until corn is 6 inches tall.
2. **Week 3:** Plant 4–6 bean seeds around the base of each corn mound, 6 inches from the corn stalk.
3. **Week 5:** Plant 2–4 squash seeds between mounds. One squash plant per every 2 corn mounds.

**What each sister contributes:**
- **Corn:** Trellis for beans to climb
- **Beans:** Fix atmospheric nitrogen via root nodules (adds 50–200 lbs nitrogen/acre/year)
- **Squash:** Large leaves shade the ground — suppresses weeds, retains moisture, deters pests with scratchy leaves

**Optional 4th Sister:** Sunflowers at the north end of the bed for additional trellis and pollinator attraction.

---

### Tomato Guild
Center plant: tomato

| Companion | Benefit |
|---|---|
| Basil | Repels aphids and tomato hornworm; improves flavor |
| Marigolds | Repel nematodes, aphids, whiteflies |
| Garlic/chives | Repel spider mites and aphids |
| Borage | Repels tomato hornworm; attracts pollinators |
| Nasturtium | Trap crop for aphids (aphids prefer it over tomatoes) |
| Carrots | Loosen soil near tomato roots |

**Avoid near tomatoes:** Fennel, brassicas, corn (shares pests), potatoes (shared blight risk)

---

### Brassica Guild
Center plants: cabbage, broccoli, kale, cauliflower

| Companion | Benefit |
|---|---|
| Marigolds | Repel cabbage moth and aphids |
| Dill, chamomile | Attract parasitic wasps that prey on cabbage worm |
| Nasturtium | Trap crop for aphids |
| Garlic/onions | Repel cabbage looper and aphids |
| Celery | Deters white cabbage butterfly |
| Thyme | Repels cabbage worm |

**Avoid:** Strawberries, tomatoes, other nightshades

---

### Root Vegetable Guild (Carrots + Alliums)
- Plant carrots between rows of onions/leeks/garlic
- Onion scent masks carrot scent → deters carrot fly
- Carrot foliage deters onion fly
- Add rosemary or sage to further repel carrot fly

---

### Potato Guild
Companions: horseradish (plant at corners — repels Colorado potato beetle), beans, cilantro, chamomile
Avoid: tomatoes, cucumbers, squash (shared pests), fennel

---

### Cucurbit Guild (Cucumbers, Squash, Melons)
- Radishes: deter cucumber beetles; plant as a sacrificial trap crop
- Nasturtiums: repel aphids and squash bugs
- Marigolds: general pest deterrent
- Dill: attracts beneficial predatory insects
- Avoid: aromatic herbs (can stunt growth), potatoes

---

### Key Companion Planting Principles
1. **Insectary plants** (yarrow, borage, dill, fennel, Queen Anne's Lace): attract beneficial insects
2. **Trap crops** (nasturtium, radish): draw pests away from main crops
3. **Repellent plants** (marigolds, garlic, mint): emit compounds that deter pests
4. **Nitrogen fixers** (legumes): enrich soil for heavy feeders
5. **Ground cover** (low-growing plants): reduce water loss and weed competition

## 4. SEED SAVING TECHNIQUES — 20+ VEGETABLES

### Core Principles
- Only save from **open-pollinated (OP) or heirloom** varieties — never hybrids (F1)
- Select seeds from the **healthiest, most vigorous, best-producing plants**
- Seeds must be **fully dry** before storage — the "snap test" (seeds snap, not bend)
- Store in **airtight glass jars** in cool, dark, dry location
- Label everything: variety name, harvest year, location

---

### Wet (Fermentation) Method Vegetables
Used for seeds surrounded by gel or fruit pulp. Fermentation removes germination inhibitors and kills seed-borne diseases.

**Universal wet method:**
1. Scoop seeds + pulp into a jar with equal amount of water
2. Stir daily; ferment at room temperature
3. After 1–3 days, good seeds sink; bad seeds and debris float
4. Pour off debris and floating seeds
5. Rinse good seeds through a fine strainer
6. Spread on paper plate or coffee filter to dry (2–4 weeks)
7. Do NOT dry in direct sun or oven

---

### Dry Method Vegetables
Used for seeds in pods, husks, or dry fruits. Seeds mature and dry on the plant.

**Universal dry method:**
1. Leave fruits/pods on the plant well past eating stage
2. Harvest when pods are brown, dry, papery, and seeds rattle
3. Bring indoors; spread in single layer on screens or paper
4. Dry 2–4 more weeks in warm, well-ventilated, shaded area
5. Shell or thresh when completely dry

---

### Vegetable-by-Vegetable Guide

| Vegetable | Method | Isolation Distance | Maturity for Seed | Storage Life |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Tomato** | Wet/ferment 1–3 days | 10–20 ft | Let fruit fully ripen past eating; wrinkle on vine | 4–10 years |
| **Pepper** | Dry on plate | 50–200 ft | Leave on plant until fully colored and starting to wrinkle | 2–3 years |
| **Eggplant** | Dry/rinse | 50–200 ft | Let fruit turn brown/purple-black, past eating stage | 4 years |
| **Green Bean** | Dry on plant | 10–20 ft | Leave pods until dry and rattling on plant | 3–5 years |
| **Pole Bean** | Dry on plant | 10–20 ft | Same as green bean | 3–5 years |
| **Lima Bean** | Dry on plant | 10–20 ft | Brown, dry pods | 3–5 years |
| **Pea** | Dry on plant | 10–20 ft | Pods turn papery, seeds rattle | 3 years |
| **Lettuce** | Dry — shake into bag | 10–25 ft | Flowers turn to white fluffy seed heads; shake daily | 3–6 years |
| **Cucumber** | Wet/rinse | 1/4 mile | Let fruit turn yellow/orange on vine | 5–10 years |
| **Zucchini/Summer Squash** | Wet/rinse | 1/2 mile (C. pepo) | Let fruit grow enormous and harden; leave until frost threatens | 4–6 years |
| **Winter Squash** | Wet/rinse | 1/2 mile (same species) | Fully ripe; seeds inside fully developed | 4–6 years |
| **Watermelon** | Wet/rinse | 1/4 mile | Harvest from fully ripe fruit | 4–6 years |
| **Melon/Cantaloupe** | Wet/rinse | 1/4 mile | Harvest from fully ripe fruit | 5 years |

| **Spinach** | Dry — shake into bag | 1/2 mile (wind-pollinated) | Seeds form along stem after bolting | 3–5 years |
| **Onion/Leek** | Dry in head | 1/4 – 1/2 mile | Year 2: seed heads dry and split | 1–2 years (short-lived!) |
| **Garlic** | Bulb division | N/A (vegetatively propagated) | Save largest bulbs; replant cloves in fall | Replant each year |
| **Potato** | Tuber division | N/A (vegetatively propagated) | Save small to medium tubers; no disease | Replant each year |
| **Radish** | Dry in pods | 1/4 mile | Let pods turn tan and papery | 5 years |
| **Parsnip** | Dry — biennial | 1 mile | Year 2: umbels dry completely | 1–2 years (short-lived!) |
| **Celery/Celeriac** | Dry — biennial | 1/2 mile | Year 2: seed umbels dry and brown | 3–5 years |

---

### Brassica Cross-Pollination Warning
ALL of these cross-pollinate with each other (same species: *Brassica oleracea*):
broccoli, Brussels sprouts, cabbage, cauliflower, collards, kale, kohlrabi

To save pure brassica seed, only grow ONE variety of *B. oleracea* to seed in a season, OR cage plants and hand-pollinate, OR use time isolation (staggered planting by 4+ weeks).

### Squash Species Groups (only cross within species)
- ***C. pepo*:** Zucchini, acorn, delicata, spaghetti, patty pan, most pumpkins, most gourds — CROSS WITH EACH OTHER
- ***C. maxima*:** Buttercup, Hubbard, Red Kuri, Atlantic Giant — CROSS WITH EACH OTHER
- ***C. moschata*:** Butternut — CROSS WITH EACH OTHER
- Different species generally do NOT cross

---

### Seed Storage Best Practices
- **Container:** Airtight glass jar (Mason jar with tight lid)
- **Desiccant:** Add small packet of silica gel OR 2 tbsp dry powdered milk wrapped in tissue
- **Temperature:** 32–41°F (refrigerator) is ideal; consistent cool dark room acceptable
- **Humidity:** Keep below 8% relative humidity inside container
- **Never:** Store in plastic bags long-term (moisture permeable)
- **Test viability before planting:** Germination test — place 10 seeds on damp paper towel, fold, keep warm 7–10 days; count sprouts. 70%+ = good; below 50% = plant extra densely or get new seed

## 5. HEIRLOOM VS. HYBRID SEEDS

### Definitions

**Open-Pollinated (OP):** Pollinated naturally by insects, wind, birds, or self. Seeds saved from OP plants grow "true to type" — offspring resemble parents. All heirlooms are OP.

**Heirloom:** Open-pollinated variety in circulation 50+ years (generally pre-WWII). Passed down through generations. Carries genetic diversity, regional adaptation, and flavor selection. In the public domain — no patents.

**F1 Hybrid:** First-generation cross of two carefully selected, inbred parent lines. Produced by hand-pollination in controlled settings. Seeds are predictable and uniform, often with "hybrid vigor." **Cannot be reliably saved** — F2 generation shows extreme variability (segregation of traits).

**GMO:** Genetically modified in a lab by inserting DNA from unrelated organisms. NOT the same as hybridization. Currently not available to home gardeners for most crops.

---

### Why Hybrids Fail for Self-Sufficiency

When you save F1 hybrid seeds:
- F2 plants are wildly variable — some may resemble neither parent
- Disease resistance, yield, and uniformity bred into the hybrid **disappear**
- You must repurchase hybrid seed every year
- This creates complete dependence on commercial seed suppliers

---

### Comparison Table

| Feature | Heirloom/OP | F1 Hybrid |
|---|---|---|
| Seed saving | Yes — breeds true | No — unreliable |
| Self-sufficiency | High | Zero (must buy annually) |
| Genetic diversity | High | Low |
| Local adaptation over generations | Yes | No |
| Disease resistance | Varies | Often bred in |
| Uniformity | Moderate | High |
| Flavor | Often superior | Variable |
| Cost | Lower | Higher |
| Regional adaptation potential | Improves over generations | Fixed |

**Bottom line for survival/self-sufficiency:** Always prioritize heirloom and open-pollinated seeds. Build a diverse seed bank of regionally adapted varieties.

## 6. CROP ROTATION BY PLANT FAMILY

### Why Rotate
- Breaks pest and disease cycles (pests are family-specific)
- Prevents soil nutrient depletion (families use different nutrients)
- Reduces soil-borne diseases that persist year to year
- Minimum rotation gap: **3–4 years** between same-family plantings in any bed

---

### Plant Families and Members

| Family | Common Name | Members |
|---|---|---|
| Solanaceae | Nightshade | Tomato, pepper, eggplant, potato, tomatillo, ground cherry |
| Brassicaceae | Brassica/Mustard | Broccoli, cabbage, cauliflower, kale, Brussels sprouts, kohlrabi, collards, radish, turnip, arugula, mustard |
| Fabaceae | Legume | Beans (all types), peas, soybeans, lentils, fava beans, clover |
| Alliaceae/Amaryllidaceae | Allium/Onion | Onion, garlic, leek, shallot, chive, elephant garlic |
| Cucurbitaceae | Cucurbit/Gourd | Cucumber, squash (all), pumpkin, zucchini, melon, watermelon, gourd |
| Apiaceae | Carrot/Umbel | Carrot, parsnip, celery, celeriac, dill, fennel, parsley, cilantro |
| Asteraceae | Daisy | Lettuce, sunflower, artichoke, endive, chicory |
| Chenopodiaceae | Goosefoot | Beet, spinach, Swiss chard, quinoa |
| Poaceae | Grass/Grain | Corn, wheat, oats, rye, barley (cover crops) |

---

### 4-Year Rotation Plan

**Bed/Section A → B → C → D → back to A**

| Year | Bed A | Bed B | Bed C | Bed D |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Year 1 | Nightshades | Brassicas | Legumes | Alliums/Roots |
| Year 2 | Alliums/Roots | Nightshades | Brassicas | Legumes |
| Year 3 | Legumes | Alliums/Roots | Nightshades | Brassicas |
| Year 4 | Brassicas | Legumes | Alliums/Roots | Nightshades |

---

### Rotation Logic (What Follows What)

| After This... | Plant This Next |
|---|---|
| Legumes (beans/peas) | Brassicas or corn (they need the nitrogen legumes left behind) |
| Brassicas | Root crops or alliums |
| Nightshades | Root crops, alliums, or legumes |
| Cucurbits | Legumes or root crops |
| Alliums | Brassicas or nightshades |

---

### Key Disease Concerns by Family

| Family | Main Soil-Borne Threats |
|---|---|
| Brassica | Clubroot (*Plasmodiophora brassicae*) — persists 10+ years in soil |
| Nightshade | Late blight, verticillium wilt, bacterial canker |
| Allium | White rot (*Sclerotium cepivorum*) — persists 20+ years |
| Cucurbit | Cucumber mosaic virus, powdery mildew, squash vine borer |
| Legume | Root rot, white mold |

**Special note on Alliums:** Onion white rot can persist in soil for 20+ years. If you have it, stop growing alliums in that bed indefinitely.

---

### Cucurbit and Nightshade Critical Rule
Never follow tomatoes with potatoes or vice versa — they share blight (*Phytophthora infestans*).
Never follow cucumbers with squash — they share cucumber mosaic virus.

---

### Cover Crops for Rotation
Plant in empty beds or between main crop seasons:

| Cover Crop | Benefit | C:N When Incorporated |
|---|---|---|
| Crimson clover | Fixes nitrogen (~100 lb N/acre) | 12:1 |
| Winter rye | Builds organic matter, suppresses weeds | 37:1 |
| Buckwheat | Fast-growing summer cover, attracts beneficials | 23:1 |
| Austrian winter peas | Nitrogen fixation | 15:1 |
| Oats | Winterkill (easy to till under spring) | 25:1 |

## 7. LIVESTOCK BASICS

### 7A. CHICKENS

#### Choosing Breeds
**Dual-purpose (eggs + meat):** Rhode Island Red, Plymouth Rock, Wyandotte, Australorp, Orpington, Dominique
**Best layers:** Leghorn (300+ eggs/year), Sex-Link hybrids, Australorp (world record: 364 eggs/year)
**Meat birds (broilers):** Cornish Cross (ready 6–8 weeks), but cannot reproduce well — use dual-purpose for self-sufficient flock

---

#### Egg Production
- Peak production: hens 6 months to 2 years old
- Average layer: 250–300 eggs/year in prime
- Production drops in winter (responds to day length, not temperature)
- **Artificial light trick:** Add 14–16 hours total light (natural + artificial) to maintain winter production — use 40-watt bulb in coop on timer
- After age 2–3: production drops ~20% per year
- Molting: hens stop laying for 4–8 weeks during annual feather replacement (fall)

**Feed requirements:**
- Laying hens: 16–18% protein feed + calcium supplement (oyster shell or crushed eggshells)
- Broilers: 20–22% protein starter, then 18% grower
- Water: hens drink 0.5–1 pint per day; more in heat
- Free-ranging reduces feed costs and improves egg nutrition (higher omega-3s)

**Space requirements:**
- Coop: 4 sq ft per bird minimum
- Run: 8–10 sq ft per bird
- Nest boxes: 1 box per 3–4 hens

---

#### Butchering Chickens
**Best age for butchering:**
- Cornish Cross broilers: 6–8 weeks (5–7 lb live weight)
- Dual-purpose cockerels (young males): 16–20 weeks
- Old hens (stewing): anytime

**Process:**
1. **Fast** birds 8–12 hours before butchering (empty crop)
2. **Kill:** Cervical dislocation (experienced) or killing cone + sharp knife to jugular
3. **Bleed out:** 2–3 minutes in killing cone head-down
4. **Scald:** Dip in 145–150°F water for 45–60 seconds; feathers should release easily
5. **Pluck:** Remove all feathers immediately while hot
6. **Eviscerate:** Remove head, feet; make incision above vent; remove entrails carefully (avoid breaking intestines)
7. **Save:** Liver, gizzard (cleaned), heart = giblets
8. **Rinse** with cold water; refrigerate immediately
9. **Age:** Let rest 24–48 hours in refrigerator before eating (rigor mortis)

---

### 7B. GOATS

#### Best Dairy Breeds
| Breed | Daily Milk | Fat % | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nubian | 1–2 gallons | 4–5% | Rich milk, cheese |
| Alpine | 1–2 gallons | 3.5% | Volume production |
| Nigerian Dwarf | 1–2 quarts | 6–10% | Small space; rich milk |
| LaMancha | 1–2 gallons | 3.9% | Docile; year-round |
| Saanen | 2–3 gallons | 3.2% | Highest volume |

**Two full-size dairy goats = enough milk for a family of four** plus surplus for cheesemaking

---

#### Milking Routine
- Does must **kid (give birth) to produce milk** — breed in fall, kids born in spring
- Milking begins immediately after kidding
- **Twice-daily milking** required to maintain production (every 12 hours)
- Production peaks 6–8 weeks post-kidding, then gradually declines
- **Dry off** does 2 months before next kidding (stop milking to allow udder rest)
- Lactation length: 9–10 months average

**Milking procedure:**
1. Clean udder with warm water and dry
2. Strip first few squirts into a strip cup (check for mastitis: clumps, blood)
3. Milk fully (10–15 minutes) — incomplete milking reduces production
4. Dip teats in iodine teat dip after milking
5. Strain milk immediately through fine mesh filter
6. Chill to below 40°F within 30 minutes

---

#### Goat Basics
- **Gestation:** 145–155 days (approx. 5 months)
- **Breeding season:** Fall (September–February); does cycle every 21 days
- **Signs of heat:** Tail wagging, vocalization, mucus discharge
- **Bucks (males):** Kept separate from does except for breeding; strong odor
- **Hoof trimming:** Every 6–8 weeks with hoof trimmers
- **Fencing:** Minimum 4 ft; goats escape any weakness — use field fencing + electric top wire
- **Shelter:** Three-sided shed minimum; deep straw bedding
- **Feed:** Quality hay (orchard grass, timothy), browse; limit grain except when milking
- **Water:** 1–3 gallons per day (more when lactating)

---

### 7C. RABBITS

#### Why Rabbits for Self-Sufficiency
- Very high feed-to-meat conversion efficiency (4 lbs feed per 1 lb meat vs. 8:1 for beef)
- Mature for butchering in 8–12 weeks
- Breed rapidly: does can have 4–8 litters/year; 4–12 kits per litter
- Manure is "cold" — can go directly on garden without composting
- Pelts usable for warmth and clothing
- Quiet, small footprint, allowed in many suburban zoning laws

#### Breeds
- **Meat:** New Zealand White, California, Rex, Flemish Giant
- **Dual-purpose:** Rex (meat + fur)

#### Breeding Basics
- **Sexual maturity:** 4–6 months (small breeds), 6–9 months (large breeds)
- **Gestation:** 28–31 days
- **Litter size:** 4–12 kits average
- **Weaning:** 4–8 weeks
- Always bring the **doe to the buck's cage** for breeding (does are territorial)
- Confirm pregnancy by palpation at day 12–14 (feel for marble-sized embryos)
- Provide nest box with straw at day 28

#### Butchering
- **Fryer:** 8 weeks (2–3 lb carcass) — tender, mild
- **Roaster:** 12 weeks (3–5 lb) — more flavor
- **Stewer:** 12+ weeks — best slow-cooked

**Process:**
1. Hold rabbit by hind legs; deliver sharp blow to back of skull behind ears (cervical dislocation)
2. Hang by hind legs; sever head
3. Bleed out 2–3 minutes
4. Make cuts at hocks; peel skin downward like a glove
5. Remove tail; peel skin off completely
6. Eviscerate: cut from vent to sternum; remove organs (save liver, kidneys)
7. Rinse, chill immediately
8. **Age 24–48 hours** before eating

**Rabbit is very lean** — needs added fat when cooking or causes "rabbit starvation" if eaten as sole protein source long-term (protein without fat leads to metabolic problems).

## 8. BEEKEEPING WITHOUT COMMERCIAL EQUIPMENT

### Top Bar Hive (TBH) — The Low-Tech Solution
Oldest style of hive, used since ancient Greece. No frames, no foundation, no extractor needed.

**Construction basics:**
- Long horizontal box (roughly 36–48 inches long × 12 inches wide × 9 inches deep)
- Trapezoidal cross-section (wider at top) mimics hollow log shape
- Bars laid across the top (~1.5 inches wide each — the "bee space" of 3/8 inch between bars is critical)
- Entrance holes at one or both ends near the bottom
- Build from untreated wood (cedar, pine); avoid pressure-treated or painted interior
- **Cost to build:** $30–$60 in materials; or use wildcrafted willow, bamboo

**Bar spacing:** 35mm (1.375 inches) center-to-center for worker comb; bees draw comb down naturally from bars

---

### Acquiring Bees
- **Swarms:** Free; catch swarm in a box by clipping branch or shaking bees in
- **Package bees:** 3 lb package from supplier with mated queen
- **Nucleus colony (nuc):** 5 frames with established colony, brood, queen
- **Cut-out:** Remove established feral colony from wall/tree (advanced)

---

### Basic Management Calendar

| Season | Tasks |
|---|---|
| Spring | Inspect every 7–10 days; watch for swarm cells; add bars if crowded |
| Summer | Inspect every 2 weeks; ensure queen laying; harvest excess honey |
| Fall | Assess honey stores; ensure 60–80 lbs honey for winter; reduce entrance |
| Winter | Do not open hive; check for signs of life on warm days (>50°F) |

---

### Inspection Basics
- **Smoke:** Use cool, white smoke to calm bees before opening
- **PPE:** Veil minimum; gloves and suit recommended for beginners
- Look for:
  - **Queen cells** (peanut-shaped): swarm preparation → remove or split
  - **Eggs and young larvae:** confirms queen is present and laying
  - **Honey stores:** capped white/yellow cells
  - **Brood pattern:** even, capped cells = healthy queen
  - **Varroa mites:** small reddish-brown dots on bees or brood

---

### Honey Harvesting Without Extractor
- In TBH: cut entire comb from bar
- Place comb in cheesecloth over bowl; let gravity drain (crush-and-strain method)
- Warm room (80°F+) speeds draining
- Use remaining wax for candles, salves, polish, waterproofing
- **Never harvest all honey** — leave at least 60 lbs for winter (weight a full hive)

---

### Natural Varroa Mite Management (Without Chemicals)
1. **Brood breaks:** Remove queen for 24 days → no brood → mites starve
2. **Drone comb removal:** Mites prefer drone brood; remove and freeze capped drone comb
3. **Screened bottom board:** Allows mites that fall off bees to drop out of hive
4. **Oxalic acid (organic):** Dissolve in sugar water; drizzle between bars in broodless winter colony — highly effective; considered organic/approved

## 9. AQUAPONICS AND HYDROPONICS WITHOUT ELECTRICITY

### Kratky Method (Completely Passive Hydroponics)
Developed by Dr. B.A. Kratky, University of Hawaii. No pumps, no electricity, no moving parts.

**How it works:**
1. Suspend plant in net cup so roots just touch nutrient solution
2. As plant drinks, water level drops — creates air gap
3. Upper roots access oxygen; lower roots access nutrients
4. Never refill completely — maintain 1–2 inch air gap at all times
5. Refill when 50–70% consumed (with fresh nutrient solution)

**Setup:**
- **Container:** Any opaque container (opaque prevents algae) — mason jar, bucket, tote, barrel
- **Net cups:** 2-inch cups for small plants; 3-inch for larger
- **Growing medium:** Hydroton (clay pebbles), perlite, or rockwool starter cubes
- **Nutrients:** Pre-mixed hydroponic nutrient solution (General Hydroponics MaxiGro or equivalent)

**Best plants:** Lettuce, spinach, kale, basil, cilantro, herbs, bok choy
**Challenging:** Tomatoes, peppers (need larger containers and more support)

**Container size guide:**
- Herbs/lettuce: 1/2 gallon per plant
- Kale/chard: 1 gallon per plant
- Tomatoes: 5 gallons per plant

---

### Wick System (Low-Electricity Option)
- Wicking material (cotton rope, felt) draws nutrient solution from reservoir up into growing medium
- Gravity-fed: reservoir below or beside growing container
- Best for: small herbs and greens
- Limitation: cannot supply enough water for large fruiting plants

---

### Passive Aquaponics (Fish + Plants, No Pump)
**System design:**
- **Grow bed** elevated above **fish tank** (gravity returns water)
- **Bell siphon** or timed manual filling creates flood-and-drain cycle
- Water flows: Fish tank → (manual pour or slow gravity drip) → grow bed → drains back to fish tank via gravity

**Fish choices for low-oxygen tolerance:**
- **Carp/Koi:** Extremely tolerant; can survive in warm, stagnant water
- **Tilapia:** Warm water (70°F+); very hardy
- **Catfish:** Tolerant of low oxygen; grows fast
- Avoid trout/salmon — require cold, highly oxygenated water

**Natural aeration without electricity:**
- Drop water from height (splash aeration)
- Use windmill or water wheel to drive air pump
- Solar panel to run small air pump

**Plant beds:** Fill with gravel or clay pebbles; plant transplants directly; roots filter fish waste
**Fish density:** No more than 1/2 lb fish per gallon of water in passive system
**Feed fish:** Duckweed (grows on pond surface), black soldier fly larvae, kitchen scraps, worms

---

### Duckweed Production (Free Fish Feed)
- Grows explosively on any still water surface with sunlight and nutrients
- Doubles every 24–48 hours under good conditions
- 35–45% protein by dry weight
- Simply harvest surface layer daily; feed directly to fish or chickens

### Diatomaceous Earth (DE)
**What it is:** Fossilized remains of diatoms (single-celled algae). Microscopic sharp edges cut insect exoskeletons, causing death by dehydration.

**CRITICAL:** Use only **food-grade DE** — pool-grade DE is heat-treated and dangerous to lungs.

**Effective against:** Ants, beetles, earwigs, slugs, cockroaches, fleas, mites, squash bugs
**NOT effective against:** Soft-bodied insects (aphids), flying insects that don't contact treated surfaces

**Application:**
- Apply dry as a thin dust on soil around plants and on leaves
- Apply in evening (harms beneficial daytime insects like bees if applied during active hours)
- Reapply after rain — DE loses effectiveness when wet
- Wear a dust mask during application — fine particles irritate lungs

**Rate:** Light dusting; a little goes a long way

### Neem Oil
**What it is:** Pressed from neem tree seeds (*Azadirachta indica*). Active compound: Azadirachtin.
**How it works:** Disrupts insect hormone systems; deters feeding; prevents larvae from molting
**Effective against:** Aphids, spider mites, whiteflies, beetles, fungal diseases (powdery mildew, black spot, rust)

**Standard spray recipe:**
- 2 tablespoons neem oil
- 1 tablespoon liquid castile soap (emulsifier)
- 1 gallon water
- Mix thoroughly; spray all leaf surfaces (top and bottom)

**Application rules:**
- Apply at **dusk or early morning** — never in full sun (causes leaf burn)
- Apply in **cool temperatures** (<90°F)
- Reapply every 7–14 days and after rain
- Avoid spraying open flowers (harms pollinators on contact)

## 12. RAIN CATCHMENT AND GREYWATER IRRIGATION

### Rainwater Catchment

**Calculation:**
- 1 inch of rain on 1,000 sq ft of roof = approximately **600 gallons** collectible
- Formula: Roof area (sq ft) × rainfall (inches) × 0.623 = gallons
- Example: 1,500 sq ft roof × 2 inch rain = 1,869 gallons

**Basic system components:**
1. **Collection surface:** Metal or asphalt roof (avoid treated cedar shakes — chemical leach)
2. **Gutters and downspouts:** Channel water to storage
3. **First flush diverter:** Diverts first 10 gallons (contains bird droppings, debris from roof) — DIY with standpipe tee
4. **Screen filter:** Keep insects and debris out of tank
5. **Storage:** Food-grade 55-gallon drums (free from food industry), IBC totes (275–330 gallons), buried cistern

**First flush diverter (DIY):**
- Install vertical standpipe at downspout junction equal to ~10 gallons capacity
- Water fills standpipe first (contaminated flush); once full, clean water flows to tank
- Small drip hole at bottom of standpipe slowly drains between rains

**Storage tips:**
- Paint tanks **dark** or store in shade — algae needs light to grow
- Keep covered to prevent mosquito breeding
- Elevate tank for gravity-fed distribution (1 psi per 2.3 feet of elevation)

**Water uses:**
- Garden irrigation: no treatment needed
- Livestock: filter through cloth; acceptable
- Drinking/cooking: must boil, filter (ceramic/activated carbon), or UV treat

---

### Greywater Systems

**What qualifies as greywater:** Laundry, shower, bath, sink water — any household wastewater NOT from toilets
**What is blackwater:** Toilet waste — requires septic or composting toilet — NEVER use on edibles

**Greywater reduces household water use by 30–40%**

---

### Simple Laundry-to-Garden System (Easiest, Often Legal)
1. Divert washing machine drain hose out window or through wall
2. Route to mulched basin or fruit tree root zone
3. Use only **plant-friendly soaps** (no boron, bleach, or softeners)
4. **Rotate** application areas to prevent salt buildup
5. Do NOT store greywater more than 24 hours — bacteria multiply

**Approved soaps for greywater:** Oasis, BioKleen, Dr. Bronner's (diluted)

---

### Branched Drain System (Gravity-Fed, No Pump)
For shower/bath diversion:

1. Combine drain lines from greywater fixtures
2. Route through solid pipe (fall: 1/4 inch per foot minimum) away from house
3. Transition to perforated pipe buried in mulch basins around trees/shrubs
4. Cover with 4 inches of wood chip mulch

**Key rules:**
- Never store or pond greywater on surface
- Outlet must be 30+ feet from house foundation
- Plant roots, not edible portions, receive greywater contact
- Do NOT use on leafy greens or root vegetables (pathogens)

---

### Drip Irrigation from Collected Water
**Gravity-fed drip system:**
- Elevate tank at least 3–4 feet for sufficient pressure
- Run 1/2-inch main line from tank; branch to 1/4-inch drip lines
- Emitters: 0.5 or 1.0 GPH drip emitters at each plant
- Simple on/off ball valve controls flow
- Filter: inline Y-strainer prevents clogging

**Water requirement estimates:**
- Tomatoes: 1–2 gallons/day at peak
- Corn: 1 gallon/day
- Lettuce: 0.3–0.5 gallons/day
- Fruit tree: 5–10 gallons/week

## 1. PRESSURE CANNING — COMPLETE GUIDELINES

### Why Pressure Canning is Required for Low-Acid Foods
*Clostridium botulinum* spores survive boiling (212°F). They are destroyed only at **240°F**, achievable only in a pressure canner (10–15 psi). Low-acid foods (pH > 4.6) MUST be pressure canned. Botulism toxin is odorless and tasteless — you cannot detect it.

**Low-acid foods (MUST pressure can):** All vegetables except tomatoes, all meats, poultry, fish, beans, soups, stews

**NEVER** water bath can: green beans, corn, beets, carrots, potatoes, meats, fish, mixed vegetable soups

---

### Equipment Requirements
- **Dial-gauge canner:** Must be tested annually for accuracy; local extension service provides testing
- **Weighted-gauge canner:** Self-regulating; easier; accurate for life
- Canning jars (Mason/Ball/Kerr) — no commercial mayo jars or recycled jars
- New lids each use (bands may be reused if undamaged)
- Jar lifter, bubble remover/headspace tool, lid wand

---

### General Pressure Canning Procedure
1. Inspect jars for cracks or chips; wash in hot soapy water
2. Prepare food (raw pack or hot pack per recipe)
3. Fill jars leaving specified **headspace** (usually 1 inch for vegetables and meats)
4. Remove air bubbles with thin spatula or bubble tool
5. Wipe jar rims with clean damp cloth
6. Apply new flat lids; apply bands fingertip-tight (not over-tightened)
7. Add 2–3 inches water to canner; load filled jars on rack
8. Lock canner lid; heat on high
9. **Vent steam** for 10 full minutes before adding weight or closing petcock — this exhausts air from canner (critical for accurate pressure)
10. Add weighted gauge or close petcock; bring to required pressure
11. **Start timing** once required pressure is reached and stable
12. Maintain steady pressure — do not let it fluctuate dramatically
13. When time is up: turn off heat; let canner **depressurize naturally** — do NOT rush
14. Wait until pressure reaches zero AND at least 10 more minutes before opening
15. Remove lid tilting away from face (steam burns)
16. Remove jars; place on towel 1–2 inches apart; do NOT tilt
17. Let cool undisturbed 12–24 hours
18. Check seals: center of lid should not flex up and down
19. Remove bands; label with date and contents; store in cool, dark place

---

All times at 0–1,000 ft altitude. Dial-gauge: 11 lbs. Weighted-gauge: 10 lbs.

## 2. WATER BATH CANNING — ACIDIC FOODS

### What Can Be Water Bath Canned (pH < 4.6)

## 3. LACTO-FERMENTATION

### How It Works
Naturally occurring *Lactobacillus* bacteria on vegetables convert sugars to lactic acid under salt-protected anaerobic conditions. Lactic acid preserves food and creates signature sour flavor. No heat, no special equipment, no vinegar needed.

**Critical pH:** Ferment must reach pH 3.5–4.0 within 3–7 days to be safe. At this pH, pathogenic bacteria (including botulism) cannot survive.

---

### Salt: The Most Important Variable

**Use only:** Non-iodized salt — kosher, pickling, sea salt, canning salt
**Avoid:** Iodized table salt (iodine kills beneficial bacteria), anti-caking agents

**Why weight-based measurement is critical:**
- 1 tbsp kosher salt (Morton) = 16g
- 1 tbsp kosher salt (Diamond Crystal) = 10g
- 1 tbsp fine sea salt = 18g
- Use a kitchen scale for consistency

---

### Salt Ratios Reference

**Dry-salted ferments (sauerkraut, kimchi, shredded vegetables):**
- 2% = 20g salt per 1kg vegetables = mild, fast fermentation
- 2.5% = 25g salt per 1kg = standard, balanced
- 3% = 30g salt per 1kg = slower, crunchier, saltier

**Brine ferments (whole pickles, vegetables in brine):**
- 2% brine = 20g salt per 1 liter water = very mild
- 3% brine = 30g per liter = standard pickles
- 5% brine = 50g per liter = slow, crunchy, very preserved
- 10% brine = used for pre-salting cabbage for kimchi (rinse before using)

---

### Sauerkraut — Step by Step
**Ingredients:** 5 lbs (2.25 kg) cabbage + 3 tbsp (45g) non-iodized salt

1. Remove outer leaves; set aside one for later
2. Quarter and core cabbage; shred thinly (1/8 inch)
3. Weigh shredded cabbage; calculate 2–2.5% of weight in salt
4. Combine cabbage and salt in large bowl; massage vigorously 5–10 minutes until cabbage releases its liquid
5. Pack tightly into clean glass jar or crock; press down firmly so brine covers cabbage
6. Place reserved cabbage leaf on top; weight down with a clean stone, ziplock bag filled with brine, or fermentation weight
7. **Cabbage must stay submerged under brine** — if not enough liquid, add 2% brine (1 tsp salt per cup water)
8. Cover jar with cloth or loose lid (must allow gas to escape)
9. Ferment at room temperature (65–75°F): taste after 3–5 days
10. Continue 1–4 weeks to desired sourness
11. When done: seal and refrigerate (slows fermentation)

**Timeline:**
- Day 1–2: Bubbling begins (CO2 from bacteria)
- Day 3–7: Mild sour taste; still crunchy
- Day 7–14: More pronounced sour; softer texture beginning
- Day 14–28: Full sour; traditional German sauerkraut flavor
- Months 2–6: Very sour, soft; excellent long-term storage

---

### Kimchi — Basic Method
**Ingredients:** 1 medium Napa cabbage (2 lbs), 1/4 cup non-iodized salt (pre-salting), 2 tbsp gochugaru (Korean red pepper flakes), 4 cloves garlic, 1 tsp ginger, 2–4 green onions, 1 tsp fish sauce or soy sauce (optional)

1. Quarter cabbage; chop into 2-inch pieces
2. Toss with 1/4 cup salt; let sit 1–2 hours; toss every 30 min until wilted
3. Rinse cabbage 2–3 times; squeeze out water; taste (should be pleasantly salty but not overpowering)
4. Mix paste: gochugaru, garlic, ginger, fish sauce
5. Toss drained cabbage with paste and green onions
6. Pack tightly into jar; press down until brine rises above cabbage
7. Leave 1–2 inches headspace (it expands during fermentation)
8. Ferment at room temperature 1–5 days; burp jar daily (open briefly to release gas)
9. Taste daily; refrigerate when reached preferred sourness

---

### Fermented Pickles (Lacto, Not Vinegar)
**Ingredients:** Cucumbers (small, firm, Kirby type), 3–5% brine, dill, garlic, grape leaf or oak leaf (tannins keep pickles crunchy)

1. Wash cucumbers; trim blossom end (contains enzyme that softens pickles)
2. Pack tightly in jar with dill heads, garlic cloves, peppercorns
3. Add 2–3 grape, oak, or horseradish leaves per jar (natural tannins = crunchy pickles)
4. Pour 3.5% brine (35g salt per liter water) to cover cucumbers
5. Weight down to keep cucumbers submerged
6. Ferment at room temperature 3–7 days
7. Taste; refrigerate when desired sourness reached

---

### Troubleshooting Lacto-Fermentation

| Problem | Cause | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| White film on surface | Kahm yeast (harmless) | Skim off; not dangerous |
| Pink/black/green mold | Bad mold — actual problem | Discard batch |
| Too salty | Over-measured salt | Rinse before eating; or use in cooking where salt is diluted |
| Too soft/mushy | Too warm, too long, or no tannins | Use cooler temps; add grape/oak/horseradish leaf |
| Not sour after 7 days | Too cold; too much salt | Move to warmer spot; or add more greens to dilute salt |
| Smells rotten (not just sour) | Contamination or anaerobic failure | Discard |
| Ammonia smell | Too much protein in ferment | Not typical for vegetable ferments; check ingredients |
| Floating vegetables | Not weighted down | Add weight; vegetables above brine will mold |

---

### Other Lacto-Fermented Foods

**Fermented hot sauce:** Blend peppers + 2% salt; ferment 5–7 days; blend smooth; refrigerate
**Fermented salsa:** Chop vegetables + 2% salt; ferment 2–3 days; refrigerate
**Fermented beets:** Cube or slice beets; 2% brine; caraway seeds; ferment 7–14 days
**Fermented turnips:** Quarter turnips + beet slice (color); 3% brine; ferment 7 days
**Kvass (fermented bread drink):** Stale rye bread + water + tiny amount of starter; ferment 2–3 days

## 4. DEHYDRATION

### Temperature Guide by Food Type

| Food Category | Temperature | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Fresh herbs | 95–100°F (35°C) | Higher temps destroy volatile oils |
| Flowers/edibles | 100°F | Preserve color and nutrients |
| Vegetables | 125°F (52°C) | Most vegetables |
| Tomatoes, onions | 145°F (63°C) | Higher sugar content; needs higher temp |
| Fruits | 135°F (57°C) | Start at 145°F for first hour if wet; then reduce |
| Fruit leather | 135°F (57°C) | 6–10 hours |
| Nuts and seeds | 115°F (46°C) | Prevent rancidity |
| Pre-cooked meats | 145°F (63°C) | |
| Raw jerky (pre-cooked per USDA) | 160°F (71°C) internal first, then 130–140°F in dehydrator | Pre-cook meat to 160°F before dehydrating |
| Poultry jerky | 165°F (74°C) internal first | Same process |

---

### USDA Jerky Safety Protocol
1. Marinate meat (if desired) in refrigerator — NOT at room temperature
2. **Before placing in dehydrator:** heat meat in boiling water 5 minutes (beef) or oven at 325°F for 25 minutes (poultry) — brings internal temp to safe level
3. Place in dehydrator at 130–140°F for 4–12 hours until dry
4. Alternative: finish jerky in 275°F oven for 10 minutes after dehydrating

---

### Preparation by Food Type

**Vegetables — must blanch most first:**
- Steam or water blanch before dehydrating to deactivate enzymes
- Blanching times: leafy greens 2–3 min; broccoli 3 min; carrots 3–5 min; green beans 3–4 min; corn 4 min; potatoes 5–6 min
- Slice uniformly (1/4 inch) for even drying
- **Exception:** Onions, tomatoes, peppers — dehydrate raw; no blanching needed

**Fruits — anti-browning treatment:**
- Sulfuring (traditional): expose cut fruit to burning sulfur — lasts longest
- Ascorbic acid dip: 1 tsp vitamin C powder per cup water; soak 3–5 minutes
- Lemon juice dip: 1/4 cup lemon juice per cup water; soak 3–5 minutes
- Honey dip: 1/4 cup honey per 3/4 cup warm water — adds sweetness

### Dryness Tests

**Vegetables:** Should be **brittle or leathery** — no soft or spongy spots. Some will shatter when struck.

**Fruits:** Should be **pliable and leathery** — like a raisin or prune. No stickiness. Should not leave moisture on your hand when squeezed.

**Jerky:** Should **crack but not snap** when bent. No soft or wet spots.

**Moisture content targets:**
- Vegetables: 5–10% residual moisture
- Fruits: 10–20% residual moisture
- Meats: 15–25% residual moisture (lower = longer shelf life)

---

### Conditioning After Dehydrating
After drying, place food in loosely packed glass jar for 7 days. Shake jar daily. If condensation forms inside, food is not dry enough — return to dehydrator. This process equalizes moisture throughout the batch.

| Herbs | 1 year | 2–3 years |
| Jerky | 1–2 months | 6 months (vacuum sealed) |
| Fruit leather | 1 month | 1 year (frozen) |

**Best storage:** Vacuum-sealed mylar bag + oxygen absorber + cool dark place. Extends all shelf lives significantly.

## 5. ROOT CELLARING

### The Four Storage Environments

Different vegetables need different combinations of temperature and humidity. A single root cellar can serve all needs with careful zoning.

| Zone | Temp | Humidity | Foods |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cold & Very Moist (90–95% RH) | 32–40°F | 90–95% | Beets, carrots, parsnips, turnips, celery, celeriac, Jerusalem artichokes, leeks, winter radishes |
| Cold & Moist (80–90% RH) | 32–40°F | 80–90% | Potatoes, cabbage, apples, pears, grapes, kohlrabi |
| Cool & Dry | 32–50°F | 60–70% | Garlic, onions, shallots, dried herbs |
| Moderately Warm & Dry | 50–60°F | 50–60% | Pumpkins, winter squash, sweet potatoes |

---

### Building a Root Cellar

**Best location:** Northeast corner of basement (coolest, darkest)
**Insulate interior walls** (not exterior walls — you want cold from outside)
**Ventilation:** Two pipes — one low (intake of cold air) and one high (exhaust of warmer air). Screen to exclude rodents.
**Dirt floor:** Ideal — maintains humidity naturally. Concrete: place pans of water on floor.
**Humidity management:** Wet burlap bags or open buckets of water raise humidity. Pans of dry lime lower humidity.

**If no basement:** Bury a trash can or barrel in the ground. Pack with straw. Store root vegetables inside with alternating straw layers.

## 6. SALT CURING AND BRINING MEATS

### Why Salt Preserves
Salt draws moisture from meat by osmosis, lowering water activity (Aw) below 0.91 — the threshold where most bacteria cannot grow. Salt also denatures proteins and alters flavor.

**Nitrites (Prague Powder):** Inhibit *C. botulinum* specifically; give cured meat its pink color; required for safe extended preservation of most cured meats.

---

### Types of Curing Salt

**Prague Powder #1 (Pink Curing Salt #1):**
- Contains: 6.25% sodium nitrite + 93.75% salt
- Dyed pink to prevent accidental use as table salt
- Use for: short-cure products (bacon, pastrami, corned beef, hot dogs, fresh sausage) — cured and consumed within 30 days

**Prague Powder #2 (Pink Curing Salt #2):**
- Contains: 6.25% sodium nitrite + 4% sodium nitrate + 89.75% salt
- Nitrate slowly converts to nitrite over weeks/months
- Use for: long-cure products (salami, prosciutto, coppa, country ham) — anything cured longer than 30 days

**Morton Tender Quick:** Pre-mixed with salt and sugar; follow package directions

---

### Equilibrium Cure Method (Safest for Home Curers)

Weigh meat; apply exact percentage of salt and cure. Meat can only absorb what you add — no over-salting possible.

**Standard dry equilibrium cure:**
- Salt: 2.25% of meat weight (about 10g per pound)
- Prague Powder #1: 0.25% of meat weight (about 1.1g per pound)
- Sugar (optional): 0.5–1% for flavor

**Example for 5 lb (2,270g) pork belly (bacon):**
- Salt: 51g (2.25% of 2270)
- Prague Powder #1: 5.7g (0.25% of 2270)
- Brown sugar: 28g (1.25%)

**Procedure:**
1. Mix cure, salt, and any spices
2. Apply evenly to all surfaces of meat; massage in
3. Seal in zip-lock bag or vacuum bag; refrigerate at 36–38°F
4. Cure 7 days per inch of thickness at thickest point
5. Flip bag daily; massage cure into meat
6. Rinse after curing; pat dry; refrigerate uncovered overnight (pellicle formation)
7. Cook, smoke, or hang as appropriate

---

### Wet Brine (Pickle Brine) Method

**Standard wet brine for poultry/pork:**
- Water: 1 gallon
- Salt: 1 cup non-iodized (kosher)
- Prague Powder #1: 1 tsp (if curing for preservation, not just flavor)
- Sugar: 1/4 cup (optional)
- Aromatics: bay, peppercorn, garlic as desired

Submerge meat completely; refrigerate; allow 1 day per 2 lbs of meat, minimum 24 hours.

---

### Curing Time by Product

| Product | Cure Type | Thickness | Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bacon (belly) | Dry equilibrium | 2 inches | 14 days |
| Corned beef brisket | Wet brine | 3–4 inches | 5–7 days |
| Ham (whole) | Dry or injection | 6+ inches | 2 days/lb |
| Pastrami | Wet brine then smoke | 3 inches | 5 days |
| Dry-cured sausage/salami | Dry + PP #2 | Per recipe | 6 weeks to 6 months |
| Gravlax (salmon) | Dry; no nitrites needed | 1 inch | 24–48 hours |

---

### Safety Rules for Curing
- **Always use a kitchen scale** — volume measurements are dangerously imprecise for curing salts
- Cure at **36–40°F** — never at room temperature
- **Never use PP #1 for long fermented sausages** — use PP #2
- **Nitrite maximum:** 200 ppm in finished product (USDA limit)
- Do not cook cured meats to very high temperatures (above 300°F) to minimize nitrosamine formation
- Store cured meats below 40°F; use within recommended timeframe

## 7. SMOKING MEATS

### Hot Smoking vs. Cold Smoking

| Feature | Hot Smoking | Cold Smoking |
|---|---|---|
| Temperature | 190–250°F (smoker) | Below 80°F (70°F ideal) |
| Result | Cooked + smoked | Flavored; NOT cooked |
| Food safety | Cooks food to safe internal temp | Requires pre-curing; does NOT kill pathogens |
| Shelf life | 4 days refrigerated; 3 months frozen | Months without refrigeration (if properly cured) |
| Equipment | Hot smoker, any grill with lid | Cold smoke generator; separate smoke chamber |
| Best for | Ribs, brisket, chicken, turkey, sausage | Bacon, salmon, cheese, salami, country ham |

---

### Hot Smoking — Internal Temperature Targets

| Meat | Smoker Temp | Target Internal Temp | Approximate Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| Beef brisket | 225°F | 195–205°F | 1–1.5 hrs/lb |
| Beef ribs | 225°F | 195–200°F | 6–8 hrs total |
| Pork shoulder/butt | 225°F | 195–205°F | 1–1.5 hrs/lb |
| Pork ribs (baby back) | 225°F | 185–195°F | 4–6 hrs total |
| Whole chicken | 250°F | 165°F breast/thigh | 3–4 hrs |
| Turkey (10–14 lb) | 250°F | 165°F | 30 min/lb |
| Whole fish/salmon | 200–225°F | 145°F | 2–4 hrs |
| Pork sausage | 225°F | 160°F | 1–2 hrs |
| Venison | 225°F | 145°F minimum | 1 hr/lb |

**The "Stall":** Brisket and pork shoulder plateau at ~165°F for several hours while evaporative cooling counteracts heat. Normal — maintain temp and wait. Wrapping in butcher paper speeds through the stall.

**Stop adding wood smoke** once meat reaches ~140°F internal — smoke uptake diminishes after this point.

---

### Wood Selection Guide

| Wood | Flavor | Best Pairing | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hickory** | Strong, bacon-like, classic BBQ | Pork, ribs, brisket, beef | Most popular US BBQ wood; can be overpowering if overused |
| **Mesquite** | Bold, earthy, strong | Beef, Texas-style | Burns very hot; use sparingly; can turn bitter |
| **Oak** | Medium, versatile, clean | Any meat | Best beginner wood; rarely overpowers |
| **Cherry** | Sweet, mild, fruity; adds red color | Poultry, pork, duck | Excellent for color on poultry skin |
| **Apple** | Very mild, sweet | Poultry, pork, fish | Excellent for delicate meats |
| **Pecan** | Mild hickory-like | Pork, poultry, beef | More refined than hickory |
| **Alder** | Delicate, slightly sweet | Fish (especially salmon) | Traditional Pacific Northwest fish smoke |
| **Maple** | Sweet, mild | Poultry, vegetables, ham | Great for sweeter profiles |

**NEVER use:** Pine, cedar, cypress, elm, eucalyptus, any treated/painted wood, plywood — all produce toxic compounds.

**Wood forms:** Chunks (best for long smokes), chips (fast; soak 30 min), pellets (pellet smokers)

---

### Cold Smoking (For Preservation)
**Critical:** Cold smoking DOES NOT cook food. Must cure first.

**Temperature range:** 68–86°F (20–30°C); keep below 90°F strictly
**Humidity:** 65–80%

**Process for cold-smoked bacon:**
1. Cure belly with equilibrium dry cure (PP #1) for 14 days
2. Rinse; pat dry; refrigerate uncovered 24 hours (pellicle forms — tacky surface helps smoke adhere)
3. Cold smoke at 68–75°F for 6–12 hours
4. Rest in refrigerator 24 hours before slicing
5. Cook before eating (hot smoke or pan-fry)

**Cold-smoked salmon (gravlax → smoked):**
1. Cure salmon fillet with equal parts salt and sugar (by weight) for 24–48 hours
2. Rinse; dry; form pellicle (refrigerate uncovered 4–8 hours)
3. Cold smoke at 70–80°F for 4–8 hours
4. Refrigerate; use within 2 weeks or vacuum seal and freeze

## 8. RENDERING FAT (LARD AND TALLOW)

### Why Render Your Own Fat
- Lard and tallow are stable cooking fats with high smoke points (375°F lard; 420°F+ tallow)
- Free of industrial processing, trans fats, or hydrogenation
- Rich in fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K)
- Long shelf life (1 year refrigerated; 4–6 months room temp sealed)
- Essential for frying, baking, pastry, and preservation (confit, rillettes)

**Lard** = rendered pork fat
**Tallow** = rendered beef or mutton fat (suet)
**Schmaltz** = rendered chicken/duck/goose fat
**Leaf lard** (from around kidneys and organs) = highest quality; nearly flavorless; best for pastry

---

### Sourcing Fat
- Ask a butcher for "suet" (beef kidney fat) or "fatback/leaf lard" (pork)
- After butchering: trim all fat; refrigerate or freeze until ready to render
- Fat can be stored frozen for months before rendering

---

### Wet Rendering Method (Recommended)

**Produces:** Whiter, milder, cleaner fat — best for pastry and general cooking

1. Cut fat into 1-inch cubes (or grind through meat grinder — speeds rendering significantly)
2. Place fat and 1 cup of water per pound of fat in heavy pot
3. Heat on low-medium; fat should slowly melt and bubble gently
4. Stir every 20–30 minutes to prevent sticking
5. Cook 1–2 hours until cracklings (solid bits) are golden and floating or sinking
6. Strain through fine mesh lined with cheesecloth into jars or a bowl
7. As it cools, fat solidifies on top of water layer
8. Once solid, remove fat disk from water surface
9. **Second rendering:** Melt again with fresh water; strain — produces purer, whiter, more neutral-smelling product
10. Pour into hot sterilized jars; seal; cool; label

**Temperature:** Keep at 220–275°F — bubbling = water evaporating (good); brown color = too hot

---

### Dry Rendering Method

**Produces:** Slightly more flavorful, yellower fat; cracklings as bonus product

1. Cut or grind fat into small pieces
2. Place in heavy pot or slow cooker; NO water added
3. Low heat (150–200°F); stir every 30 min
4. Cook until cracklings are golden brown and most fat has liquefied (2–4 hours)
5. Strain carefully through cheesecloth into jars
6. **Risk:** Can scorch easily — watch temperature

**Cracklings (chicharrónes):** The solid bits left after straining — season with salt, eat as snack or add to cornbread

---

### Storage

| Storage Method | Duration |
|---|---|
| Room temperature (sealed) | 4–6 months |
| Refrigerator | 10–12 months |
| Freezer | 2–3 years |
| Canning (pressure canned) | 1+ year shelf stable |

**Signs of rancidity:** Yellow-orange color (lard should be white); sour or chemical smell; bitter taste
**Prevention:** Keep away from light, heat, and air; use clean utensils every time

## 9. CHEESEMAKING WITHOUT COMMERCIAL CULTURES

### Natural Starter Cultures Available to Home Cheesemakers

**1. Kefir grains:** Purchase once, maintain forever. Add to fresh milk, incubate at room temp overnight, strain. Use the fermented liquid as a starter for any mesophilic cheese.

**2. Clabber:** Raw milk left at 70–75°F for 24 hours forms a naturally fermented culture. Use 2 tbsp per quart of fresh milk. Works best with high-quality raw milk from healthy animals.

**3. Whey from previous batch:** Save 1–2 cups of whey from a successful cheese; use as starter for next batch. Traditional method for Parmigiano Reggiano and many other cheeses.

**4. Sourdough starter:** Can inoculate milk for simple fresh cheese.

---

### Natural Rennet Sources
**Commercial rennet** (animal or vegetarian) is most reliable — stock up.
**Natural plant rennets:**
- **Fig branch/leaves:** Latex from fresh fig branches coagulates milk at room temperature
- **Nettles (stinging):** Infuse fresh nettles in boiled water; add to warm milk
- **Lady's bedstraw (*Galium verum*):** Traditional European plant rennet
- **Thistle flowers:** Used in traditional Portuguese cheese (Serpa, Serra da Estrela)
- Limitation: Plant rennets produce softer, less predictable coagulation; must test quantities

---

### Simple Fresh Cheeses (Beginner-Friendly)

**Fromage Blanc / Quark (no rennet):**
1. Warm 1 gallon milk to 75–80°F
2. Add 1/4 cup kefir or clabber as starter
3. Let set at room temperature 12–24 hours until thick and tangy (like yogurt)
4. Pour into cheesecloth; hang and drain 4–12 hours
5. Salt to taste; refrigerate. Ready immediately. Shelf life: 1–2 weeks refrigerated

**Farmer's Cheese / Queso Fresco:**
1. Heat 1 gallon whole milk to 195°F (not quite boiling)
2. Add 1/4 cup white vinegar or lemon juice; stir gently
3. Curds form immediately; remove from heat; let sit 10 minutes
4. Strain through cheesecloth; drain 30–60 minutes
5. Salt; press in mold; refrigerate. Ready same day.

---

### Process for Aged Cheeses (Intermediate)

**Cheddar-style (using kefir as starter):**
1. **Ripen:** Heat 2 gallons whole milk to 86°F; add 1/4 cup kefir; stir; let ripen 30 minutes
2. **Add rennet:** Dilute 1/4 tsp liquid rennet in 1/4 cup cool water; stir into milk gently; stop stirring
3. **Set:** Let sit undisturbed 45–60 minutes at 86°F until clean break (insert knife; curd should split cleanly)
4. **Cut curd:** Cut into 1/2-inch cubes with long knife
5. **Cook curd:** Slowly raise temperature to 102°F over 45 minutes, stirring gently; this releases whey and firms curd
6. **Drain:** Pour into cheesecloth-lined colander; drain whey
7. **Cheddar:** Mill curd into pieces; salt at 2% of curd weight; press at 10–50 lbs for 12–24 hours, flipping every few hours
8. **Air dry:** Remove from mold; age at 50–55°F, 80–85% humidity, turning daily until rind forms (1–2 weeks)
9. **Wax or bandage:** Coat with cheese wax or wrap in cloth with lard rub
10. **Age:** 60 days minimum for mild cheddar; 6+ months for sharp

---

### Testing Raw Milk Quality for Natural Fermentation
Place a small sample (2 oz) of raw milk in a clean jar at 72°F. Check at 12 and 24 hours:
- Forms yogurt-like curd in 12–24 hours with slightly sour smell = excellent quality; proceed
- Still liquid at 24 hours = insufficient bacteria or antibiotic residue — do not use for natural fermentation
- Bitter, putrid smell = contaminated milk — do not use

---

### Critical Temperatures for Cheesemaking
- **Mesophilic cultures** (most fresh and cheddar-type): work at 68–86°F
- **Thermophilic cultures** (Swiss, Italian types): work at 92–110°F
- **Rennet action:** Optimum 86–104°F; stops below 55°F and above 120°F
- **Pasteurization** (if desired): 145°F for 30 min (vat method) or 161°F for 15 seconds (HTST)

## 10. GRAIN STORAGE

### The Critical Rule: Moisture Below 10%
Food with more than 10% moisture + oxygen absorbers + sealed container = botulism risk.
**Never seal high-moisture food in airtight containers.**

**Test grain moisture:** Bite a kernel — it should be very hard, not at all yielding. No flexibility. For home storage, commercially dried grain from grain elevators is generally dry enough.

---

### Mylar Bag + Oxygen Absorber Method

**Best for:** Wheat, white rice, corn, oats, dried beans, pasta, flour, powdered milk, salt, sugar (most dry goods)

**Oxygen absorber sizing:**
| Container Size | Absorber Size |
|---|---|
| 1 gallon bag/jar | 100–300cc |
| 1-gallon bag (dense grains) | 300cc |
| 5-gallon bag | 2000–2500cc |
| 6-gallon bucket | 2000–3000cc |

**Procedure:**
1. Freeze grain 3–5 days (kills insect eggs) — allow to return to room temperature before sealing
2. Work quickly with oxygen absorbers — they activate on contact with air
3. Fill mylar bag inside bucket (bucket provides structural support and rodent resistance)
4. Drop oxygen absorber(s) into bag on top of grain
5. Seal bag with household iron or flat iron (seal 1/4 inch from top at 375°F for 3–5 seconds)
6. Seal bucket with airtight gamma lid
7. Label with contents, date, and weight
8. Within 4 hours: bag should begin to vacuum against grain (oxygen consumed by absorber)
9. If bag does NOT tighten within 24 hours: absorber may be exhausted; replace

**Storage location:** Cool, dark, dry. Every 10°F decrease in temperature roughly doubles shelf life.

---

### Shelf Life by Grain

| Grain/Food | Optimal Storage Life | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Hard winter/spring wheat | 25–30 years | Best long-term staple |
| White rice | 25–30 years | Brown rice only 1–2 years (oils go rancid) |
| Rolled oats | 20–30 years | |
| Corn (field/dried) | 20–25 years | |
| Dried beans/lentils | 20–30 years (nutrition) | Edible but harder to cook over time |
| Pasta | 20–30 years | |
| Flour (white) | 5–10 years | |
| Powdered milk | 20–25 years | |
| Sugar | Indefinite | Does not need oxygen absorber |
| Salt | Indefinite | Does not need oxygen absorber |
| Honey | Indefinite | Store in sealed container |
| Instant coffee | 20–25 years | |
| Hard candy | 25+ years | |

**Brown rice exception:** Contains oils in bran layer → goes rancid in 1–2 years even when sealed. Store only white rice for long-term.

---

### Threats to Grain Storage

**Insects:** Grain weevils, flour beetles, meal moths — freezing before storage kills eggs
**Rodents:** Store in metal cans or hard plastic buckets; mylar alone does NOT stop rodents
**Moisture:** Even small moisture intrusion ruins entire batch — inspect containers periodically
**Light:** Degrades vitamins; store in dark location
**Heat:** Every 10°F above 70°F shortens shelf life significantly

---

### Rotating Stock (FIFO)
"First In, First Out" — use oldest stock first, replace with fresh. Mark each container with purchase/storage date. Check grain annually; look for:
- Insect frass (fine powder or webbing)
- Off smell (musty, sour, rancid)
- Clumping (moisture infiltration)
- Discoloration

## 11. MAKING VINEGAR FROM SCRATCH

### The Two-Stage Process

**Stage 1 — Alcoholic fermentation (yeast):**
Sugar → Alcohol (wine/cider/beer)

**Stage 2 — Acetic acid fermentation (acetobacter bacteria):**
Alcohol + Oxygen → Acetic acid (vinegar)

These are sequential — alcoholic fermentation must complete before acetic fermentation begins.

---

### Apple Cider Vinegar (ACV) — Full Process

**Ingredients:** 10–12 apples (any variety), water, optional 1 tbsp raw ACV with mother (starter)

**Part 1 — Make the Cider (4–6 weeks):**
1. Wash and chop apples (core, skin, all); pack into wide-mouth glass jar
2. Dissolve 1 tbsp sugar per cup of water; pour over apples to cover
3. Add 1 tbsp raw ACV with mother (optional, speeds fermentation)
4. Cover with cheesecloth secured with rubber band — allows air, keeps bugs out
5. Push down apple pieces daily (keep submerged)
6. After 2–3 weeks: apple pieces sink, liquid turns cloudy with light bubbling
7. Strain out apple pieces; return liquid to jar
8. Cover with cloth; let sit 2–4 more weeks

**Part 2 — Convert to Vinegar (4–8 weeks):**
1. Fermented cider now 4–7% alcohol — ready for acetobacter
2. Keep covered with cloth (acetobacter NEEDS oxygen)
3. Stir daily; keep at 60–80°F
4. White-beige mat forms on surface = the "mother" — acetobacter colony; this is good
5. After 4–8 weeks: taste regularly; sharp, sour, vinegary = done
6. Taste test: should taste like vinegar with no alcoholic taste remaining
7. Strain through cloth; bottle in clean glass; seal

**Note:** Do not use metal containers during fermentation — acid corrodes metal.

---

### Vinegar From Other Bases

| Base | Alcohol Source | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Apple scraps/juice | Wild ferment or wine yeast | Apple cider vinegar |
| Grape juice/wine | Wine yeast → allow to turn | Wine vinegar |
| Honey + water | Mead | Mead vinegar (rare; excellent) |
| Grain (rice, barley) | Sake or beer | Rice vinegar, malt vinegar |
| Any fruit | Wild ferment | Fruit vinegar |

---

### Maintaining a Mother of Vinegar
- Save 1–2 cups of raw vinegar with visible mother from each batch
- Use as starter for next batch — adds acetobacter directly
- Store mother in small jar of vinegar at room temperature; feed occasionally with small amounts of wine/cider
- A healthy mother lasts indefinitely and becomes more potent over time

---

### Testing Vinegar Acidity
Commercial vinegar is standardized at 5% acidity. Homemade vinegar varies. For canning recipes requiring ≥5% acidity:
- Test with pH strips: should read pH 2.5–3.5
- For canning safety: use commercial vinegar with labeled ≥5% acidity
- Homemade vinegar is safe for salad dressings, cooking, and preservation but should not be used in canning recipes unless tested with titration kit

## 12. WILD FOOD FORAGING — 20 MOST COMMON EDIBLE PLANTS

### Golden Rules of Foraging
1. **100% positive identification before eating anything** — use multiple references; Latin names are definitive
2. **When in doubt, leave it out**
3. Learn the **dangerous lookalikes** in your region first
4. Start with **small quantities** of any new plant (2 oz max) in case of individual sensitivity
5. Avoid harvesting from roadsides (vehicle contamination), treated areas, or near industrial sites
6. Learn plants through all seasons — many are recognizable at one stage but not another
7. **Never harvest more than 1/3** of any plant population — leave enough for regeneration and wildlife

---

### The 20 Most Common Edible Wild Plants

---

**1. Dandelion (*Taraxacum officinale*)**
**Where found:** Lawns, fields, roadsides — virtually everywhere in North America
**Edible parts:** All parts — roots, leaves, flowers
**Season:** Spring (young leaves best), summer flowers, fall roots
**Identification:** Rosette of toothed leaves (teeth point back toward center), hollow stems, single yellow flower per stem, white milky sap, no hair on leaves
**Lookalike concerns:** Chicory (blue flowers), wild lettuce (taller, branching), cat's ear (hairy leaves) — all edible
**Uses:**
- Young leaves: raw salad (bitter); cook reduces bitterness
- Flowers: tempura batter and fry; make wine; add to salads
- Root: roast until dark brown; grind as coffee substitute

---

**2. Cattail (*Typha latifolia*, *T. angustifolia*)**
**Where found:** Wetland margins, pond edges, ditches across North America
**Edible parts:** Almost the entire plant depending on season
**Season:** Spring through fall
**Identification:** Tall (4–8 ft) grass-like plant; distinctive brown cigar-shaped seed head; ribbon-like leaves; grows in water
**No dangerous lookalikes** (iris may grow nearby but has different leaves; toxic)
**Uses by season:**
- Early spring: dig rhizomes (starchy roots); peel and eat raw or cook; grind dried roots into flour
- Spring: young green shoots (inner core of stem); eat raw or cooked like asparagus
- Spring/early summer: pollen (brilliant yellow coating on male flower spike above brown head); collect by bending spike into bag; use as flour supplement — very nutritious
- Early summer: green female head; boil and eat like corn on the cob
- Fall/winter: rhizomes starchiest; can be dried and ground to flour

---

**3. Lamb's Quarters (*Chenopodium album*)**
**Where found:** Gardens, disturbed soil, fields — common summer weed
**Edible parts:** Young leaves and stems; seeds when mature
**Season:** Spring through fall before frost
**Identification:** Arrow-shaped leaves with powdery/mealy white coating on undersides; small green flowers in clusters; stems often have pink/red streaks; grows 2–6 ft tall
**Uses:** Cook like spinach; higher in calcium and vitamins than spinach; mild flavor; excellent sautéed with garlic; seeds can be cooked like quinoa (which is in same family)

---

**4. Purslane (*Portulaca oleracea*)**
**Where found:** Garden beds, sidewalk cracks, disturbed soil — summer weed
**Edible parts:** All above-ground parts
**Season:** Summer through early fall
**Identification:** Fleshy succulent stems (red/green); paddle-shaped thick succulent leaves; low-growing flat rosettes; tiny yellow 5-petaled flowers; tastes slightly sour/lemony
**Lookalike warning:** Spurge (*Euphorbia*) — TOXIC; has milky white sap when broken; thinner leaves; grows upright. Purslane has NO milky sap.
**Uses:** Raw in salads; cooked in stews; stir-fry; pickle; one of the best plant sources of omega-3 fatty acids; contains more omega-3s than any other leafy green

---

**5. Chickweed (*Stellaria media*)**
**Where found:** Cool-season weed in gardens, fields, shaded areas
**Edible parts:** Leaves, stems, flowers
**Season:** Early spring and fall (dies in summer heat)
**Identification:** Low-growing; pairs of oval leaves; tiny white star-shaped flowers with 5 deeply notched petals (looks like 10); definitive ID: **single line of fine hairs on one side of stem that alternates sides at each node** — unique to chickweed
**Uses:** Best raw in salads; mild, pleasant flavor; also as cooked green; rich in vitamins C, B, and minerals

---

**6. Stinging Nettle (*Urtica dioica*)**
**Where found:** Moist, nitrogen-rich soil; woodland edges; stream banks
**Season:** Early spring best; harvest before flowering
**Identification:** Toothed, heart-shaped leaves in opposite pairs; covered with stinging hairs; square stem; grows 3–7 ft; stinging sensation on contact is itself an ID characteristic
**Uses:**
- Wear gloves to harvest; top 4–6 inches of young spring growth
- Cooking instantly neutralizes sting: boil, steam, sauté, or dry
- Nutritional powerhouse: iron, calcium, magnesium, vitamins A, C, K
- Use exactly like spinach; excellent in soup, pasta, pesto, tea
- Dried: make into tea; store for winter nutritional supplement

---

**7. Wood Sorrel (*Oxalis* spp.)**
**Where found:** Forests, lawns, garden beds — widespread
**Edible parts:** Leaves, flowers, seed pods
**Identification:** Three heart-shaped leaflets (like a shamrock); close at night; small yellow, pink, or white flowers; seed pods look like tiny okra
**Taste:** Tart, lemony — contains oxalic acid
**Caution:** Oxalic acid — eat in moderation; avoid if prone to kidney stones
**Uses:** Excellent trail nibble; add to salads for lemon flavor; squeeze juice into water for refreshing drink

---

**8. Plantain (*Plantago major*, *P. lanceolata*)**
**Where found:** Lawns, paths, roadsides, disturbed ground
**Edible parts:** Leaves, seeds
**Season:** Spring through fall
**Identification:** Broad plantain (*P. major*): oval ribbed leaves in basal rosette; parallel veins; short broad seed spike. Narrow plantain (*P. lanceolata*): lance-shaped leaves; taller seed spike
**Uses:**
- Young leaves: raw in salad (bitter, chewy); best cooked; cook like spinach
- Older leaves: tough; best in soups or as cooked green
- Seeds: thresh and grind; used as psyllium husk (fiber supplement); add to bread
**Medicinal note:** Crushed fresh leaves applied to insect stings and wounds — traditional anti-inflammatory

---

**9. Wild Violet (*Viola* spp.)**
**Where found:** Woodland edges, lawns, shaded gardens
**Edible parts:** Leaves and flowers
**Season:** Spring and early summer for flowers; leaves available longer
**Identification:** Heart-shaped toothed leaves; 5-petaled purple (or white/yellow) flowers; 2 upper petals, 3 lower; grows 4–8 inches tall
**Uses:** Flowers and leaves in salads; flowers candied; leaves cooked like spinach; flowers made into jelly; very high in vitamins C and A

---

**10. Wild Garlic / Ramps (*Allium ursinum*, *Allium tricoccum*)**
**Where found:** Moist deciduous woodlands; *Allium vineale* (field garlic) in lawns and fields
**Season:** Early spring leaves (March–May); ramps especially prized; bulbs available longer
**Identification:** Broad smooth leaves; distinct garlic/onion smell when crushed — this smell is the definitive ID; bulbs in soil; white flowers later in season
**Critical lookalike warning:** Lily of the valley (*Convallaria majalis*) — EXTREMELY TOXIC; similar leaves but NO garlic smell. ALWAYS smell before eating any Allium.
**Uses:** Leaves used raw in salads or cooked; bulbs like scallions; pickle; ferment; dry; pesto

---

**11. Elderberry (*Sambucus canadensis*, *S. nigra*)**
**Where found:** Forest edges, roadsides, stream banks; widely distributed
**Season:** Flowers May–July; berries August–October
**Identification:** Shrub or small tree 5–12 ft; compound leaves (5–11 leaflets); large flat-topped white flower clusters; small dark purple-black berries in drooping clusters
**Lookalike warning:** Pokeweed has similar berries but single-stemmed umbels and TOXIC red-purple sap; elderberry branches have opposite compound leaves
**Caution:** Raw berries, leaves, stems, and bark are toxic (cyanogenic glycosides causing nausea). Always COOK berries before eating.
**Uses:**
- Flowers: edible raw; tempura fry; elderflower cordial; elderflower fritters
- Cooked berries: jams, jelly, pies, elderberry syrup (immune-supporting), wine
- Elderberry syrup: simmer berries with water, strain, add honey — traditional cold remedy

---

**12. Blackberry / Raspberry (*Rubus* spp.)**
**Where found:** Forest edges, clearings, roadsides — extremely common
**Edible parts:** Berries, young leaf tips (dried for tea)
**Season:** Summer (berries)
**Identification:** Thorny canes; compound leaves with 3–5 leaflets; white 5-petaled flowers; aggregated berries
**Blackberry vs. Raspberry:** Blackberry has solid core; raspberry is hollow when picked
**No toxic lookalikes** — aggregate berries of Rubus are safe; identify by thorny canes and characteristic berry structure
**Uses:** Eat fresh; jams; jellies; pies; wine; dry for storage; young leaves dried for tea (high in tannins — good for diarrhea)

---

**13. Hawthorn (*Crataegus* spp.)**
**Where found:** Hedgerows, forest edges, fields
**Season:** Flowers spring; berries fall
**Identification:** Thorny shrub/small tree; deeply lobed leaves; white flower clusters; small red-orange berries (haws) September–November
**Uses:**
- Berries: high pectin — excellent for jelly; also in wine, ketchup-style sauce, fruit leather
- Remove seeds before processing (seeds contain cyanogenic compounds; small amounts unlikely to cause harm but avoid large quantities of raw seeds)
- Flowers: edible; add to salads; make tea
- Leaves: young spring leaves edible in salads (mild, nutty)

---

**14. Rose Hip (*Rosa* spp.)**
**Where found:** Wild roses at forest edges, roadsides, thickets
**Season:** Late summer through winter
**Identification:** Thorny rose shrub; familiar rose flowers; round to oval red-orange hips develop after petals fall
**Uses:**
- Highest vitamin C of any plant (20× more than oranges per weight)
- Remove seeds and hairs before eating (hairs irritate digestive tract)
- Make tea, jam, jelly, syrup, wine, soup
- Dry for long-term vitamin C supplement
- Slice open hip; scoop out seeds and hairs with small spoon; use fleshy outer part

---

**15. Acorns (*Quercus* spp.)**
**Where found:** Wherever oaks grow — most of North America
**Season:** Fall collection
**All acorns are edible** — tannin content varies by species
**White oak acorns** (rounded lobes on leaves, no bristle tips): lower tannins; less processing needed
**Red/black oak acorns** (bristle tips on leaf lobes): higher tannins; require more leaching
**Processing (leaching tannins):**
1. Shell and grind into coarse meal
2. Place in cloth bag or colander; run cold water over continuously for several hours (cold leaching)
3. OR place in pot of water; boil 15–30 min; pour off dark water; add fresh; repeat 4–8 times until water runs clear and taste is no longer bitter
4. Test: eat small amount; should have no bitter astringency
5. Dry the leached meal; use as flour (mix with wheat flour; 25–50% substitution)
**Uses:** Acorn flour bread, pancakes, porridge; roast whole (sweet); acorn coffee

---

**16. Wild Strawberry (*Fragaria vesca*, *F. virginiana*)**
**Where found:** Open fields, forest edges, roadsides
**Season:** Spring–early summer
**Identification:** Three leaflets with serrated edges; white 5-petaled flowers; tiny (marble-sized) red berries; runners along ground
**No toxic lookalikes** (mock strawberry *Duchesnea indica* has yellow flowers; insipid but not toxic)
**Uses:** Intensely flavorful compared to commercial strawberries; eat fresh; jam; dry

---

**17. Watercress (*Nasturtium officinale*)**
**Where found:** Shallow running streams, springs, seeps
**Season:** Year-round in mild climates; spring/fall best
**Identification:** Small rounded leaflets; grows directly in water; peppery taste; small white 4-petaled flowers
**Lookalike warning:** Fool's watercress (*Apium nodiflorum*) — edible but less flavorful; true watercress is more aquatic
**Caution:** Never harvest from stagnant water or downstream of agricultural land; potential for liver fluke contamination — cooking eliminates risk
**Uses:** Raw in salads and sandwiches; cooked in soups; high in vitamins C, A, K and calcium

---

**18. Pine (*Pinus* spp.)**
**Where found:** Throughout North America
**Edible parts:** Young needles (spring growth), inner bark, pollen, pine nuts (from nut pines)
**Uses:**
- **Young needles:** Very high in vitamin C (5× oranges); make tea by steeping in hot (not boiling) water; use fresh needles not dried (vitamin C degrades)
- **Pine pollen:** Golden pollen from male cones in spring; collect in bags; nutritious and high in protein; add to food
- **Pine nuts:** Only from piñon pine (*P. edulis*) and related species; collect from female cones; roast or eat raw
- **Inner bark (cambium):** Scrape from young branches in spring; high in sugars; eat raw or dry and grind as flour additive
**Caution:** Yew (*Taxus*) — NOT a pine; has single needles arranged flat, red berries, extremely toxic. True pines have needles in bundles of 2, 3, or 5.

---

**19. Burdock (*Arctium lappa*, *A. minus*)**
**Where found:** Disturbed soil, roadsides, forest edges
**Season:** Spring (young leaves); first-year root best in fall; second-year root in spring
**Identification:** Very large heart-shaped leaves (2–3 ft wide); hollow leaf stalks; burrs in second year; biennial plant
**Uses:**
- First-year root (like parsnip): peel; slice; boil or stir-fry; "gobo" in Japanese cuisine; nutty, slightly sweet
- Young leaf stalks: peel the bitter outer layer; boil and eat like celery
- Seeds of leaves if young enough
**Lookalike warning:** Rhubarb leaves — TOXIC (oxalic acid); but rhubarb grows in gardens, has red stalks; burdock has hollow stalks

---

**20. Redbud (*Cercis canadensis*) / Clover (*Trifolium* spp.) / Chicory (*Cichorium intybus*)**
Three bonus plants commonly found together in fields and edges:

**Redbud flowers:** Bright pink flowers before leaves emerge in spring; eat raw or sauté; high in vitamin C; tart

**Clover (*Trifolium repens*, *T. pratense*):** White and red clover widespread in lawns/fields; flowers edible raw (sweet); young leaves edible; dry flowers for flour additive; tea from flowers; high in protein

**Chicory:** Blue daisy-like flowers; basal rosette of leaves (like dandelion but leaves are hairy); all parts edible; young leaves in salad (bitter); root roasted as coffee substitute (same as commercial chicory coffee); flowers edible

**Pacific Northwest:** Focus on nettles, miner's lettuce, salal berries, Oregon grape berries, fiddlehead ferns (ostrich fern only)

**Plains/Midwest:** Lamb's quarters, purslane, wild plum, ground cherry
