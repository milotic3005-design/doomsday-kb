## PART 1: PLANT BIOLOGY FOR FOOD PRODUCTION

### Plant Physiology Fundamentals

**Photosynthesis**:
6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

- **Light reactions**: occur in thylakoid membranes; capture solar energy; produce ATP and NADPH
- **Calvin cycle** (dark reactions): occur in stroma; use ATP/NADPH to fix CO₂ into sugars
- **Chlorophyll absorption**: peaks at red (670 nm) and blue (430 nm); reflects green → why plants appear green
- **C3 plants**: most vegetables, wheat, rice; efficient in cool, humid conditions; less efficient at high temps
- **C4 plants**: corn, sorghum, sugarcane; more efficient at high temps, high light, drought
- **CAM plants**: cactus, agave, pineapple; open stomata at night; extremely drought-tolerant

**Transpiration and Water Movement**:
- Roots absorb water via osmosis (soil water potential > root cell water potential)
- Water moves through xylem by negative pressure (transpiration pull)
- Stomata open for CO₂ entry during photosynthesis → water vapor exits
- Hot, windy, low-humidity conditions → maximum transpiration → water stress

**Stomata Control**:
- Guard cells: become turgid when potassium enters → stomata open
- Light triggers opening; darkness triggers closing
- CO₂ concentration: high CO₂ → stomata close (drought adaptation)
- Water stress: ABA (abscisic acid) hormone → stomata close → wilting before damage

**Nutrient Functions** (the 17 essential elements):

*Primary macronutrients*:
- **N (Nitrogen)**: amino acids, proteins, chlorophyll; deficiency → yellowing of older leaves (chlorosis)
- **P (Phosphorus)**: ATP, DNA, root development, flowering; deficiency → purple coloration, poor roots
- **K (Potassium)**: enzyme activation, stomata, disease resistance; deficiency → brown leaf margins (scorch)

*Secondary macronutrients*:
- **Ca (Calcium)**: cell walls, meristem function; deficiency → blossom end rot (tomato), tip burn (lettuce)
- **Mg (Magnesium)**: center of chlorophyll molecule; deficiency → interveinal chlorosis on young leaves
- **S (Sulfur)**: amino acids (cysteine, methionine); deficiency → yellowing of young leaves

*Micronutrients*:
- **Fe (Iron)**: chlorophyll synthesis; deficiency → interveinal chlorosis on young leaves (similar to Mg but young leaves)
- **Mn (Manganese)**: oxygen evolution in photosynthesis; deficiency → interveinal chlorosis
- **Zn (Zinc)**: auxin synthesis, many enzymes; deficiency → small leaves, shortened internodes
- **B (Boron)**: cell wall formation, pollen germination; deficiency → hollow stems, bud death
- **Cu (Copper)**: lignin synthesis, enzyme function; deficiency → wilting, blue-green tint
- **Mo (Molybdenum)**: nitrogen fixation, nitrate reduction; deficiency → whiptail (cauliflower), marginal scorch

### Soil Science

**Soil Texture Triangle**:
- Sand: 0.05–2 mm particles; fast drainage; low nutrient holding; warms quickly
- Silt: 0.002–0.05 mm; medium drainage; moderate nutrient holding
- Clay: <0.002 mm; slow drainage; high nutrient holding; compacts easily
- Loam: balanced mixture (~40% sand, 40% silt, 20% clay) → ideal for most crops

**Determining Soil Texture by Feel**:
1. Take moist soil sample
2. Sand: gritty feel; falls apart
3. Silt: silky/floury feel; ribbon forms but breaks quickly
4. Clay: sticky; forms a ribbon >25mm before breaking
5. Loam: ribbons 12–25mm; slightly gritty with some silkiness

**Soil pH Effects**:
- 6.0–7.0: optimal for most vegetables
- <6.0: acidic → Al and Mn become toxic; P unavailable; reduced bacterial activity
- >7.5: alkaline → Fe, Mn, Zn become unavailable; classic "lime-induced chlorosis"

**Adjusting Soil pH**:
- Raise pH (reduce acidity): agricultural lime (calcium carbonate) — 2–4 tons/acre for 1 unit change
- Faster: burned lime (calcium oxide) or slaked lime (calcium hydroxide)
- Lower pH (increase acidity): elemental sulfur (bacteria convert to sulfuric acid — slow process)
- Faster: aluminum sulfate — avoid overuse (aluminum toxicity)
- Always test before amending; add lime 3–6 months before planting when possible

**Cation Exchange Capacity (CEC)**:
- Measure of soil's ability to hold positively charged nutrients (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, K⁺, Na⁺)
- High CEC (clay, organic matter): high nutrient-holding capacity
- Low CEC (sand): nutrients leach quickly; need more frequent fertilization
- Target base saturation: Ca 60–80%, Mg 10–20%, K 2–5%, Na <5%

**Soil Organic Matter Benefits**:
- Every 1% organic matter holds 20,000+ gallons water per acre
- Improves structure (aggregation) → better drainage AND water holding
- Feeds soil biology (fungi, bacteria, earthworms)
- Source of nutrients through mineralization
- Buffers pH changes
- Target: 3–5% organic matter for most soils (most depleted soils are 0.5–2%)

**Soil Biology**:
- 1 tablespoon healthy soil: 1 billion bacteria, several yards of fungal hyphae
- **Bacteria**: decompose organic matter; fix nitrogen (Rhizobium in legume roots)
- **Fungi**: extend root network via mycorrhizae; decompose tough organic matter; improve phosphorus uptake
- **Nematodes**: predatory nematodes control pests; plant-parasitic nematodes cause damage
- **Earthworms**: create channels, mix layers, produce castings (excellent fertilizer)
- **Arthropods**: millipedes, springtails, mites — break down organic matter

**Building Soil Organic Matter**:
- Compost: add 2–4 inches annually and till in, or use as mulch
- Cover crops: plant winter rye, crimson clover, hairy vetch — turn under or mulch
- Mulching: 2–4 inches wood chips, straw, leaves — suppresses weeds, feeds fungi
- Avoid tillage: disrupts fungal networks, oxidizes organic matter → no-till best long-term

### Composting Science

**C:N Ratio (Carbon to Nitrogen)**:
- Target: 25–30:1 for hot composting
- Too much carbon (>60:1): slow decomposition, white fungal growth
- Too much nitrogen (<15:1): slimy, anaerobic, smelly

**Carbon-Rich Materials** (browns, ~30:1 or higher):
- Dry leaves: 40–80:1
- Straw: 40–100:1
- Cardboard: 350:1
- Wood chips: 400–500:1
- Sawdust: 300–500:1

**Nitrogen-Rich Materials** (greens, <20:1):
- Food scraps: 15:1
- Grass clippings: 20:1
- Fresh manure: 5–25:1 (varies by animal)
- Coffee grounds: 20:1
- Green leaves: 30:1

**Hot Composting Process**:
1. Build pile minimum 3'×3'×3' (1 cubic yard) — critical mass for heat
2. Layer browns and greens (or mix thoroughly)
3. Moisten like wrung-out sponge
4. Temperature reaches 55–65°C (130–150°F) within 3–7 days
5. Turn pile when temperature drops below 55°C (usually every 3–7 days)
6. Finished when temperature no longer rises after turning; smells earthy; crumbly

**Pathogen Kill Temperatures**:
- 55°C (131°F) for 3 days: kills most pathogens
- 60°C (140°F) for 1 hour: kills most weed seeds
- Maintain throughout pile by turning — outer edges don't heat as much

## PART 2: FOOD PRODUCTION SYSTEMS

### Garden Planning and Layout

**Crop Planning Calculations**:
- **Per person caloric needs from garden**: ~4,000 sq ft for one person's calories (varies by crop)
- Higher caloric density (more efficient per sq ft): potatoes (800 cal/sq ft/yr), sweet corn, beans
- Lower caloric density: tomatoes, cucumbers, lettuce (important nutrients but few calories)

**Three Sisters Planting** (Iroquois companion planting):
- **Corn**: tall stalk provides structure; plant first
- **Beans**: nitrogen fixers; climb corn stalks; plant when corn 6" tall
- **Squash**: large leaves shade soil, suppress weeds, retain moisture; plant 2 weeks after beans
- Benefits: beans fix 50–200 lbs N/acre; squash reduces soil moisture loss 25%; all three edible

**Square Foot Gardening Spacing** (crops per square foot):
| Crops per 1 sq ft | 4 per sq ft | 9 per sq ft | 16 per sq ft |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cabbage, broccoli | Lettuce, chard | Spinach, beets | Carrots, radishes |
| Peppers, tomatoes | Parsley, basil | Beans | Onions |

**Succession Planting**: plant same crop every 2–3 weeks for continuous harvest
- Lettuce: plant every 14 days from frost date to 6 weeks before fall frost
- Radishes: every 10 days
- Beans: every 3 weeks through mid-summer

**Crop Rotation (4-year cycle)**:
1. Legumes (peas, beans): fix nitrogen
2. Brassicas (cabbage family): use N from legumes
3. Root vegetables: minimal N needed, avoid after brassicas
4. Solanums (tomato, pepper, potato): heavy feeders; follow roots
- Never plant same family in same bed two years in a row (disease buildup)
- Never plant potatoes after tomatoes (both Solanaceae — same diseases)

### Seed Saving

**Open-Pollinated vs Hybrid Seeds**:
- Open-pollinated (OP): breed true from seed; can be saved year after year
- Heirloom: OP varieties 50+ years old
- Hybrid (F1): cross of two distinct parents; high performance first generation; offspring revert to variable types
- GMO: genetically engineered; save seed may violate patents; cannot be grown organically

**Isolation Distances** (prevent cross-pollination):
| Crop | Pollination | Isolation Distance |
|------|------------|-------------------|
| Tomatoes | Self | 25–50 feet (avoid touching flowers) |
| Peppers | Self (some cross) | 150–300 feet |
| Corn | Wind | 1000–1600 feet (or tassels timed differently) |
| Squash | Insect | 1500 feet (or hand pollinate) |
| Beans | Self | 150 feet |
| Lettuce | Self | 25 feet |
| Spinach | Wind | 5 miles! (or cage) |
| Beets/Chard | Wind | 5 miles (biennial — don't flower until year 2) |

**Seed Maturity Signs**:
- Tomatoes: fully colored, slightly soft; let ripen past eating stage
- Corn: husks brown, kernels hard; let dry on plant
- Beans: pods dry, rattling; shell before full desiccation
- Lettuce: seedheads brown, fluffy; collect before wind disperses

**Seed Cleaning**:
- **Wet processing** (tomato, cucumber, squash): ferment in water 2–4 days; viable seeds sink, pulp floats; rinse, dry
- **Dry processing** (beans, corn, lettuce): thresh, winnow (toss in breeze), screen

**Seed Storage**:
- Ideal: cold (40°F/4°C), dry (<40% RH), dark
- Moisture content: dry seeds to <8% moisture before storage
- Test: seeds should break, not bend, when dry enough
- Container: sealed glass jars; add silica gel packet
- Longevity (cool, dry storage): tomato 4–7 years; corn 2–3 years; onion 1–2 years; beans 4–5 years

### Pest and Disease Management

**Integrated Pest Management (IPM) Principles**:
1. Identification: know what you're dealing with
2. Monitoring: regular inspection (weekly at minimum)
3. Threshold: accept some damage; treat when threshold exceeded
4. Prevention: cultural practices first
5. Control: least toxic effective method

**Cultural Controls** (prevention first):
- Crop rotation: breaks pest/disease cycles
- Resistant varieties: many diseases have resistant cultivars
- Timing: early or late planting avoids pest peaks
- Physical barriers: row cover (spunbond fabric), copper tape for slugs
- Sanitation: remove and destroy infected plant material

**Biological Controls**:
- Beneficial insects: ladybugs (aphids), lacewings (many pests), parasitic wasps
- Attract beneficials: plant dill, fennel, yarrow, alyssum near garden
- Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt): bacterial spray for caterpillars; safe for beneficial insects
- Spinosad: derived from soil bacteria; effective on caterpillars, thrips, leafminers
- Neem oil: disrupts insect hormones; broad spectrum; fungicide too

**Common Vegetable Diseases**:

*Fungal diseases (most common)*:
- **Early blight (tomato)**: concentric ring spots on older leaves → use resistant varieties, copper spray
- **Late blight (tomato/potato)**: water-soaked lesions in cool/wet weather → no good organic treatment; destroy plants
- **Powdery mildew**: white powder on leaves; warm days, cool nights, dry leaves → baking soda spray, sulfur
- **Damping off**: seedlings collapse at soil line → good drainage, air circulation, don't overwater

*Bacterial diseases*:
- **Bacterial wilt** (cucumber, squash): wilts rapidly even with water; spread by cucumber beetles
- **Fire blight** (fruit trees): shoots die back as if burned; prune 12" below, disinfect pruners

*Viral diseases*:
- **Mosaic viruses**: mottled yellow-green leaves; spread by aphids; remove infected plants
- **Tomato spotted wilt**: bronze ring spots; spread by thrips; use reflective mulch

**Natural Pesticide Recipes**:
- **Soap spray** (soft-bodied insects): 1 tbsp liquid castile soap per quart water; spray directly on pests
- **Garlic spray**: blend 3-4 cloves + water; strain; apply to repel insects
- **Diatomaceous earth**: dust around plants; kills insects by abrasion/desiccation; reapply after rain
- **Kaolin clay**: white coating on plants; confuses insects; effective against many pests

## PART 3: FOOD PRESERVATION SCIENCE

### Canning Principles

**Why Food Spoils**:
- Bacteria: Clostridium botulinum produces botulinum toxin at 4–121°C in anaerobic conditions
- Yeasts and molds: grow on surface; generally destroyed by boiling
- Enzymes: continue chemical reactions after harvest; inactivated by heat/acid

**Water Bath Canning** (high-acid foods only, pH <4.6):
- Appropriate for: fruits, pickles, jams, tomatoes with added acid (lemon juice or citric acid)
- NOT for: vegetables, meat, beans, low-acid foods
- Process: fully submerge sealed jars in boiling water; process specified time (varies by altitude)
- Altitude adjustment: above 1000 feet, increase processing time (boiling point is lower)
- At 5000 ft: water boils at 95°C, not 100°C → heat sterilization less effective → add 5 min/1000 ft

**Pressure Canning** (low-acid foods):
- Necessary for: vegetables, meat, fish, beans, soups
- Kills Clostridium botulinum spores (which water bath cannot)
- Temperature needed: 116°C (240°F) = achievable only with pressure
- 10 lbs pressure at sea level = 116°C
- Altitude adjustment: increase pressure 1 lb for every 2000 feet elevation

**Botulism Warning Signs**:
- Spurting liquid when opened, off odors, unusual texture, broken seal
- Swollen lids (CO₂ from bacteria)
- BUT: botulism toxin may be present with NO visible signs — follow tested recipes exactly
- Never taste-test suspicious canned food — botulism can be lethal in micrograms

**Fermentation Science**:
- Lactic acid bacteria (LAB): convert sugars to lactic acid; lower pH → preserve food
- Salt concentration: 2–3% by weight inhibits pathogens but allows LAB
  - Too little salt (<1.5%): mold growth, spoilage
  - Too much salt (>5%): inhibits LAB; pickles only, not ferments
- Temperature: 65–75°F optimal; warmer = faster but higher spoilage risk
- Anaerobic environment: keep vegetables submerged below brine; no air contact

**Salt Calculation for Fermentation**:
- 2% brine by weight of vegetables
- Example: 1000g cabbage for sauerkraut → 20g salt (about 1 tablespoon)
- 3.5% brine for whole fermented vegetables
- To make 1L of 3.5% brine: dissolve 35g salt in 1L water

### Food Dehydration

**Why Drying Preserves Food**:
- Microorganisms need water (water activity, aw) to grow
- aw >0.91: bacteria can grow
- aw >0.70: molds can grow
- aw >0.60: yeasts can grow
- Dried foods target aw <0.60 → microbes cannot grow

**Water Activity Targets**:
- Jerky: aw <0.85 (USDA requires further heat treatment after drying)
- Fruits: aw <0.70
- Vegetables: aw <0.60
- Herbs: aw <0.65

**Drying Temperatures**:
| Food | Temperature | Time |
|------|------------|------|
| Herbs | 95°F (35°C) | 2–4 hours |
| Fruits | 130–140°F (55–60°C) | 6–36 hours |
| Vegetables | 125–135°F (52–57°C) | 4–12 hours |
| Meat/jerky | 160–165°F (71–74°C) | 4–15 hours |

**Solar Drying** (off-grid):
- Effective in hot, dry climates with low humidity
- Construction: wooden frame with mesh/screen shelves; angled glass or plastic top; dark interior
- Temperature inside: should reach 60–70°C on sunny day
- Airflow: openings at bottom and top (chimney effect)
- Limitations: humidity >60% makes drying difficult; risk of spoilage if temp too low

**Pretreatment Before Drying**:
- **Blanching** (vegetables): boil 2–4 min, ice bath; inactivates enzymes; preserves color/texture
- **Sulfuring** (fruits): prevents browning; soak in 1 tsp sodium metabisulfite per quart water 5 min; controversial
- **Ascorbic acid** (fruits): 1 tsp vitamin C powder per quart water; prevents browning, gentler than sulfur

### Root Cellaring and Cold Storage

**Temperature and Humidity Requirements**:
| Condition | Examples |
|-----------|---------|
| Cold (32–40°F) + Moist (90–95% RH) | Carrots, beets, turnips, parsnips, celery, cabbage |
| Cold (32–40°F) + Dry (60–70% RH) | Garlic, onions, dried beans |
| Cool (40–50°F) + Moist (85–90% RH) | Potatoes |
| Moderate (50–60°F) + Dry (60–70% RH) | Winter squash, pumpkins, sweet potatoes |

**Building a Simple Root Cellar**:
- Best: below-grade room on north side of house
- Temperature stabilization: 4–6 feet of soil provides stable temps
- Ventilation: two pipes — one at floor level (cool air in), one at ceiling (warm air out)
- Insulate from heated house interior
- Drainage: French drain if water infiltration possible

**Storage Times** (optimal conditions):
- Potatoes: 4–6 months
- Carrots: 4–6 months (in sand or leaves)
- Beets: 3–5 months
- Winter squash: 3–6 months
- Onions/garlic: 6–8 months
- Apples: 2–6 months (keep away from vegetables — ethylene ripens them)

## PART 4: ANIMAL HUSBANDRY

### Chickens

**Production Basics**:
- Laying hens: 250–300 eggs/year (peak), drops to 150–200 in year 2–3
- Egg production requires 14–16 hours light (supplement with bulbs in winter)
- Feed requirements: 1/4 lb (115g) commercial feed per bird per day
- Water: ~0.5 liter per bird per day (doubles in heat)

**Nutritional Requirements**:
- Laying hens: 16–18% protein; 3.5–4.5% calcium (for shells); adequate phosphorus
- Calcium supplements: oyster shell free-choice; or crush and feed back used eggshells
- Scratch grains: treat only (<10% diet); no nutritional balance
- Grit: necessary for digestion (grinding food in gizzard); small gravel works

**Broiler Production Timeline**:
- Cornish Cross: market weight (5–6 lb) at 6–8 weeks
- Heritage breeds: 12–16 weeks to market weight, but more flavorful
- Feed conversion: 2–3 lbs feed per 1 lb gain

**Common Diseases**:
- **Marek's Disease**: neurological paralysis; vaccinate at hatch (must be done within 24 hours)
- **Coccidiosis**: bloody diarrhea in young birds; use medicated chick starter or coccidiostat in water
- **Newcastle Disease**: respiratory, neurological; no treatment; vaccination for large flocks
- **Infectious Bronchitis**: respiratory; secondary infections; supportive care
- **Mycoplasma (CRD)**: chronic respiratory; bubbling eyes; treat with tylosin or tetracycline

**Pasture Management**:
- Rotate to fresh ground frequently; prevents worm buildup
- Chickens improve pasture with manure but destroy vegetation if overcrowded
- Maximum stocking: 50–75 birds per acre in rotation; up to 500 in commercial managed systems

### Goats and Sheep

**Comparative Overview**:
| Property | Goats | Sheep |
|----------|-------|-------|
| Browsers/Grazers | Browsers (shrubs, varied) | Grazers (grass) |
| Fencing | Very difficult (climbers) | Moderate |
| Milk per day | 2–3 liters (dairy breeds) | 0.5–1 liter |
| Fiber | Mohair, cashmere | Wool |
| Disease resistance | Better | More susceptible |
| Cold hardiness | Moderate | Good (wool) |

**Goat Feed Requirements**:
- Forage (hay/browse): 4–5 lbs per 100 lbs body weight per day
- Grain supplement for milking does: 1 lb per 2–3 lbs milk production
- Free-choice minerals: goat-specific (different from cattle minerals)
- Fresh water: 1–4 gallons/day depending on lactation, temperature

**Goat Health Basics**:
- **Deworming**: FAMACHA method — assess eyelid color (anemia from barber pole worm)
  - Score 1 (pink/red): no treatment needed
  - Score 3: borderline
  - Score 4–5 (white): treat immediately; use dewormers strategically to prevent resistance
- **CDT vaccination**: annual booster for Clostridium perfringens types C&D and tetanus
- **Caseous lymphadenitis (CL)**: lumps under jaw/shoulder; chronic; vaccine available
- **Foot rot/foot scald**: trim hooves regularly; dry conditions; zinc sulfate foot bath

**Basic Sheep Production Calendar**:
- Breeding: October–November (5-month gestation)
- Lambing: March–April (matches spring grass)
- Shearing: before hot weather (May in most regions)
- Weaning: 8–12 weeks
- Market lambs: 5–7 months old

### Swine

**Growth Performance**:
- Birth weight: ~3 lbs
- Market weight: 250 lbs at 5–6 months
- Feed conversion: 3 lbs feed per lb gain
- Daily gain: ~1.8–2.0 lbs/day with adequate feeding

**Feeding Strategy**:
- Starter (birth–50 lbs): 22–24% protein
- Grower (50–125 lbs): 16–18% protein
- Finisher (125 lbs–market): 14–15% protein
- Water: 1 gallon per pound of dry feed consumed (critical)

**Swine Foraging Supplementation**:
- Pigs are excellent foragers — can get 20–30% nutrition from pasture
- Excellent for converting food scraps, garden waste, dairy byproducts to meat
- Pannage (feeding on fallen mast — acorns, chestnuts): traditional European practice; produces exceptional flavored pork

**Breeding**:
- Gilts (young females): breed at 7–8 months, ~250 lbs
- Gestation: 114 days (3 months, 3 weeks, 3 days — easy mnemonic)
- Litter size: 10–12 piglets
- Pigs: 2 litters per year possible

### Cattle

**Basic Cattle Production**:
- Beef breeds: Angus, Hereford, Simmental — frame sizes vary
- Dairy breeds: Holstein (highest milk volume), Jersey (highest butterfat), Guernsey
- Market steer: 18–24 months, 1000–1200 lbs
- Cow gestation: 285 days (approximately 9.5 months)
- Calving interval: aim for one calf per cow per year

**Pasture and Hay Requirements**:
- 1 Animal Unit (1000 lb cow): needs 1–2 acres pasture in good grass regions
- Supplemental hay: 2–3% of body weight per day = 25–30 lbs/day for 1000 lb cow
- Minerals: salt + trace mineral free-choice; selenium deficiency common in many regions

**Basic Health Protocols**:
- Annual vaccines: IBR, BVD, PI3, BRSV (Bovine Respiratory Disease complex); Blackleg (7-way clostridial)
- Deworming: strategic — don't treat entire herd; FAMACHA or fecal egg count for targeting
- Foot care: inspect regularly; copper sulfate foot bath prevents foot rot in wet conditions
- Brucellosis: vaccinate heifers 4–12 months old with RB51 (legal requirement many states)

## PART 5: PERENNIAL FOOD SYSTEMS

### Fruit Tree Establishment

**Site Selection**:
- Sun: minimum 6–8 hours direct; south-facing slopes ideal in Northern Hemisphere
- Air drainage: cold air flows down; avoid frost pockets (low valleys)
- Soil: deep, well-drained; most fruit trees tolerate pH 6.0–7.0
- Water: no flooding; most fruit trees die in standing water >24 hours

**Rootstock Selection**:
- Determines mature tree size, precocity (speed to bearing), and adaptability
- Dwarf rootstocks (M9, G41): 8–10 feet; bear in 2–3 years; need support and good soil
- Semi-dwarf (M26, M7): 12–16 feet; bear in 3–5 years; most practical for homesteads
- Standard (seedling): 20–30+ feet; bear in 7–10 years; longest-lived; drought tolerant

**Pollination Requirements**:
- Apples: most need cross-pollination; plant 2+ compatible varieties within 100 feet
- Pears: cross-pollination required; Bartlett × Bosc
- Sweet cherries: most need cross-pollinator
- Sour cherries: self-fertile
- Peaches: most self-fertile
- Plums: Japanese plums need cross-pollinator; European plums often self-fertile

**Pruning Principles**:
- Remove: dead/diseased wood, water sprouts (vertical), crossing branches, inward-growing
- Shape: open center (peaches, cherries) or central leader (apples, pears)
- When: late dormant pruning (February–March) before bud break; avoid fall pruning
- Wound treatment: NOT necessary for most pruning cuts (sap flow carries pathogens away); large cuts may benefit from pruning paint

**Chill Hours** (cold requirement for fruit production):
- Most apples: 800–1200 chill hours (<45°F)
- Low-chill varieties: 200–400 hours (for warm climates)
- Peaches: most need 600–900 hours
- Figs, persimmons: very low chill requirement (<300 hours)
- Track your local chill hours to select appropriate varieties

### Permaculture Principles

**Observe and Interact**: Study your land through all seasons before making permanent changes

**Zones** (design based on frequency of use):
- Zone 1: Closest to house; daily attention; kitchen garden, herbs, salad greens
- Zone 2: Frequent visits; main vegetable beds, small fruits, chickens
- Zone 3: Weekly; orchards, main crops, beehives
- Zone 4: Occasional; woodlot, large animals, nut trees
- Zone 5: Rarely visited; wilderness, observation, inspiration

**Stacking Functions**: Every element serves multiple purposes
- Tree: fruit + shade + windbreak + wildlife habitat + carbon sequestration
- Chicken: eggs + meat + pest control + fertility + tilling

**Guilds**: Beneficial plant associations
- Apple tree guild: apple + comfrey (dynamic accumulator, mulch) + yarrow (attracts beneficials) + daffodil (repels voles from roots) + chives (repels aphids) + nasturtium (trap crop)

**Key Perennial Vegetables** (plant once, harvest for years):
- Jerusalem artichoke: 50–150 bushels/acre; extremely productive; invasive potential
- Asparagus: 15–20 year production; bear in year 3+
- Rhubarb: reliably productive; only stalks edible (leaves toxic)
- Perennial kale/tree collards: regrows from cuttings; woody stem; productive for years
- Walking onions: self-propagating topset onions
- Comfrey: deep roots bring up minerals; use as mulch/green manure; do NOT eat
